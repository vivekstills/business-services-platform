## What Are TDS and TCS? A Quick Overview

Tax Deducted at Source (TDS) and Tax Collected at Source (TCS) are two mechanisms through which the Indian government collects income tax at the point of transaction rather than at the end of the financial year. While both serve the broader goal of widening the tax net and preventing evasion, they operate very differently in terms of who bears the responsibility, when they apply, and how they are reported.

This article cuts through the confusion and gives you a clear, side-by-side understanding of both concepts, with practical examples and compliance pointers.

---

## The Core Difference: Who Deducts or Collects?

The most fundamental distinction lies in the party responsible:

- **TDS** is deducted by the payer (buyer, employer, or service recipient) at the time of making a payment.
- **TCS** is collected by the seller (or specified collector) at the time of receiving payment or raising a sale consideration.

In TDS, the burden falls on the one making the payment. In TCS, the seller adds tax on top of the sale price and collects it from the buyer.

## TDS vs TCS: Comprehensive Comparison Table

| Parameter | TDS (Tax Deducted at Source) | TCS (Tax Collected at Source) |
|---|---|---|
| Full Form | Tax Deducted at Source | Tax Collected at Source |
| Governed By | Sections 192 to 196D of Income Tax Act | Section 206C of Income Tax Act |
| Who is Responsible | Payer / Deductor | Seller / Collector |
| Who Bears the Tax | Payee / Deductee | Buyer / Collectee |
| When Applied | At the time of payment or credit, whichever is earlier | At the time of sale / receipt of payment |
| Nature of Transaction | Services, salary, rent, professional fees, interest, etc. | Sale of specified goods (scrap, timber, minerals, alcohol, etc.) |
| Form for Return Filing | 24Q (salary), 26Q (non-salary), 27Q (NRI payments) | 27EQ |
| Certificate Issued | Form 16 / Form 16A | Form 27D |
| Deposit Deadline | 7th of following month (30th April for March deductions) | 7th of following month |
| Reflected in | Form 26AS / AIS | Form 26AS / AIS |
| PAN Applicability | Mandatory; higher rate if PAN absent | Mandatory; higher rate if PAN absent |

## Applicability: When Does Each Apply?

### TDS is applicable on:
- Salary payments (Section 192)
- Interest on securities and bank deposits (Section 193, 194A)
- Dividends (Section 194)
- Commission and brokerage (Section 194H)
- Rent (Section 194I)
- Professional and technical fees (Section 194J)
- Payments to contractors (Section 194C)
- Purchase of immovable property (Section 194IA)

### TCS is applicable on sale of:
- Scrap
- Timber obtained under a forest lease
- Tendu leaves
- Alcoholic liquor for human consumption
- Minerals (coal, lignite, iron ore)
- Motor vehicles above Rs. 10 lakhs
- Foreign remittances under Liberalised Remittance Scheme (LRS)
- Overseas tour packages
- Sale of goods above Rs. 50 lakhs (Section 206C(1H))

## Practical Example: TDS vs TCS in Action

**Example 1 (TDS):**
Company XYZ hires a chartered accountant for audit services and agrees to pay Rs. 1,00,000. Since this exceeds the Rs. 30,000 threshold under Section 194J, XYZ must deduct TDS at 10%, i.e., Rs. 10,000. The CA receives Rs. 90,000 net and can claim credit for Rs. 10,000 in their ITR.

**Example 2 (TCS):**
A licensed alcohol manufacturer sells liquor worth Rs. 2,00,000 to a distributor. The manufacturer must collect TCS at 1% on Rs. 2,00,000 = Rs. 2,000. The distributor pays Rs. 2,02,000 in total. The Rs. 2,000 collected is deposited by the manufacturer with the government, and the distributor gets credit for it in Form 26AS.

## Compliance Steps for Both

### For TDS Compliance:
1. Obtain TAN (Tax Deduction and Collection Account Number)
2. Deduct tax at applicable rate at the time of payment or credit, whichever is earlier
3. Deposit TDS with the government using Challan 281 by 7th of the next month
4. File quarterly TDS returns (Form 24Q / 26Q / 27Q)
5. Issue TDS certificates (Form 16 / 16A) to deductees

### For TCS Compliance:
1. Obtain TAN
2. Collect tax from buyer at the time of receipt or debiting the buyer's account
3. Deposit TCS using Challan 281 by 7th of the next month
4. File quarterly TCS returns in Form 27EQ
5. Issue Form 27D to the buyer

| Disallowance of expense (TDS) | 30% of expenditure disallowed u/s 40(a)(ia) | Not applicable |

## FAQs

**Q1. Can a transaction attract both TDS and TCS?**
Generally no. However, under Section 194Q (TDS on purchase of goods) and Section 206C(1H) (TCS on sale of goods), both could technically apply to the same transaction. The CBDT has clarified that if TDS is deducted under 194Q, the seller is not required to collect TCS under 206C(1H) on that transaction.

**Q2. What happens if the deductee does not have a PAN?**
Under both TDS and TCS, if the deductee or collectee does not furnish a PAN, tax must be deducted or collected at the higher of: the applicable rate, 5%, or 20% (for TCS under certain provisions). The Income Tax Department has also introduced Aadhaar-based linking as a partial remedy.

**Q3. Is TCS an additional tax burden on the buyer?**
No. TCS is a tax collected in advance and is fully adjustable against the buyer's final tax liability while filing their Income Tax Return. It reflects in their Form 26AS, from where it can be claimed as credit.

**Q4. Who is exempt from TDS deduction obligations?**
Individuals and Hindu Undivided Families (HUFs) are generally not required to deduct TDS unless they are subject to tax audit under Section 44AB. However, Section 194IA (property purchase) applies to all buyers regardless of audit status.

**Q5. Can TDS be avoided through a lower deduction certificate?**
Yes. A deductee can apply to the Assessing Officer under Section 197 for a certificate authorising the deductor to deduct TDS at a lower rate or nil rate. This is common for professionals and vendors with substantial business expenses that reduce their net taxable income.
