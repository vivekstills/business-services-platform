## Introduction to Section 194I

Section 194I covers the deduction of TDS on rent paid by businesses and specified individuals to landlords. This provision applies not just to traditional property rentals but also to machine and equipment hire, ensuring that rental income across asset categories is brought within the TDS framework.

Given the scale of commercial real estate transactions and equipment leasing in India, Section 194I is one of the most frequently applicable TDS provisions for businesses of all sizes.

---

## Definition of Rent Under Section 194I

"Rent" under Section 194I means any payment, by whatever name called, for the use of:
- Land
- Building (including factory buildings)
- Machinery
- Plant
- Equipment
- Furniture or fittings
- Whether or not owned by the payee

This broad definition means that even informal arrangements where a business uses a third-party's assets for a recurring payment can trigger Section 194I.

---

## Rate Table: TDS Under Section 194I

| Asset Type | TDS Rate |
|---|---|
| Land and building (including factory buildings) | 10% |
| Plant and machinery | 2% |
| Equipment (computers, servers, etc.) | 2% |
| Furniture and fittings | 10% |
| Payee without PAN | 20% |

---

## Threshold Limits

| Parameter | Annual Threshold |
|---|---|
| Rent paid to a single landlord | Rs. 2,40,000 per financial year |
| Below this | No TDS required |
| Monthly equivalent | Approximately Rs. 20,000/month |

---

## Applicability

### Who Must Deduct TDS Under 194I?
- Companies and LLPs
- Firms
- HUFs and individuals liable to tax audit
- Government departments and statutory bodies

### Who Is Exempt?
- Individuals and HUFs not subject to tax audit
- Tenants paying rent for residential purposes in a personal capacity

---

## Special Provision: Section 194IB (For Individuals and HUFs)

Individuals and HUFs not subject to tax audit but paying monthly rent above Rs. 50,000 must deduct TDS under Section 194IB at 5%. This is a separate provision for individual tenants in high-value residential rentals and requires filing only once a year (not quarterly), using Form 26QC.

| Section | Applicability | Rate | Threshold |
|---|---|---|---|
| 194I | Businesses, audit-liable individuals | 10% / 2% | Rs. 2,40,000/year |
| 194IB | Non-audit individuals / HUFs | 5% | Rs. 50,000/month |

## Example Calculation

**Scenario 1: Office Rent**
Nexus Technologies Pvt. Ltd. rents an office in Bangalore for Rs. 2,00,000 per month from Global Properties Ltd.

Annual rent = Rs. 24,00,000
Since this exceeds Rs. 2,40,000, TDS applies.
Monthly TDS = 10% of Rs. 2,00,000 = Rs. 20,000
Nexus pays Global Properties: Rs. 1,80,000 per month and deposits Rs. 20,000 with the government.

**Scenario 2: Machinery Hire**
A construction company hires an excavator from a plant hire company for Rs. 5,00,000 for the quarter.
TDS = 2% of Rs. 5,00,000 = Rs. 10,000
Net payment to plant hire company = Rs. 4,90,000

---

## Handling GST on Rent

TDS under Section 194I is calculated on the rent amount exclusive of GST. If the landlord raises a GST invoice separately showing rent and GST, TDS is deducted only on the rent portion. If the invoice does not separate GST, TDS may be computed on the entire amount, which creates an anomaly. It is therefore advisable to always ask landlords to issue GST-compliant invoices that clearly bifurcate the base rent and GST.

---

## Compliance Steps

1. Verify the annual rent liability to determine if it crosses Rs. 2,40,000
2. Obtain PAN of the landlord; if not available, deduct at 20%
3. Deduct TDS at 10% (land/building) or 2% (machinery/equipment) at the time of payment or credit
4. Deposit TDS using Challan 281 by the 7th of the following month
5. File quarterly TDS return in Form 26Q
6. Issue Form 16A to the landlord after each quarter

---

## Penalties

| Default | Consequence |
|---|---|
| Non-deduction of TDS on rent | Interest 1% per month + 30% disallowance of rent expense |
| Late deposit | Interest 1.5% per month |
| Late / non-filing of return | Rs. 200 per day under Section 234E |
| Non-filing | Penalty Rs. 10,000 to Rs. 1,00,000 under Section 271H |
| Non-issuance of Form 16A | Rs. 100 per day under Section 272A |

## FAQs

**Q1. Is TDS applicable on lease premium (one-time deposit) paid at the start of a lease?**
Generally no. A security deposit or lease premium that is refundable is not "rent" in the traditional sense and does not attract TDS under Section 194I. However, if the lease premium is a non-refundable upfront payment for the right to use property, it may be treated as advance rent and TDS would apply. The classification depends on the terms of the lease agreement.

**Q2. A company rents two separate properties from the same landlord, each at Rs. 1,50,000 per year (total Rs. 3,00,000). Does TDS apply?**
Yes. The threshold of Rs. 2,40,000 is assessed on total payments made to the same landlord during the year, regardless of whether they arise from one or multiple properties. Since the aggregate is Rs. 3,00,000, TDS applies on all payments.

**Q3. Is TDS applicable on rent paid to a State Government or Central Government entity?**
No. Payments to the Government (Central or State) are not subject to TDS since the Government cannot pay taxes to itself. However, rent paid to a Government-owned public sector undertaking (PSU) is subject to TDS, since PSUs are separate legal entities.

**Q4. What if the property is jointly owned by two people? Should TDS be split?**
Yes. If a property is jointly owned by two people (say, husband and wife), the tenant should ideally split the rental payment and deduct TDS separately, based on their ownership shares, and issue two separate Form 16A certificates. In practice, if the joint owners have not communicated their share, TDS is often deducted on the total amount against one PAN, which may need to be corrected later.

**Q5. Does Section 194I apply to Co-working space membership fees?**
This is a nuanced area. If a co-working membership gives an exclusive right to use a specific desk or office, it may qualify as rent under Section 194I. If it is a flexi-desk arrangement (no fixed desk, access to shared space), it is more likely a service and may be covered under Section 194J instead. Courts and CBDT have not issued a definitive ruling, so professional advice is recommended based on the specific agreement.
