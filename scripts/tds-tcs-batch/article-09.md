## What is Section 194H?

Section 194H mandates deduction of TDS on commission or brokerage paid or credited to a resident person. This section applies to intermediaries in trade, financial transactions, insurance, and real estate, among others.

Commission and brokerage are among the most common payments in business-to-business transactions, making Section 194H particularly relevant for distributors, marketing agents, insurance agents, and brokers.

---

## Definition of Commission and Brokerage

Under the Income Tax Act, commission or brokerage includes any payment received or receivable:
- Directly or indirectly, for services rendered in connection with any transaction relating to any asset, valuable article, or thing (not being securities)
- For services rendered in course of buying and selling of goods, services, or transactions

**Excluded from 194H:** Commission paid on securities transactions (covered separately under SEBI regulations / Section 196D for FIIs).

---

## Rate Table: TDS Under Section 194H

| Category | TDS Rate |
|---|---|
| Commission or brokerage (with PAN) | 5% |
| Commission or brokerage (without PAN) | 20% |
| Insurance commission under Section 194D | 5% (separate section) |

---

## Threshold Limits

| Parameter | Limit |
|---|---|
| Annual threshold for TDS | Rs. 15,000 |
| Below this limit | No TDS required |

---

## Applicability

### Who Must Deduct?
- Companies
- Firms and LLPs
- HUFs subject to tax audit
- Individuals subject to tax audit
- Central and State Government bodies

### Who Is Excluded?
- Individuals and HUFs not covered by tax audit
- Payments to banking institutions for their professional services (not commission)

### Common Transactions Covered:
- Distributor commission from a company to its sales representatives
- Real estate broker fees
- Clearing and forwarding agent commissions
- Advertising agency commission
- Referral fees for business leads

---

## Transactions NOT Covered Under Section 194H

| Payment | Reason Not Covered |
|---|---|
| Discount given to dealer on MRP | Not commission; it is a trade discount |
| Overriding commission on secondary sales | Debated; some courts treat it as not 194H if not for direct services |
| Commission to banks and financial institutions | Exempt explicitly under proviso to Section 194H |
| Brokerage on securities transactions | Covered under SEBI regulations or Section 196D |

## Example Calculation

**Scenario 1: FMCG Company and Distributor**
FreshFoods Ltd. pays Rs. 3,00,000 as commission to its regional distributor Super Traders in Q3.
Since Rs. 3,00,000 exceeds Rs. 15,000, TDS applies:
TDS = 5% of Rs. 3,00,000 = Rs. 15,000
Super Traders receives: Rs. 2,85,000 net.

**Scenario 2: Real Estate Broker**
A real estate developer pays Rs. 2,50,000 as brokerage to a property agent on completion of a transaction.
TDS = 5% of Rs. 2,50,000 = Rs. 12,500
Effective receipt for broker = Rs. 2,37,500

---

## Compliance Steps

1. Determine whether the payment qualifies as commission or brokerage under Section 194H
2. Check if the aggregate commission to the same person in the financial year exceeds Rs. 15,000
3. Deduct 5% TDS at the time of credit or payment, whichever is earlier
4. Deposit TDS by the 7th of the following month using Challan 281
5. File quarterly return in Form 26Q
6. Issue Form 16A to the agent or broker after each quarter

---

## Penalties

| Default | Consequence |
|---|---|
| Non-deduction | Interest 1% per month + disallowance of expense under Section 40(a)(ia) |
| Late deposit after deduction | Interest 1.5% per month |
| Late or non-filing of 26Q | Rs. 200 per day; penalty up to Rs. 1 lakh under Section 271H |
| Wrong rate applied | Treated as short deduction; interest applies |

## FAQs

**Q1. Does trade discount given by a manufacturer to a dealer attract TDS under Section 194H?**
No. A trade discount is a reduction in price offered to dealers for bulk purchases and is not considered commission or brokerage. It does not represent payment for a service rendered by the dealer; it is simply a price concession. Therefore, TDS under Section 194H does not apply to trade discounts.

**Q2. If an insurance company pays commission to an individual agent, is it covered under 194H or 194D?**
Insurance commission is specifically governed by Section 194D, not Section 194H. Section 194D applies to commission paid by insurers to insurance agents at a rate of 5%, with a threshold of Rs. 15,000. Section 194H covers other forms of commission and brokerage not specifically addressed by other sections.

**Q3. How is TDS handled on commission paid to NRI agents?**
Commission paid to NRI agents falls under Section 195, not Section 194H. The applicable rate depends on the nature of income, the relevant DTAA, and whether the NRI has a Permanent Establishment in India. The deductor must apply for a certificate under Section 197 or use the treaty rate.

**Q4. Is TDS applicable when commission is received by a company from another company?**
Yes. There is no exemption for company-to-company commission payments under Section 194H. If a company pays commission exceeding Rs. 15,000 to another company or entity, TDS at 5% must be deducted regardless of both parties being corporates.

**Q5. Can a broker or agent claim a refund if TDS is deducted but their income is below the taxable limit?**
Yes. TDS is an advance tax and not the final tax. If the broker's total income after allowable deductions is below the taxable threshold, the entire TDS deducted is refundable. The broker must file an Income Tax Return to claim the refund. They may also submit Form 15G to the payer upfront if they are below the taxable threshold and below 60 years of age.
