## Introduction

Buying a flat, plot, or commercial property in India comes with a lesser-known obligation: the buyer is required to deduct TDS at the time of payment to the seller. This is governed by Section 194IA of the Income Tax Act, 1961, and was introduced to curb underreporting of property transactions and bring high-value real estate deals within the tax net.

This article explains everything a property buyer needs to know to stay compliant, including rates, calculation examples, and the exact filing process.

---

## What is Section 194IA?

Section 194IA mandates that any person purchasing immovable property (other than agricultural land) from a resident seller must deduct TDS at the rate of **1%** of the total sale consideration, provided the consideration exceeds **Rs. 50 lakhs**.

The term "immovable property" here includes:
- Residential apartments and houses
- Commercial properties (offices, shops)
- Plots of land (non-agricultural)
- Under-construction properties

Agricultural land, as defined in the Income Tax Act, is specifically excluded from this provision.

---

## Rate Table: TDS Under Section 194IA

| Scenario | Rate of TDS |
|---|---|
| Sale consideration is Rs. 50 lakhs or more | 1% |
| Sale consideration is below Rs. 50 lakhs | Nil (no TDS applicable) |
| Seller does not furnish PAN | 20% |
| With valid PAN, normal rate applies | 1% |

> Note: From June 2013 onwards, this section applies to every buyer, whether an individual, company, or HUF. There is no requirement to obtain a TAN (Tax Deduction Account Number) for this specific section. A PAN suffices.

---

## Applicability

### Who Must Deduct?
Any buyer of immovable property, regardless of their tax status or size of business. This includes:
- Salaried individuals
- Self-employed professionals
- Companies and LLPs
- HUFs

### Who Is Excluded?
- If the seller is the Government or a Government-notified entity
- Agricultural land transactions
- Property valued below Rs. 50 lakhs

### Important: Stamp Duty Value vs. Consideration

As amended by Finance Act 2022, if the stamp duty value of the property exceeds the actual sale consideration, TDS must be calculated on the **higher of the two values**. This plugs the gap where buyers understate consideration to reduce TDS.

## Threshold Limits

| Parameter | Limit |
|---|---|
| Minimum sale consideration for TDS | Rs. 50,00,000 |
| Properties below this | No TDS obligation |
| Agricultural land | Exempt regardless of value |

## Example Calculation

**Scenario:** Ramesh purchases a residential flat in Pune from Suresh for Rs. 75,00,000. The stamp duty value of the flat is Rs. 80,00,000.

**Step 1:** Determine the applicable amount for TDS.
Since stamp duty value (Rs. 80 lakhs) exceeds sale consideration (Rs. 75 lakhs), TDS is computed on Rs. 80,00,000.

**Step 2:** Apply TDS rate.
TDS = 1% of Rs. 80,00,000 = Rs. 80,000

**Step 3:** Payment to seller.
Ramesh pays Suresh: Rs. 75,00,000 - Rs. 80,000 = Rs. 74,20,000 (net of TDS deducted on 80 lakh base, adjusted as applicable).

In practice, the TDS amount is deducted from the consideration amount being paid, and the challan is filed in the government's name on behalf of the seller.

## Compliance Steps

### Step 1: No TAN Required
Unlike most TDS provisions, Section 194IA does not require the buyer to hold a TAN. The buyer's PAN is sufficient.

### Step 2: Deduct TDS at Payment
TDS must be deducted at the time of making payment to the seller, whether it is an instalment or a lump sum. If multiple payments are made, TDS must be deducted from each instalment.

### Step 3: Deposit TDS Using Form 26QB
The buyer must deposit TDS online using Form 26QB (discussed in detail in the next article) within **30 days from the end of the month** in which TDS was deducted. This is different from the 7th-of-next-month deadline that applies to most other TDS sections.

### Step 4: Issue Form 16B
After depositing TDS, the buyer must download and issue **Form 16B** (TDS certificate) to the seller within 15 days of the due date for furnishing Form 26QB. Form 16B can be downloaded from the TRACES portal.

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Correct Approach |
|---|---|
| Not deducting TDS on installments | TDS must be deducted on every payment, not just the final one |
| Deducting on consideration while ignoring stamp duty value | Deduct on higher of consideration or stamp duty value |
| Applying 1% on total property value when partial payment is made | Apply on the amount actually paid in each installment |
| Not filing 26QB for each co-buyer separately | Each buyer must file a separate 26QB for their share |

---

## Penalties Under Section 194IA

| Default | Consequence |
|---|---|
| Failure to deduct TDS | Interest at 1% per month from the date TDS was deductible |
| Failure to deposit after deduction | Interest at 1.5% per month from date of deduction to date of deposit |
| Late filing / non-filing of 26QB | Rs. 200 per day under Section 234E, subject to maximum of TDS amount |
| Penalty for non-compliance | Rs. 10,000 to Rs. 1,00,000 under Section 271H |

## FAQs

**Q1. If I buy a property for Rs. 48 lakhs, do I need to deduct TDS?**
No. The threshold under Section 194IA is Rs. 50 lakhs. If the sale consideration is below Rs. 50 lakhs and the stamp duty value is also below Rs. 50 lakhs, TDS is not applicable.

**Q2. What if the property has two buyers? Who files 26QB?**
Each buyer must file a separate Form 26QB for their respective share of the purchase consideration. For example, if a property is bought jointly by two individuals each paying Rs. 40 lakhs (total Rs. 80 lakhs), both must file 26QB independently since the aggregate exceeds Rs. 50 lakhs.

**Q3. Is TDS applicable on under-construction property payments to builders?**
Yes. TDS under Section 194IA applies to payments made to a builder for an under-construction property, as long as the total consideration exceeds Rs. 50 lakhs. Each instalment paid to the builder attracts 1% TDS.

**Q4. Can the seller claim refund of the TDS deducted?**
Yes. The TDS deducted reflects in the seller's Form 26AS. If the seller's total tax liability is lower than the TDS amount, they can claim a refund by filing their Income Tax Return.

**Q5. Does Section 194IA apply to NRI sellers?**
No. Section 194IA applies only when the seller is a resident Indian. If the seller is a Non-Resident Indian (NRI), TDS must be deducted under Section 195, which carries different rates and requires a TAN from the buyer.
