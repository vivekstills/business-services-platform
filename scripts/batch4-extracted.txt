ARTICLE 1 OF 5  ·  CRYPTO TAXATION
Taxation of Cryptocurrency & Virtual Digital 
Assets in India (FY 2025-26)
 Updated: May 2026📅  10 min read⏱  AY 2026-27📋
ARTICLE SUMMARY
India introduced a dedicated tax framework for Virtual Digital Assets (VDAs) — including 
cryptocurrency, NFTs, and other digital tokens — through Finance Act 2022, effective from 
AY 2022-23. Income from VDAs is taxed at a flat 30% (plus surcharge and 4% cess) 
regardless of the holding period or the investor's income level. A 1% TDS under Section 
194S applies on VDA transactions above Rs. 10,000 per year. Loss from one VDA cannot 
be set off against gains from another, and no deductions except the cost of acquisition are 
allowed. This article explains every aspect of India's VDA tax regime for FY 2025-26.
What are Virtual Digital Assets (VDAs)?
Section 2(47A) of the Income Tax Act defines Virtual Digital Asset to include: any information or 
code or number or token generated through cryptographic means or otherwise (Bitcoin, 
Ethereum, and all other cryptocurrencies), non-fungible tokens (NFTs), and any other digital 
asset notified by the Central Government. Traditional currencies (including digital forms of fiat 
currency like the RBI's Digital Rupee / e-Rupee) and gift cards are excluded from the VDA 
definition.
The term 'cryptocurrency' is not used in the Income Tax Act — the law uses 'Virtual Digital 
Asset' as the umbrella term. All VDAs — regardless of their specific technology, use case 
(currency, utility token, governance token), or underlying blockchain — are covered by the same 
tax regime under Section 115BBH.

TDS deducted under Section 194S is visible in Form 26AS and the Annual Information 
Statement (AIS). It is a credit against the final tax liability computed in the ITR. For taxpayers in 
a tax loss position (VDA losses that cannot be offset), the TDS represents a cash outflow with 
no immediate refund — since VDA losses cannot be carried forward to offset future gains.
Cost of Acquisition — The Only Deductible Expense
Section 115BBH allows only one deduction from VDA income: the cost of acquisition. No other 
deductions are permitted — not internet charges, not exchange fees, not transaction gas fees, 
not electricity costs for mining, not depreciation on mining hardware, not interest on loans taken 
to buy crypto. This is significantly more restrictive than other capital assets where various 
expenses can be deducted.
For crypto received as salary, mining income, staking rewards, or airdrops — the FMV on the 
date of receipt is the cost of acquisition for the purpose of computing gains when subsequently 
sold.

Gifts of Crypto — Tax in Giver's and Receiver's Hands
If crypto is gifted to another person: (1) In the giver's hands: the gift is treated as a 'transfer' and 
capital gains arise — FMV on date of gift minus the giver's cost of acquisition, taxed at 30% 
under Section 115BBH. (2) In the receiver's hands: crypto received as a gift is taxable as 
'Income from Other Sources' under Section 56(2)(x) if the FMV exceeds Rs. 50,000 and the 
receiver is not a specified relative. If from a relative (as defined under gift tax provisions), it is 
exempt. See the gift tax article for the complete relatives list.
VDA Reporting in ITR — Schedule VDA
From AY 2022-23, ITR forms include a dedicated 'Schedule VDA' where all VDA transactions 
must be reported. Each transaction must be listed with: asset type, date of acquisition, cost of 
acquisition, date of transfer, and sale consideration. Taxpayers with significant VDA activity 
must use ITR-2 or ITR-3 (depending on other income sources). The AIS (Annual Information 
Statement) pulls data from crypto exchanges that report transactions to the IT department — 
always reconcile your Schedule VDA with the AIS before filing.
 Pro Tip💡
Use a crypto portfolio tracker (CoinTracker, Koinly, or similar tools) that supports India's tax 
regime and generates Schedule VDA-compatible reports. These tools automatically compute 
FIFO-based cost of acquisition across hundreds of transactions across multiple wallets and 
exchanges — essential for accurate VDA tax compliance.
KEY TAKEAWAYS
1.  VDA income (crypto, NFTs) is taxed at a flat 30% under Section 115BBH — regardless of 
holding period, slab, or tax regime chosen.
2.  Loss from one VDA CANNOT be set off against gains from another VDA or any other income 
head — losses lapse permanently.
3.  Only deductible expense is the cost of acquisition — exchange fees, mining costs, gas fees, 
and hardware are NOT deductible.
4.  Section 194S mandates 1% TDS on VDA transactions exceeding Rs. 10,000/Rs. 50,000 per 
year — credit available in Form 26AS.

5.  Crypto-to-crypto swaps, staking rewards, airdrops, mining income, and purchase of goods 
with crypto are all taxable events.
6.  Gifting crypto creates a taxable event for the giver (30% on gains) and may be taxable for the 
receiver under Section 56(2)(x).
7.  All VDA transactions must be reported in Schedule VDA in ITR-2 or ITR-3 — reconcile with 
AIS data from exchanges.
Frequently Asked Questions
Q1. I bought Bitcoin in 2019 and sold it in 2025. Is my gain taxed at LTCG rate or 30%?
At 30% flat rate under Section 115BBH. The holding period is completely irrelevant for VDA 
taxation — there is no LTCG/STCG distinction. Whether you held Bitcoin for 1 day or 10 
years, the gain is taxed at 30% plus cess. The LTCG/STCG framework that applies to 
shares, property, and gold does NOT apply to VDAs.
Q2. I received crypto salary from my foreign employer. How is it taxed?
Crypto received as salary is taxable as 'Salaries' at the FMV on the date of credit in INR. 
The employer should have withheld TDS (or the equivalent in the home country). 
Subsequently, when you sell the crypto, the gain (sale price minus FMV at receipt, which is 
your cost of acquisition) is taxed at 30% under Section 115BBH. So crypto salary faces two 
tax events: receipt (at slab rate as salary) and sale (at 30% on the subsequent gain).
Q3. My crypto exchange is based outside India and does not deduct TDS. What are my 
obligations?
You are responsible for paying the tax directly even if no TDS is deducted. Compute your 
VDA gains for the year, pay advance tax by March 15 (or self-assessment tax before filing), 
and file the ITR with Schedule VDA disclosing all transactions. CBDT has been expanding 
data-sharing agreements with overseas exchanges and using AIS data — undisclosed VDA 
income discovered in scrutiny attracts 30% tax plus 200% penalty under the Black Money 
Act if it involves foreign assets.
Q4. Can I claim deduction for the losses made in the previous year on crypto?
No. VDA losses from prior years cannot be carried forward to offset VDA gains in 
subsequent years. This is explicitly prohibited under Section 115BBH. Each financial year's 
VDA losses are permanently extinguished. This is in sharp contrast to stock market 
STCG/LTCG losses, which can be carried forward for 8 years.
Q5. I use USDT (a stablecoin pegged to USD) only for trading — never converting to INR. 
Are my trades taxable?
Yes. Each crypto-to-crypto trade — including trades involving stablecoins like USDT — is a 
taxable event. When you sell Bitcoin and receive USDT, you must compute the INR value of 
the USDT received on that date and compare it against your Bitcoin cost of acquisition. The 
gain/loss in INR terms is taxable under Section 115BBH. The fact that you never directly 
received INR does not exempt the transaction.

ARTICLE 2 OF 5  ·  SHARE TAXATION
Taxation of Income from Selling Shares — 
Complete Guide for FY 2025-26
 Updated: May 2026📅  10 min read⏱  AY 2026-27📋
ARTICLE SUMMARY
Gains from selling shares in India are taxed under two distinct regimes depending on the 
type of share, how long it was held, and whether Securities Transaction Tax (STT) was 
paid on the transaction. Budget 2024 revised LTCG on equity from 10% to 12.5% and 
STCG from 15% to 20%, while also enhancing the annual LTCG exemption from Rs. 1 lakh 
to Rs. 1.25 lakh. This article covers listed shares, unlisted shares, grandfathering of pre-
2018 gains, F&O income treatment, dividend taxation, and ITR reporting for AY 2026-27.

Rs. 1.25 Lakh Annual Exemption — How It Works
The Rs. 1.25 lakh annual exemption applies to the AGGREGATE of all equity LTCG across all 
shares and equity mutual funds in a financial year — not per share or per fund. The first Rs. 1.25 
lakh of total equity LTCG is tax-free. Only gains above Rs. 1.25 lakh are taxed at 12.5%. This 
exemption cannot be carried forward — use it or lose it each year.
Grandfathering — Pre-January 31, 2018 Gains
For listed equity shares and equity mutual funds, gains accrued up to January 31, 2018 are 
protected under the grandfathering provision. The cost of acquisition for LTCG purposes is the 
HIGHER of: (a) actual cost of purchase, or (b) the lower of the FMV on January 31, 2018 and 
the actual sale price. This prevents taxation of gains that were tax-free under the pre-2018 zero-
LTCG regime.
 Grandfathering Computation📊
Example: Shares bought in 2015 at Rs. 100. FMV on Jan 31, 2018: Rs. 300. 
Sold in FY 2025-26 at Rs. 500.
Step 1: FMV on Jan 31, 2018 = Rs. 300
Step 2: Lower of (FMV Jan 31, 2018 vs Actual Sale Price) = Lower of Rs. 
300 and Rs. 500 = Rs. 300
Step 3: Cost = Higher of (Actual cost Rs. 100 vs Rs. 300) = Rs. 300
Step 4: LTCG = Rs. 500 - Rs. 300 = Rs. 200 per share
Pre-2018 gain (Rs. 300 - Rs. 100 = Rs. 200) is permanently exempt.
Only post-2018 gain (Rs. 500 - Rs. 300 = Rs. 200) is taxable at 12.5%.
Futures & Options (F&O) — Business Income, Not Capital Gains
Income from trading in equity Futures and Options (F&O) is NOT treated as capital gains — it is 
classified as 'Income from Business or Profession' under the Income Tax Act. F&O is treated as 
a non-speculative business activity. Consequences:
• F&O profits are taxed at the applicable slab rate — not at 12.5% or 20%.
• F&O losses CAN be set off against business income and carried forward for 8 years 
(unlike VDA losses).
---PAGE 9---
• Expenses (brokerage, internet, advisory fees, subscription costs) are deductible from 
F&O income.
• If F&O turnover (aggregate of profit + loss on settlement) exceeds Rs. 10 crore, tax audit 
under Section 44AB is mandatory. Below that, Section 44AD presumptive taxation at 
6%/8% may be applicable if turnover is within Rs. 3 crore.
• F&O traders must use ITR-3 (not ITR-2) since F&O is business income.
Intraday Equity Trading — Speculative Business
Gains from intraday trading (buying and selling shares on the same day without taking delivery) 
are treated as 'Speculative Business Income' — taxable at slab rates. Speculative losses can 
only be set off against speculative income (not against F&O or salary income) and can be 
carried forward for 4 years (not 8 years). Intraday traders must also use ITR-3.
Dividend Income — Now Taxable at Slab Rate
Before April 1, 2020, dividends were exempt in the recipient's hands (Dividend Distribution Tax 
was paid by the company). From FY 2020-21 onwards, dividends are taxable in the 
shareholder's hands at the applicable income tax slab rate. TDS at 10% is deducted by the 
company on dividends exceeding Rs. 5,000 per year per shareholder. The dividend income is 
reported under 'Income from Other Sources' in the ITR.

 Pro Tip💡
Tax harvesting strategy: If your equity LTCG is approaching Rs. 1.25 lakh but not exceeding it, 
consider selling enough units to crystallise exactly Rs. 1.25 lakh in gains before March 31 and 
repurchasing the same shares/units. This books the annual exemption while maintaining your 
investment position. Repeat annually to progressively step up your cost base and permanently 
shelter gains that would otherwise be taxable in the future.

2.  Rs. 1.25 lakh annual LTCG exemption is aggregate across all equity shares and equity MFs

Frequently Asked Questions
Q1. I have LTCG of Rs. 2 lakh from shares and Rs. 50,000 LTCG loss from equity MFs. 
What is my net taxable LTCG?
LTCG losses from equity MFs can be set off against LTCG gains from shares (and vice 
versa) since both are long-term capital gains on equity-oriented instruments. Net LTCG = 
Rs. 2,00,000 - Rs. 50,000 = Rs. 1,50,000. After the Rs. 1.25 lakh exemption: taxable LTCG 
= Rs. 1,50,000 - Rs. 1,25,000 = Rs. 25,000. Tax at 12.5% = Rs. 3,125. LTCG from equity 
shares can be offset against LTCG losses from equity MFs freely.
Q2. I sold shares at a loss. Can I carry forward the loss?
For listed equity STCG losses: can be set off against any LTCG or STCG (equity or non-
equity) in the same year, and carried forward for 8 years to be set off against capital gains in 
future years. For listed equity LTCG losses: can be set off against LTCG only and carried 
forward 8 years. The ITR must be filed on time (before the July 31 due date) to preserve the 
carry-forward benefit.
Q3. My F&O trading showed a loss of Rs. 5 lakh this year. Can I set it off against my 
salary income?
F&O is classified as non-speculative business income. A non-speculative business loss can 
be set off against any income head EXCEPT salary in the same year — it can offset rental 
income, capital gains, or other income but NOT salary. The remaining loss after same-year 
set-off can be carried forward for 8 years to be set off against any future business income 
(speculative or non-speculative).
Q4. I am an NRI selling listed shares in India. What is the tax treatment?
---PAGE 11---
NRIs selling listed equity shares in India pay the same LTCG (12.5% above Rs. 1.25L 
exemption) and STCG (20%) rates as residents. However, TDS applies on the gross sale 
proceeds for NRIs — the buyer or broker must deduct TDS at applicable rates. NRIs can 
claim refund of excess TDS (if actual tax liability is lower) by filing an ITR in India. NRIs also 
have the option to invest NRI capital in shares through the PIS (Portfolio Investment 
Scheme) route via an NRE/NRO account.
Q5. I received bonus shares from a company I invested in. What is the cost of acquisition 
of bonus shares?
The cost of acquisition of bonus shares is considered as NIL (zero) for income tax purposes. 
When you sell bonus shares, the entire sale price is taxable as LTCG (if held more than 12 
months) or STCG. The Rs. 1.25 lakh annual exemption applies to the LTCG from bonus 
shares as well. The holding period for bonus shares starts from the date of allotment of the 
bonus shares — not from the date the original shares were purchased.

ARTICLE 3 OF 5  ·  MARKET TAXATION
Securities Transaction Tax (STT) — Latest Rates, 
F&O Hike & Updates
 Updated: May 2026📅  8 min read⏱  FY 2025-26📋
ARTICLE SUMMARY
Securities Transaction Tax (STT) is a tax levied on purchase and sale of securities listed 
on Indian stock exchanges. Introduced in 2004, STT replaced the earlier capital gains 
exemption on listed securities and has since become a significant revenue source. Budget 
2024 significantly hiked STT on F&O (Futures & Options) — index futures rate doubled and 
options STT increased by 60%. This article covers all current STT rates across equity, 
F&O, and mutual funds, how STT is collected, its deductibility, and the impact of the 
Budget 2024 F&O hike.
What is STT and Why Does It Exist?
Securities Transaction Tax (STT) is a direct tax levied under the Securities Transaction Tax Act, 
2004. It is collected at source by the stock exchange or mutual fund house at the time of the 
securities transaction. STT is paid by the buyer and/or seller depending on the transaction type. 
It cannot be avoided, refunded, or set off (with limited exceptions) — it is a transaction cost, 
similar to stamp duty, built into every securities trade.
STT was introduced as part of a policy package: the government abolished the long-term capital 
gains exemption on equities in 2004 (which was re-introduced only in 2018) and replaced it with 
STT. The logic was to make tax collection seamless and reduce avoidance. STT is a significant 
recurring transaction cost for active traders and must be factored into return calculations.

Is STT Deductible as an Expense?
STT paid is NOT deductible as an expense for computing capital gains on delivery-based equity 
transactions. The gain is simply: Sale price minus Purchase price (no adjustment for STT paid). 
However, for F&O traders (where income is classified as business income), STT paid is 
deductible as a business expense — it reduces the net F&O profit or increases the F&O loss. 
This is one of the practical differences between capital gains treatment (STT not deductible) and 
business income treatment (STT deductible) for securities transactions.
STT and the LTCG/STCG Framework
For listed equity shares, the lower LTCG rate of 12.5% and STCG rate of 20% are available 
ONLY if STT has been paid on both the purchase and sale of the shares (for delivery-based 
transactions). If shares are sold off-market (not through a recognised stock exchange), or if STT 
was not paid, the normal capital gains rates apply — LTCG at 12.5% (without the preferential 
rate) for listed shares and slab rate for STCG. In practice, off-market transfers of listed shares 
are rare, but the condition is important for shares transferred through private deals.
 Pro Tip💡
Active F&O and intraday traders should review their annual STT outflow carefully. With the 
Budget 2024 rate hike, STT has become a larger drag on returns. When comparing pre- and 
post-tax returns, include STT (as a transaction cost that reduces gross P&L). For F&O business 
income reporting, ensure STT is claimed as a deductible expense in the ITR-3 business income 
computation.
KEY TAKEAWAYS
1.  STT is a transaction-level tax on securities purchases and sales, collected by exchanges at 
source — it cannot be avoided or refunded.
2.  Delivery equity purchase and sale: 0.1% each; Intraday sale only: 0.025%.
3.  Budget 2024 (effective Oct 1, 2024): Index and equity futures STT doubled to 0.02%; options 
STT increased to 0.1% on premium.
4.  STT is NOT deductible for capital gains computation on delivery shares — but IS deductible 
as a business expense for F&O traders.
5.  LTCG/STCG preferential rates on listed equity are available only when STT has been paid on 
---PAGE 15---
both purchase and sale.
6.  Unlisted securities, bonds, and debentures are not subject to STT.
7.  F&O traders must factor the higher post-Budget 2024 STT into their per-trade profitability 
calculations.
Frequently Asked Questions
Q1. I bought shares off-market (not through a broker). Will my LTCG still be taxed at 
12.5%?
For off-market purchase (no STT paid on purchase), the LTCG at sale is taxed at 12.5% 
without the STT-condition issue — because the STT condition for the preferential rate 
applies primarily to the sale side for listed shares. However, if both purchase and sale are 
off-market (STT not paid at either end), the preferential LTCG rate may be denied and 
normal rates apply. Most litigation in this area involves inherited shares, ESOP shares, or 
bonus shares where the 'purchase' was off-market. Consult a CA for specific off-market 
transactions.
Q2. Is STT included in the purchase price of shares for LTCG computation?
No. STT paid on purchase of shares is NOT added to the cost of acquisition for capital gains 
purposes. The cost is the actual price paid for the shares (excluding STT and brokerage). 
Similarly, STT paid on sale is not deducted from the sale consideration. Both STT payments 
are ignored entirely in the capital gains computation for delivery-based shares. Only the 
purchase price and sale price matter.
Q3. I am a foreign portfolio investor (FPI). Do I pay STT?
Yes. Foreign Portfolio Investors (FPIs) pay STT on their equity transactions in India at the 
same rates as resident investors. STT is a transaction-level tax regardless of the investor's 
residency status. FPIs cannot claim a credit for Indian STT against tax payable in their home 
country in most cases — it is an unrecoverable transaction cost.
Q4. I trade Nifty options. After Budget 2024, how much more STT am I paying annually?
The STT on index options increased from 0.0625% to 0.1% of option premium — a 60% 
increase. For every Rs. 1 lakh of option premium sold, STT increased from Rs. 62.50 to Rs. 
100. For an active trader selling Rs. 50 lakh of option premium monthly, the annual STT 
increase = (0.1% - 0.0625%) x Rs. 50L x 12 = 0.0375% x Rs. 6 crore = Rs. 22,500 
additional STT per year. High-frequency options traders face significantly larger absolute 
increases.
Q5. Can I get a refund of STT if I cancel my trade or a trade is rejected?
If a trade is not executed (rejected before execution), no STT is deducted since STT arises 
only on executed transactions. If a trade is executed and then reversed/cancelled (which is 
rare — most cancellations happen before execution), the STT on both legs may be 
recoverable through the exchange's dispute mechanism. In normal circumstances, once an 
equity transaction is executed and confirmed, the STT is final and not refundable.

ARTICLE 4 OF 5  ·  GIFT TAXATION
Tax on Diwali and New Year Gifts — Employer, 
Business & Personal Gifts
 Updated: May 2026📅  7 min read⏱  AY 2026-27📋
ARTICLE SUMMARY
Gifts received during Diwali, New Year, and other festive occasions are subject to income 
tax in India, but the tax treatment varies significantly depending on the source of the gift — 
employer, business counterpart, or personal contact. Gifts from employers are taxable as 
salary perquisites above Rs. 5,000 per year. Gifts from unrelated individuals or companies 
are taxable under Section 56(2)(x) above Rs. 50,000 per year. Gifts from specified relatives 
are fully exempt. This article explains every category with the applicable limits, tax rates, 
and documentation requirements.
Category 1: Gifts from Employer — Perquisite Taxation
Any gift, voucher, or token received from an employer is taxable as a perquisite under Section 
17(2)(viii) read with Rule 3(7)(iv). However, a specific exemption is available: gifts not exceeding 
Rs. 5,000 in aggregate during a financial year are fully exempt. Beyond Rs. 5,000, the entire 
amount (not just the excess) becomes a taxable perquisite.

professional income.

For the business or employer providing the Diwali gift, the tax treatment of the gift expense is:
• Gifts to employees: Deductible as business expense (salary/perquisite) — but employee 
pays tax on amounts above Rs. 5,000.
• Gifts to business clients/associates: Section 37(2B) explicitly disallows deduction for 
expenses on gifts, freebies, and entertainment beyond the limits notified by CBDT — 
typically Rs. 0 (i.e., gifts to clients are generally non-deductible). This was tightened by 
CBDT.
• Gifts qualifying as samples or business promotion materials (e.g., branded items with 
company logo used for marketing): May be deductible as marketing expenses if 
genuinely for business promotion and not personal gifts.

Employers can optimise gift tax for employees by ensuring total annual gifts (across Diwali, New

Tracking cumulative gift values throughout the year prevents inadvertent crossing of the

the excess above Rs. 5,000.
2.  Cash gifts from employer have NO Rs. 5,000 exemption — fully taxable as salary from the

3.  Business/client gifts: taxable under Section 56(2)(x) if aggregate from all non-relatives

4.  Gifts from specified relatives are ALWAYS fully exempt regardless of the amount.
5.  Freebies received by professionals (doctors, influencers) in exchange for services are taxable 
as professional income, not exempt gifts.
6.  Businesses cannot deduct client gift expenses — Section 37(2B) disallows deduction on gifts 
to non-employees.
7.  Both monetary gifts and gifts in kind (jewellery, vouchers, property) are taxable — FMV is 
used for non-cash gifts.

Frequently Asked Questions
Q1. My company gave all employees a Rs. 4,500 Diwali hamper and Rs. 1,000 bonus. 
What is taxable?
The Rs. 4,500 hamper is a gift in kind below the Rs. 5,000 threshold — fully exempt as a 
perquisite. The Rs. 1,000 bonus in cash has no exemption (cash gifts from employer are 
fully taxable as salary from the first rupee). So Rs. 1,000 cash is taxable; Rs. 4,500 hamper 
is exempt — total taxable perquisite is Rs. 1,000.
Q2. I received gift hampers worth Rs. 30,000 from various business associates during 
Diwali. Is it taxable?
The aggregate value of Rs. 30,000 is below the Rs. 50,000 threshold under Section 56(2)
(x). Therefore, no tax arises on these gifts. The Rs. 50,000 threshold is the aggregate 
across all non-relative givers during the entire financial year. If you receive additional gifts 
(cash or kind) from unrelated parties before March 31 that push the total above Rs. 50,000, 
the entire aggregate becomes taxable.
Q3. My uncle gave me Rs. 1 lakh as a Diwali gift. Is this taxable?
The taxability depends on whether your uncle qualifies as a 'specified relative' under Section 
56(2)(x). For individuals, specified relatives include spouse, siblings, siblings of spouse, 
parents, lineal ascendants/descendants and their spouses. An uncle (parent's brother) is 
generally NOT on the specified relatives list for individuals. Therefore, the Rs. 1 lakh gift 
from your uncle is taxable under Section 56(2)(x) as it exceeds Rs. 50,000. See the gift tax 
and relatives list article for the complete relatives list.
Q4. A pharmaceutical company sent me a luxury gift hamper worth Rs. 80,000 as a Diwali 
gift. I am a doctor. Is it taxable?
Yes, and it may be taxable as professional income, not just a gift. CBDT's Circular 5/2012 
and subsequent clarifications specifically address pharma company gifts to doctors as 
taxable professional income — on the grounds that such gifts are given in exchange for the 
prescription-writing relationship (even if informal). The Rs. 50,000 Section 56(2)(x) threshold 
does not help here since the income may be reclassified as professional income taxable at 
slab rates. The pharma company also cannot deduct the gift expense.
Q5. I gifted my sister-in-law Rs. 2 lakh for Diwali. Is it taxable in her hands?
Sister-in-law (brother's wife or husband's sister) IS on the specified relatives list for the gift 
tax provisions under Section 56(2)(x). Gifts from specified relatives are fully exempt 
regardless of the amount. Your sister-in-law does not need to declare this Rs. 2 lakh gift as 
income. For you (the giver), no tax arises either — gifting cash does not trigger any tax in 
the giver's hands (unlike gifting appreciated assets, which may trigger capital gains if the 
asset has appreciated).

ARTICLE 5 OF 5  ·  GIFT TAXATION
How Are Gifts Taxed in India — Gift Tax 
Exemption & Complete Relatives List
 Updated: May 2026📅  9 min read⏱  AY 2026-27📋
ARTICLE SUMMARY
India does not have a standalone 'Gift Tax Act' (the old Gift Tax Act was repealed in 1998). 
Instead, gifts are taxed under Section 56(2)(x) of the Income Tax Act as 'Income from 
Other Sources' if received from non-relatives above Rs. 50,000 in a financial year. Gifts 
from specified relatives are completely exempt without any cap. This article provides the 
complete relatives list for different taxpayer categories (individuals, HUFs), the types of 
taxable gifts, special occasions exemptions, how gifted property is valued, and the capital 
gains implications of gifting appreciated assets.
The Legal Framework — Section 56(2)(x)
Section 56(2)(x), introduced with effect from April 1, 2017, provides that if any person receives 
any sum of money or property from any person without adequate consideration (i.e., as a gift or 
below market value), the received amount is taxable as 'Income from Other Sources' if: (a) the 
aggregate value of all such receipts during the financial year exceeds Rs. 50,000, and (b) the 
giver is not a 'specified relative' or the gift is not on a specified occasion.
Before April 1, 2017, Section 56(2)(vii) applied — Section 56(2)(x) replaced it with broader 
coverage that now also applies to firms, companies, and trusts receiving inadequate 
consideration, not just individuals and HUFs. However, the relative exemption and occasion 
exemptions apply only to individuals and HUFs.

capital — not as taxable income.

Marriage Gift Exemption — Important Details
The marriage occasion exemption is one of the most valuable gift tax provisions. Any gift 
received by the bride or groom on the occasion of their marriage is completely exempt from 
Section 56(2)(x) — regardless of the amount, regardless of whether the giver is a relative or 
unrelated, and regardless of whether the gift is cash or property. The exemption is specifically 
for the person getting married, not for their parents or other family members. Gifts to parents for 
hosting the wedding are NOT covered by the marriage exemption.
There is no time limit precisely defined in the law for 'on the occasion of marriage' — gifts 
received a few weeks before or after the wedding are generally treated as covered by this 
exemption if there is a clear nexus to the marriage event. Large gifts received months before an 
informal engagement but formally gifted at the wedding are sometimes challenged by the tax 
department if the timing seems contrived.
Capital Gains When Gifted Property Is Later Sold
When a recipient sells property received as a gift, capital gains are computed based on the 
original cost of the property (i.e., the cost paid by the original donor — not the FMV at the time 
of gifting). The holding period also includes the donor's holding period. This means:
• If the donor held shares for 8 years and gifted them, the recipient is deemed to have 
held them for 8 years from the moment they receive the gift — immediately qualifying as 
LTCG if sold.
• If shares were originally purchased at Rs. 100 per share, gifted when FMV was Rs. 500, 
and then sold by the recipient at Rs. 600 — the LTCG = Rs. 600 - Rs. 100 = Rs. 500 per 
share (not Rs. 100 per share).
---PAGE 23---
• The recipient pays capital gains tax on the entire appreciation — including the 
appreciation during the donor's ownership period.
 Pro Tip💡
Gifting appreciated shares or property to a low-income family member (e.g., a non-working 
spouse, or a student child) is a legal income-splitting strategy. The capital gains when the asset 
is eventually sold will be taxed in the recipient's hands at their (lower) slab rate rather than the 
donor's higher rate. The gift itself is exempt if from a specified relative. This is a common and 
accepted tax planning technique.
KEY TAKEAWAYS
1.  Section 56(2)(x) taxes gifts from non-relatives as 'Income from Other Sources' if aggregate 
value exceeds Rs. 50,000 in a year.
2.  Gifts from specified relatives are FULLY EXEMPT regardless of amount — the relatives list is 
specific and narrow.
3.  Marriage gifts are fully exempt — regardless of amount, source, or type of gift (cash or 
property).
4.  Inherited property and gifts received under a Will are fully exempt.
5.  Uncle, aunt, nephew, niece, and cousins are NOT on the specified relatives list — their gifts 
are taxable above Rs. 50,000.
6.  When gifted property is sold, capital gains are computed using the original donor's cost and 
holding period.
7.  For HUFs, any gift from any member of the HUF to the HUF is exempt — HUF members are 
'specified relatives' for the HUF.
Frequently Asked Questions
Q1. My mother gave me Rs. 10 lakh to buy a house. Is this taxable?
No. Your mother (lineal ascendant) is a specified relative under Section 56(2)(x). Gifts from 
specified relatives are completely exempt regardless of the amount. The Rs. 10 lakh from 
your mother is not taxable in your hands. You do not need to report this gift as income. 
However, if you invest the money and earn interest, that interest income is taxable in your 
hands (clubbing provisions would not apply since you are an adult child).
Q2. I received Rs. 75,000 in total from various friends and colleagues during my wedding. 
Is it taxable?
No. The marriage occasion exemption covers all gifts received on the occasion of marriage 
— from anyone (relatives and non-relatives alike) and in any amount. The Rs. 50,000 
threshold under Section 56(2)(x) does not apply to marriage gifts. The entire Rs. 75,000 is 
---PAGE 24---
exempt. Maintain a simple record of the wedding date and the gifts received as supporting 
documentation in case it is ever questioned.
Q3. I gifted my daughter Rs. 5 lakh in shares (appreciated from Rs. 1 lakh original cost). 
What are the tax consequences for me and for her?
For you (the giver): Gifting to a specified relative (daughter = lineal descendant) does NOT 
trigger capital gains in your hands — the gift is not a 'transfer' that attracts capital gains 
when given to a relative (Section 47(iii) exemption). For your daughter (the receiver): The 
gift is exempt since you are a specified relative. When she eventually sells the shares, 
LTCG will be computed using your original cost of Rs. 1 lakh (not the FMV at time of gift) 
and your holding period will be included.
Q4. I received a plot of land worth Rs. 20 lakh from my friend as a gift. What is taxable?
Your friend is not a specified relative. The FMV (or stamp duty value) of Rs. 20 lakh exceeds 
the Rs. 50,000 threshold. The ENTIRE Rs. 20 lakh is taxable as 'Income from Other 
Sources' in your hands at the applicable slab rate — not just the amount above Rs. 50,000. 
This is a very significant tax implication: receiving high-value property gifts from non-
relatives creates a large income tax liability.
Q5. Does clubbing apply when I gift money to my spouse and they invest it?
Yes — the Income Tax Act has clubbing provisions for gifts to spouses. If you gift money to 
your spouse and the spouse invests it, the income earned on that investment is clubbed with 
your income (not your spouse's) and taxed in your hands. The clubbing applies to the 
'immediate income' from the gifted amount. However, if the spouse reinvests the income 
from the original investment, the income-on-income may not be clubbed — this is a nuanced 
area. Gifting to a non-working adult child does not attract clubbing (only gifts to minor 
children attract limited clubbing provisions).
