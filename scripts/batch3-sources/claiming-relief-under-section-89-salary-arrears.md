# Claiming Relief Under Section 89(1) on Salary Arrears - Complete Guide

When employees receive salary arrears, advance salary, or gratuity in a lump sum that relates to prior financial years, this income - though taxed in the year of receipt - may attract a higher rate than if it had been received in the years it was earned. Section 89(1) provides relief from this additional tax burden by computing tax as if the income had been received in the respective years. Critically, Form 10E must be filed on the IT portal before filing the ITR - failing to do so results in the relief being denied, even if the calculation is correct.

## Why Section 89(1) Relief is Needed

Under the Income Tax Act, salary income is taxed on a receipt basis - it is taxed in the year it is received, regardless of which year it relates to. This creates a potential inequity when a taxpayer receives arrears for several prior years in a single year. The bunching of multiple years' income into one year can push the taxpayer into a higher tax bracket than would have applied if the income had been received in the respective years.

Section 89(1) provides relief by computing a hypothetical tax - what the total tax would have been if the lump sum income had been received in the year(s) to which it relates. If this hypothetical tax is lower than the actual tax on the bunched income, the difference is allowed as relief - reducing the current year's tax liability.

## Types of Income Eligible for Section 89(1) Relief

| Income Type | Eligible for 89(1)? | Common Examples |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Salary arrears | Yes | Pay commission arrears, court-ordered back pay, delayed increments, pending promotions |
| Advance salary | Yes | Salary received in advance for future months |
| Arrears of family pension | Yes | Family pension arrears for deceased employee's family |
| Gratuity in excess of exemption | Yes - limited relief | Gratuity over Rs. 20 lakh for non-govt employees |
| Compensation on voluntary retirement | Yes | VRS compensation over the Rs. 5 lakh exemption |
| Commuted pension | Yes - specific provisions | Lump sum pension commutation above exemption limit |

## Form 10E - Mandatory Filing Before ITR

The most critical compliance requirement for Section 89(1) is the mandatory filing of Form 10E on the IT portal before submitting the ITR. This was made mandatory by the IT department from FY 2014-15 onwards. If Form 10E is not filed before the ITR, the relief will be disallowed - even if the mathematical calculation is perfectly correct and the relief is legitimately due.

### Important

If you claim Section 89(1) relief in your ITR without first filing Form 10E on the portal, the Income Tax Department's CPC will automatically disallow the relief and raise a demand for the unpaid tax. This is one of the most common causes of CPC demand notices for salaried taxpayers. Always file Form 10E first, get the acknowledgment, then file the ITR.

Form 10E is available on the IT portal under: **e-File**, then **Income Tax Forms**, then **File Income Tax Forms**, then **Form 10E**. It must be submitted and verified (via Aadhaar OTP or other e-verification) online. The portal automatically links the Form 10E filing to your PAN - you do not need to attach any document to your ITR; the portal verifies it internally.

## Step-by-Step Calculation of Section 89(1) Relief

The calculation involves the following steps. Below is an example of a government employee receiving 7th Pay Commission arrears.

### Section 89(1) - Worked Calculation Example

**Employee:** A govt employee receiving pay revision arrears in FY 2025-26

- Arrears received in FY 2025-26 relating to FY 2023-24: Rs. 1,00,000
- Arrears received in FY 2025-26 relating to FY 2024-25: Rs. 1,20,000
- **Total arrears received in FY 2025-26:** Rs. 2,20,000
- Regular salary income in FY 2025-26 (excluding arrears): Rs. 8,00,000

**STEP 1: Tax on total income in FY 2025-26 (with arrears):**

- Total Income = Rs. 8,00,000 + Rs. 2,20,000 = Rs. 10,20,000
- Tax (new regime, after std deduction Rs. 75,000 = Rs. 9,45,000 taxable):
- Approx Tax: Rs. 97,500 **[Amount A]**

**STEP 2: Tax on regular income without arrears:**

- Income = Rs. 8,00,000 (less std deduction Rs. 75,000 = Rs. 7,25,000)
- Tax: Rs. 0 (below Rs. 12L rebate threshold) **[Amount B]**

**STEP 3: Difference (A - B) = Rs. 97,500 - Rs. 0 = Rs. 97,500**

**STEP 4: Hypothetical tax if arrears had been received in prior years:**

- FY 2023-24: Income + Rs. 1,00,000 - tax increase: Rs. 0 (still below threshold)
- FY 2024-25: Income + Rs. 1,20,000 - tax increase: Rs. 0 (still below threshold)
- Total hypothetical additional tax **[Amount C]** = Rs. 0

**STEP 5: Relief = A - B - C = Rs. 97,500 - Rs. 0 - Rs. 0 = Rs. 97,500**

Employee claims Rs. 97,500 relief under Section 89(1).

## How to Enter Section 89(1) in ITR

After filing Form 10E, the Section 89(1) relief appears in your ITR under the head 'Relief under Section 89' in the Tax Computation section. In the online ITR form, there is a specific row for Section 89 relief - enter the amount of relief computed (which Form 10E has already captured). This amount reduces the gross tax liability before application of rebates and after applying surcharge and cess.

## Common Situations - Pay Commission Arrears

The most common scenario for Section 89(1) claims is government pay commission revisions. When the Central Government implements a new pay commission (e.g., 8th Pay Commission recommendations expected to be implemented from FY 2026-27), employees receive large arrears relating to prior years. These arrears, if taxed entirely in the year of receipt, can create significant additional tax burden that Section 89(1) is designed to mitigate.

State government pay revisions, PSU wage settlements, and delayed salary increments following promotions or court orders are other common triggers. Private sector employees may also receive Section 89(1) benefits from settlement arrears, back-dated salary revisions, or severance packages.

### Pro Tip

Government employees who received 7th CPC arrears in prior years may receive 8th CPC arrears in FY 2026-27 or later. Start maintaining year-wise salary details now. When arrears are announced, compute your Section 89(1) relief early and file Form 10E as soon as the arrear is credited - do not wait until the last moment before the ITR deadline.

## Key Takeaways

1. Section 89(1) relief prevents over-taxation when salary arrears, advance salary, or similar payments are received as a lump sum relating to prior years.
2. Form 10E MUST be filed on the IT portal before submitting the ITR - non-filing results in automatic disallowance of the relief.
3. Relief = (Tax on total income including arrears) minus (Tax without arrears) minus (Hypothetical tax if arrears received in respective years).
4. Eligible income includes salary arrears, advance salary, gratuity over exemption, VRS compensation over limit, and commuted pension.
5. Form 10E is available under e-File, Income Tax Forms on the IT portal and requires Aadhaar OTP e-verification.
6. The relief is particularly valuable for government employees receiving Pay Commission arrears spanning multiple prior years.
7. Section 89(1) relief does not require any additional payment - it is a reduction in current year tax computed during ITR filing.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### I received salary arrears from my employer but my employer already deducted TDS on the full amount. Should I still file Form 10E?

Yes. Even if your employer has deducted TDS on the arrears without computing Section 89(1) relief, you should file Form 10E and claim the relief in your ITR. The relief will reduce your net tax payable, and the excess TDS already deducted will be refunded by the Income Tax Department when your return is processed. The employer's TDS computation and the Section 89(1) relief are separate - the employer is not required to compute 89(1) relief; you must claim it yourself in the ITR.

### What if the arrears relate to years before FY 2018-19? How far back can the calculation go?

Section 89(1) requires computation going back to the actual years to which the arrears relate - there is no statutory time limit on how far back you can go. However, practically, you need to know your actual taxable income for each prior year to perform the calculation in Form 10E. The Form 10E on the IT portal currently supports calculations going back to FY 2000-01. You should have records of your prior year tax returns or Form 16s to accurately compute the relief for older arrears.

### If Section 89(1) relief results in zero tax liability, do I still need to file Form 10E?

Yes. Filing Form 10E is mandatory irrespective of whether the relief is large or small, or whether it reduces the tax to zero. The CPC's automated processing checks the Form 10E database before allowing the relief in the ITR. If it is not on record, the claim is rejected. Do not skip Form 10E even if you believe the relief is obvious or the amounts are small.

### Can I claim Section 89(1) on a bonus received in advance for future years?

Yes. Advance salary and advance bonus are explicitly covered by Section 89(1). If you receive a bonus in FY 2025-26 that relates to work that will be performed in FY 2026-27, this can be treated as advance salary and the relief computation in Form 10E will project the hypothetical tax for the future year. The calculation determines whether receiving it in advance causes a higher tax than if it were received in the applicable future year.

### My arrears were for a period when I was in a lower tax bracket. Will Section 89(1) always provide relief?

Not necessarily. Section 89(1) relief is the difference between (a) actual tax on bunched income and (b) hypothetical tax if spread over the relevant years. If you were in a lower slab in the arrear years and the bunching does not actually push you to a higher slab in the current year either, the relief may be zero. The relief is mathematically zero when the bunching does not result in more tax than the sum of taxes in the respective years. Form 10E performs this calculation automatically and shows the relief (even if zero).
