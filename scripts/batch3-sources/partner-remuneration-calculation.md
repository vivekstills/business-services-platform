# Partner's Remuneration and How It Is Calculated - Section 40(b)

In a partnership firm, partners can be remunerated (salary, bonus, commission, fee) for their work and also earn interest on their capital contributions. However, the Income Tax Act imposes strict limits on how much remuneration and interest can be claimed as a deduction by the firm under Section 40(b). Beyond these limits, the excess is disallowed, increasing the firm's taxable income. This article explains the book profit concept, remuneration calculation formula, interest limits, tax treatment in partners' hands, and the distinction between working and non-working partners.

## Overview - Why Section 40(b) Exists

In an unregulated scenario, a partnership firm could pay excessive remuneration to its partners, reducing the firm's taxable income (and thus the 30% firm-level tax) while shifting income to partners who might be in a lower tax bracket. Section 40(b) was introduced to prevent this income-shifting arrangement by capping the deductible remuneration and interest at prescribed limits.

Any remuneration or interest paid to partners beyond these limits is disallowed as a deduction in the firm's computation, which increases the firm's taxable profit. The disallowed amount is still taxable - but at the firm level at 30%, rather than being split among partners at potentially lower slab rates. This ensures that the overall tax collection is protected.

## Who is a Working Partner?

Remuneration under Section 40(b) can only be paid to working partners - not to all partners. A working partner is defined as an individual who is actively engaged in conducting the affairs of the business or profession of the firm. The partnership deed must specify which partners are working partners. A partner who merely contributes capital and receives profit share but does not actively work in the business is a non-working (sleeping) partner and cannot receive deductible remuneration.

| Feature | Working Partner | Non-Working / Sleeping Partner |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Definition | Actively engaged in firm's business/profession | Contributes capital only; not active in business |
| Remuneration allowed? | Yes - subject to Section 40(b) limits | No - remuneration not deductible for firm |
| Interest on capital allowed? | Yes - up to 12% p.a. | Yes - up to 12% p.a. |
| Tax on remuneration | Taxable as 'Income from Business/Profession' in partner's hands | N/A - no remuneration received |
| Mentioned in deed? | Must be specified in partnership deed | Typically also specified in deed |

## Book Profit - The Foundation of the Calculation

The entire Section 40(b) remuneration calculation is based on 'Book Profit.' Book Profit is the net profit of the firm computed under the normal provisions of the Income Tax Act, but BEFORE deducting the partner's remuneration (but after allowing all other legitimate deductions - expenses, depreciation, etc.). It is essentially the firm's taxable profit computed as if no remuneration had been paid to partners.

**Book Profit calculation steps:** Start with the firm's Profit & Loss Account net profit. Add back any remuneration paid to partners (since we need profit before remuneration). Adjust for any income tax disallowances (Section 40A, 43B, etc.). Adjust for any depreciation excess or shortfall. The result is the Book Profit for Section 40(b) purposes.

## Maximum Deductible Remuneration - The Section 40(b) Formula

| Book Profit Slab | Maximum Deductible Remuneration |
| --- | --- |
| First Rs. 3,00,000 of book profit (or in case of loss) | Rs. 1,50,000 or 90% of book profit, whichever is higher |
| On the balance of book profit (above Rs. 3,00,000) | 60% of book profit above Rs. 3,00,000 |

### Section 40(b) - Remuneration Limit Calculation

**Partnership Firm ABC & Co. - FY 2025-26**

Net Profit (before partner remuneration): Rs. 20,00,000 (Book Profit)

**Applying Section 40(b) formula:**

- On first Rs. 3,00,000: 90% = Rs. 2,70,000
- On balance Rs. 17,00,000 (@ 60%): Rs. 10,20,000
- **Maximum Deductible Remuneration:** Rs. 12,90,000

**Scenario 1:** Actual remuneration paid = Rs. 10,00,000

- Allowed deduction: Rs. 10,00,000 (within limit)
- Firm's Taxable Income: Rs. 20,00,000 - Rs. 10,00,000 = Rs. 10,00,000
- Firm Tax @ 30%: Rs. 3,00,000

**Scenario 2:** Actual remuneration paid = Rs. 15,00,000

- Allowed deduction: Rs. 12,90,000 (capped at limit)
- Disallowed: Rs. 2,10,000 (added back to firm income)
- Firm's Taxable Income: Rs. 20,00,000 - Rs. 12,90,000 = Rs. 7,10,000
- **Note:** The full Rs. 15,00,000 is STILL taxable in partners' hands as income

## Interest on Partner's Capital - Section 40(b) Limit

Apart from remuneration, partners also earn interest on the capital they contribute to the firm. Section 40(b) allows the firm to deduct interest paid to partners on their capital, subject to a maximum rate of 12% per annum. Interest paid above 12% is disallowed.

- Maximum interest rate: 12% per annum simple interest on the partner's capital account balance.
- Both working and non-working partners can receive interest on capital - the 12% limit applies to all.
- Interest is computed on the opening balance of the partner's capital account for the year.
- Any excess interest above 12% is disallowed in the firm's computation but remains taxable in the partner's hands.

## Tax Treatment in Partners' Hands

Remuneration and interest received by partners from the firm are taxed as follows:

| Income Component | Head of Income in Partner's ITR | Tax Treatment |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Remuneration (salary, bonus, commission) within 40(b) limits | Income from Business or Profession | Taxed at applicable slab rates; partner can claim related business expenses |
| Interest on capital within 12% p.a. | Income from Business or Profession | Taxed at slab rates |
| Share of profit from firm | Exempt under Section 10(2A) | Fully exempt - no tax in partner's hands |
| Remuneration beyond 40(b) limits | Income from Business or Profession (still taxable in partner's hands) | Taxable even though firm cannot deduct it - double exposure |
| Interest above 12% p.a. | Income from Business or Profession | Taxable in partner's hands; firm gets no deduction |

### Share of Profit is Tax-Free for Partners

The most important tax benefit in partnership taxation: a partner's share of profit from the firm is completely exempt under Section 10(2A), since the firm has already paid 30% tax on the profit. This prevents double taxation of the same income. Only the remuneration and interest components are additionally taxable in partners' hands. Partners should structure their withdrawals to maximise the exempt profit share and minimise the taxable remuneration where possible.

## Partnership Deed Requirements for Deductible Remuneration

For remuneration paid to partners to be deductible under Section 40(b), two conditions must be simultaneously met:

- The partnership deed must expressly authorise the payment of remuneration to the specific working partners (naming them or their designations). A general clause permitting 'remuneration to partners' is acceptable.
- The remuneration must be paid only for the period covered by the partnership deed - not for any prior period to which the deed does not apply. A retrospective deed cannot authorise deductible remuneration for prior periods.

If either condition is not met, the entire remuneration paid is disallowed under Section 40(b)(iv), even if the amount is within the prescribed limits. Courts have consistently held that remuneration without a properly authorising partnership deed is fully disallowed. Firms should review their partnership deed at the start of each year to ensure both conditions are met.

### Pro Tip

Partnership firms with high book profits can optimise tax by paying partners the maximum allowable remuneration under Section 40(b). For a book profit of Rs. 20 lakh, the maximum deductible remuneration is Rs. 12.9 lakh. If paid to partners who are in lower tax brackets (say 20% or below), the overall tax burden is lower than if the income remained with the firm at 30%. Always compute both scenarios before finalising remuneration for the year.

## Key Takeaways

1. Section 40(b) limits the deductible remuneration a firm can pay to its working partners - excess remuneration is disallowed at the firm level.
2. Book Profit is the firm's net profit before deducting partner remuneration - this is the base for Section 40(b) calculation.
3. Maximum deductible remuneration: 90% of first Rs. 3 lakh of book profit (or Rs. 1.5 lakh if higher) + 60% of balance book profit.
4. Interest on partner's capital is deductible only up to 12% per annum - interest above this rate is disallowed.
5. Partner's share of profit is fully exempt in the partner's hands under Section 10(2A) - no double taxation of profits.
6. Remuneration and interest received by partners are taxable as 'Business Income' in the partner's hands at slab rates.
7. The partnership deed must expressly authorise remuneration to specific working partners - absence of this clause results in full disallowance.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can a limited liability partnership (LLP) also claim remuneration deduction under Section 40(b)?

Yes. Section 40(b) applies to all forms of partnerships including LLPs. The designated partners of an LLP who are actively involved in the business qualify as working partners for Section 40(b) purposes. The LLP agreement must authorise the payment of remuneration to designated partners (equivalent to the partnership deed requirement). The same book profit formula and limits apply to LLPs as to traditional partnership firms.

### What if the firm has a loss (negative book profit)? Can partners still receive deductible remuneration?

Yes. In case of a loss (negative book profit), Section 40(b) provides a floor: the maximum deductible remuneration is Rs. 1,50,000 or 90% of book profit, whichever is higher. Since 90% of a negative number would be even more negative, the floor of Rs. 1,50,000 applies. This means a loss-making firm can still deduct up to Rs. 1,50,000 as partner remuneration - which would further increase the business loss and allow it to be carried forward. This is a useful provision for firms in their startup or loss-making years.

### A partner received Rs. 5 lakh remuneration but the firm can only deduct Rs. 3 lakh under 40(b). Is the Rs. 2 lakh disallowance also taxable in the partner's hands?

Yes. The Rs. 2 lakh disallowed at the firm level is still fully taxable as business income in the partner's hands. Section 40(b) disallowance operates at the firm level for deduction purposes - it does not affect the taxability of the amount in the recipient's hands. This is one of the harsh consequences of exceeding Section 40(b) limits: the firm pays tax on the disallowed amount (as part of increased firm income at 30%) AND the partner pays tax on the entire remuneration at their slab rate. Careful planning to stay within 40(b) limits avoids this double exposure.

### Can a partner claim deduction for expenses incurred personally for the firm's business against the remuneration income?

Yes. Remuneration received by a partner is taxable as 'Income from Business or Profession'. The partner can claim all expenses incurred wholly and exclusively for the purposes of earning that remuneration as deductions - for example, professional subscriptions, books, travel for business purposes (not reimbursed by firm), home office expenses. However, expenses must be genuine and supported by bills. The partner's capital contribution to the firm is not an expense but a balance sheet item.

### Is it mandatory for all working partners to receive the same remuneration, or can it vary?

There is no requirement for equal remuneration among working partners. The partnership deed can specify different remuneration for different working partners based on their roles, expertise, seniority, or contribution. The Section 40(b) limit applies to the total remuneration paid to all working partners collectively - not to each partner individually. As long as the total remuneration paid to all working partners is within the overall 40(b) limit, the distribution among partners can be in any ratio as specified in the deed.
