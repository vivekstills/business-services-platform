# Is an Interest-Free Loan from Employer Taxable?

When an employer provides a loan to an employee at zero interest or at a rate below the SBI benchmark rate, the interest subsidy is treated as a taxable perquisite under the Income Tax Act. However, there are important exemptions - loans up to Rs. 20,000 are fully exempt, and loans for medical treatment of specified diseases are entirely exempt regardless of amount. This article explains the valuation rules, the SBI benchmark rate mechanism, tax computation, Form 12BA reporting, and practical examples.

## Legal Framework - When Does a Loan Become a Taxable Perquisite?

Under Section 17(2)(viii) read with Rule 3(7)(i) of the Income Tax Rules, 1962, any loan provided by an employer to an employee (or any member of the employee's household) at an interest rate lower than the SBI benchmark lending rate is treated as a perquisite in the hands of the employee. The taxable value of the perquisite is the interest concession - the difference between what the employee actually pays and what they would have paid at the SBI benchmark rate.

This rule applies to both interest-free loans (where the employee pays zero interest) and concessional rate loans (where the employee pays some interest, but at a rate below SBI's benchmark). The perquisite is calculated on a monthly basis on the maximum outstanding loan balance during each month of the financial year.

## The SBI Benchmark Rate - How It Works

The benchmark rate used for computing the taxable perquisite is the State Bank of India's lending rate for the same type of loan (as of April 1 of the relevant financial year). Different SBI rates apply for different types of loans:

| Loan Type | Benchmark Rate Reference | Typical Rate (FY 2025-26) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Housing loan | SBI Home Loan Rate (1-year MCLR or repo-linked) | ~8.75% p.a. |
| Car loan / vehicle loan | SBI Car Loan Rate | ~9.00% p.a. |
| Personal loan / multipurpose | SBI Personal Loan Rate | ~11.00% p.a. |
| Education loan | SBI Education Loan Rate | ~9.15% p.a. |
| Any other type | SBI's applicable rate for that category | Varies by category |

### SBI Rate as of April 1 Is Used for the Full Year

The SBI benchmark rate prevailing on April 1 of the financial year is used for the entire year's perquisite computation - even if SBI revises its rates during the year. For FY 2025-26, the rate prevailing on April 1, 2025 applies for the entire April 2025 to March 2026 period. Employers must note this rate at the start of the year for payroll computation.

## Exemptions - When Is the Loan Not Taxable?

| Exemption Category | Condition | Limit |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Small loans | Aggregate loan balance at any time during the year does not exceed Rs. 20,000 | Full exemption - no perquisite at all |
| Medical treatment loans | Loan taken for medical treatment of employee or family member for specified diseases listed in Rule 3A | Full exemption - no cap on amount |
| Loan repaid in full before March 31 | If entire loan is repaid during the year and balance is zero at year end | No perquisite (no outstanding balance) |
| Loans covered under medical insurance | If medical treatment expenses are reimbursed by insurer | Loan to that extent is excluded from perquisite |

### Important

The Rs. 20,000 exemption is on the AGGREGATE outstanding loan balance across all loans from the employer at any time during the year - not per loan. If an employee has a housing loan of Rs. 15,000 outstanding and a personal loan of Rs. 10,000 outstanding simultaneously, the aggregate is Rs. 25,000 which exceeds the threshold, making the entire outstanding balance taxable.

## Valuation: How is the Perquisite Calculated?

The taxable perquisite value is computed as follows: for each month, take the maximum outstanding loan balance during that month, apply the SBI benchmark rate, and compute the monthly interest. Subtract any interest actually paid by the employee. The balance is the monthly perquisite. Sum all 12 months to get the annual perquisite value.

### Perquisite Calculation - Interest-Free Housing Loan

Employee receives Rs. 10,00,000 housing loan at 0% from employer on April 1, 2025. Employee repays Rs. 20,000 per month. SBI Home Loan Rate (April 1, 2025): 8.75% p.a.

**Month 1 (April 2025):**

- Maximum outstanding balance: Rs. 10,00,000
- SBI Interest @ 8.75% p.a.: Rs. 10,00,000 x 8.75% / 12 = Rs. 7,292
- Interest paid by employee: Rs. 0 (interest-free loan)
- Perquisite for April: Rs. 7,292

**Month 2 (May 2025):**

- Outstanding balance: Rs. 9,80,000 (after Rs. 20,000 repayment)
- SBI Interest: Rs. 9,80,000 x 8.75% / 12 = Rs. 7,146
- Perquisite for May: Rs. 7,146

**Annual perquisite (approx.):** ~Rs. 82,000 - Rs. 86,000 (declining balance)

This amount is added to salary income and subject to TDS.

## Tax Treatment and Form 12BA Reporting

The annual perquisite value is added to the employee's gross salary and is taxable at the applicable income tax slab rate. The employer includes the perquisite value in the TDS computation. It is reported in Form 12BA (Statement showing particulars of perquisites, other fringe benefits or amenities and profits in lieu of salary) which the employer must provide to the employee along with Form 16.

In the employee's ITR, the perquisite value appears under 'Salary as per Form 16' under the head 'Salaries' - it is already included in the salary figure provided by the employer. The employee does not need to separately compute or report it as long as the employer has correctly valued it in Form 12BA and TDS has been deducted accordingly.

## Concessional Rate Loan - Partial Perquisite

If the employer charges some interest but at a rate below the SBI benchmark, only the difference is a perquisite. For example, if SBI rate is 8.75% and employer charges 4%, the perquisite is based on 4.75% (8.75% minus 4%) applied on the outstanding balance. This partial perquisite is computed in the same way as for a zero-interest loan - monthly, on maximum outstanding balance.

### Pro Tip

If you have a large interest-free loan from your employer and want to reduce the perquisite tax, consider paying nominal interest at or above the SBI benchmark rate. Once you pay interest at or above the benchmark rate, there is zero perquisite. Many well-structured employee loan policies are designed with interest at SBI rate to avoid any perquisite tax burden while still providing attractive financing.

## Key Takeaways

1. Interest-free or concessional loans from employers are taxable perquisites under Section 17(2)(viii) read with Rule 3(7)(i).
2. The taxable value is the interest concession: SBI benchmark rate minus actual rate paid, applied to monthly maximum outstanding balance.
3. Loans up to Rs. 20,000 aggregate outstanding at any time are fully exempt - no perquisite arises.
4. Loans for medical treatment of specified diseases are fully exempt regardless of the outstanding amount.
5. The SBI rate as on April 1 of the financial year is used for the entire year's computation.
6. The perquisite is included in gross salary in Form 16 / Form 12BA and taxed at the employee's slab rate.
7. Paying interest at or above the SBI benchmark rate eliminates the perquisite entirely.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### My employer gave me a loan of Rs. 15,000 at zero interest. Is any perquisite taxable?

No. Since the outstanding loan balance of Rs. 15,000 is below the Rs. 20,000 threshold, no perquisite arises at any time during the year. The exemption applies as long as the aggregate outstanding loan balance from the employer never exceeds Rs. 20,000 at any point during the financial year. If you take another loan later in the year that pushes the aggregate above Rs. 20,000, the entire outstanding balance (not just the excess) becomes taxable from that point.

### Can an employee get both a housing loan interest deduction under Section 24(b) and avoid perquisite tax on an employer housing loan?

For Section 24(b) deduction (interest on home loan for self-occupied or let-out property), the loan must be from a bank, NBFC, or employer. Loans from employers do qualify for Section 24(b) deduction. However, the Section 24(b) deduction and the employer loan perquisite operate independently. If the employer charges zero interest, a perquisite arises (taxed as salary). Simultaneously, there is zero actual interest paid, so there is nothing to deduct under Section 24(b). If the employer charges the SBI rate, no perquisite, but the interest paid qualifies for Section 24(b) deduction.

### What diseases qualify for the medical loan exemption under Rule 3A?

Rule 3A specifies the following diseases for which medical loans are exempt: cancer, full-blown AIDS, chronic renal failure, haematological disorders (haemophilia, thalassaemia), neurological diseases (dementia, dystonia musculorum deformans, motor neuron disease, ataxia, chorea, hemiballismus, aphasia, Parkinson's disease), and any illness requiring surgery. The loan must be for treatment of the employee, spouse, children, parents, or dependents. The treating specialist's certificate may be required as evidence.

### What if the employee takes the loan from a group company (not the direct employer)?

If the loan is from an associated employer, group company, or subsidiary that is a connected employer, it is still treated as a loan from the employer for perquisite computation purposes. The definition of 'employer' in the perquisite rules includes connected employers and group entities. Loan from an unrelated third party (e.g., friend, bank) even at zero interest is not a perquisite - the rule specifically covers employer-provided benefits.

### If the employer adjusts the perquisite value incorrectly in Form 12BA, what should the employee do?

The employee should first bring the error to the employer's attention before Form 16 is issued. If the employer issues a wrong Form 16, the employee should request a revised Form 16. If the error is not corrected by the employer, the employee must independently compute the correct perquisite value, declare the correct amount in their ITR (which may differ from the employer's figures), and maintain supporting documentation. Filing a revised ITR after receiving the correct Form 16 later is also an option if the original return was filed with wrong data.
