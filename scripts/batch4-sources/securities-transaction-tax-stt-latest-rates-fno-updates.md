# Securities Transaction Tax (STT): Latest Rates, F&O Hike & Updates

## Article summary

Securities Transaction Tax (STT) is a tax levied on purchase and sale of securities listed on Indian stock exchanges. Introduced in 2004, STT replaced the earlier capital gains exemption on listed securities and has since become a significant revenue source. Budget 2024 significantly hiked STT on F&O (Futures & Options) - index futures rate doubled and options STT increased by 60%. This article covers all current STT rates across equity, F&O, and mutual funds, how STT is collected, its deductibility, and the impact of the Budget 2024 F&O hike.

## What is STT and why does it exist?

 Securities Transaction Tax (STT) is a direct tax levied under the Securities Transaction Tax Act, 2004. It is collected at source by the stock exchange or mutual fund house at the time of the securities transaction. STT is paid by the buyer and/or seller depending on the transaction type. It cannot be avoided, refunded, or set off (with limited exceptions) - it is a transaction cost, similar to stamp duty, built into every securities trade. STT was introduced as part of a policy package: the government abolished the long-term capital gains exemption on equities in 2004 (which was re-introduced only in 2018) and replaced it with STT. The logic was to make tax collection seamless and reduce avoidance. STT is a significant recurring transaction cost for active traders and must be factored into return calculations. Factor STT into per-trade P&L calculations. ## Is STT deductible as an expense?

 STT paid is NOT deductible as an expense for computing capital gains on delivery-based equity transactions. The gain is simply: Sale price minus Purchase price (no adjustment for STT paid). However, for F&O traders (where income is classified as business income), STT paid is deductible as a business expense - it reduces the net F&O profit or increases the F&O loss. This is one of the practical differences between capital gains treatment (STT not deductible) and business income treatment (STT deductible) for securities transactions. STT and the LTCG/STCG Framework For listed equity shares, the lower LTCG rate of 12.5% and STCG rate of 20% are available ONLY if STT has been paid on both the purchase and sale of the shares (for delivery-based transactions). If shares are sold off-market (not through a recognised stock exchange), or if STT was not paid, the normal capital gains rates apply - LTCG at 12.5% (without the preferential rate) for listed shares and slab rate for STCG. In practice, off-market transfers of listed shares are rare, but the condition is important for shares transferred through private deals. Pro Tip Active F&O and intraday traders should review their annual STT outflow carefully. With the Budget 2024 rate hike, STT has become a larger drag on returns. When comparing pre- and post-tax returns, include STT (as a transaction cost that reduces gross P&L). For F&O business income reporting, ensure STT is claimed as a deductible expense in the ITR-3 business income computation. KEY TAKEAWAYS 1. ## Frequently asked questions

**Q1.** I bought shares off-market (not through a broker). Will my LTCG still be taxed at 12.5%? For off-market purchase (no STT paid on purchase), the LTCG at sale is taxed at 12.5% without the STT-condition issue - because the STT condition for the preferential rate applies primarily to the sale side for listed shares. However, if both purchase and sale are off-market (STT not paid at either end), the preferential LTCG rate may be denied and normal rates apply. Most litigation in this area involves inherited shares, ESOP shares, or bonus shares where the 'purchase' was off-market. Consult a CA for specific off-market transactions.

**Q2.** Is STT included in the purchase price of shares for LTCG computation? No. STT paid on purchase of shares is NOT added to the cost of acquisition for capital gains purposes. The cost is the actual price paid for the shares (excluding STT and brokerage). Similarly, STT paid on sale is not deducted from the sale consideration. Both STT payments are ignored entirely in the capital gains computation for delivery-based shares. Only the purchase price and sale price matter.

**Q3.** I am a foreign portfolio investor (FPI). Do I pay STT? Yes. Foreign Portfolio Investors (FPIs) pay STT on their equity transactions in India at the same rates as resident investors. STT is a transaction-level tax regardless of the investor's residency status. FPIs cannot claim a credit for Indian STT against tax payable in their home country in most cases - it is an unrecoverable transaction cost.

**Q4.** I trade Nifty options. After Budget 2024, how much more STT am I paying annually? The STT on index options increased from 0.0625% to 0.1% of option premium - a 60% increase. For every Rs. 1 lakh of option premium sold, STT increased from Rs. 62.50 to Rs. 100. For an active trader selling Rs. 50 lakh of option premium monthly, the annual STT increase = (0.1% - 0.0625%) x Rs. 50L x 12 = 0.0375% x Rs. 6 crore = Rs. 22,500 additional STT per year. High-frequency options traders face significantly larger absolute increases.

**Q5.** Can I get a refund of STT if I cancel my trade or a trade is rejected? If a trade is not executed (rejected before execution), no STT is deducted since STT arises only on executed transactions. If a trade is executed and then reversed/cancelled (which is rare - most cancellations happen before execution), the STT on both legs may be recoverable through the exchange's dispute mechanism. In normal circumstances, once an equity transaction is executed and confirmed, the STT is final and not refundable.
