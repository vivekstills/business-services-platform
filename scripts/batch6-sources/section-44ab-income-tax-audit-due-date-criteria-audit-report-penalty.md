# Section 44AB Income Tax Audit: Applicability, Due Date and Penalty

## Article summary

Section 44AB of the Income Tax Act mandates that certain businesses and professionals get their accounts audited by a Chartered Accountant and submit a Tax Audit Report (Form 3CA/3CB along with Form 3CD) on or before the due date. For FY 2025-26, the threshold for businesses is Rs. 1 crore (or Rs. 10 crore if cash transactions are below 5%). The penalty for non-compliance is 0.5% of turnover or Rs. 1.5 lakh, whichever is lower. This article covers every applicability condition, the enhanced cash-based threshold, presumptive scheme interaction, due dates, and the consequences of non-compliance.

## Who must get a tax audit under Section 44AB?

A tax audit is mandatory for the following categories of taxpayers:

| Category | Threshold for mandatory audit | Condition |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Business (general) | Turnover or gross receipts exceed Rs. 1 crore | Standard rule |
| Business (cash threshold benefit) | Turnover exceeds Rs. 10 crore | Applies only if aggregate cash receipts are at most 5% of total receipts AND aggregate cash payments are at most 5% of total payments during the year |
| Profession | Gross receipts exceed Rs. 50 lakh | Applies to specified professionals not covered by Section 44ADA |
| Business or profession opting out of presumptive scheme (44AD or 44ADA) | Income declared below the prescribed presumptive rate | Audit mandatory even if turnover is within the normal threshold, if the taxpayer declares lower income and total income exceeds the basic exemption limit |
| Business under Section 44AE (goods vehicles) claiming lower income | Any turnover | Audit required if income declared is lower than the Section 44AE prescribed amount |

## The Rs. 10 crore cash threshold benefit

The enhanced Rs. 10 crore threshold (introduced in Budget 2021) provides significant relief to businesses that operate primarily through banking channels. To qualify for this higher threshold, TWO conditions must both be satisfied throughout the financial year:

- Cash receipts condition: Aggregate cash receipts during the year must not exceed 5% of total receipts.

- Cash payments condition: Aggregate cash payments during the year must not exceed 5% of total payments.

If either condition is breached at any point during the year, the standard Rs. 1 crore threshold applies. This means a business with Rs. 8 crore turnover that makes even 6% of its payments in cash loses the benefit and must get its accounts audited. The conditions must be monitored throughout the year, not just at year-end.

## Digital receipts incentive

The Rs. 10 crore threshold is effectively a reward for businesses that operate digitally. A trading firm with Rs. 9 crore annual turnover that collects and pays entirely through NEFT, UPI, or account payee cheques needs no tax audit. The same firm, if it accepts 6% of receipts in cash, must get audited. This provision strongly incentivises digital transactions in the business sector.

## Presumptive scheme opt-out and audit requirement

Section 44AD allows businesses with turnover up to Rs. 3 crore to declare 6% or 8% of turnover as income without maintaining books. If a taxpayer opting for Section 44AD in prior years now wants to declare actual income that is lower than the prescribed 6%/8%, a tax audit becomes mandatory provided their total income (including income from all other sources) exceeds the basic exemption limit.

### Example: Presumptive opt-out audit trigger

Business owner: Turnover Rs. 80 lakh | Prior year opted for 44AD This year: Actual profit Rs. 3 lakh (below 6% of Rs. 80L = Rs. 4.8L)

Since declared income < prescribed 44AD rate AND total income exceeds basic exemption limit of Rs. 3 lakh: Tax audit under Section 44AB is MANDATORY.

Even though turnover is only Rs. 80 lakh (far below Rs. 1 crore), the opt-out from presumptive taxation triggers the audit requirement.

## Forms used in tax audit

| Form | When used | Who signs |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Form 3CA | When accounts are already audited under another law (e.g., Companies Act, Co-operative Societies Act) | Chartered Accountant |
| Form 3CB | When accounts are NOT audited under any other law (most businesses and professionals) | Chartered Accountant |
| Form 3CD | Statement of particulars required under Section 44AB, filed along with Form 3CA or 3CB | Chartered Accountant (with taxpayer confirmation for specific clauses) |

## Due dates for tax audit report submission

| Category | Tax audit due date | ITR due date |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Business or professional requiring tax audit (non-TP case) | September 30 of the assessment year (September 30, 2026 for AY 2026-27) | October 31 of the assessment year |
| Cases involving international or specified domestic transactions (Transfer Pricing) | October 31 of the assessment year | November 30 of the assessment year |
| Partner of a firm requiring tax audit | Same as the firm's audit due date (October 31) | October 31 |

## Important

The tax audit report must be uploaded to the IT portal by the CA and then accepted by the taxpayer before it is treated as filed. If the CA uploads the report but the taxpayer does not accept it on the portal before the due date, it is treated as not filed. Both actions (CA upload and taxpayer acceptance) must happen on or before September 30 to avoid penalty.

## Penalty for non-compliance

Section 271B prescribes a penalty for failure to get accounts audited or failure to submit the audit report by the due date:

- Penalty amount: 0.5% of total sales, turnover, or gross receipts for the year, OR Rs. 1,50,000, whichever is lower.

- The penalty is levied on the taxpayer, not the CA.

- Reasonable cause defence: The penalty can be waived if the taxpayer can demonstrate a reasonable cause for the failure (e.g., natural disaster, medical emergency, CA non- availability). However, the burden of proof lies on the taxpayer.

- Prosecution: Wilful failure to get accounts audited can also attract prosecution under Section 276B in addition to the Section 271B penalty.

## Pro tip

Businesses that hover near the Rs. 1 crore or Rs. 10 crore threshold should track cumulative turnover from April itself. If turnover is likely to exceed the threshold, engage a CA early and start maintaining accounts in audit-ready format. Engaging a CA in February or March when turnover has already crossed the limit results in rushed audits, higher fees, and higher error risk in Form 3CD.
