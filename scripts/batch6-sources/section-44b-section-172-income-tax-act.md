# Section 44B and Section 172: Income Tax on Shipping Business

## Article summary

India has two special provisions for taxing the income of non-resident shipping companies: Section 44B provides a presumptive income computation for non-resident persons engaged in the shipping business, deeming 7.5% of specified receipts as their Indian income. Section 172 provides a mechanism for collecting advance tax on a voyage-by-voyage basis from ships of foreign companies calling at Indian ports. This article explains both provisions, the taxable receipts covered, the procedures for Section 172 clearance, treaty override considerations, and the interaction with the Tonnage Tax Scheme.

## Section 44B: Presumptive taxation for non-resident shipping

| Component | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Applicable to | Non-resident persons and foreign companies operating ships |
| Presumed income rate | 7.5% of specified receipts from Indian voyages |
| Tax rate on deemed income | 40% (foreign company rate) plus surcharge and cess |
| Effective rate as percentage of receipts | Approximately 3% of gross shipping receipts (7.5% x 40%) |
| Deductions allowed from 7.5% | No deductions are permitted from the deemed income |
 Section 44B applies to non-resident persons (including foreign companies) engaged in the business of operation of ships. It provides that the income of such a non-resident from the operation of ships shall be presumed to be an amount equal to 7.5% of the aggregate of the following amounts received or deemed to be received in India: • The amount paid or payable (whether in or out of India) to the non-resident or to any person on their behalf, on account of the carriage of passengers, livestock, mail, or goods from any port in India. • The amount received or deemed to be received in India on account of the carriage of passengers, livestock, mail, or goods from any port outside India.

The 7.5% presumed income is the final income for Indian tax purposes. No further deductions are allowed from this amount. ## Section 172: Voyage-by-voyage tax collection Section 172 provides a separate, voyage-specific collection mechanism for non-resident shipping income. It operates as follows: 1. When a ship of a non-resident owner or charterer carries passengers, cargo, or mail from an Indian port, before the ship is granted port clearance, the owner or agent must file a return of income for that voyage. 2. The return covers the income from that specific voyage (7.5% of voyage receipts under the presumptive formula). 3. Tax on that voyage income must be paid at the time of filing the return. 4. Once the tax is paid, the port authority will issue clearance to the ship. 5. Any overpayment or underpayment is adjusted when the regular annual assessment is completed.

## Section 172 vs Section 44B relationship Section 172 is a collection mechanism, not a separate charging provision. The actual income computation remains under Section 44B (7.5% of receipts). Section 172 ensures that the government collects tax before the foreign ship leaves India and there is no enforcement risk from a vessel that has departed. The voyage-by-voyage advance is credited against the final annual tax liability of the shipping company.

## Tax treaty override India has signed Double Taxation Avoidance Agreements (DTAAs) with many countries that contain specific provisions for shipping income. Under most DTAAs, shipping income of a resident of one country is taxable only in that country (the country of the ship's owner). This means a Norwegian shipping company operating ships that call at Indian ports may be entirely exempt from Indian tax under the India-Norway DTAA, despite Section 44B. Where a DTAA provides for exclusive taxation in the foreign country, the shipping company or its agent can claim treaty benefits and avoid Section 44B taxation. The claim must be supported by a Tax Residency Certificate (TRC) from the foreign country and a Form 10F filing in India. The port clearance under Section 172 is still required procedurally, but the tax payable may be nil under treaty protection.

## Tonnage Tax Scheme: alternative for Indian shipping companies Indian shipping companies (domestic companies) can opt for the Tonnage Tax Scheme under Sections 115V to 115VZC instead of the regular profit-based computation. Under this scheme, income from the operation of ships is computed based on the net tonnage of ships in the fleet (not actual profits). The scheme provides certainty and stability in tax liability and is not subject to MAT. Once opted, the company must stay in the scheme for a minimum of 10 years.

## Pro tip Non-resident shipping companies regularly operating in Indian waters should review applicable DTAAs before paying Section 172 voyage-by-voyage taxes. Many shipping companies from countries with favourable DTAAs overpay Section 172 tax and file refund claims. Setting up proper treaty protection documentation (TRC, Form 10F) at the start of operations avoids this cash flow issue.
