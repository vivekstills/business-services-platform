# Section 44ADA: Presumptive Tax Scheme for Professionals

## Article summary

## Eligible professions under Section 44ADA

Section 44ADA applies to resident individuals carrying on any of the following professions as specified under Section 44AA(1):

| Profession | Category examples |
| --- | --- |
| Legal | Advocates, barristers, solicitors, notaries |
| Medical | Doctors, dentists, physiotherapists, anaesthetists, radiologists, pathologists |
| Engineering | Civil engineers, structural engineers, mechanical engineers in consulting practice |
| Architectural | Architects, town planners, landscape designers |
| Accountancy | Chartered Accountants, Cost Accountants, Company Secretaries in practice |
| Technical consultancy | IT consultants, management consultants, software development consultants |
| Interior decoration | Interior designers, space planners |
| Other notified professions | Any profession notified by CBDT under the relevant rule |

Section 44ADA provides a simplified tax computation for resident individual professionals by allowing them to declare 50% of gross receipts as their taxable income, without maintaining books of accounts or undergoing a tax audit. For FY 2025-26, the gross receipts threshold is Rs. 75 lakh (provided cash receipts are at most 5%; otherwise Rs. 50 lakh). This article covers eligible professions, the receipt threshold in detail, how the deduction works, interaction with the new and old tax regimes, advance tax rules, and the consequences of opting out.

Who Is NOT Eligible Section 44ADA is available only to resident individuals. Partnership firms (including LLPs), companies, HUFs, and non-resident individuals cannot use this scheme. A firm of doctors or architects must maintain full books of accounts and cannot opt for 44ADA at the firm level, even though the individual partners may be eligible for 44ADA on their individual professional income.

## The gross receipts threshold for FY 2025-26

| Cash receipt condition | Applicable gross receipts limit | | --- | --- | | Cash receipts are at most 5% of total gross professional receipts | Rs. 75 lakh (enhanced limit) | | Cash receipts exceed 5% of total gross professional receipts | Rs. 50 lakh (standard limit) |

The 5% condition is assessed at the end of the financial year based on the actual cash versus digital split of total receipts. A professional who receives Rs. 4 lakh in cash out of Rs. 70 lakh total receipts (5.7%) would be treated as exceeding the 5% threshold and the Rs. 50 lakh limit applies. Since Rs. 70 lakh exceeds Rs. 50 lakh, that professional must either opt out (and maintain full books with audit) or stay in 44ADA but note that their receipts now exceed the applicable threshold.

## How the income computation works Under Section 44ADA, the professional's income from the profession is deemed to be 50% of gross professional receipts. No further deductions are allowed from this deemed income on account of any business expenses such as rent, salaries to staff, equipment, depreciation, or professional subscriptions. The presumed 50% income is the final income from the profession for all purposes. Deductions under Chapter VI-A (such as Section 80C, 80D, 80G) are still available against the total income (which includes the Section 44ADA professional income) to arrive at the net taxable income. ## Interaction with new tax regime Section 44ADA can be used under both the old and new tax regimes. The 50% presumed income figure is the same regardless of the regime chosen. The difference lies in which deductions are then available: ## Advance tax under Section 44ADA One of the compliance advantages of Section 44ADA is the simplified advance tax schedule. Instead of the four quarterly instalments required under the regular regime (15% by June 15, 45% by September 15, 75% by December 15, and 100% by March 15), professionals under Section 44ADA are required to pay only ONE instalment of advance tax equal to 100% of the estimated tax liability for the year, by March 15. Missing this single deadline attracts interest under Section 234B and 234C.

## Opting out and audit requirement Section 44ADA does not have the same strict 5-year lock-out as Section 44AD. A professional can freely opt in or opt out of 44ADA from year to year without any restriction. This flexibility makes it particularly suitable for professionals whose receipts or expense ratios fluctuate year to year. However, if the professional opts out in a year and their gross receipts exceed Rs. 50 lakh, they must maintain full books and get a tax audit under Section 44AB.

## Pro tip Professionals whose actual net profit margin is higher than 50% of receipts benefit most from Section 44ADA. For example, a doctor working from a hospital where the hospital provides all facilities (equipment, staff, space) may earn 70% to 80% net margins. By opting for 44ADA, only 50% is taxed, effectively sheltering 20%-30% of actual profit from tax. Always compare actual profit versus 50% of receipts before deciding.
