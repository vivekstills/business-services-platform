# Minimum Alternate Tax (MAT): Eligibility, Calculation and Credit

## Article summary

Minimum Alternate Tax (MAT) under Section 115JB ensures that companies that report book profits in their financial statements but show zero or negligible taxable income due to various deductions and exemptions pay at least a minimum amount of tax. The MAT rate is 15% of book profit (plus surcharge and cess). MAT paid in excess of regular tax can be carried forward as MAT Credit Entitlement for up to 15 years. This article explains the MAT computation, the book profit adjustments, MAT credit mechanism, exemptions from MAT, and the interaction with the new concessional tax regime under Section 115BAA.

## What is MAT and why does it exist? Companies use various legitimate deductions and exemptions under the Income Tax Act to reduce their taxable income to very low levels or even to zero, despite showing healthy book profits in their financial statements. MAT was introduced to ensure that such companies pay a minimum level of tax proportionate to their reported profitability. It prevents the situation where a company reports Rs. 100 crore profit to its shareholders but pays zero income tax due to exemptions and deductions. MAT applies when the tax payable under regular provisions is less than 15% of book profit. In that case, the company pays MAT at 15% of book profit. The excess (MAT paid over regular tax) is available as MAT Credit, which can be used in future years when regular tax exceeds MAT.

## MAT rate and its components

| Component | Rate |
| --- | --- |
| Base MAT rate | 15% of book profit |
| Surcharge (if book profit tax base > Rs. 1 crore) | 7% on MAT (for net income > Rs. 1 crore to Rs. 10 crore) |
| Surcharge (if book profit tax base > Rs. 10 crore) | 12% on MAT (for net income above Rs. 10 crore) |
| Health and Education Cess | 4% on (MAT + Surcharge) |
| Effective MAT rate (no surcharge) | 15% + 4% cess = 15.6% |
| Effective MAT rate (with 12% surcharge) | 15% + 12% surcharge + 4% cess = 17.47% approximately |
 

## Book profit computation under Section 115JB Book profit is the net profit as per the Profit and Loss Account (prepared under the Companies Act), after making the following adjustments: Amounts Added Back to Net Profit • Income tax paid or payable and any provision for tax (including deferred tax) • Amounts transferred to any reserves (general reserves, capital reserves, etc.) • Provision for unascertained liabilities (including contingency provisions) • Provision for losses of subsidiary companies • Dividends paid or proposed • Expenditure related to exempt income under Section 10 (other than Section 10(38) which no longer applies) • Notional loss on transfer of capital assets to special economic zones

Amounts Deducted from Net Profit • Amount withdrawn from reserves (if credited to P&L, now reversed) • Income exempt under Section 10 (dividends, long-term capital gains in certain cases) • Depreciation debited to P&L (then added back at book value depreciation under Companies Act) • Loss brought forward or unabsorbed depreciation, whichever is less

## MAT vs regular tax: which is payable? ### Example: MAT vs regular tax decision Company XYZ Ltd | FY 2025-26 Book Profit (as per P&L after adjustments): Rs. 10,00,00,000 Taxable Income (after IT Act deductions): Rs. 1,00,00,000

Regular Tax @ 25% (domestic company): Rs. 25,00,000 Add: Surcharge 7% (income > Rs. 1 crore): Rs. 1,75,000 Add: Cess 4%: Rs. 1,07,000 Total Regular Tax: Rs. 27,82,000

MAT @ 15% of Rs. 10 crore book profit: Rs. 1,50,00,000 Add: Surcharge 12% (book profit > Rs. 10 crore): Rs. 18,00,000 Add: Cess 4%: Rs. 6,72,000 Total MAT: Rs. 1,74,72,000

Since MAT (Rs. 1.74 crore) > Regular Tax (Rs. 27.82 lakh): Company pays MAT of Rs. 1,74,72,000. MAT Credit = Rs. 1,74,72,000 minus Rs. 27,82,000 = Rs. 1,46,90,000

## MAT credit entitlement under Section 115JAA The excess of MAT paid over the regular tax payable in any year is available as MAT Credit. This credit can be carried forward for up to 15 assessment years immediately following the year in which it arose. The credit can be utilised in a future year when regular tax payable exceeds MAT - the excess of regular tax over MAT in that year can be set off against the brought-forward MAT credit. ## Who is exempt from MAT? MAT does not apply to the following: • Companies opting for the new concessional tax rate under Section 115BAA (22% base rate) are exempt from MAT. Once a company opts for 115BAA, it cannot pay MAT and cannot utilise any MAT credit brought forward from earlier years. • New manufacturing companies opting for Section 115BAB (15% base rate for new domestic manufacturers set up after October 1, 2019) are also exempt from MAT. • Income accruing or arising to a company from life insurance business (taxed under the Insurance Act framework). • Shipping companies opting for the Tonnage Tax Scheme under Sections 115V to 115VZC.

## Pro tip Companies with large accumulated MAT credit should carefully evaluate whether to switch to the Section 115BAA regime. Opting for 115BAA (which offers a lower 22% tax rate) means forfeiting all accumulated MAT credit permanently. Compute the present value of the MAT credit versus the annual tax saving from the lower rate before making the switch. For some companies with large MAT credit balances, continuing under the regular regime may be more beneficial.
