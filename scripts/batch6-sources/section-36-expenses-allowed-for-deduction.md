# Section 36 of Income Tax Act: Expenses Allowed as Deductions

## Article summary

## Overview of Section 36 deductions

Section 36 is a list of specific allowable deductions from business income that supplement the general deduction under Section 37 (which allows any expenditure wholly and exclusively for business purposes). While Section 37 has a residual catch-all character, Section 36 provides explicit statutory authority for specific categories of expenditure. Understanding Section 36 is important because some of these deductions have specific conditions, limits, or computational rules that differ from the general principles under Section 37.

Section 36 of the Income Tax Act specifies a list of expenses that are expressly allowed as deductions in computing income from business or profession, in addition to the general deduction provision under Section 37. These include insurance premiums, employer contributions to recognised provident fund and approved gratuity fund, bad debts, scientific research expenditure, and several other specific items. This article covers each deduction under Section 36 with the applicable conditions and limits for FY 2025-26.

## Bad debts under Section 36(1)(vii): key rules Bad debt deduction is one of the most commonly claimed and contested deductions under Section 36. The critical conditions are: • The debt must have been taken into account in computing the income of the assessee in any previous year. This means the corresponding income (sale or service revenue) must have been offered to tax in an earlier year. • The debt must be written off as irrecoverable in the books of account of the assessee for the year in which deduction is claimed. • It is not necessary to prove that the debt has become irrecoverable in fact. The Supreme Court has held that the business judgment of the taxpayer regarding irrecoverability is sufficient. • The bad debt written off but subsequently recovered is taxable as business income in the year of recovery under Section 41(4).

## Important

Prior to Amendment in AY 2014-15:Prior to Amendment in AY 2014-15: Bad debts were allowed only if actually written off in the books. Post amendment, the requirement is that the debt must have been included in income in a prior year. Banks and NBFCs cannot claim Section 36(1)(vii) for bad debts that were previously covered by the provision for bad debts under Section 36(1)(viia). The two provisions are mutually exclusive for such entities.

## Interest on borrowed capital: Section 36(1)(iii) Interest on capital borrowed for business purposes is deductible under Section 36(1)(iii). However, there is one important restriction: interest paid on capital borrowed for acquiring a capital asset for business use is not deductible for the period starting from the date of borrowing to the date the asset is first put to use. This pre-use interest must be capitalised as part of the asset's cost. Once the asset is in use, subsequent interest is deductible as a revenue expense.

## Section 43B: payment timing rule for several Section 36 items Several deductions under Section 36 are subject to Section 43B, which requires that certain payments must be actually paid on or before the due date of filing the income tax return for the relevant year. If not paid by that date, the deduction is allowed only in the year of actual payment. Section 43B applies to: • Employer's contribution to PF, gratuity fund, and other welfare funds (Section 36(1)(iv), (v)) • Bonus and commission to employees (Section 36(1)(ii)) • Interest on loans from public financial institutions and scheduled banks

## Pro tip Businesses that claim bonus and PF contributions as deductions should ensure all such payments are actually made (not just accrued or provided for) on or before the ITR filing due date. A bonus accrued in the books for FY 2025-26 but not actually paid to employees by October 31, 2026 (ITR due date for audit cases) will not be deductible in FY 2025-26 and will shift to FY 2026-27.
