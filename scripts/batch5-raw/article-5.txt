How Are Gifts Taxed in India — Gift Tax 
Exemption & Complete Relatives List 
📅 Updated: May 2026 📅 9 min read 📅 AY 2026-27 
 
ARTICLE SUMMARY 
India does not have a standalone 'Gift Tax Act' (the old Gift Tax Act was repealed in 1998). 
Instead, gifts are taxed under Section 56(2)(x) of the Income Tax Act as 'Income from Other 
Sources' if received from non-relatives above Rs. 50,000 in a financial year. Gifts from 
specified relatives are completely exempt without any cap. This article provides the 
complete relatives list for different taxpayer categories (individuals, HUFs), the types of 
taxable gifts, special occasions exemptions, how gifted property is valued, and the capital 
gains implications of gifting appreciated assets. 
 
The Legal Framework — Section 56(2)(x) 
Section 56(2)(x), introduced with effect from April 1, 2017, provides that if any person receives 
any sum of money or property from any person without adequate consideration (i.e., as a gift or 
below market value), the received amount is taxable as 'Income from Other Sources' if: (a) the 
aggregate value of all such receipts during the financial year exceeds Rs. 50,000, and (b) the 
giver is not a 'specified relative' or the gift is not on a specified occasion. 
Before April 1, 2017, Section 56(2)(vii) applied — Section 56(2)(x) replaced it with broader 
coverage that now also applies to firms, companies, and trusts receiving inadequate 
consideration, not just individuals and HUFs. However, the relative exemption and occasion 
exemptions apply only to individuals and HUFs.

📅 Important 
Relatives NOT on the List — Common Mistakes: Uncle (parent's brother), Aunt (parent's sister), 
Nephew, Niece, Cousin — none of these are 'specified relatives' for Section 56(2)(x). Gifts from 
these relatives are treated like gifts from unrelated persons — taxable above Rs. 50,000. Many 
taxpayers incorrectly assume that any family member qualifies as an exempt relative. 
 
Specified Relatives for HUF 
For a Hindu Undivided Family (HUF) receiving a gift, the specified relative is defined as any 
member of the HUF. Therefore, any gift from any HUF member to the HUF is fully exempt. 
When the HUF receives property from a member, it is treated as a contribution to the HUF 
capital — not as taxable income.

Marriage Gift Exemption — Important Details 
The marriage occasion exemption is one of the most valuable gift tax provisions. Any gift 
received by the bride or groom on the occasion of their marriage is completely exempt from 
Section 56(2)(x) — regardless of the amount, regardless of whether the giver is a relative or 
unrelated, and regardless of whether the gift is cash or property. The exemption is specifically 
for the person getting married, not for their parents or other family members. Gifts to parents for 
hosting the wedding are NOT covered by the marriage exemption. 
There is no time limit precisely defined in the law for 'on the occasion of marriage' — gifts 
received a few weeks before or after the wedding are generally treated as covered by this 
exemption if there is a clear nexus to the marriage event. Large gifts received months before an 
informal engagement but formally gifted at the wedding are sometimes challenged by the tax 
department if the timing seems contrived. 
 
Capital Gains When Gifted Property Is Later Sold  
When a recipient sells property received as a gift, capital gains are computed based on the 
original cost of the property (i.e., the cost paid by the original donor — not the FMV at the time 
of gifting). The holding period also includes the donor's holding period. This means: 
• If the donor held shares for 8 years and gifted them, the recipient is deemed to have 
held them for 8 years from the moment they receive the gift — immediately qualifying as 
LTCG if sold. 
• If shares were originally purchased at Rs. 100 per share, gifted when FMV was Rs. 500, 
and then sold by the recipient at Rs. 600 — the LTCG = Rs. 600 - Rs. 100 = Rs. 500 per 
share (not Rs. 100 per share). 
• The recipient pays capital gains tax on the entire appreciation — including the 
appreciation during the donor's ownership period. 
 
📅 Pro Tip 
Gifting appreciated shares or property to a low-income family member (e.g., a non-working 
spouse, or a student child) is a legal income-splitting strategy. The capital gains when the asset 
is eventually sold will be taxed in the recipient's hands at their (lower) slab rate rather than the 
donor's higher rate. The gift itself is exempt if from a specified relative. This is a common and 
accepted tax planning technique. 
 
KEY TAKEAWAYS 
1.  Section 56(2)(x) taxes gifts from non-relatives as 'Income from Other Sources' if aggregate 
value exceeds Rs. 50,000 in a year. 
2.  Gifts from specified relatives are FULLY EXEMPT regardless of amount — the relatives list is 
specific and narrow. 
3.  Marriage gifts are fully exempt — regardless of amount, source, or type of gift (cash or 
property). 
4.  Inherited property and gifts received under a Will are fully exempt. 
5.  Uncle, aunt, nephew, niece, and cousins are NOT on the specified relatives list — their gifts 
are taxable above Rs. 50,000. 
6.  When gifted property is sold, capital gains are computed using the original donor's cost and 
holding period. 
7.  For HUFs, any gift from any member of the HUF to the HUF is exempt — HUF members are 
'specified relatives' for the HUF. 
 
Frequently Asked Questions 
Q1. My mother gave me Rs. 10 lakh to buy a house. Is this taxable? 
No. Your mother (lineal ascendant) is a specified relative under Section 56(2)(x). Gifts from 
specified relatives are completely exempt regardless of the amount. The Rs. 10 lakh from 
your mother is not taxable in your hands. You do not need to report this gift as income. 
However, if you invest the money and earn interest, that interest income is taxable in your 
hands (clubbing provisions would not apply since you are an adult child). 
Q2. I received Rs. 75,000 in total from various friends and colleagues during my wedding. 
Is it taxable? 
No. The marriage occasion exemption covers all gifts received on the occasion of marriage 
— from anyone (relatives and non-relatives alike) and in any amount. The Rs. 50,000 
threshold under Section 56(2)(x) does not apply to marriage gifts. The entire Rs. 75,000 is 
exempt. Maintain a simple record of the wedding date and the gifts received as supporting 
documentation in case it is ever questioned. 
Q3. I gifted my daughter Rs. 5 lakh in shares (appreciated from Rs. 1 lakh original cost). 
What are the tax consequences for me and for her? 
For you (the giver): Gifting to a specified relative (daughter = lineal descendant) does NOT 
trigger capital gains in your hands — the gift is not a 'transfer' that attracts capital gains 
when given to a relative (Section 47(iii) exemption). For your daughter (the receiver): The 
gift is exempt since you are a specified relative. When she eventually sells the shares, 
LTCG will be computed using your original cost of Rs. 1 lakh (not the FMV at time of gift) 
and your holding period will be included. 
Q4. I received a plot of land worth Rs. 20 lakh from my friend as a gift. What is taxable? 
Your friend is not a specified relative. The FMV (or stamp duty value) of Rs. 20 lakh exceeds 
the Rs. 50,000 threshold. The ENTIRE Rs. 20 lakh is taxable as 'Income from Other 
Sources' in your hands at the applicable slab rate — not just the amount above Rs. 50,000. 
This is a very significant tax implication: receiving high-value property gifts from non-
relatives creates a large income tax liability. 
Q5. Does clubbing apply when I gift money to my spouse and they invest it? 
Yes — the Income Tax Act has clubbing provisions for gifts to spouses. If you gift money to 
your spouse and the spouse invests it, the income earned on that investment is clubbed with 
your income (not your spouse's) and taxed in your hands. The clubbing applies to the 
'immediate income' from the gifted amount. However, if the spouse reinvests the income 
from the original investment, the income-on-income may not be clubbed — this is a nuanced 
area. Gifting to a non-working adult child does not attract clubbing (only gifts to minor 
children attract limited clubbing provisions).
