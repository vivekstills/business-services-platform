# Trademark registration in India: complete process and classes

A trademark is any sign, symbol, word, phrase, logo, design, or combination thereof that distinguishes your goods or services from those of competitors. It is the visual and linguistic identity of your brand.

Registering a trademark in India gives you the exclusive right to use that mark across the country in relation to the goods and services for which it is registered. Without registration, your brand name or logo has limited legal protection, and anyone can replicate it without much legal recourse.

This guide covers the entire trademark registration process in India under the Trade Marks Act, 1999, including the class system, costs, timelines, and what to do after registration.

## Why register a trademark?

| Benefit | Without registration | With registration |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Legal protection | Common law rights only (limited geography) | Nationwide statutory protection |
| Right to sue for infringement | Complex and expensive | Straightforward under Sections 29 to 30 |
| Licensing | Can be licensed but enforcement is weak | Formally licensable and recordable |
| Asset value | Not a formal IP asset | Intangible asset on balance sheet |
| TM vs R symbol | Can use TM | Can use (R) after registration |
| Customs protection | Limited | Can record with Customs to block counterfeits |
| Duration | Perpetual but harder to enforce | 10 years, renewable indefinitely |

## Understanding the Nice classification system

India follows the International Classification of Goods and Services under the Nice Agreement. There are 45 classes: Classes 1 to 34 cover goods, and Classes 35 to 45 cover services.

### Selected goods classes

| Class | Description | Examples |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 5 | Pharmaceutical and veterinary products | Medicines, dietary supplements |
| 9 | Electronics and scientific instruments | Software, smartphones, apps |
| 14 | Jewellery and timepieces | Rings, watches, clocks |
| 16 | Paper goods and printed matter | Stationery, books, packaging |
| 25 | Clothing and footwear | Apparel, shoes, caps |
| 29 | Processed food (meat, dairy, etc.) | Cheese, packaged vegetables |
| 30 | Staple foods (flour, coffee, bakery) | Bread, spices, chocolates |
| 33 | Alcoholic beverages | Whisky, wine, beer |

### Selected service classes

| Class | Description | Examples |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 35 | Advertising and business services | Marketing, retail services |
| 36 | Financial and insurance services | Banking, investment advisory |
| 38 | Telecommunications | Internet services, broadcast |
| 41 | Education and entertainment | Online courses, events |
| 42 | Scientific and IT services | Software development, SaaS |
| 43 | Food and hospitality services | Restaurants, catering |
| 45 | Legal and security services | Law firms, security agencies |

> Choosing the right class is critical. If you register in the wrong class, your trademark will not protect you in the category where you actually operate. Many businesses register in multiple classes.

## Step-by-step trademark registration process in India

### Step 1: Trademark search

Before filing, conduct a comprehensive trademark search on the IP India Trademark Search portal (`https://ipindiaonline.gov.in/tmrpublicsearch/`). Search for:

- Identical marks
- Phonetically similar marks
- Visually similar logos
- Marks in the same or related classes

This step prevents filing a mark that will be rejected on grounds of similarity to an existing registered mark.

### Step 2: Determine the class(es)

Identify which Nice Classification class(es) your goods or services fall under. If your brand covers multiple categories (for example, clothing and an app), file in multiple classes.

### Step 3: Prepare the application

Gather the following:

- Applicant's name and address
- Nature of applicant (individual, company, partnership, LLP, trust)
- Representation of the mark (logo file, word mark)
- Description of goods or services
- Class(es) to be covered
- User affidavit if claiming prior use (Form TM-M)

### Step 4: File the application

File online through the IP India portal or physically at the Trade Marks Registry offices in Mumbai, Delhi, Chennai, Kolkata, or Ahmedabad.

**Government filing fees:**

| Applicant type | Per class (e-filing) | Per class (physical filing) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Individual, startup, MSME | Rs. 4,500 | Rs. 5,000 |
| Others (companies, LLPs, etc.) | Rs. 9,000 | Rs. 10,000 |

Upon filing, you receive an application number and a filing date. The TM symbol can now be used immediately (even before registration is complete).

### Step 5: Vienna codification (for logo marks)

If your mark includes a device or logo, the examiner applies Vienna Codes to classify the figurative elements for search purposes.

### Step 6: Examination

The Trademark Examiner reviews the application within 12 to 18 months and issues an Examination Report. The report may:

- Accept the application: proceed to publication
- Raise objections: common grounds include similarity to existing marks, descriptiveness, or deceptive marks

### Step 7: Reply to examination report (if objected)

If an objection is raised, you must file a Reply within 30 days (extendable to 2 months). If the objection persists, you can request a Hearing before the Registrar.

### Step 8: Publication in trademark journal

If accepted (after examination or after overcoming objections), the mark is published in the Trade Marks Journal. This invites the public to oppose the mark within 4 months.

### Step 9: Opposition (if any)

If a third party files an opposition, the matter enters an adversarial proceeding before the Trademark Registrar. Both parties file evidence and arguments. This can extend the timeline by 2 to 4 years in contested cases.

### Step 10: Registration

If no opposition is filed (or opposition is dismissed), the Registrar issues the Registration Certificate. The (R) symbol can now be used. The registration is effective from the date of application, not the date of registration.

## Timeline overview

| Stage | Approximate duration |
| --- | --- |
| Filing to examination report | 12 to 18 months |
| Reply to objection plus hearing | 3 to 6 months |
| Publication in journal | 2 to 3 months after acceptance |
| Opposition period | 4 months |
| Registration after no opposition | 2 to 4 months |
| Total (uncontested) | 18 to 24 months |
| Total (contested) | 3 to 5 years |

## Trademark renewal

A registered trademark is valid for 10 years from the date of registration. It can be renewed indefinitely every 10 years by filing Form TM-R with the renewal fee. Renewal must be applied for before the expiry date. A six-month grace period with a surcharge is available if the initial deadline is missed.

## Penalties for trademark infringement

| Violation | Consequence |
| --- | --- |
| Using a registered trademark without permission | Civil suit for injunction and damages |
| Counterfeiting a registered trademark | Criminal prosecution; imprisonment 6 months to 3 years plus fine Rs. 50,000 to Rs. 2 lakhs |
| Falsely applying a trademark | Imprisonment up to 3 years plus fine |
| Using similar marks to cause confusion | Injunction plus damages plus account of profits |

## Frequently asked questions

**Q1. Can I use the TM symbol before my trademark is officially registered?**

Yes. The TM symbol (and SM for service marks) can be used immediately upon filing the trademark application. It signals to the public that you are claiming trademark rights over the mark. The (R) symbol, however, can only be used after the Trademark Registrar issues the Certificate of Registration. Using (R) before registration is a legal offence under the Trade Marks Act.

**Q2. If someone else is using my brand name but has not registered it, can I still register the trademark?**

Yes, you can apply for registration, but it is not guaranteed. If the other party has been using the mark for a long time and has built substantial goodwill, they can oppose your application based on prior use. Indian law recognises common law rights (under the tort of passing off) for unregistered but well-known marks. To avoid a costly dispute, conduct a thorough market search before filing, not just a registry search.

**Q3. Do I need a separate trademark registration for each state in India?**

No. India has a unified national trademark registry. A single trademark registration provides protection across all states and union territories. There is no state-level trademark registration system in India. However, if you want international protection, you need to file separately under the Madrid Protocol for each target country.

**Q4. What is the difference between a wordmark and a device mark?**

A wordmark is a trademark consisting solely of text, such as the brand name Tata or Infosys in a standard font. A device mark includes a logo, stylised font, or graphical element. Many businesses file both a wordmark (for the word itself, regardless of font or design) and a device mark (for the specific logo). This provides broader protection. Filing a wordmark is generally stronger for long-term brand protection.

**Q5. My trademark registration was rejected. What are my options?**

If the Trademark Registry rejects your application after examination and hearing, you can appeal to the Intellectual Property Appellate Board (IPAB). With recent reforms, trademark appeals now go to the High Court (Commercial Courts) since the IPAB was abolished in 2021. You have three months from the date of the order to file an appeal. Alternatively, you can file a fresh application with a different or modified mark that addresses the objections raised.
