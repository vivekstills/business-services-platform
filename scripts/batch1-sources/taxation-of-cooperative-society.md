# Taxation of Co-operative Society in India

Co-operative societies occupy a unique and privileged position in India's tax framework. Treated as a separate class of assessees under the Income Tax Act, they have their own slab rates, powerful deductions under Section 80P, and are subject to Alternative Minimum Tax (AMT) rather than MAT. The Finance Act 2023 introduced Section 115BAD offering a flat 22% tax rate. This article explains every aspect of co-operative society taxation for AY 2026-27 - from basic tax rates and the critical 80P deduction to AMT computation, filing requirements, and compliance deadlines.

## What is a Co-operative Society? Legal Definition

A co-operative society is defined under Section 2(19) of the Income Tax Act, 1961 as a society registered under the Co-operative Societies Act, 1912, or under any other law for the time being in force in any State for the registration of co-operative societies. These are voluntary democratic organisations formed by members to meet their collective economic, social, and cultural needs.

The co-operative sector in India is vast - spanning agricultural credit, dairy farming, fisheries, housing, consumer goods, and banking. Major categories include: Primary Agricultural Credit Societies (PACS), Urban Co-operative Banks, District Central Co-operative Banks (DCCBs), State Co-operative Banks, Milk Producers Co-operatives (like AMUL), Housing Co-operative Societies, Consumer Co-operative Stores, and Primary Co-operative Marketing Societies.

## Tax Rates - Option 1: Regular Slab-Based Rates

Under the regular regime, co-operative societies are taxed on their net taxable income (after 80P deductions) as per the following slab rates:

| Income Slab | Tax Rate | Example Calculation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| First Rs. 10,000 | 10% | Max Rs. 1,000 |
| Rs. 10,001 to Rs. 20,000 | 20% | Max Rs. 2,000 |
| Above Rs. 20,000 | 30% | On entire amount above Rs. 20,000 |

To the tax computed above, the following additions are made: Surcharge @ 12% if total income exceeds Rs. 1 crore. Health and Education Cess @ 4% on the total of income tax plus surcharge. For income between Rs. 1 crore and Rs. 10 crore, the effective tax rate including cess is approximately 34.944%.

## Tax Rates - Option 2: Concessional Rate Under Section 115BAD

The Finance Act 2023 inserted Section 115BAD to offer co-operative societies an alternative flat tax rate of 22%, similar to the concessional rate available to domestic companies under Section 115BAA. This option was introduced to reduce the tax burden on societies willing to forego specified exemptions and deductions.

| Parameter | Section 115BAD Details |
| --- | --- |
| Flat Tax Rate | 22% on total income (no slabs) |
| Surcharge Rate | 10% (regardless of income level - fixed cap) |
| Health & Education Cess | 4% on (tax + surcharge) |
| Effective Tax Rate (all-in) | 25.168% - approximately |
| AMT Applicability | NOT applicable - major advantage |
| Section 80P deductions | NOT available - must be forgone |
| Depreciation method | As per Section 32 (WDV method) only |
| Set-off of brought forward losses | Allowed only if computed under new regime |
| Irrevocability | Once opted, CANNOT switch back to old regime |
| Form to file | Form 10-IF before due date of ITR (one-time election) |

### Important

The election to opt for Section 115BAD is irrevocable. A co-operative society that opts for this regime permanently forgoes Section 80P deductions - the most valuable tax benefit for co-operative societies. Before opting, carefully compute the tax liability under both regimes for at least 3-5 years. Societies with large 80P-eligible income (e.g., primary agricultural credit societies) may be significantly better off under the old regime despite the lower 22% rate.

## Section 80P - The Heart of Co-operative Society Tax Benefits

Section 80P is the most powerful tax benefit available to co-operative societies. It provides a 100% deduction (or limited deduction for specified activities) from the Gross Total Income of the society. The effect is to make income from qualifying cooperative activities virtually tax-exempt. The deduction is available only under the old (regular) tax regime - not under Section 115BAD.

| Activity / Income Category | Section | Deduction |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Banking or credit activities - primary credit society / PACS | 80P(2)(a)(i) | 100% of profits from such activity |
| Cottage industries (handlooms, handicrafts, etc.) | 80P(2)(a)(ii) | 100% of profits |
| Marketing of agricultural produce grown by members | 80P(2)(a)(iii) | 100% of profits |
| Purchase of agricultural implements for members | 80P(2)(a)(iv) | 100% of profits |
| Processing of agricultural produce without power | 80P(2)(a)(v) | 100% of profits |
| Collective disposal of labour of members | 80P(2)(a)(vi) | 100% of profits |
| Fishing or allied activities with members' boats | 80P(2)(a)(vii) | 100% of profits |
| Income from letting property to members for residential use | 80P(2)(c) | 100% of profits |
| Consumer co-operative society (all income) | 80P(2)(b) | Up to Rs. 1,00,000 |
| Interest / dividend from investments in co-operative societies | 80P(2)(d) | 100% of such income |
| Interest on government securities | 80P(2)(e) | Up to Rs. 50,000 |
| Income from dairy activities (milk producers' society) | 80P(2)(a)(vi) | 100% of profits |

## What Income Does NOT Qualify for 80P Deduction

The following income categories of co-operative societies are explicitly excluded from Section 80P benefits or have been held to be excluded by courts and tribunals:

- Income from co-operative banks (other than PACS and PCARDB): Excluded by Finance Act 2006; urban co-operative banks do not get 80P on their banking income.
- Rental income from properties let to non-members: Regular property rental without a co-operative activity link.
- Capital gains on sale of investments, property, or other assets: Capital gains are not from co-operative business activities.
- Income from non-members: If the society transacts with non-members, proportionate income from such transactions may not qualify.
- Interest from commercial banks on fixed deposits (beyond Rs. 50,000 limit): Excess interest income is taxable at normal rates.

## Alternative Minimum Tax (AMT) for Co-operative Societies

Co-operative societies are NOT subject to MAT (Minimum Alternate Tax under Section 115JB, which applies to companies). Instead, they are liable to Alternate Minimum Tax (AMT) under Section 115JC. AMT applies when the regular income tax payable is less than 18.5% of the 'Adjusted Total Income' (ATI). ATI is broadly the total income before certain deductions including Chapter VI-A deductions like 80P.

If AMT is triggered, the society pays 18.5% of ATI plus surcharge (12% if income exceeds Rs. 1 crore) plus cess (4%). AMT Credit equal to (AMT paid minus regular tax) can be carried forward and set off in future years when regular tax exceeds AMT, for up to 15 assessment years. Form 29C (AMT computation report) must be filed along with the ITR.

| AMT Parameter | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| AMT Rate | 18.5% of Adjusted Total Income |
| Surcharge on AMT | 12% if income exceeds Rs. 1 crore |
| Cess | 4% on (AMT + surcharge) |
| AMT Credit carry forward | Up to 15 assessment years |
| Form for AMT computation | Form 29C (CA certificate) |
| Under Section 115BAD | AMT NOT applicable - major advantage |
| Trigger condition | Regular tax < 18.5% of Adjusted Total Income |

### Pro Tip

For co-operative societies with high 80P deductions that significantly reduce taxable income, AMT can become the binding tax computation. Before the financial year ends, compute both regular tax and AMT to estimate advance tax accurately. Ignoring AMT when 80P deductions are large is a common cause of advance tax shortfall and consequent interest under Section 234B and 234C.

## Key Takeaways

1. Co-operative societies are taxed at 10%, 20%, and 30% slabs on net taxable income, or may opt for a flat 22% under Section 115BAD.
2. Section 80P provides 100% deduction on profits from core co-operative activities - effectively making most PACS and milk societies near tax-exempt.
3. Urban co-operative banks do NOT get Section 80P on banking income since the Finance Act 2006 amendment.
4. AMT under Section 115JC applies at 18.5% of Adjusted Total Income - AMT credit can be carried forward for 15 years.
5. Section 115BAD eliminates AMT applicability but permanently removes Section 80P deductions - evaluate carefully before opting.
6. Form ITR-5 is the applicable ITR; non-audit societies file by July 31 and audit cases by October 31.
7. Form 10-IF must be filed to opt into the 115BAD regime; this election is irrevocable once made.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can a housing co-operative society claim Section 80P on its maintenance charge collections from members?

The taxability and 80P deductibility of maintenance charges collected by housing societies has been a subject of significant litigation. The prevailing view, supported by several ITAT decisions and High Court rulings, is that maintenance charges collected for upkeep of common areas and facilities are in the nature of mutual receipts and are not taxable income at all - under the principle of mutuality. If they are held taxable, 80P(2)(c) may apply to income from letting properties to members. Each society must evaluate its specific activity mix with a qualified CA.

### An MSME buys agricultural produce from a farmers' co-op. The co-op sells non-members too. Does the 80P deduction apply to income from non-members?

Section 80P(2) deductions generally apply to income from co-operative activities with members. Income from non-members does not qualify under most clauses of 80P. Courts have held that the deduction is limited to income arising from transactions with members. Income from non-members should be segregated in accounts and taxed at normal slab rates. Some courts have allowed proportionate deduction - however, this is fact-specific and should be determined on advice of a tax professional.

### Is a co-operative society required to audit its accounts even if turnover is below Rs. 1 crore?

Under income tax law, tax audit under Section 44AB is triggered when turnover exceeds Rs. 1 crore (business) or Rs. 50 lakh (profession). However, co-operative societies may also be subject to audit under the Co-operative Societies Act of their respective state - which has independent criteria. State law audit requirements may mandate audit even below the income tax audit threshold. Societies must comply with the most stringent applicable audit requirement - whichever is triggered first.

### Can a new co-operative society opt for Section 115BAD in its very first year of existence?

Yes. A newly registered co-operative society can opt for Section 115BAD right from its first assessment year. The option must be exercised by filing Form 10-IF on or before the due date for filing the ITR for that first year. Since the society has no prior years under the old regime, there is no comparison loss from switching. However, since 80P deductions would be lost permanently, new societies should project their income streams and whether their activities would have qualified for 80P before making this election.

### If a co-operative society has unabsorbed depreciation from earlier years, can it set it off against income in the current year?

Yes, subject to conditions. Unabsorbed depreciation can be carried forward indefinitely and set off against business income in subsequent years under Section 32(2) of the Income Tax Act. For societies that have opted for Section 115BAD, set-off of brought-forward losses and depreciation is allowed only if those losses or depreciation were computed under the 115BAD regime. Losses computed under the old regime (with 80P deductions) cannot be set off in years when the society is operating under the new 115BAD regime.
