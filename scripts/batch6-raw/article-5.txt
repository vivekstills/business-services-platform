Section 36 of Income Tax Act: Expenses Allowed 
as Deductions 
Updated: May 2026 Read time: 8 min read AY: 2026-27 
 
ARTICLE SUMMARY 
Section 36 of the Income Tax Act specifies a list of expenses that are expressly allowed as 
deductions in computing income from business or profession, in addition to the general 
deduction provision under Section 37. These include insurance premiums, employer 
contributions to recognised provident fund and approved gratuity fund, bad debts, scientific 
research expenditure, and several other specific items. This article covers each deduction 
under Section 36 with the applicable conditions and limits for FY 2025-26. 
 
Overview of Section 36 Deductions 
Section 36 is a list of specific allowable deductions from business income that supplement the 
general deduction under Section 37 (which allows any expenditure wholly and exclusively for 
business purposes). While Section 37 has a residual catch-all character, Section 36 provides 
explicit statutory authority for specific categories of expenditure. Understanding Section 36 is 
important because some of these deductions have specific conditions, limits, or computational 
rules that differ from the general principles under Section 37.

Bad Debts Under Section 36(1)(vii) - Key Rules 
Bad debt deduction is one of the most commonly claimed and contested deductions under 
Section 36. The critical conditions are: 
• The debt must have been taken into account in computing the income of the assessee in 
any previous year. This means the corresponding income (sale or service revenue) must 
have been offered to tax in an earlier year. 
• The debt must be written off as irrecoverable in the books of account of the assessee for 
the year in which deduction is claimed. 
• It is not necessary to prove that the debt has become irrecoverable in fact. The Supreme 
Court has held that the business judgment of the taxpayer regarding irrecoverability is 
sufficient. 
• The bad debt written off but subsequently recovered is taxable as business income in 
the year of recovery under Section 41(4). 
 
Important 
Prior to Amendment in AY 2014-15: Bad debts were allowed only if actually written off in the 
books. Post amendment, the requirement is that the debt must have been included in income in 
a prior year. Banks and NBFCs cannot claim Section 36(1)(vii) for bad debts that were previously 
covered by the provision for bad debts under Section 36(1)(viia). The two provisions are mutually 
exclusive for such entities. 
 
Interest on Borrowed Capital - Section 36(1)(iii) 
Interest on capital borrowed for business purposes is deductible under Section 36(1)(iii). 
However, there is one important restriction: interest paid on capital borrowed for acquiring a 
capital asset for business use is not deductible for the period starting from the date of borrowing 
to the date the asset is first put to use. This pre-use interest must be capitalised as part of the 
asset's cost. Once the asset is in use, subsequent interest is deductible as a revenue expense. 
 
Section 43B - Payment Timing Rule for Several Section 36 Items 
Several deductions under Section 36 are subject to Section 43B, which requires that certain 
payments must be actually paid on or before the due date of filing the income tax return for the 
relevant year. If not paid by that date, the deduction is allowed only in the year of actual 
payment. Section 43B applies to: 
• Employer's contribution to PF, gratuity fund, and other welfare funds (Section 36(1)(iv), 
(v)) 
• Bonus and commission to employees (Section 36(1)(ii)) 
• Interest on loans from public financial institutions and scheduled banks 
 
Pro Tip 
Businesses that claim bonus and PF contributions as deductions should ensure all such 
payments are actually made (not just accrued or provided for) on or before the ITR filing due 
date. A bonus accrued in the books for FY 2025-26 but not actually paid to employees by 
October 31, 2026 (ITR due date for audit cases) will not be deductible in FY 2025-26 and will 
shift to FY 2026-27. 
 
KEY TAKEAWAYS 
1.  Section 36 provides a specific list of deductions for business income, complementing the 
general provision of Section 37. 
2.  Key deductions include: insurance on stock, employee bonuses (Section 43B compliant), 
interest on borrowed capital, PF and gratuity contributions, and bad debts written off. 
3.  Bad debt deduction requires the corresponding income to have been offered to tax in a prior 
year and the debt to be written off in the books. 
4.  Interest on borrowings for capital assets cannot be deducted for the pre-use period and must 
be capitalised as part of asset cost. 
5.  STT and CTT paid are deductible under Section 36 only for traders where the securities 
income is classified as business income. 
6.  Several Section 36 deductions (bonus, PF, gratuity) are subject to the actual payment timing 
condition under Section 43B. 
7.  Banks have a special provision (Section 36(1)(viia)) for provisions for bad debts based on 
specified percentages of advances. 
 
Frequently Asked Questions 
Q1. I run an e-commerce business. Can I claim insurance premium on my warehouse 
stock under Section 36(1)(i)? 
Yes. Insurance premium paid to protect your stock in trade (goods stored in the warehouse 
for sale) against fire, flood, theft, or other damage is expressly deductible under Section 
36(1)(i). Ensure the insurance policy covers trading stock (inventory) and not fixed assets 
like the warehouse building itself (which would be covered under Section 37 as a general 
business expense). 
Q2. My company accrued Rs. 20 lakh bonus for employees in March 2026 but paid it in 
May 2026. Can I claim the deduction in FY 2025-26? 
No. Bonus is subject to Section 43B. Even if accrued in the books for FY 2025-26, the 
deduction is allowed only in the year of actual payment. Since the bonus was paid in May 
2026 (FY 2026-27), the deduction will be available only in AY 2027-28. To claim the 
deduction in FY 2025-26, the bonus must be paid on or before the ITR filing due date for FY 
2025-26 (October 31, 2026 for audit cases; July 31, 2026 for others). 
Q3. A customer declared bankruptcy and owes us Rs. 5 lakh. Can we claim bad debt 
deduction? 
Yes, provided the following: the Rs. 5 lakh sales revenue was included in your income and 
offered to tax in the year of sale (not just recorded as a receivable). You must write off the 
Rs. 5 lakh as bad debt in your books for FY 2025-26. The customer's bankruptcy is strong 
evidence of irrecoverability, but you do not need a court order - writing off the books based 
on business judgment is sufficient. If you later recover any amount, the recovered amount 
will be taxable as business income in the year of recovery under Section 41(4). 
Q4. Can an individual professional claim bad debt deduction under Section 36(1)(vii)? 
A professional can claim bad debt deduction only if they maintain their accounts on the 
mercantile (accrual) basis. Under the mercantile system, professional fees are credited to 
income when earned (even if not received), and subsequent write-off qualifies as bad debt. 
Professionals maintaining accounts on the cash basis cannot claim bad debt deduction 
because unpaid fees are never included in income in the first place - so there is nothing to 
write off. 
Q5. Is an employer's contribution to ESOP or ESOPs considered deductible under 
Section 36? 
Employee Stock Option Plans (ESOPs) are not specifically listed in Section 36. However, 
the value of ESOPs at the time of exercise may be deductible as employee compensation 
under Section 37 (general business expenditure) in certain circumstances. The treatment of 
ESOP expense deduction is complex and depends on the structure of the plan. For 
employers, the deduction is generally claimed in the year the options are exercised by 
employees (when the compensation value can be ascertained), subject to judicial 
interpretation. Seek specific CA advice on ESOP deductibility for your entity.
