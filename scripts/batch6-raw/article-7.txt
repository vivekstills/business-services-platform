Form 67 and Foreign Tax Credit: How to Claim 
Relief on Taxes Paid Abroad 
Updated: May 2026 Read time: 8 min read AY: 2026-27 
 
ARTICLE SUMMARY 
Indian residents who earn income from foreign sources (salary, dividends, interest, capital 
gains, or business income) may have tax withheld by the foreign country on that income. To 
avoid double taxation, the Income Tax Act under Section 90 or 91 allows a credit for foreign 
taxes paid against the Indian tax liability. The claim is made by filing Form 67 on the IT 
portal on or before the ITR filing due date. This article explains the foreign tax credit 
framework, the eligible foreign taxes, the Form 67 filing procedure, computation rules, and 
the consequences of late filing. 
 
Legal Basis for Foreign Tax Credit 
The foreign tax credit (FTC) framework in India is governed by Section 90 (for countries with 
which India has a Double Taxation Avoidance Agreement - DTAA) and Section 91 (for countries 
with which India does not have a DTAA). Rule 128 of the Income Tax Rules, 1962 provides the 
detailed computation rules and the Form 67 requirement. The FTC rules were substantially 
revised in 2016 (effective AY 2017-18) to provide a more structured framework. 
 
Who Can Claim Foreign Tax Credit? 
Condition Details 
Eligible claimant Resident and Ordinarily Resident (ROR) individuals, companies, and other 
entities in India 
RNOR and NRI Not eligible for FTC since their foreign income is generally not taxable in 
India 
Income type Foreign income that is included in the Indian tax computation and also 
taxed abroad 
Foreign tax eligible Tax paid in a foreign country on income that is also taxed in India; must be 
an income tax (not sales tax, VAT, customs, or other non-income taxes) 
DTAA countries Credit under Section 90; generally limited to the lower of Indian tax or 
foreign tax on the same income 
Non-DTAA countries Credit under Section 91; limited to the lower of Indian tax rate or foreign 
tax rate on the doubly taxed income 
 
Foreign Tax Credit Computation 
The FTC is computed separately for each country and for each type or source of income. The 
credit for any given income is limited to the LOWER of: 
• The actual foreign tax paid on that income, converted to Indian Rupees at the 
telegraphic transfer buying rate (TTBR) of the State Bank of India on the last day of the 
month preceding the month of tax payment. 
• The Indian tax payable on that foreign income (i.e., the Indian tax attributable to that 
specific income item). 
This ensures that FTC cannot exceed the Indian tax on the same income and does not result in 
a net refund. 
Example: Foreign Tax Credit Calculation 
Resident individual: Salary from US employer in FY 2025-26 
Gross US salary (converted to INR):         Rs. 30,00,000 
US Federal Income Tax withheld (converted): Rs.  4,50,000 
 
Indian tax computation: 
  Total income including US salary:         Rs. 45,00,000 
  Indian tax on total income (new regime):  Rs.  6,75,000 (approx)  
  Indian tax attributable to US salary: 
    = Rs. 6,75,000 x (Rs. 30L / Rs. 45L) = Rs. 4,50,000 
 
FTC = LOWER of: 
  (a) US tax paid:                          Rs. 4,50,000 
  (b) Indian tax on US income:              Rs. 4,50,000 
  FTC = Rs. 4,50,000 
 
Indian tax payable after FTC: Rs. 6,75,000 - Rs. 4,50,000 = Rs. 2,25,000 
(on remaining Indian income) 
 
Form 67: Filing Requirement and Procedure 
Rule 128(9) mandates that any taxpayer claiming FTC must file Form 67 on the IT portal on or 
before the due date of filing the income tax return. Form 67 is filed separately from the ITR itself. 
6. Log in to the IT portal at incometax.gov.in. 
7. Navigate to: e-File > Income Tax Forms > File Income Tax Forms > Form 67. 
8. Select the relevant Assessment Year and fill in: the country where tax was paid, the 
nature of income (salary, dividends, interest, etc.), the amount of foreign income in the 
foreign currency and INR, the amount of foreign tax paid in foreign currency and INR, 
and the exchange rate used. 
9. Upload supporting documents: Tax assessment order or withholding tax certificate from 
the foreign country showing the tax paid (must be in the foreign country's official 
language; if not in English, attach a certified translation). 
10. Submit and e-verify Form 67 using Aadhaar OTP or digital signature. 
11. Then proceed to file the ITR, which will reference the Form 67 claim. 
 
Important 
Form 67 must be filed ON OR BEFORE the ITR due date. If Form 67 is filed after the ITR due 
date (even a day late), the FTC claim is denied entirely. This is one of the most common and 
costly mistakes made by taxpayers with foreign income. The Delhi High Court has upheld the 
denial of FTC for late Form 67 filing in multiple cases. 
 
Documents Required for Form 67 
• Tax Residency Certificate (if claiming DTAA benefits along with FTC) 
• Foreign tax payment proof: withholding certificate issued by the foreign employer or 
payer (e.g., W-2 form from US, P60 from UK, or equivalent) 
• Foreign tax assessment order or foreign ITR showing taxes paid (for countries where the 
taxpayer files their own return abroad) 
• Bank statement showing remittance of tax to the foreign government (if self-assessed) 
• Exchange rate certificate from SBI or authorised dealer bank for the conversion rate 
used 
 
Pro Tip 
NRIs returning to India who become ROR in their first or second year back should track foreign 
income and foreign taxes paid from day one. Once ROR status is attained, global income 
becomes taxable in India. Set up the Form 67 filing as a recurring calendar reminder well before 
July 31 each year to ensure it is never filed late. 
 
KEY TAKEAWAYS 
1.  Foreign Tax Credit (FTC) under Section 90/91 allows a credit for taxes paid in a foreign 
country against Indian tax on the same income. 
2.  FTC is limited to the lower of: actual foreign tax paid (in INR) or Indian tax attributable to the 
foreign income. 
3.  Form 67 must be filed on the IT portal on or before the ITR due date. Filing after the due date 
results in total denial of the FTC claim. 
4.  FTC is available to Resident and Ordinarily Resident (ROR) taxpayers only; NRIs and 
RNORs are generally not eligible. 
5.  Separate FTC computation is required for each country and each type of income. 
6.  Supporting documents must include the foreign tax withholding certificate or equivalent proof 
of tax paid abroad. 
7.  Under Section 91, for non-DTAA countries, FTC is the lower of Indian tax rate or foreign tax 
rate on the doubly taxed income. 
 
Frequently Asked Questions 
Q1. I received dividends from a US company. The US withheld 25% tax. Can I claim FTC 
in India? 
Yes. US-source dividends are taxable in India for ROR residents. If the US withheld 25% (or 
the treaty rate of 15% under the India-US DTAA), you can claim FTC for the foreign tax 
withheld. Under the India-US DTAA, the treaty rate for dividends is 15% or 25% depending 
on shareholding. Compute the Indian tax attributable to the dividend income and claim FTC 
for the lower of: foreign tax withheld or Indian tax on the dividend. File Form 67 before your 
ITR due date. 
Q2. My Form 67 was filed on August 5 but my ITR due date was July 31. What happens? 
The FTC claim will be denied. Rule 128(9) makes filing Form 67 on or before the ITR due 
date a mandatory condition for claiming FTC. If you missed the July 31 deadline, the late 
Form 67 filed on August 5 does not preserve the FTC claim. You may explore whether the 
ITR itself was filed under a condonation of delay (Section 119(2)(b)) and whether the Form 
67 can be considered under the same condonation. However, such condonation is 
discretionary and not guaranteed. 
Q3. I paid tax in a country that does not have a DTAA with India. Can I still claim FTC? 
Yes. Section 91 provides FTC for countries that do not have a DTAA with India (currently 
applicable for a limited number of countries). The credit is computed as the lower of the 
Indian tax rate on the income or the foreign country's tax rate on the same income, applied 
to the doubly taxed income. The procedure (Form 67 filing) and conditions remain the same 
as for DTAA countries. 
Q4. Can FTC be carried forward if it exceeds my Indian tax liability in the current year? 
No. FTC is not carrried forward or refunded. The credit is available only in the year the 
foreign income is taxable in India. Any excess FTC (foreign tax paid exceeding Indian tax on 
that income) is simply lost for that year. There is no mechanism to utilise the excess FTC in 
future years. This is one of the limitations of the current FTC framework and differs from the 
treatment in some other countries. 
Q5. I am a returning NRI who has been RNOR for 2 years. Am I eligible for FTC? 
During the RNOR period, foreign income that arises from a business controlled outside India 
or from a profession set up outside India is not taxable in India. Since such income is not 
included in Indian tax base, no FTC is applicable for those years. Once you attain ROR 
status (from year 3 typically), your global income is taxable in India and FTC becomes 
available for foreign taxes on that global income. Plan your Form 67 filing from the first year 
you attain ROR status.
