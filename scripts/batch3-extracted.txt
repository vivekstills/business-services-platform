ARTICLE 1 OF 6  ·  SALARY TAXATION 
Dearness Allowance (DA) — Meaning, Types, DA 
Calculation & Tax Treatment 
📅 Updated: May 2026 📅 9 min read 📅 AY 2026-27 
 
ARTICLE SUMMARY 
Dearness Allowance (DA) is a cost-of-living adjustment paid by the Central Government, 
State Governments, and PSUs to their employees and pensioners to offset the impact of 
inflation. DA is fully taxable as salary income for most employees. This article explains the 
two types of DA — CDA and IDA — the AICPI-based calculation formula, current DA rates 
for central government employees as of 2026, tax treatment, DA for pensioners, and how 
DA interacts with other salary components like HRA. 
 
What is Dearness Allowance? 
Dearness Allowance is a component of the salary structure of government employees and 
pensioners designed to protect their real purchasing power against inflation. As the price level in 
the economy rises, the purchasing power of a fixed salary erodes. DA is periodically revised 
upward to compensate for this erosion, ensuring that the effective salary keeps pace with 
inflation. 
DA was introduced post-World War II when prices rose sharply and fixed-income government 
employees faced severe hardship. It has since become a permanent and substantial component 
of government employee compensation. For central government employees under the 7th Pay 
Commission, DA can form a significant percentage of the basic pay — in many cases, DA itself 
exceeds the basic pay when accumulated revisions are considered.

How DA is Calculated — The AICPI Formula (CDA) 
For central government employees under CDA, the DA rate is determined using the All India 
Consumer Price Index for Industrial Workers (AICPI-IW) with base year 2016=100. The formula 
used by the 7th Central Pay Commission is: 
📊 CDA Calculation Formula 
DA% = [ (Average AICPI-IW for past 12 months - 261.33) / 261.33 ] x 100

DA and HRA Linkage 
House Rent Allowance (HRA) for central government employees is linked to the city 
classification (X, Y, Z cities) as a percentage of basic pay. However, when DA crosses 25% and 
50% thresholds, HRA rates are revised upward. When DA crossed 50% (January 2024), HRA 
rates for X-cities moved from 27% to 30%, Y-cities from 18% to 20%, and Z-cities from 9% to 
10% of basic pay. This DA-HRA linkage means DA revisions indirectly increase HRA as well. 
 
Tax Treatment of Dearness Allowance 
Dearness Allowance is fully taxable as salary income for all employees — both government and 
private sector. There are no exemptions available for DA under the Income Tax Act for regular 
employees. DA is included in gross salary and subject to TDS by the employer.

Dearness Allowance for Pensioners — Dearness Relief (DR) 
Retired central government employees receive Dearness Relief (DR) on their basic pension, in 
lieu of the DA paid to serving employees. DR is revised at the same rate as DA and follows the 
same AICPI-based calculation. DR is fully taxable as pension income. The employer (paying 
authority — pension sanctioning authority) deducts TDS on pension plus DR under Section 192. 
For family pensioners (spouse/dependents receiving family pension after death of pensioner), 
the basic family pension and DR thereon are taxable under 'Income from Other Sources'. A 
standard deduction of 1/3rd of family pension or Rs. 25,000 (whichever is lower) is available 
under Section 57(iia) — this limit was enhanced to Rs. 25,000 in Budget 2024. 
 
DA and Its Role in Other Salary Calculations 
DA is not merely an inflation compensator — it forms the base for computing several other 
salary benefits: 
---PAGE 5---
• Provident Fund (EPF/GPF): Employer and employee PF contributions are computed on 
Basic Pay + DA in many PSUs. This increases the PF accumulation significantly. 
• Gratuity: Under the Payment of Gratuity Act, gratuity is computed as (Last Drawn Salary 
including DA / 26) x 15 x years of service. Higher DA = higher gratuity. 
• Leave Encashment: Leave encashment at retirement is computed on Basic Pay + DA. 
The tax exemption ceiling under Section 10(10AA) is Rs. 25 lakh. 
• HRA: As explained above, DA thresholds trigger HRA revisions for government 
employees. 
• House Building Advance (HBA): The loan limit for HBA from government is based on 34 
months of Basic Pay + DA. 
 
📅 Pro Tip 
Government employees should ensure their DA arrears (if received for prior months) are 
correctly reflected in Form 16 for the year of actual receipt. Arrear DA is taxable in the year of 
receipt. If the arrear relates to prior years and results in a higher tax burden, claim relief under 
Section 89(1) by filing Form 10E on the IT portal before filing your ITR. 
 
KEY TAKEAWAYS 
1.  DA is a cost-of-living adjustment paid to government and PSU employees — it is fully taxable 
as salary income with no exemption. 
2.  CDA (Central DA) is revised twice yearly (January and July) based on 12-month average 
AICPI-IW; IDA is revised quarterly. 
3.  For central govt employees under 7th CPC, DA as of July 2025 stands at 58% of basic pay. 
4.  When DA crosses 25% and 50% thresholds, HRA rates for government employees are 
revised upward. 
5.  Gratuity, leave encashment, EPF, and HBA are all calculated on Basic Pay + DA — higher 
DA directly increases these benefits. 
6.  Dearness Relief (DR) for pensioners follows the same rates as DA and is fully taxable as 
pension income. 
7.  DA arrears received in a lump sum can trigger higher tax — claim Section 89(1) relief by filing 
Form 10E before filing ITR. 
 
Frequently Asked Questions 
Q1. Is there any DA exemption for private sector employees? 
No. Dearness Allowance paid by private sector employers is fully taxable under the head 
'Salaries' without any exemption. Unlike HRA (which has a partial exemption formula), there 
is no provision in the Income Tax Act that exempts DA for private sector employees. The 
entire DA amount is added to gross salary and subject to TDS. Only employees of certain 
---PAGE 6---
state governments who receive DA under notified conditions (prior to service rules for 
retirement benefit computation) may have limited specific exemptions — but these are rare 
and state-specific. 
Q2. My DA was merged with basic pay during a pay revision. Is the merged amount 
treated differently for tax purposes? 
No. Once DA is merged with basic pay (as happened during earlier Pay Commission 
revisions), it becomes part of basic pay for all purposes — including tax. The merged 
amount is fully taxable as salary. However, for computation of perquisites, the 'basic salary' 
used in formulas (like HRA, PF, gratuity) is the revised basic after merger. The merger does 
not create any tax benefit or disadvantage — it is purely a structural change in how salary is 
presented. 
Q3. How is DA different from HRA? Can both be exempt from tax? 
DA and HRA serve different purposes. DA compensates for inflation and is fully taxable. 
HRA compensates for accommodation costs and has a partial exemption formula under 
Section 10(13A) — the exempt portion is the least of: actual HRA received, rent paid minus 
10% of basic salary, or 50%/40% of basic salary (metro/non-metro). So HRA has a 
conditional partial exemption; DA has none. A government employee receives both DA and 
HRA, but only HRA qualifies for partial exemption. 
Q4. Are DA arrears (for previous years) taxed in the year of receipt or accrual? 
DA arrears are taxed in the year of actual receipt (cash basis for salary income), not the 
year they relate to. If DA for April 2024 to September 2024 is received in January 2025, the 
entire arrear is taxable in FY 2024-25 (AY 2025-26). This bunching of income may push you 
into a higher slab. To mitigate this, file Form 10E on the IT portal and claim relief under 
Section 89(1) when filing your ITR, which spreads the tax over the years to which the 
arrears relate. 
Q5. A central government employee retired with DA at 58%. Is their pension calculated on 
basic pay alone or basic pay plus DA? 
Pension for central government employees is calculated on the last drawn basic pay only — 
NOT on basic pay + DA. The pension formula under the 7th CPC is: Pension = Last Basic 
Pay / 2. After retirement, Dearness Relief (DR) is added separately on the basic pension, 
starting at 0% and revised periodically in line with DA revisions for serving employees. So 
the pensioner eventually receives pension + DR, which replicates the DA benefit on their 
pension amount.

ARTICLE 2 OF 6  ·  SALARY PERQUISITES 
Is an Interest-Free Loan from Employer Taxable? 
📅 Updated: May 2026 📅 7 min read 📅 AY 2026-27 
 
ARTICLE SUMMARY 
When an employer provides a loan to an employee at zero interest or at a rate below the 
SBI benchmark rate, the interest subsidy is treated as a taxable perquisite under the 
Income Tax Act. However, there are important exemptions — loans up to Rs. 20,000 are 
fully exempt, and loans for medical treatment of specified diseases are entirely exempt 
regardless of amount. This article explains the valuation rules, the SBI benchmark rate 
mechanism, tax computation, Form 12BA reporting, and practical examples. 
 
Legal Framework — When Does a Loan Become a Taxable 
Perquisite? 
Under Section 17(2)(viii) read with Rule 3(7)(i) of the Income Tax Rules, 1962, any loan 
provided by an employer to an employee (or any member of the employee's household) at an 
interest rate lower than the SBI benchmark lending rate is treated as a perquisite in the hands of 
the employee. The taxable value of the perquisite is the interest concession — the difference 
between what the employee actually pays and what they would have paid at the SBI benchmark 
rate. 
This rule applies to both interest-free loans (where the employee pays zero interest) and 
concessional rate loans (where the employee pays some interest, but at a rate below SBI's 
benchmark). The perquisite is calculated on a monthly basis on the maximum outstanding loan 
balance during each month of the financial year. 
 
The SBI Benchmark Rate — How It Works 
The benchmark rate used for computing the taxable perquisite is the State Bank of India's 
lending rate for the same type of loan (as of April 1 of the relevant financial year). Different SBI 
rates apply for different types of loans:

The SBI benchmark rate prevailing on April 1 of the financial year is used for the entire year's

prevailing on April 1, 2025 applies for the entire April 2025 to March 2026 period. Employers 
must note this rate at the start of the year for payroll computation.

📅 Important 
The Rs. 20,000 exemption is on the AGGREGATE outstanding loan balance across all loans 
from the employer at any time during the year — not per loan. If an employee has a housing loan 
of Rs. 15,000 outstanding and a personal loan of Rs. 10,000 outstanding simultaneously, the 
aggregate is Rs. 25,000 which exceeds the threshold, making the entire outstanding balance 
taxable. 
 
Valuation: How is the Perquisite Calculated? 
The taxable perquisite value is computed as follows: for each month, take the maximum 
outstanding loan balance during that month, apply the SBI benchmark rate, and compute the 
monthly interest. Subtract any interest actually paid by the employee. The balance is the 
monthly perquisite. Sum all 12 months to get the annual perquisite value.

Tax Treatment and Form 12BA Reporting 
The annual perquisite value is added to the employee's gross salary and is taxable at the 
applicable income tax slab rate. The employer includes the perquisite value in the TDS 
computation. It is reported in Form 12BA (Statement showing particulars of perquisites, other 
fringe benefits or amenities and profits in lieu of salary) which the employer must provide to the 
employee along with Form 16. 
In the employee's ITR, the perquisite value appears under 'Salary as per Form 16' under the 
head 'Salaries' — it is already included in the salary figure provided by the employer. The 
employee does not need to separately compute or report it as long as the employer has 
correctly valued it in Form 12BA and TDS has been deducted accordingly. 
 
Concessional Rate Loan — Partial Perquisite 
If the employer charges some interest but at a rate below the SBI benchmark, only the 
difference is a perquisite. For example, if SBI rate is 8.75% and employer charges 4%, the 
perquisite is based on 4.75% (8.75% minus 4%) applied on the outstanding balance. This partial 
perquisite is computed in the same way as for a zero-interest loan — monthly, on maximum 
outstanding balance. 
 
📅 Pro Tip 
If you have a large interest-free loan from your employer and want to reduce the perquisite tax, 
consider paying nominal interest at or above the SBI benchmark rate. Once you pay interest at 
or above the benchmark rate, there is zero perquisite. Many well-structured employee loan 
---PAGE 10---
policies are designed with interest at SBI rate to avoid any perquisite tax burden while still 
providing attractive financing. 
 
KEY TAKEAWAYS 
1.  Interest-free or concessional loans from employers are taxable perquisites under Section 
17(2)(viii) read with Rule 3(7)(i). 
2.  The taxable value is the interest concession: SBI benchmark rate minus actual rate paid, 
applied to monthly maximum outstanding balance. 
3.  Loans up to Rs. 20,000 aggregate outstanding at any time are fully exempt — no perquisite 
arises. 
4.  Loans for medical treatment of specified diseases are fully exempt regardless of the 
outstanding amount. 
5.  The SBI rate as on April 1 of the financial year is used for the entire year's computation. 
6.  The perquisite is included in gross salary in Form 16 / Form 12BA and taxed at the 
employee's slab rate. 
7.  Paying interest at or above the SBI benchmark rate eliminates the perquisite entirely. 
 
Frequently Asked Questions 
Q1. My employer gave me a loan of Rs. 15,000 at zero interest. Is any perquisite taxable? 
No. Since the outstanding loan balance of Rs. 15,000 is below the Rs. 20,000 threshold, no 
perquisite arises at any time during the year. The exemption applies as long as the 
aggregate outstanding loan balance from the employer never exceeds Rs. 20,000 at any 
point during the financial year. If you take another loan later in the year that pushes the 
aggregate above Rs. 20,000, the entire outstanding balance (not just the excess) becomes 
taxable from that point. 
Q2. Can an employee get both a housing loan interest deduction under Section 24(b) and 
avoid perquisite tax on an employer housing loan? 
For Section 24(b) deduction (interest on home loan for self-occupied or let-out property), the 
loan must be from a bank, NBFC, or employer. Loans from employers do qualify for Section 
24(b) deduction. However, the Section 24(b) deduction and the employer loan perquisite 
operate independently. If the employer charges zero interest, a perquisite arises (taxed as 
salary). Simultaneously, there is zero actual interest paid, so there is nothing to deduct 
under Section 24(b). If the employer charges the SBI rate, no perquisite, but the interest 
paid qualifies for Section 24(b) deduction. 
Q3. What diseases qualify for the medical loan exemption under Rule 3A? 
Rule 3A specifies the following diseases for which medical loans are exempt: cancer, full-
blown AIDS, chronic renal failure, haematological disorders (haemophilia, thalassaemia), 
---PAGE 11---
neurological diseases (dementia, dystonia musculorum deformans, motor neuron disease, 
ataxia, chorea, hemiballismus, aphasia, Parkinson's disease), and any illness requiring 
surgery. The loan must be for treatment of the employee, spouse, children, parents, or 
dependents. The treating specialist's certificate may be required as evidence. 
Q4. What if the employee takes the loan from a group company (not the direct 
employer)? 
If the loan is from an associated employer, group company, or subsidiary that is a connected 
employer, it is still treated as a loan from the employer for perquisite computation purposes. 
The definition of 'employer' in the perquisite rules includes connected employers and group 
entities. Loan from an unrelated third party (e.g., friend, bank) even at zero interest is not a 
perquisite — the rule specifically covers employer-provided benefits. 
Q5. If the employer adjusts the perquisite value incorrectly in Form 12BA, what should 
the employee do? 
The employee should first bring the error to the employer's attention before Form 16 is 
issued. If the employer issues a wrong Form 16, the employee should request a revised 
Form 16. If the error is not corrected by the employer, the employee must independently 
compute the correct perquisite value, declare the correct amount in their ITR (which may 
differ from the employer's figures), and maintain supporting documentation. Filing a revised 
ITR after receiving the correct Form 16 later is also an option if the original return was filed 
with wrong data.

ARTICLE 3 OF 6  ·  NPS & RETIREMENT 
Employer's Contribution to NPS in Taxable 
Salary — Section 80CCD(2) 
📅 Updated: May 2026 📅 8 min read 📅 AY 2026-27 
 
ARTICLE SUMMARY 
Employer contributions to the National Pension System (NPS) on behalf of employees are 
one of the most tax-efficient benefits available in India's compensation structure. Under 
Section 80CCD(2), the employer's NPS contribution — up to 10% of salary for private 
sector and 14% for Central Government employees — is deductible from the employee's 
taxable income, and this benefit is available even under the new tax regime. This article 
covers the full deduction mechanism, how it appears in Form 16, interaction with the 
additional 80CCD(1B) deduction, and a worked payroll example. 
 
NPS — A Brief Overview 
The National Pension System (NPS) is a voluntary, defined contribution retirement savings 
scheme regulated by the Pension Fund Regulatory and Development Authority (PFRDA). It was 
originally introduced for central government employees in 2004 and opened to all citizens in 
2009. NPS offers market-linked returns through investment in equity (E), corporate bonds (C), 
government securities (G), and alternative assets (A). 
From a taxation standpoint, NPS has one of the most generous deduction structures: employee 
contributions are deductible under Section 80CCD(1) within the overall Section 80C limit of Rs. 
1.5 lakh, an additional Rs. 50,000 deduction is available under Section 80CCD(1B) over and 
above 80C, and employer contributions are deductible under Section 80CCD(2) without any 
overlap with the 80C limit. 
 
Section 80CCD(2) — The Employer Contribution Deduction 
Section 80CCD(2) provides that where an employer contributes to the NPS account of an 
employee, the amount so contributed is deductible from the employee's gross total income 
subject to a limit. The employee does not pay any tax on the employer's NPS contribution up to 
this limit — it is as if the contribution was never received as taxable salary.

Available in New Tax Regime Too 
Section 80CCD(2) deduction is one of the very few deductions available under the new tax 
regime (Section 115BAC). While most deductions (80C, 80D, HRA, home loan) are disallowed 
under the new regime, employer NPS contribution deduction under 80CCD(2) continues. This 
makes it a powerful tax-saving tool for employees who have opted for the new regime and 
cannot claim other deductions. 
 
How Employer NPS Contribution Appears in Salary and Form 16  
Here is how the employer NPS contribution flows through the payroll and tax documentation: 
1. The employer decides to contribute X% of the employee's basic salary to NPS. This is 
typically structured as part of the CTC (Cost to Company). 
2. Every month, the employer deducts the employee's share of NPS from the salary and 
also contributes the employer's share. Both are deposited into the employee's NPS Tier I 
account. 
3. For income tax purposes, the employer's contribution is first included in the employee's 
gross salary (it is a benefit paid by the employer on behalf of the employee). 
4. Section 80CCD(2) then allows a deduction of this amount (up to 10%/14% of salary) 
from gross total income — effectively removing it from the tax base. 
5. In Form 16, the employer's NPS contribution appears as a deduction under Part B — 
specifically under 'Deductions under Chapter VI-A, Section 80CCD(2)'.

Over and above the employer contribution benefit under 80CCD(2), employees can also claim a

Rs. 50,000 per year. This is over and above the Section 80C limit of Rs. 1.5 lakh. However, 
unlike 80CCD(2), the 80CCD(1B) deduction is NOT available under the new tax regime.

📅 Pro Tip 
For employees under the new tax regime, the employer NPS contribution under 80CCD(2) is the 
single most valuable tax deduction available. If your employer currently does not structure NPS 
as part of your CTC, consider requesting a salary restructuring to include employer NPS 
contributions. A 10% employer NPS contribution on basic salary can reduce taxable income by 
Rs. 1.2 lakh per year for someone with Rs. 10 lakh basic — saving Rs. 12,000 to Rs. 36,000 in 
tax depending on the slab. 
 
KEY TAKEAWAYS 
1.  Employer NPS contributions under Section 80CCD(2) are deductible up to 10% of salary 
(private sector) or 14% (Central/State Govt from FY 2024-25). 
2.  This deduction is available even under the new tax regime (Section 115BAC) — making it 
one of the most important new regime tax-saving tools. 
3.  The deduction is completely separate from the Section 80C limit of Rs. 1.5 lakh — it is an 
additional benefit. 
4.  Employer NPS contributions are first included in gross salary, then reduced via the 80CCD(2) 
deduction in the tax computation.

5.  Employee's own additional NPS contribution (80CCD(1B) — Rs. 50,000) is available only 
under the old regime. 
6.  Both employee and employer NPS contributions must go to the employee's NPS Tier I 
account to qualify for deductions. 
7.  Form 16 Part B shows the 80CCD(2) deduction; employees should verify this against their 
NPS statement from their PRAN account. 
 
Frequently Asked Questions 
Q1. My employer contributes 12% of my basic to NPS. Is the excess 2% (above 10%) 
taxable? 
Yes. The Section 80CCD(2) deduction is capped at 10% of salary (Basic + DA) for private 
sector employees. If the employer contributes 12%, the excess 2% is included in the 
employee's gross salary and taxed as a perquisite / salary income at the applicable slab 
rate. There is no way to claim deduction on the excess. This is an important structuring point 
— the employer NPS contribution should ideally be set at exactly 10% of basic to maximise 
the deduction without creating taxable income. 
Q2. What does 'salary' mean for the 80CCD(2) calculation — does it include allowances? 
For the purpose of Section 80CCD(2), 'salary' means Basic Pay + Dearness Allowance (only 
if DA is part of retirement benefits). It does NOT include HRA, special allowances, 
conveyance allowance, or any other allowances. This is the same definition of salary used 
for PF contribution computation. If an employee has Basic Pay of Rs. 50,000 and DA of Rs. 
10,000 (which forms part of retirement benefits), the salary for 80CCD(2) = Rs. 60,000 and 
the max deductible employer NPS = Rs. 6,000 per month. 
Q3. Is the NPS Tier II account contribution by the employer also deductible under 
80CCD(2)? 
No. Section 80CCD(2) deduction is available only for contributions to the NPS Tier I 
(pension account). Employer contributions to Tier II (which is essentially a 
savings/investment account without pension lock-in) do not qualify for the Section 80CCD(2) 
deduction. Similarly, employee contributions to Tier II do not qualify for 80CCD(1) or 
80CCD(1B) deductions. All NPS-related tax benefits are exclusively for Tier I contributions. 
Q4. If I withdraw from NPS during my employment (partial withdrawal), does it affect the 
80CCD(2) deduction taken earlier? 
Partial withdrawals from NPS Tier I (permitted for specified purposes like home purchase, 
medical treatment, children's education after 3 years of NPS membership) are tax-free up to 
25% of the employee's own contributions. However, these withdrawals do not reverse or 
affect the 80CCD(2) deductions taken in prior years on employer contributions. The 
employer contribution and its deduction history remain intact regardless of the employee's 
withdrawal decisions. 
Q5. My employer did not contribute to my NPS but I contributed voluntarily. Can I claim 
80CCD(2)? 
No. Section 80CCD(2) is specifically for employer contributions — it cannot be claimed for 
the employee's own contributions. Your own NPS contributions are covered under 
---PAGE 16---
80CCD(1) (within the Rs. 1.5 lakh 80C umbrella) and 80CCD(1B) (additional Rs. 50,000 
under old regime). Only the amount actually contributed by your employer to your NPS Tier I 
PRAN account qualifies for the 80CCD(2) deduction.

ARTICLE 4 OF 6  ·  SALARY RELIEF 
Claiming Relief Under Section 89(1) on Salary 
Arrears — Complete Guide 
📅 Updated: May 2026 📅 9 min read 📅 AY 2026-27 
 
ARTICLE SUMMARY 
When employees receive salary arrears, advance salary, or gratuity in a lump sum that 
relates to prior financial years, this income — though taxed in the year of receipt — may 
attract a higher rate than if it had been received in the years it was earned. Section 89(1) 
provides relief from this additional tax burden by computing tax as if the income had been 
received in the respective years. Critically, Form 10E must be filed on the IT portal before 
filing the ITR — failing to do so results in the relief being denied, even if the calculation is 
correct. 
 
Why Section 89(1) Relief is Needed 
Under the Income Tax Act, salary income is taxed on a receipt basis — it is taxed in the year it 
is received, regardless of which year it relates to. This creates a potential inequity when a 
taxpayer receives arrears for several prior years in a single year. The bunching of multiple 
years' income into one year can push the taxpayer into a higher tax bracket than would have 
applied if the income had been received in the respective years. 
Section 89(1) provides relief by computing a hypothetical tax — what the total tax would have 
been if the lump sum income had been received in the year(s) to which it relates. If this 
hypothetical tax is lower than the actual tax on the bunched income, the difference is allowed as 
relief — reducing the current year's tax liability.

After filing Form 10E, the Section 89(1) relief appears in your ITR under the head 'Relief under

Section 89 relief — enter the amount of relief computed (which Form 10E has already

applying surcharge and cess. 
 
Common Situations — Pay Commission Arrears 
The most common scenario for Section 89(1) claims is government pay commission revisions. 
When the Central Government implements a new pay commission (e.g., 8th Pay Commission 
recommendations expected to be implemented from FY 2026-27), employees receive large 
arrears relating to prior years. These arrears, if taxed entirely in the year of receipt, can create 
significant additional tax burden that Section 89(1) is designed to mitigate. 
State government pay revisions, PSU wage settlements, and delayed salary increments 
following promotions or court orders are other common triggers. Private sector employees may 
also receive Section 89(1) benefits from settlement arrears, back-dated salary revisions, or 
severance packages.

credited — do not wait until the last moment before the ITR deadline.

Frequently Asked Questions 
Q1. I received salary arrears from my employer but my employer already deducted TDS 
on the full amount. Should I still file Form 10E? 
Yes. Even if your employer has deducted TDS on the arrears without computing Section 
89(1) relief, you should file Form 10E and claim the relief in your ITR. The relief will reduce 
your net tax payable, and the excess TDS already deducted will be refunded by the Income 
Tax Department when your return is processed. The employer's TDS computation and the 
Section 89(1) relief are separate — the employer is not required to compute 89(1) relief; you 
must claim it yourself in the ITR. 
Q2. What if the arrears relate to years before FY 2018-19? How far back can the 
calculation go? 
Section 89(1) requires computation going back to the actual years to which the arrears 
relate — there is no statutory time limit on how far back you can go. However, practically, 
you need to know your actual taxable income for each prior year to perform the calculation 
in Form 10E. The Form 10E on the IT portal currently supports calculations going back to FY 
2000-01. You should have records of your prior year tax returns or Form 16s to accurately 
compute the relief for older arrears. 
Q3. If Section 89(1) relief results in zero tax liability, do I still need to file Form 10E? 
Yes. Filing Form 10E is mandatory irrespective of whether the relief is large or small, or 
whether it reduces the tax to zero. The CPC's automated processing checks the Form 10E 
database before allowing the relief in the ITR. If it is not on record, the claim is rejected. Do 
not skip Form 10E even if you believe the relief is obvious or the amounts are small. 
Q4. Can I claim Section 89(1) on a bonus received in advance for future years? 
Yes. Advance salary and advance bonus are explicitly covered by Section 89(1). If you 
receive a bonus in FY 2025-26 that relates to work that will be performed in FY 2026-27, this 
can be treated as advance salary and the relief computation in Form 10E will project the 
---PAGE 21---
hypothetical tax for the future year. The calculation determines whether receiving it in 
advance causes a higher tax than if it were received in the applicable future year. 
Q5. My arrears were for a period when I was in a lower tax bracket. Will Section 89(1) 
always provide relief? 
Not necessarily. Section 89(1) relief is the difference between (a) actual tax on bunched 
income and (b) hypothetical tax if spread over the relevant years. If you were in a lower slab 
in the arrear years and the bunching does not actually push you to a higher slab in the 
current year either, the relief may be zero. The relief is mathematically zero when the 
bunching does not result in more tax than the sum of taxes in the respective years. Form 
10E performs this calculation automatically and shows the relief (even if zero).

What is the EPF Joint Declaration Form? 
The EPF Joint Declaration Form is a formal request submitted to the Employees' Provident 
Fund Organisation (EPFO) to correct demographic or service details recorded in a member's 
EPF account. Errors in EPF records can arise from data entry mistakes at the time of account

signature requirement ensures that the correction is acknowledged by both parties and reduces 
fraud. Once submitted and approved by EPFO, the correction is reflected in the member's UAN

Frequently Asked Questions 
Q1. My previous employer has closed down. How can I get the Joint Declaration signed 
by them? 
If the employer has closed or is untraceable, EPFO has a provision for corrections through 
the concerned Regional PF Commissioner (RPFC) without the employer's signature. You 
must submit a written application to the RPFC with supporting documents (Aadhaar, PAN, 
birth certificate, affidavit) and explain why the employer's signature cannot be obtained. The 
RPFC may approve the correction based on the supporting documents after due verification. 
This process takes longer than the regular joint declaration route. 
Q2. My name in Aadhaar is in a different format from my EPF records (e.g., initials vs full 
name). Which should I correct? 
EPFO requires the EPF name to match the Aadhaar name exactly — including the format 
(expanded name vs initials). If your Aadhaar shows 'Rajesh Kumar Sharma' but EPF shows 
'R. K. Sharma', a joint declaration is needed to update the EPF name to match Aadhaar 
exactly. It is generally easier to correct the EPF record (via joint declaration) than to change 
the Aadhaar name. However, if the Aadhaar name itself has an error, correct Aadhaar first, 
then update EPF. 
Q3. How long does EPFO take to approve a joint declaration for date of birth correction? 
Date of birth corrections are among the most sensitive and time-consuming because DOB 
affects retirement age, pension eligibility, and EPS pension amount. The standard 
processing time is 30 to 60 working days. If the Aadhaar DOB matches the requested 
correction, it may be faster. If documents are inconsistent (e.g., birth certificate shows one 
date, school certificate shows another), EPFO may seek additional clarification through the 
employer, extending the timeline further. 
Q4. Can an employee independently update their EPF profile without employer 
involvement? 
Very limited changes can be made directly by employees on the UAN member portal — 
such as bank account updates (KYC), mobile number, email, and nomination details. For 
demographic corrections (name, DOB, father's name, gender, dates of service), employer 
involvement is mandatory — both the submission through the employer portal and the 
employee's Aadhaar OTP approval. This dual-control is an EPFO governance requirement 
to prevent fraudulent changes. 
Q5. I transferred my PF from a previous employer but the service period from the old 
employer shows incorrectly. Is a joint declaration needed? 
For service period corrections relating to a previous employer, the joint declaration must be 
initiated by the previous employer (not the current one) since the service records originate 
from them. If the previous employer is inaccessible, the current employer may assist by 
submitting supporting documents to the RPFC. The transfer of PF brings the accumulated 
balance but the underlying service records remain with the originating establishment. 
Pension calculations under EPS depend on the total qualifying service, so correct service 
period recording is critical for pension benefit computation.

ARTICLE 6 OF 6  ·  PARTNERSHIP TAXATION 
Partner's Remuneration and How It Is Calculated 
— Section 40(b) 
📅 Updated: May 2026 📅 9 min read 📅 AY 2026-27 
 
ARTICLE SUMMARY 
In a partnership firm, partners can be remunerated (salary, bonus, commission, fee) for 
their work and also earn interest on their capital contributions. However, the Income Tax 
Act imposes strict limits on how much remuneration and interest can be claimed as a 
deduction by the firm under Section 40(b). Beyond these limits, the excess is disallowed, 
increasing the firm's taxable income. This article explains the book profit concept, 
remuneration calculation formula, interest limits, tax treatment in partners' hands, and the 
distinction between working and non-working partners. 
 
Overview — Why Section 40(b) Exists 
In an unregulated scenario, a partnership firm could pay excessive remuneration to its partners, 
reducing the firm's taxable income (and thus the 30% firm-level tax) while shifting income to 
partners who might be in a lower tax bracket. Section 40(b) was introduced to prevent this 
income-shifting arrangement by capping the deductible remuneration and interest at prescribed 
limits. 
Any remuneration or interest paid to partners beyond these limits is disallowed as a deduction in 
the firm's computation, which increases the firm's taxable profit. The disallowed amount is still 
taxable — but at the firm level at 30%, rather than being split among partners at potentially 
lower slab rates. This ensures that the overall tax collection is protected. 
 
Who is a Working Partner? 
Remuneration under Section 40(b) can only be paid to working partners — not to all partners. A 
working partner is defined as an individual who is actively engaged in conducting the affairs of 
the business or profession of the firm. The partnership deed must specify which partners are 
working partners. A partner who merely contributes capital and receives profit share but does 
not actively work in the business is a non-working (sleeping) partner and cannot receive 
deductible remuneration.

Book Profit — The Foundation of the Calculation 
The entire Section 40(b) remuneration calculation is based on 'Book Profit.' Book Profit is the 
net profit of the firm computed under the normal provisions of the Income Tax Act, but BEFORE 
deducting the partner's remuneration (but after allowing all other legitimate deductions — 
expenses, depreciation, etc.). It is essentially the firm's taxable profit computed as if no 
remuneration had been paid to partners. 
Book Profit calculation steps: Start with the firm's Profit & Loss Account net profit. Add back any 
remuneration paid to partners (since we need profit before remuneration). Adjust for any income 
tax disallowances (Section 40A, 43B, etc.). Adjust for any depreciation excess or shortfall. The 
result is the Book Profit for Section 40(b) purposes.

Interest on Partner's Capital — Section 40(b) Limit 
Apart from remuneration, partners also earn interest on the capital they contribute to the firm. 
Section 40(b) allows the firm to deduct interest paid to partners on their capital, subject to a 
maximum rate of 12% per annum. Interest paid above 12% is disallowed. 
• Maximum interest rate: 12% per annum simple interest on the partner's capital account 
balance. 
• Both working and non-working partners can receive interest on capital — the 12% limit 
applies to all. 
• Interest is computed on the opening balance of the partner's capital account for the year. 
• Any excess interest above 12% is disallowed in the firm's computation but remains 
taxable in the partner's hands.

For remuneration paid to partners to be deductible under Section 40(b), two conditions must be 
simultaneously met: 
• The partnership deed must expressly authorise the payment of remuneration to the 
specific working partners (naming them or their designations). A general clause 
permitting 'remuneration to partners' is acceptable. 
• The remuneration must be paid only for the period covered by the partnership deed — 
not for any prior period to which the deed does not apply. A retrospective deed cannot 
authorise deductible remuneration for prior periods. 
If either condition is not met, the entire remuneration paid is disallowed under Section 40(b)(iv), 
even if the amount is within the prescribed limits. Courts have consistently held that 
remuneration without a properly authorising partnership deed is fully disallowed. Firms should 
review their partnership deed at the start of each year to ensure both conditions are met. 
 
📅 Pro Tip 
Partnership firms with high book profits can optimise tax by paying partners the maximum 
allowable remuneration under Section 40(b). For a book profit of Rs. 20 lakh, the maximum 
deductible remuneration is Rs. 12.9 lakh. If paid to partners who are in lower tax brackets (say 
20% or below), the overall tax burden is lower than if the income remained with the firm at 30%. 
Always compute both scenarios before finalising remuneration for the year. 
 
KEY TAKEAWAYS 
1.  Section 40(b) limits the deductible remuneration a firm can pay to its working partners — 
excess remuneration is disallowed at the firm level. 
2.  Book Profit is the firm's net profit before deducting partner remuneration — this is the base for 
Section 40(b) calculation. 
3.  Maximum deductible remuneration: 90% of first Rs. 3 lakh of book profit (or Rs. 1.5 lakh if 
higher) + 60% of balance book profit. 
4.  Interest on partner's capital is deductible only up to 12% per annum — interest above this 
rate is disallowed. 
5.  Partner's share of profit is fully exempt in the partner's hands under Section 10(2A) — no 
double taxation of profits.

Frequently Asked Questions 
Q1. Can a limited liability partnership (LLP) also claim remuneration deduction under 
Section 40(b)? 
Yes. Section 40(b) applies to all forms of partnerships including LLPs. The designated 
partners of an LLP who are actively involved in the business qualify as working partners for 
Section 40(b) purposes. The LLP agreement must authorise the payment of remuneration to 
designated partners (equivalent to the partnership deed requirement). The same book profit 
formula and limits apply to LLPs as to traditional partnership firms. 
Q2. What if the firm has a loss (negative book profit)? Can partners still receive 
deductible remuneration? 
Yes. In case of a loss (negative book profit), Section 40(b) provides a floor: the maximum 
deductible remuneration is Rs. 1,50,000 or 90% of book profit, whichever is higher. Since 
90% of a negative number would be even more negative, the floor of Rs. 1,50,000 applies. 
This means a loss-making firm can still deduct up to Rs. 1,50,000 as partner remuneration 
— which would further increase the business loss and allow it to be carried forward. This is a 
useful provision for firms in their startup or loss-making years. 
Q3. A partner received Rs. 5 lakh remuneration but the firm can only deduct Rs. 3 lakh 
under 40(b). Is the Rs. 2 lakh disallowance also taxable in the partner's hands? 
Yes. The Rs. 2 lakh disallowed at the firm level is still fully taxable as business income in the 
partner's hands. Section 40(b) disallowance operates at the firm level for deduction 
purposes — it does not affect the taxability of the amount in the recipient's hands. This is 
one of the harsh consequences of exceeding Section 40(b) limits: the firm pays tax on the 
disallowed amount (as part of increased firm income at 30%) AND the partner pays tax on 
the entire remuneration at their slab rate. Careful planning to stay within 40(b) limits avoids 
this double exposure. 
Q4. Can a partner claim deduction for expenses incurred personally for the firm's 
business against the remuneration income? 
Yes. Remuneration received by a partner is taxable as 'Income from Business or 
Profession'. The partner can claim all expenses incurred wholly and exclusively for the 
purposes of earning that remuneration as deductions — for example, professional 
subscriptions, books, travel for business purposes (not reimbursed by firm), home office 
expenses. However, expenses must be genuine and supported by bills. The partner's capital 
contribution to the firm is not an expense but a balance sheet item. 
Q5. Is it mandatory for all working partners to receive the same remuneration, or can it 
vary? 
There is no requirement for equal remuneration among working partners. The partnership 
deed can specify different remuneration for different working partners based on their roles, 
expertise, seniority, or contribution. The Section 40(b) limit applies to the total remuneration

paid to all working partners collectively — not to each partner individually. As long as the 
total remuneration paid to all working partners is within the overall 40(b) limit, the distribution 
among partners can be in any ratio as specified in the deed.
