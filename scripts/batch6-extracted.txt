ARTICLE 1 OF 9 | TAX AUDIT 
Section 44AB Income Tax Audit: Applicability, 
Due Date and Penalty 
Updated: May 2026 Read time: 10 min read AY: 2026-27 
 
ARTICLE SUMMARY 
Section 44AB of the Income Tax Act mandates that certain businesses and professionals 
get their accounts audited by a Chartered Accountant and submit a Tax Audit Report (Form 
3CA/3CB along with Form 3CD) on or before the due date. For FY 2025-26, the threshold 
for businesses is Rs. 1 crore (or Rs. 10 crore if cash transactions are below 5%). The 
penalty for non-compliance is 0.5% of turnover or Rs. 1.5 lakh, whichever is lower. This 
article covers every applicability condition, the enhanced cash-based threshold, 
presumptive scheme interaction, due dates, and the consequences of non-compliance.

The Rs. 10 Crore Cash Threshold Benefit 
---PAGE 3---
The enhanced Rs. 10 crore threshold (introduced in Budget 2021) provides significant relief to 
businesses that operate primarily through banking channels. To qualify for this higher threshold, 
TWO conditions must both be satisfied throughout the financial year: 
• Cash receipts condition: Aggregate cash receipts during the year must not exceed 5% of 
total receipts. 
• Cash payments condition: Aggregate cash payments during the year must not exceed 
5% of total payments. 
If either condition is breached at any point during the year, the standard Rs. 1 crore threshold 
applies. This means a business with Rs. 8 crore turnover that makes even 6% of its payments in 
cash loses the benefit and must get its accounts audited. The conditions must be monitored 
throughout the year, not just at year-end. 
 
Digital Receipts Incentive 
The Rs. 10 crore threshold is effectively a reward for businesses that operate digitally. A trading 
firm with Rs. 9 crore annual turnover that collects and pays entirely through NEFT, UPI, or 
account payee cheques needs no tax audit. The same firm, if it accepts 6% of receipts in cash, 
must get audited. This provision strongly incentivises digital transactions in the business sector. 
 
Presumptive Scheme Opt-Out and Audit Requirement 
Section 44AD allows businesses with turnover up to Rs. 3 crore to declare 6% or 8% of turnover 
as income without maintaining books. If a taxpayer opting for Section 44AD in prior years now 
wants to declare actual income that is lower than the prescribed 6%/8%, a tax audit becomes 
mandatory provided their total income (including income from all other sources) exceeds the 
basic exemption limit.

Important 
The tax audit report must be uploaded to the IT portal by the CA and then accepted by the 
taxpayer before it is treated as filed. If the CA uploads the report but the taxpayer does not 
accept it on the portal before the due date, it is treated as not filed. Both actions (CA upload and 
taxpayer acceptance) must happen on or before September 30 to avoid penalty. 
 
Penalty for Non-Compliance 
Section 271B prescribes a penalty for failure to get accounts audited or failure to submit the 
audit report by the due date: 
• Penalty amount: 0.5% of total sales, turnover, or gross receipts for the year, OR Rs. 
1,50,000, whichever is lower. 
• The penalty is levied on the taxpayer, not the CA. 
• Reasonable cause defence: The penalty can be waived if the taxpayer can demonstrate 
a reasonable cause for the failure (e.g., natural disaster, medical emergency, CA non-
availability). However, the burden of proof lies on the taxpayer. 
• Prosecution: Wilful failure to get accounts audited can also attract prosecution under 
Section 276B in addition to the Section 271B penalty. 
 
Pro Tip 
---PAGE 5---
Businesses that hover near the Rs. 1 crore or Rs. 10 crore threshold should track cumulative 
turnover from April itself. If turnover is likely to exceed the threshold, engage a CA early and start 
maintaining accounts in audit-ready format. Engaging a CA in February or March when turnover 
has already crossed the limit results in rushed audits, higher fees, and higher error risk in Form 
3CD. 
 
KEY TAKEAWAYS 
1.  Section 44AB mandates a tax audit by a CA when business turnover exceeds Rs. 1 crore (or 
Rs. 10 crore for cash-light businesses) or professional receipts exceed Rs. 50 lakh. 
2.  The Rs. 10 crore threshold applies only when BOTH cash receipts and cash payments are 
each at most 5% of total receipts and payments. 
3.  Opting out of the presumptive taxation scheme (44AD/44ADA) by declaring lower income 
triggers mandatory audit even within the normal thresholds. 
4.  Form 3CB + Form 3CD is filed for most taxpayers; Form 3CA + Form 3CD if accounts are 
already audited under another statute. 
5.  Tax audit report due date: September 30 for AY 2026-27 (non-TP cases); October 31 for 
transfer pricing cases. 
6.  Both the CA upload and the taxpayer acceptance on the IT portal must happen before the 
deadline for the report to be treated as filed. 
7.  Penalty for late filing or non-filing: 0.5% of turnover or Rs. 1.5 lakh, whichever is lower, under 
Section 271B. 
 
Frequently Asked Questions 
Q1. My business turnover is Rs. 1.1 crore but 90% of all transactions are digital. Do I still 
need a tax audit? 
Yes. The standard threshold is Rs. 1 crore. The enhanced Rs. 10 crore threshold requires 
both cash receipts and cash payments to be at most 5% each. As long as those two 
conditions are met, you would qualify for the Rs. 10 crore threshold and would NOT need a 
tax audit at Rs. 1.1 crore turnover. Verify that your cash receipts are at most 5% of Rs. 1.1 
crore (at most Rs. 5.5 lakh in cash received) and cash payments are similarly capped. If 
both are within 5%, no audit is required. 
Q2. I am a freelance architect with gross receipts of Rs. 60 lakh. Am I covered by Section 
44ADA or Section 44AB? 
Architecture is a specified profession under Section 44ADA. If your gross receipts are Rs. 60 
lakh (below the Rs. 75 lakh limit) and you choose to opt for 44ADA by declaring 50% of 
receipts as income, you are not required to maintain books and no tax audit is needed. 
However, if you choose not to opt for 44ADA and prefer to declare actual income lower than 
50% of receipts, a tax audit under Section 44AB becomes mandatory (since receipts exceed 
Rs. 50 lakh and you are not in the presumptive scheme). 
---PAGE 6---
Q3. What happens if my CA misses the September 30 audit deadline through no fault of 
mine? 
The penalty under Section 271B is on the taxpayer, not the CA. However, if you can 
demonstrate that the delay was due to circumstances beyond your control (illness of the CA, 
natural calamity, platform outages on the IT portal), you can file a reasonable cause 
explanation when responding to the penalty notice. Courts have accepted CA-related delays 
as reasonable cause in some cases. Document all communications with your CA requesting 
timely completion and keep evidence of the cause of delay. 
Q4. A partnership firm's turnover is Rs. 1.5 crore. Does each partner also need a separate 
tax audit? 
The tax audit obligation is on the FIRM, not on individual partners. The firm gets its accounts 
audited and files Form 3CB + Form 3CD. Partners are not individually required to get 
audited solely because the firm's accounts are audited. However, if a partner individually 
has business or professional income that independently triggers Section 44AB (e.g., a 
partner also runs a separate consulting business with Rs. 60 lakh receipts), that partner 
must get a separate audit for their individual income. 
Q5. I filed the tax audit report on September 29 but made an error in Form 3CD. Can I 
revise it? 
Yes. A revised tax audit report can be filed if there is an error in the original report, provided 
the revision is made before the ITR filing due date. The CA must upload the revised Form 
3CD with a note explaining the reason for revision. If the error is discovered after the ITR is 
filed, a revised ITR along with the revised audit report can be filed before December 31. 
Revisions after a scrutiny notice is received require the approval of the assessing officer.

ARTICLE 2 OF 9 | PRESUMPTIVE TAXATION 
Section 44ADA: Presumptive Tax Scheme for 
Professionals 
Updated: May 2026 Read time: 8 min read AY: 2026-27 
 
ARTICLE SUMMARY 
Section 44ADA provides a simplified tax computation for resident individual professionals 
by allowing them to declare 50% of gross receipts as their taxable income, without 
maintaining books of accounts or undergoing a tax audit. For FY 2025-26, the gross 
receipts threshold is Rs. 75 lakh (provided cash receipts are at most 5%; otherwise Rs. 50 
lakh). This article covers eligible professions, the receipt threshold in detail, how the 
deduction works, interaction with the new and old tax regimes, advance tax rules, and the 
consequences of opting out. 
 
Eligible Professions Under Section 44ADA 
Section 44ADA applies to resident individuals carrying on any of the following professions as 
specified under Section 44AA(1): 
Profession Category Examples 
Legal Advocates, barristers, solicitors, notaries 
Medical Doctors, dentists, physiotherapists, anaesthetists, radiologists, 
pathologists 
Engineering Civil engineers, structural engineers, mechanical engineers in 
consulting practice 
Architectural Architects, town planners, landscape designers 
Accountancy Chartered Accountants, Cost Accountants, Company Secretaries in 
practice 
Technical Consultancy IT consultants, management consultants, software development 
consultants 
Interior Decoration Interior designers, space planners 
Other notified professions Any profession notified by CBDT under the relevant rule 
 
Who Is NOT Eligible 
Section 44ADA is available only to resident individuals. Partnership firms (including LLPs), 
companies, HUFs, and non-resident individuals cannot use this scheme. A firm of doctors or 
architects must maintain full books of accounts and cannot opt for 44ADA at the firm level, even 
though the individual partners may be eligible for 44ADA on their individual professional income.

The 5% condition is assessed at the end of the financial year based on the actual cash versus 
digital split of total receipts. A professional who receives Rs. 4 lakh in cash out of Rs. 70 lakh 
total receipts (5.7%) would be treated as exceeding the 5% threshold and the Rs. 50 lakh limit 
applies. Since Rs. 70 lakh exceeds Rs. 50 lakh, that professional must either opt out (and 
maintain full books with audit) or stay in 44ADA but note that their receipts now exceed the 
applicable threshold. 
 
How the Income Computation Works 
Under Section 44ADA, the professional's income from the profession is deemed to be 50% of 
gross professional receipts. No further deductions are allowed from this deemed income on 
account of any business expenses such as rent, salaries to staff, equipment, depreciation, or 
professional subscriptions. The presumed 50% income is the final income from the profession 
for all purposes. 
Deductions under Chapter VI-A (such as Section 80C, 80D, 80G) are still available against the 
total income (which includes the Section 44ADA professional income) to arrive at the net 
taxable income. The standard deduction of Rs. 75,000 available to salaried individuals does not 
apply to professional income under 44ADA.

Advance Tax Under Section 44ADA 
One of the compliance advantages of Section 44ADA is the simplified advance tax schedule. 
Instead of the four quarterly instalments required under the regular regime (15% by June 15, 
45% by September 15, 75% by December 15, and 100% by March 15), professionals under 
Section 44ADA are required to pay only ONE instalment of advance tax equal to 100% of the 
estimated tax liability for the year, by March 15. Missing this single deadline attracts interest 
under Section 234B and 234C. 
 
Opting Out and the 5-Year Restriction 
Section 44ADA does not have the same strict 5-year lock-out as Section 44AD. A professional 
can freely opt in or opt out of 44ADA from year to year without any restriction. This flexibility 
makes it particularly suitable for professionals whose receipts or expense ratios fluctuate year to 
year. However, if the professional opts out in a year and their gross receipts exceed Rs. 50 lakh, 
they must maintain full books and get a tax audit under Section 44AB. 
 
Pro Tip 
Professionals whose actual net profit margin is higher than 50% of receipts benefit most from 
Section 44ADA. For example, a doctor working from a hospital where the hospital provides all 
facilities (equipment, staff, space) may earn 70% to 80% net margins. By opting for 44ADA, only 
50% is taxed, effectively sheltering 20%-30% of actual profit from tax. Always compare actual 
profit versus 50% of receipts before deciding.

KEY TAKEAWAYS 
1.  Section 44ADA allows specified resident individual professionals to declare 50% of gross 
receipts as income without maintaining books or getting an audit. 
2.  Eligible professions include legal, medical, engineering, architecture, accountancy, technical 
consultancy, and interior decoration. 
3.  Gross receipts threshold: Rs. 75 lakh if cash receipts are at most 5% of total; Rs. 50 lakh if 
cash exceeds 5%. 
4.  No business expense deductions are allowed from the 50% presumed income. Chapter VI-A 
deductions (80C, 80D, etc.) remain available from total income. 
5.  Advance tax is a single 100% instalment due by March 15 - no quarterly payments. 
6.  Section 44ADA has no 5-year lock-out on opting out, unlike Section 44AD. 
7.  Professionals with actual profit margins above 50% benefit most since the excess profit 
above 50% is effectively untaxed. 
 
Frequently Asked Questions 
Q1. Can a radiologist employed full-time in a hospital also use Section 44ADA for 
freelance professional work? 
Yes. If the radiologist earns professional income from freelance consultations (outside the 
employment) in addition to their salary, the freelance professional receipts can be declared 
under Section 44ADA (50% deemed income) provided the total freelance receipts are within 
the applicable threshold. The salary component remains taxable as regular salary income. 
Both income heads are reported separately in ITR-4. 
Q2. My professional receipts are Rs. 80 lakh with only 2% cash. I am outside the 44ADA 
threshold. What are my options? 
Since Rs. 80 lakh exceeds the Rs. 75 lakh enhanced threshold (even though the 5% cash 
condition is met), you cannot opt for Section 44ADA. You must maintain books of accounts 
under Section 44AA and since gross receipts exceed Rs. 50 lakh, you are required to get a 
tax audit under Section 44AB and file Form 3CB plus Form 3CD by September 30. The 
44ADA option is not available above the threshold regardless of cash ratio. 
Q3. I am a technical consultant. My actual expenses are only Rs. 5 lakh on receipts of Rs. 
40 lakh (actual profit Rs. 35 lakh = 87.5%). Should I use 44ADA? 
Section 44ADA would deem your income at Rs. 20 lakh (50% of Rs. 40 lakh), which is much 
lower than your actual profit of Rs. 35 lakh. You would pay tax only on Rs. 20 lakh (less any 
Chapter VI-A deductions) instead of Rs. 35 lakh. This is a significant tax saving. Yes, in this 
situation 44ADA is highly beneficial. The only consideration is whether the scheme fits your 
overall tax planning (old vs new regime comparison). 
Q4. Can a Section 44ADA professional claim deduction for home loan interest under 
Section 24(b)? 
---PAGE 11---
Yes. Section 24(b) home loan interest deduction (for self-occupied property, up to Rs. 2 
lakh) is available under the old tax regime regardless of whether income is computed under 
44ADA or actual books. The home loan deduction is against the 'Income from House 
Property' head, not against professional income. Since 44ADA only restricts deductions from 
the professional income head, deductions under other income heads are unaffected. Under 
the new tax regime, Section 24(b) deduction is not available. 
Q5. Is a management consultant covered under Section 44ADA's 'technical consultancy' 
category? 
The term 'technical consultancy' in Section 44ADA refers to consultancy in technical fields 
such as engineering, IT, science, or technology. Pure management consulting (strategy, 
HR, operations) may or may not qualify depending on the nature of work. Several ITAT 
decisions have held that management consultancy can fall under technical consultancy if it 
involves technical knowledge or expertise. However, there is no definitive ruling, and the 
classification is fact-specific. A CA's guidance on whether your specific consulting activity 
qualifies is advisable.

ARTICLE 3 OF 9 | CORPORATE TAXATION 
Minimum Alternate Tax (MAT): Eligibility, 
Calculation and Credit 
Updated: May 2026 Read time: 9 min read AY: 2026-27 
 
ARTICLE SUMMARY 
Minimum Alternate Tax (MAT) under Section 115JB ensures that companies that report 
book profits in their financial statements but show zero or negligible taxable income due to 
various deductions and exemptions pay at least a minimum amount of tax. The MAT rate is 
15% of book profit (plus surcharge and cess). MAT paid in excess of regular tax can be 
carried forward as MAT Credit Entitlement for up to 15 years. This article explains the MAT 
computation, the book profit adjustments, MAT credit mechanism, exemptions from MAT, 
and the interaction with the new concessional tax regime under Section 115BAA. 
 
What is MAT and Why Does It Exist? 
Companies use various legitimate deductions and exemptions under the Income Tax Act to 
reduce their taxable income to very low levels or even to zero, despite showing healthy book 
profits in their financial statements. MAT was introduced to ensure that such companies pay a 
minimum level of tax proportionate to their reported profitability. It prevents the situation where a 
company reports Rs. 100 crore profit to its shareholders but pays zero income tax due to 
exemptions and deductions. 
MAT applies when the tax payable under regular provisions is less than 15% of book profit. In 
that case, the company pays MAT at 15% of book profit. The excess (MAT paid over regular 
tax) is available as MAT Credit, which can be used in future years when regular tax exceeds 
MAT.

Book Profit Computation Under Section 115JB 
Book profit is the net profit as per the Profit and Loss Account (prepared under the Companies 
Act), after making the following adjustments: 
Amounts Added Back to Net Profit 
• Income tax paid or payable and any provision for tax (including deferred tax) 
• Amounts transferred to any reserves (general reserves, capital reserves, etc.) 
• Provision for unascertained liabilities (including contingency provisions) 
• Provision for losses of subsidiary companies 
• Dividends paid or proposed 
• Expenditure related to exempt income under Section 10 (other than Section 10(38) 
which no longer applies) 
• Notional loss on transfer of capital assets to special economic zones 
 
Amounts Deducted from Net Profit 
• Amount withdrawn from reserves (if credited to P&L, now reversed) 
• Income exempt under Section 10 (dividends, long-term capital gains in certain cases) 
• Depreciation debited to P&L (then added back at book value depreciation under 
Companies Act) 
• Loss brought forward or unabsorbed depreciation, whichever is less

MAT Credit Entitlement Under Section 115JAA 
The excess of MAT paid over the regular tax payable in any year is available as MAT Credit. 
This credit can be carried forward for up to 15 assessment years immediately following the year 
in which it arose. The credit can be utilised in a future year when regular tax payable exceeds 
MAT - the excess of regular tax over MAT in that year can be set off against the brought-forward 
MAT credit. 
MAT Credit Rule Detail

Utilisation condition Regular tax in future year must exceed MAT in that year 
Amount utilisable Regular tax minus MAT in the utilisation year (not the full credit at 
once) 
Interest on MAT credit No interest is paid on MAT credit carried forward 
MAT credit on amalgamation The transferee company can carry forward the transferor's MAT 
credit (subject to conditions) 
 
Who is Exempt from MAT? 
MAT does not apply to the following: 
• Companies opting for the new concessional tax rate under Section 115BAA (22% base 
rate) are exempt from MAT. Once a company opts for 115BAA, it cannot pay MAT and 
cannot utilise any MAT credit brought forward from earlier years. 
• New manufacturing companies opting for Section 115BAB (15% base rate for new 
domestic manufacturers set up after October 1, 2019) are also exempt from MAT. 
• Income accruing or arising to a company from life insurance business (taxed under the 
Insurance Act framework). 
• Shipping companies opting for the Tonnage Tax Scheme under Sections 115V to 
115VZC. 
 
Pro Tip 
Companies with large accumulated MAT credit should carefully evaluate whether to switch to the 
Section 115BAA regime. Opting for 115BAA (which offers a lower 22% tax rate) means forfeiting 
all accumulated MAT credit permanently. Compute the present value of the MAT credit versus 
the annual tax saving from the lower rate before making the switch. For some companies with 
large MAT credit balances, continuing under the regular regime may be more beneficial.

KEY TAKEAWAYS 
1.  MAT at 15% of book profit applies when regular tax payable is lower than 15% of the 
company's book profit as per its financial statements. 
2.  Book profit is the P&L net profit adjusted for items like tax provisions, reserves, contingencies, 
and exempt income. 
3.  The effective MAT rate including surcharge and cess can range from 15.6% to 17.47% 
depending on income level. 
4.  Excess MAT over regular tax generates MAT Credit Entitlement, carry-forwardable for 15 
years under Section 115JAA. 
5.  MAT credit is utilised in future years when regular tax exceeds MAT in those years. 
6.  Companies opting for Section 115BAA (22% concessional rate) or Section 115BAB are 
exempt from MAT but lose all prior MAT credit. 
7.  Accumulated MAT credit must be evaluated before switching regimes since the switch forfeits 
the credit permanently. 
 
Frequently Asked Questions 
Q1. A company has Rs. 5 crore book profit but Rs. 20 lakh regular taxable income due to 
80IC deduction. What is payable? 
Regular tax on Rs. 20 lakh at 25% = Rs. 5 lakh (plus cess). MAT on Rs. 5 crore at 15% = 
Rs. 75 lakh (plus surcharge and cess). Since MAT (Rs. 75 lakh+) exceeds regular tax (Rs. 5 
lakh+), the company pays MAT. MAT credit = MAT paid minus regular tax = approximately 
Rs. 70 lakh. This credit can be carried forward for 15 years. 
Q2. Can a company utilise MAT credit and carry-forward business loss in the same year? 
Yes, but in a specific order. First, the regular tax is computed after setting off carry-forward 
business losses and unabsorbed depreciation. Then MAT is computed on book profit. If 
regular tax is lower than MAT, the company pays MAT. The business losses are still set off 
(reducing regular tax), and MAT credit is generated. In a future year when regular tax 
exceeds MAT, the MAT credit is used. The business losses and MAT credit operate in 
parallel but through different mechanisms. 
Q3. My company switched to Section 115BAA last year. Can I now switch back to regular 
provisions? 
No. The option to avail Section 115BAA is a one-time, irrevocable choice. Once a company 
opts for the 22% concessional rate under Section 115BAA, it cannot revert to the regular 
provisions in any subsequent year. The switch is permanent. Additionally, all MAT credit 
accumulated prior to the switch is forfeited permanently once the company opts for 115BAA. 
Q4. Does MAT apply to foreign companies operating in India? 
---PAGE 16---
MAT under Section 115JB applies to domestic companies. Foreign companies (those 
registered outside India) are subject to a separate provision - Section 115JB applies to them 
only to the extent their income is computed under the Companies Act provisions. In practice, 
foreign companies operating through a branch in India are subject to MAT only on that 
portion of book profit attributable to their Indian operations. The full MAT provisions are 
primarily designed for domestic companies. 
Q5. If MAT credit is not utilised within 15 years, does it lapse? 
Yes. MAT credit entitlement that is not set off within 15 assessment years following the year 
in which it was generated lapses and cannot be carried forward further or refunded. This is 
one reason why companies evaluate MAT credit utilisation as part of their long-term tax 
planning. If a company has accumulated large MAT credit that is unlikely to be utilised within 
15 years (because it has transitioned to a low-profit phase), the credit may effectively be 
lost.

ARTICLE 4 OF 9 | TAX COMPUTATION 
Income Tax Surcharge Rates and Marginal Relief 
for AY 2026-27 
Updated: May 2026 Read time: 8 min read AY: 2026-27 
 
ARTICLE SUMMARY 
Income tax surcharge is an additional tax levied on the income tax payable by taxpayers 
whose income exceeds specified thresholds. For individuals, surcharge rates range from 
10% to 25% (or 37% under old regime for income above Rs. 5 crore). Marginal relief 
ensures that the extra tax payable due to crossing a surcharge slab does not exceed the 
marginal income that caused the slab to be breached. This article covers all surcharge 
rates for individuals, HUFs, firms, and companies under both regimes, the marginal relief

Marginal Relief: What It Is and How It Works 
Marginal relief is a provision that ensures a taxpayer does not pay more additional tax on 
account of surcharge than the amount by which their income exceeds the surcharge threshold. 
Without marginal relief, a taxpayer earning Rs. 50,00,001 (just Rs. 1 above the Rs. 50 lakh 
surcharge threshold) would pay 10% surcharge on their entire tax, resulting in a 
disproportionately large jump in total tax for just Rs. 1 extra income. 
The marginal relief formula ensures: (Total tax with surcharge at income X) minus (Total tax 
without surcharge at the threshold) must not exceed (Income X minus the threshold income).

Surcharge on Capital Gains with Special Tax Rates 
For capital gains that are subject to special tax rates (such as LTCG at 12.5% on equity or

• LTCG under Section 112A (equity shares and equity mutual funds) 
• STCG under Section 111A (equity shares and equity mutual funds) 
• LTCG under Section 112 (other than equity) 
This means that even a very high income taxpayer with income above Rs. 5 crore pays only 
15% surcharge (not 25% or 37%) on their LTCG and STCG from listed equity transactions. 
 
Pro Tip

next financial year by invoicing in April instead of March. However, this must be done genuinely 
and must reflect actual transaction timing, not just paper deferral.

Frequently Asked Questions 
Q1. My total income is Rs. 52 lakh. What is my effective total tax under the new regime? 
Under the new regime for AY 2026-27: Tax on Rs. 52 lakh using new regime slabs 
(approximate) is around Rs. 9,60,000. Surcharge at 10% = Rs. 96,000. However, marginal 
relief applies since income is just Rs. 2 lakh above the Rs. 50 lakh threshold. The marginal 
relief computation ensures your total tax does not exceed tax on Rs. 50 lakh plus Rs. 2 lakh 
(the marginal income). Consult an ITR calculator for the precise figure with marginal relief 
applied. 
Q2. I have salary income of Rs. 60 lakh and LTCG on shares of Rs. 40 lakh. What 
surcharge rates apply? 
Two different surcharge rates apply to different parts of your income. On the salary income 
portion, surcharge is at 10% (total income above Rs. 50 lakh). On the LTCG on listed equity 
shares (Section 112A), the surcharge is capped at 15% regardless of total income. The tax 
computation splits the income into regular income and special rate income, applies the 
respective surcharge caps, and totals them. 
Q3. Does a partnership firm get marginal relief on surcharge? 
Partnership firms pay a flat 12% surcharge regardless of income level. There are no 
graduated surcharge slabs for firms. Since there is no threshold beyond which the 
surcharge rate jumps (it is 12% from the first rupee of income), the concept of marginal relief 
does not apply to partnership firms or LLPs. 
Q4. How is surcharge calculated when a company has both regular income and income 
from capital gains? 
For companies, surcharge is applied to the total tax payable on all income combined. The 
company applies the applicable surcharge rate (7% or 12% for regular domestic companies) 
on the total tax computed on all income heads including capital gains. Unlike individuals, 
companies do not have a separate surcharge cap for capital gains income. 
Q5. The new regime surcharge cap at 25% - does it apply to all income or only specific 
types? 
The 25% surcharge cap under the new regime applies to all income of an individual who has 
opted for Section 115BAC, for income above Rs. 5 crore. There is no distinction between 
regular income and capital gains for this cap under the new regime. An individual in the new 
regime with Rs. 6 crore total income (including salary and capital gains) pays a maximum of 
25% surcharge on all components, unlike the old regime where income above Rs. 5 crore 
faces 37% surcharge on regular income.

Updated: May 2026 Read time: 8 min read AY: 2026-27 
 
ARTICLE SUMMARY 
Section 36 of the Income Tax Act specifies a list of expenses that are expressly allowed as 
deductions in computing income from business or profession, in addition to the general 
deduction provision under Section 37. These include insurance premiums, employer 
contributions to recognised provident fund and approved gratuity fund, bad debts, scientific 
research expenditure, and several other specific items. This article covers each deduction 
under Section 36 with the applicable conditions and limits for FY 2025-26. 
 
Overview of Section 36 Deductions 
Section 36 is a list of specific allowable deductions from business income that supplement the 
general deduction under Section 37 (which allows any expenditure wholly and exclusively for 
business purposes). While Section 37 has a residual catch-all character, Section 36 provides 
explicit statutory authority for specific categories of expenditure. Understanding Section 36 is 
important because some of these deductions have specific conditions, limits, or computational 
rules that differ from the general principles under Section 37.

Bad Debts Under Section 36(1)(vii) - Key Rules 
Bad debt deduction is one of the most commonly claimed and contested deductions under 
Section 36. The critical conditions are: 
• The debt must have been taken into account in computing the income of the assessee in 
any previous year. This means the corresponding income (sale or service revenue) must 
have been offered to tax in an earlier year. 
• The debt must be written off as irrecoverable in the books of account of the assessee for 
the year in which deduction is claimed. 
• It is not necessary to prove that the debt has become irrecoverable in fact. The Supreme 
Court has held that the business judgment of the taxpayer regarding irrecoverability is 
sufficient. 
• The bad debt written off but subsequently recovered is taxable as business income in 
the year of recovery under Section 41(4). 
 
---PAGE 23---
Important 
Prior to Amendment in AY 2014-15: Bad debts were allowed only if actually written off in the 
books. Post amendment, the requirement is that the debt must have been included in income in 
a prior year. Banks and NBFCs cannot claim Section 36(1)(vii) for bad debts that were previously 
covered by the provision for bad debts under Section 36(1)(viia). The two provisions are mutually 
exclusive for such entities. 
 
Interest on Borrowed Capital - Section 36(1)(iii) 
Interest on capital borrowed for business purposes is deductible under Section 36(1)(iii). 
However, there is one important restriction: interest paid on capital borrowed for acquiring a 
capital asset for business use is not deductible for the period starting from the date of borrowing 
to the date the asset is first put to use. This pre-use interest must be capitalised as part of the 
asset's cost. Once the asset is in use, subsequent interest is deductible as a revenue expense. 
 
Section 43B - Payment Timing Rule for Several Section 36 Items 
Several deductions under Section 36 are subject to Section 43B, which requires that certain 
payments must be actually paid on or before the due date of filing the income tax return for the 
relevant year. If not paid by that date, the deduction is allowed only in the year of actual 
payment. Section 43B applies to: 
• Employer's contribution to PF, gratuity fund, and other welfare funds (Section 36(1)(iv), 
(v)) 
• Bonus and commission to employees (Section 36(1)(ii)) 
• Interest on loans from public financial institutions and scheduled banks 
 
Pro Tip 
Businesses that claim bonus and PF contributions as deductions should ensure all such 
payments are actually made (not just accrued or provided for) on or before the ITR filing due 
date. A bonus accrued in the books for FY 2025-26 but not actually paid to employees by 
October 31, 2026 (ITR due date for audit cases) will not be deductible in FY 2025-26 and will 
shift to FY 2026-27. 
 
KEY TAKEAWAYS 
1.  Section 36 provides a specific list of deductions for business income, complementing the 
general provision of Section 37. 
2.  Key deductions include: insurance on stock, employee bonuses (Section 43B compliant), 
interest on borrowed capital, PF and gratuity contributions, and bad debts written off. 
3.  Bad debt deduction requires the corresponding income to have been offered to tax in a prior 
year and the debt to be written off in the books. 
---PAGE 24---
4.  Interest on borrowings for capital assets cannot be deducted for the pre-use period and must 
be capitalised as part of asset cost. 
5.  STT and CTT paid are deductible under Section 36 only for traders where the securities 
income is classified as business income. 
6.  Several Section 36 deductions (bonus, PF, gratuity) are subject to the actual payment timing 
condition under Section 43B. 
7.  Banks have a special provision (Section 36(1)(viia)) for provisions for bad debts based on 
specified percentages of advances. 
 
Frequently Asked Questions 
Q1. I run an e-commerce business. Can I claim insurance premium on my warehouse 
stock under Section 36(1)(i)? 
Yes. Insurance premium paid to protect your stock in trade (goods stored in the warehouse 
for sale) against fire, flood, theft, or other damage is expressly deductible under Section 
36(1)(i). Ensure the insurance policy covers trading stock (inventory) and not fixed assets 
like the warehouse building itself (which would be covered under Section 37 as a general 
business expense). 
Q2. My company accrued Rs. 20 lakh bonus for employees in March 2026 but paid it in 
May 2026. Can I claim the deduction in FY 2025-26? 
No. Bonus is subject to Section 43B. Even if accrued in the books for FY 2025-26, the 
deduction is allowed only in the year of actual payment. Since the bonus was paid in May 
2026 (FY 2026-27), the deduction will be available only in AY 2027-28. To claim the 
deduction in FY 2025-26, the bonus must be paid on or before the ITR filing due date for FY 
2025-26 (October 31, 2026 for audit cases; July 31, 2026 for others). 
Q3. A customer declared bankruptcy and owes us Rs. 5 lakh. Can we claim bad debt 
deduction? 
Yes, provided the following: the Rs. 5 lakh sales revenue was included in your income and 
offered to tax in the year of sale (not just recorded as a receivable). You must write off the 
Rs. 5 lakh as bad debt in your books for FY 2025-26. The customer's bankruptcy is strong 
evidence of irrecoverability, but you do not need a court order - writing off the books based 
on business judgment is sufficient. If you later recover any amount, the recovered amount 
will be taxable as business income in the year of recovery under Section 41(4). 
Q4. Can an individual professional claim bad debt deduction under Section 36(1)(vii)? 
A professional can claim bad debt deduction only if they maintain their accounts on the 
mercantile (accrual) basis. Under the mercantile system, professional fees are credited to 
income when earned (even if not received), and subsequent write-off qualifies as bad debt. 
Professionals maintaining accounts on the cash basis cannot claim bad debt deduction 
because unpaid fees are never included in income in the first place - so there is nothing to 
write off. 
Q5. Is an employer's contribution to ESOP or ESOPs considered deductible under 
Section 36? 
---PAGE 25---
Employee Stock Option Plans (ESOPs) are not specifically listed in Section 36. However, 
the value of ESOPs at the time of exercise may be deductible as employee compensation 
under Section 37 (general business expenditure) in certain circumstances. The treatment of 
ESOP expense deduction is complex and depends on the structure of the plan. For 
employers, the deduction is generally claimed in the year the options are exercised by 
employees (when the compensation value can be ascertained), subject to judicial 
interpretation. Seek specific CA advice on ESOP deductibility for your entity.

ARTICLE 6 OF 9 | INTERNATIONAL TAXATION 
Section 44B and Section 172: Income Tax on 
Shipping Business 
Updated: May 2026 Read time: 8 min read AY: 2026-27 
 
ARTICLE SUMMARY 
India has two special provisions for taxing the income of non-resident shipping companies: 
Section 44B provides a presumptive income computation for non-resident persons engaged 
in the shipping business, deeming 7.5% of specified receipts as their Indian income. 
Section 172 provides a mechanism for collecting advance tax on a voyage-by-voyage basis 
from ships of foreign companies calling at Indian ports. This article explains both provisions, 
the taxable receipts covered, the procedures for Section 172 clearance, treaty override 
considerations, and the interaction with the Tonnage Tax Scheme. 
 
Section 44B: Presumptive Taxation for Non-Resident Shipping 
Section 44B applies to non-resident persons (including foreign companies) engaged in the 
business of operation of ships. It provides that the income of such a non-resident from the 
operation of ships shall be presumed to be an amount equal to 7.5% of the aggregate of the 
following amounts received or deemed to be received in India: 
• The amount paid or payable (whether in or out of India) to the non-resident or to any 
person on their behalf, on account of the carriage of passengers, livestock, mail, or 
goods from any port in India. 
• The amount received or deemed to be received in India on account of the carriage of 
passengers, livestock, mail, or goods from any port outside India. 
 
The 7.5% presumed income is the final income for Indian tax purposes. No further deductions 
are allowed from this amount. The non-resident shipping company pays tax at the rate 
applicable to foreign companies (40% base rate plus applicable surcharge and cess) on this 
7.5% deemed income.

Section 172: Voyage-by-Voyage Tax Collection 
Section 172 provides a separate, voyage-specific collection mechanism for non-resident 
shipping income. It operates as follows: 
1. When a ship of a non-resident owner or charterer carries passengers, cargo, or mail 
from an Indian port, before the ship is granted port clearance, the owner or agent must 
file a return of income for that voyage. 
2. The return covers the income from that specific voyage (7.5% of voyage receipts under 
the presumptive formula). 
3. Tax on that voyage income must be paid at the time of filing the return. 
4. Once the tax is paid, the port authority will issue clearance to the ship. 
5. Any overpayment or underpayment is adjusted when the regular annual assessment is 
completed. 
 
Section 172 vs Section 44B Relationship 
Section 172 is a collection mechanism, not a separate charging provision. The actual income 
computation remains under Section 44B (7.5% of receipts). Section 172 ensures that the 
government collects tax before the foreign ship leaves India and there is no enforcement risk 
from a vessel that has departed. The voyage-by-voyage advance is credited against the final 
annual tax liability of the shipping company. 
 
Tax Treaty Override 
India has signed Double Taxation Avoidance Agreements (DTAAs) with many countries that 
contain specific provisions for shipping income. Under most DTAAs, shipping income of a 
resident of one country is taxable only in that country (the country of the ship's owner). This 
means a Norwegian shipping company operating ships that call at Indian ports may be entirely 
exempt from Indian tax under the India-Norway DTAA, despite Section 44B. 
Where a DTAA provides for exclusive taxation in the foreign country, the shipping company or 
its agent can claim treaty benefits and avoid Section 44B taxation. The claim must be supported 
by a Tax Residency Certificate (TRC) from the foreign country and a Form 10F filing in India. 
The port clearance under Section 172 is still required procedurally, but the tax payable may be 
nil under treaty protection. 
 
Tonnage Tax Scheme: Alternative for Indian Shipping Companies 
Indian shipping companies (domestic companies) can opt for the Tonnage Tax Scheme under 
Sections 115V to 115VZC instead of the regular profit-based computation. Under this scheme, 
income from the operation of ships is computed based on the net tonnage of ships in the fleet 
(not actual profits). The scheme provides certainty and stability in tax liability and is not subject 
to MAT. Once opted, the company must stay in the scheme for a minimum of 10 years. 
 
---PAGE 28---
Pro Tip 
Non-resident shipping companies regularly operating in Indian waters should review applicable 
DTAAs before paying Section 172 voyage-by-voyage taxes. Many shipping companies from 
countries with favourable DTAAs overpay Section 172 tax and file refund claims. Setting up 
proper treaty protection documentation (TRC, Form 10F) at the start of operations avoids this 
cash flow issue. 
 
KEY TAKEAWAYS 
1.  Section 44B deems 7.5% of gross shipping receipts from Indian voyages as the taxable 
income of non-resident shipping operators. 
2.  Tax on this 7.5% deemed income is computed at the foreign company rate of 40% plus 
applicable surcharge and cess. 
3.  Section 172 requires voyage-by-voyage advance tax payment and return filing before a non-
resident ship receives port clearance in India. 
4.  Section 172 is a collection mechanism only; the income computation follows Section 44B's 
7.5% presumptive formula. 
5.  DTAA provisions may exempt the shipping income from Indian tax entirely if the vessel's 
owner is resident in a treaty country with an exclusive taxation clause. 
6.  Treaty protection requires a Tax Residency Certificate and Form 10F filing in India. 
7.  Indian domestic shipping companies can opt for the Tonnage Tax Scheme under Sections 
115V to 115VZC as an alternative to profit-based computation. 
 
Frequently Asked Questions 
Q1. An Indian company hires a foreign ship (time charter) to carry cargo. Is the charter 
fee subject to Section 44B? 
Section 44B applies to non-resident persons in the business of operating ships. If the foreign 
ship owner is a non-resident and receives charter hire from India, the income accruing in 
India from the charter may be subject to Section 44B or Section 9(1)(i) (income accruing in 
India). However, under many DTAAs, charter income for ships is classified under the 
shipping income article and may be taxable only in the owner's country. The Indian company 
paying charter hire should check whether TDS under Section 195 applies and whether the 
non-resident can claim treaty relief. 
Q2. A Section 172 return was filed and tax was paid, but the voyage was cancelled. Can 
the tax be refunded? 
If a voyage is cancelled after the Section 172 return and tax payment have been made, the 
advance tax paid becomes excess and is refundable. The shipping company or its agent 
should file a claim for refund with the assessing officer, demonstrating that the voyage did 
---PAGE 29---
not occur and the income giving rise to the Section 172 tax did not arise. The refund is 
processed through the regular refund mechanism of the Income Tax Department. 
Q3. Does Section 44B apply to a non-resident company that only provides container 
terminal services and does not operate ships itself? 
No. Section 44B specifically covers income from the operation of ships - carriage of 
passengers, cargo, or mail. A company providing port services, container handling, or 
logistics support at an Indian port (without operating ships) is not covered by Section 44B. 
Its income from Indian operations would be assessed under the regular provisions 
applicable to non-residents (Sections 44DA or 9 depending on the nature of services) or 
under the applicable DTAA. 
Q4. Can a non-resident shipping company maintain books of accounts and compute 
actual profits instead of using the Section 44B presumptive rate? 
No. Section 44B is mandatory for non-residents in the shipping business, not optional. 
Unlike Section 44AD or 44ADA (which are opt-in schemes), Section 44B is the statutory 
method for computing income of non-resident shipping operators. The non-resident cannot 
opt out and declare actual profits instead. The only alternative to Section 44B taxation is 
treaty protection under an applicable DTAA. 
Q5. An Indian shipping company opted for the Tonnage Tax Scheme 3 years ago. Can it 
switch back to regular taxation this year? 
The Tonnage Tax Scheme under Sections 115V to 115VZC requires a minimum 
commitment of 10 years. Once opted, an Indian shipping company cannot switch back to 
regular profit-based taxation before completing 10 years. The scheme provides stability and 
predictability, but the 10-year lock-in means companies must carefully evaluate their long-
term position before opting in.

ARTICLE 7 OF 9 | INTERNATIONAL TAXATION 
Form 67 and Foreign Tax Credit: How to Claim 
Relief on Taxes Paid Abroad 
Updated: May 2026 Read time: 8 min read AY: 2026-27 
 
ARTICLE SUMMARY 
Indian residents who earn income from foreign sources (salary, dividends, interest, capital 
gains, or business income) may have tax withheld by the foreign country on that income. To 
avoid double taxation, the Income Tax Act under Section 90 or 91 allows a credit for foreign 
taxes paid against the Indian tax liability. The claim is made by filing Form 67 on the IT 
portal on or before the ITR filing due date. This article explains the foreign tax credit 
framework, the eligible foreign taxes, the Form 67 filing procedure, computation rules, and 
the consequences of late filing. 
 
Legal Basis for Foreign Tax Credit 
The foreign tax credit (FTC) framework in India is governed by Section 90 (for countries with 
which India has a Double Taxation Avoidance Agreement - DTAA) and Section 91 (for countries 
with which India does not have a DTAA). Rule 128 of the Income Tax Rules, 1962 provides the 
detailed computation rules and the Form 67 requirement. The FTC rules were substantially 
revised in 2016 (effective AY 2017-18) to provide a more structured framework. 
 
Who Can Claim Foreign Tax Credit? 
Condition Details 
Eligible claimant Resident and Ordinarily Resident (ROR) individuals, companies, and other 
entities in India 
RNOR and NRI Not eligible for FTC since their foreign income is generally not taxable in 
India

Foreign Tax Credit Computation 
The FTC is computed separately for each country and for each type or source of income. The 
credit for any given income is limited to the LOWER of: 
• The actual foreign tax paid on that income, converted to Indian Rupees at the 
telegraphic transfer buying rate (TTBR) of the State Bank of India on the last day of the 
month preceding the month of tax payment. 
• The Indian tax payable on that foreign income (i.e., the Indian tax attributable to that 
specific income item). 
This ensures that FTC cannot exceed the Indian tax on the same income and does not result in 
a net refund.

(on remaining Indian income) 
 
Form 67: Filing Requirement and Procedure 
Rule 128(9) mandates that any taxpayer claiming FTC must file Form 67 on the IT portal on or 
before the due date of filing the income tax return. Form 67 is filed separately from the ITR itself. 
6. Log in to the IT portal at incometax.gov.in. 
7. Navigate to: e-File > Income Tax Forms > File Income Tax Forms > Form 67. 
8. Select the relevant Assessment Year and fill in: the country where tax was paid, the 
nature of income (salary, dividends, interest, etc.), the amount of foreign income in the 
foreign currency and INR, the amount of foreign tax paid in foreign currency and INR, 
and the exchange rate used. 
9. Upload supporting documents: Tax assessment order or withholding tax certificate from 
the foreign country showing the tax paid (must be in the foreign country's official 
language; if not in English, attach a certified translation). 
10. Submit and e-verify Form 67 using Aadhaar OTP or digital signature. 
11. Then proceed to file the ITR, which will reference the Form 67 claim.

Important 
Form 67 must be filed ON OR BEFORE the ITR due date. If Form 67 is filed after the ITR due 
date (even a day late), the FTC claim is denied entirely. This is one of the most common and 
costly mistakes made by taxpayers with foreign income. The Delhi High Court has upheld the 
denial of FTC for late Form 67 filing in multiple cases. 
 
Documents Required for Form 67 
• Tax Residency Certificate (if claiming DTAA benefits along with FTC) 
• Foreign tax payment proof: withholding certificate issued by the foreign employer or 
payer (e.g., W-2 form from US, P60 from UK, or equivalent) 
• Foreign tax assessment order or foreign ITR showing taxes paid (for countries where the

• Bank statement showing remittance of tax to the foreign government (if self-assessed) 
• Exchange rate certificate from SBI or authorised dealer bank for the conversion rate 
used

NRIs returning to India who become ROR in their first or second year back should track foreign 
income and foreign taxes paid from day one. Once ROR status is attained, global income 
becomes taxable in India. Set up the Form 67 filing as a recurring calendar reminder well before 
July 31 each year to ensure it is never filed late. 
 
KEY TAKEAWAYS 
1.  Foreign Tax Credit (FTC) under Section 90/91 allows a credit for taxes paid in a foreign 
country against Indian tax on the same income. 
2.  FTC is limited to the lower of: actual foreign tax paid (in INR) or Indian tax attributable to the 
foreign income. 
3.  Form 67 must be filed on the IT portal on or before the ITR due date. Filing after the due date 
results in total denial of the FTC claim. 
4.  FTC is available to Resident and Ordinarily Resident (ROR) taxpayers only; NRIs and 
RNORs are generally not eligible. 
5.  Separate FTC computation is required for each country and each type of income. 
6.  Supporting documents must include the foreign tax withholding certificate or equivalent proof 
of tax paid abroad. 
7.  Under Section 91, for non-DTAA countries, FTC is the lower of Indian tax rate or foreign tax 
rate on the doubly taxed income.

Frequently Asked Questions 
Q1. I received dividends from a US company. The US withheld 25% tax. Can I claim FTC 
in India? 
Yes. US-source dividends are taxable in India for ROR residents. If the US withheld 25% (or 
the treaty rate of 15% under the India-US DTAA), you can claim FTC for the foreign tax 
withheld. Under the India-US DTAA, the treaty rate for dividends is 15% or 25% depending 
on shareholding. Compute the Indian tax attributable to the dividend income and claim FTC 
for the lower of: foreign tax withheld or Indian tax on the dividend. File Form 67 before your 
ITR due date. 
Q2. My Form 67 was filed on August 5 but my ITR due date was July 31. What happens? 
The FTC claim will be denied. Rule 128(9) makes filing Form 67 on or before the ITR due 
date a mandatory condition for claiming FTC. If you missed the July 31 deadline, the late 
Form 67 filed on August 5 does not preserve the FTC claim. You may explore whether the 
ITR itself was filed under a condonation of delay (Section 119(2)(b)) and whether the Form 
67 can be considered under the same condonation. However, such condonation is 
discretionary and not guaranteed. 
Q3. I paid tax in a country that does not have a DTAA with India. Can I still claim FTC? 
Yes. Section 91 provides FTC for countries that do not have a DTAA with India (currently 
applicable for a limited number of countries). The credit is computed as the lower of the 
Indian tax rate on the income or the foreign country's tax rate on the same income, applied 
to the doubly taxed income. The procedure (Form 67 filing) and conditions remain the same 
as for DTAA countries. 
Q4. Can FTC be carried forward if it exceeds my Indian tax liability in the current year? 
No. FTC is not carrried forward or refunded. The credit is available only in the year the 
foreign income is taxable in India. Any excess FTC (foreign tax paid exceeding Indian tax on 
that income) is simply lost for that year. There is no mechanism to utilise the excess FTC in 
future years. This is one of the limitations of the current FTC framework and differs from the 
treatment in some other countries. 
Q5. I am a returning NRI who has been RNOR for 2 years. Am I eligible for FTC? 
During the RNOR period, foreign income that arises from a business controlled outside India 
or from a profession set up outside India is not taxable in India. Since such income is not 
included in Indian tax base, no FTC is applicable for those years. Once you attain ROR 
status (from year 3 typically), your global income is taxable in India and FTC becomes 
available for foreign taxes on that global income. Plan your Form 67 filing from the first year 
you attain ROR status.

ARTICLE 9 OF 9 | TAX AUDIT 
Tax Audit Report Form 3CD: Key Clauses 
Explained 
Updated: May 2026 Read time: 10 min read AY: 2026-27 
 
ARTICLE SUMMARY 
Form 3CD is the Statement of Particulars to be furnished under Section 44AB of the 
Income Tax Act as part of the tax audit report. It contains 44 clauses (as amended) 
covering virtually every aspect of the taxpayer's business or profession: method of 
accounting, depreciation, loans and advances, related party transactions, deductions under 
various sections, compliance with TDS obligations, and more. This article explains the most 
important and complex clauses in Form 3CD and what auditors and taxpayers must 
disclose. 
 
What is Form 3CD and How It Fits Into the Tax Audit 
Form 3CD is the annexure to either Form 3CA (when accounts are audited under another law) 
or Form 3CB (when accounts are audited only for income tax purposes). It is a detailed factual 
statement prepared by the Chartered Accountant based on the books of account, financial 
statements, and other documents of the taxpayer. Form 3CD does not express an opinion - it 
discloses facts and particulars in a standardised format that allows the Income Tax Department 
to efficiently assess the return. 
Form 3CD has undergone significant revisions. Clause 30C on permissible deductions under 
GAAR (General Anti-Avoidance Rules) and Clause 44 on GST turnover reconciliation were 
added in recent amendments. The current Form 3CD has 44 clauses numbered from 1 to 44.

Depreciation (Clause 18) 
Clause 18 requires disclosure of depreciation computed under the Income Tax Act (Sections 32 
and 35) versus depreciation debited to the P&L account as per the companies or accounting 
standards. Key sub-clauses include: 
• 18(a): Depreciation under Section 32 - separately for each asset block, rate applied, 
opening WDV, additions and disposals, closing WDV, and depreciation amount. 
• 18(b): Amounts admissible under Sections 33AB (tea/coffee/rubber development), 
33ABA (site restoration fund), 35 (scientific research), 35ABB (telecom licence fee), 
35AC, 35AD, 35CCC, 35CCD. 
• Auditors must ensure that the asset additions are supported by invoices, that disposals 
are correctly computed, and that the correct block rates are applied. 
 
TDS Compliance (Clause 34) 
Clause 34 is one of the most detailed and scrutinised clauses. It requires disclosure of: 
• Whether TDS has been deducted at correct rates on all payments liable to TDS (salary, 
rent, interest, professional fees, contractor payments, etc.). 
• Whether TDS deducted has been paid on time to the government. 
• Whether TDS returns (Form 24Q, 26Q, 27Q, 27EQ) have been filed on time. 
• Details of any TDS deducted but not paid, or any payment made without deduction of 
TDS where TDS was required. 
---PAGE 40---
• Details of interest paid or payable under Section 201 for late TDS payment or late filing 
of TDS returns. 
The IT Department cross-references Clause 34 disclosures with the TRACES TDS database. 
Any mismatch between what is declared in Clause 34 and the TRACES records triggers 
scrutiny notices. 
 
GAAR and International Transactions (Clauses 30C and 30D) 
Clause 30C (added by the 2018 amendment) requires the tax auditor to report whether any 
impermissible avoidance arrangement within the meaning of Section 95 (GAAR) has been 
identified in the books of the taxpayer. If the auditor identifies any arrangement, its nature, 
parties involved, and the claimed tax benefit must be disclosed. 
Clause 30D requires disclosure of whether the taxpayer has entered into specified domestic 
transactions under Section 92BA or international transactions under Section 92B, and whether 
transfer pricing documentation under Section 92D has been maintained. 
 
GST Reconciliation (Clause 44) 
Clause 44, added by a recent amendment, requires the tax auditor to provide a reconciliation 
between the total turnover or gross receipts reported in the income tax return and the total 
turnover reported in GST returns (GSTR-1 and GSTR-3B) for the year. This reconciliation must 
explain all differences arising from: advance receipts, exempt supplies, zero-rated exports, non-
GST supplies, and any other reconciling items. 
Clause 44 has become one of the most labour-intensive clauses for tax auditors since it 
requires co-ordination with the GST consultant and reconciliation of two different tax returns 
prepared under two different accounting systems.

Frequently Asked Questions 
Q1. The CA found an error in the Form 3CD after filing the tax audit report. What is the 
process to revise it? 
A revised tax audit report (revised Form 3CD) can be filed before the ITR due date. The CA 
uploads the revised form with a note in the report explaining the reason for revision. If the 
error is discovered after the ITR due date, a revised ITR along with the revised audit report 
can be filed before December 31 of the assessment year. Both the revised audit report and 
the revised ITR must be consistent with each other. 
Q2. Clause 34 shows that TDS was not deducted on a Rs. 10 lakh payment to a 
contractor. What is the consequence? 
Non-deduction of TDS on a payment that required TDS under Section 194C results in 30% 
of that payment being disallowed as a deduction under Section 40(a)(ia). So Rs. 3 lakh of 
the Rs. 10 lakh payment is added back to taxable income. Additionally, the taxpayer is 
treated as an 'assessee in default' for the TDS amount and interest under Section 201(1A) is 
payable. The TDS disallowance is permanent unless the payee can demonstrate that they 
have paid tax on that income (in which case disallowance under 40(a)(ia) is not triggered). 
---PAGE 42---
Q3. What is the consequence of reporting an impermissible avoidance arrangement 
under Clause 30C? 
Disclosure under Clause 30C puts the Income Tax Department on notice about the 
arrangement. The GAAR provisions (Sections 95 to 102) allow the Commissioner of Income 
Tax (GAAR) to examine the arrangement and if found impermissible, disregard it and 
recompute the tax consequences. GAAR can apply to any arrangement where the main 
purpose is to obtain a tax benefit and the arrangement lacks commercial substance. 
Voluntary disclosure in Clause 30C (rather than concealment) is expected to be treated 
more favourably during any GAAR examination. 
Q4. My business has no international transactions. Do I need to fill in Clause 30D? 
If your business has no international transactions (as defined under Section 92B) or 
specified domestic transactions (Section 92BA), you declare 'Not Applicable' for those sub-
clauses of Clause 30D. However, the CA must verify through the books and related party 
schedules that no such transactions have occurred. Transactions with foreign group 
companies or overseas subsidiaries, even if not substantial, may trigger the international 
transaction definition. 
Q5. Our GST turnover and income tax turnover differ by Rs. 12 lakh due to an advance 
received. How is this handled in Clause 44? 
Differences between GST and income tax turnover arise frequently from advances, exempt 
supplies, exports, and timing differences. In Clause 44, the auditor is required to prepare a 
reconciliation explaining each difference item. An advance receipt (which may be included in 
GST turnover as supply has been deemed to occur but may not yet be income for income 
tax purposes) would be listed as a reconciling item in Clause 44 with the amount and 
reason. Proper reconciliation documentation reduces the risk of the IT Department treating 
the difference as undisclosed income.
