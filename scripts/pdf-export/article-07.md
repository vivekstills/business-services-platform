## Standard Deduction — What It Is, Who Gets It, and How It Works

### One Deduction That Requires Zero Documentation

In a tax system that often feels like a paperwork marathon -- investment receipts, medical bills, rent agreements, insurance premium certificates -- the Standard Deduction stands out as the most refreshingly simple benefit available to Indian taxpayers. No investment required. No bill to submit. No form to fill separately. Just a flat deduction that reduces your taxable salary automatically.

Yet many salaried employees do not fully understand what it covers, how much they actually save, or whether it applies to them in the regime they have chosen.

## What Is the Standard Deduction?

The Standard Deduction is a flat deduction from gross salary income (and pension income) that the Income Tax Act allows without requiring the taxpayer to produce any supporting documentation. It was reintroduced in Budget 2018 at Rs. 40,000, enhanced to Rs. 50,000 in Budget 2019, and further revised to Rs. 75,000 in Budget 2024 (effective from FY 2024-25) under the new tax regime.

The deduction serves as a proxy for work-related expenses that a salaried employee incurs but cannot typically itemise -- commuting costs, home office expenses, work clothing, and so on.

## Standard Deduction Across Regimes and Years

| Financial Year | Old Regime | New Regime |
| --- | --- | --- |
| FY 2018-19 | Rs. 40,000 | Not applicable (new regime did not exist) |
| FY 2019-20 to 2022-23 | Rs. 50,000 | Not available initially |
| FY 2023-24 | Rs. 50,000 | Rs. 50,000 (introduced in Budget 2023) |
| FY 2024-25 | Rs. 50,000 | Rs. 75,000 |
| FY 2025-26 | Rs. 50,000 | Rs. 75,000 |

Key Takeaway: From FY 2024-25 onwards, the new regime offers a higher standard deduction (Rs. 75,000) than the old regime (Rs. 50,000), adding another reason for salaried individuals to consider the new regime.

## Who Is Eligible for the Standard Deduction?

| Category | Eligible? | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Salaried employees (private sector) | Yes | Auto-applied by employer in Form 16 |
| Salaried government employees | Yes | Applied in salary computation |
| Pensioners (receiving pension from former employer) | Yes | Available on pension income |
| Family pension recipients | Partial | Exempt: Rs. 15,000 or 1/3rd of pension, whichever is lower |
| Freelancers/self-employed | No | They fall under PGBP, not Salary |
| NRIs receiving salary in India | Yes | If salary is earned in India |
| Working partners in a firm drawing salary | Situation-specific | Only if salary qualifies as "salary" under IT Act |

## What Did Standard Deduction Replace?

Before its reintroduction in 2018, the following allowances existed:

| Old Allowance | What It Covered | Annual Limit |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Medical Reimbursement Allowance | Doctor bills submitted by employee | Rs. 15,000 |
| Transport Allowance | Commuting expenses | Rs. 19,200 |
| Total | -- | Rs. 34,200 |

The standard deduction of Rs. 40,000 was introduced partly to compensate for the removal of these allowances and simplify compliance. At Rs. 75,000 now, it significantly exceeds what the old allowances offered.

## Tax Saving from Standard Deduction — What Does It Actually Mean?

The deduction itself is not the saving -- the tax saved is. Here is how to calculate the actual monetary benefit:

| Taxable Income Slab | Applicable Tax Rate | Tax Saved (on Rs. 75,000 deduction) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Rs. 4 lakh to Rs. 8 lakh (new regime) | 5% | Rs. 3,750 |
| Rs. 8 lakh to Rs. 12 lakh (new regime) | 10% | Rs. 7,500 |
| Rs. 12 lakh to Rs. 16 lakh (new regime) | 15% | Rs. 11,250 |
| Rs. 16 lakh to Rs. 20 lakh (new regime) | 20% | Rs. 15,000 |
| Above Rs. 24 lakh (new regime) | 30% | Rs. 22,500 |

The higher your income bracket, the more rupees the standard deduction saves you.

## Practical Example

Scenario: Aryan, salaried engineer, FY 2025-26

Aryan's CTC is Rs. 14 lakh. He opts for the new tax regime.

| Computation Step | Amount (Rs.) |
| --- | --- |
| Gross CTC | 14,00,000 |
| Less: Standard Deduction | 75,000 |
| Net Taxable Income | 13,25,000 |
| Tax on Rs. 13,25,000 (at new regime slabs) | Approx. Rs. 1,18,750 |
| If no standard deduction (on Rs. 14 lakh) | Approx. Rs. 1,30,000 |
| Tax Saved by Standard Deduction | Rs. 11,250 |

Without any documentation, Aryan saves Rs. 11,250 in taxes simply because the standard deduction reduced his taxable income by Rs. 75,000.

## How Is Standard Deduction Applied?

For salaried employees, the employer applies the standard deduction automatically while computing TDS on salary. It appears in Form 16 Part B as a deduction from gross salary, and it is pre-filled in ITR-1 and ITR-2 on the income tax portal once you link your Form 16.

You do not need to claim it separately. You do not need to remember to tick a box. It happens by default.

## Important Notes

If a person has more than one employer during the year (for example, they changed jobs), each employer may apply the standard deduction to their own salary computation. However, at the time of ITR filing, the taxpayer is entitled to only one standard deduction of Rs. 75,000 (or Rs. 50,000 under the old regime) for the entire year, irrespective of how many employers they had. If both employers have independently applied it, an adjustment must be made in the final ITR computation.

For pensioners: the standard deduction is available on pension received from a former employer (treated as salary). However, pension received from a bank (as a pension disbursement agent) still qualifies. The deduction is the same -- Rs. 75,000 (new regime) or Rs. 50,000 (old regime).

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

Q1. I switched jobs midway through the year. My first employer applied a standard deduction of Rs. 75,000 and so did my second. What should I do when filing my ITR? When you file your ITR, you must combine the salary from both employers and apply the standard deduction only once (Rs. 75,000 under the new regime). The pre-filled form may show an inflated figure if both employers applied it. Manually correct the standard deduction to Rs. 75,000 in the salary schedule. Any excess tax paid (due to the double deduction at employer level being corrected in the ITR) will result in a refund.

Q2. I am a retired IAS officer receiving pension of Rs. 80,000 per month. Am I eligible for the standard deduction? Yes. Pension received from a former government employer is treated as salary under the Income Tax Act and is eligible for the standard deduction of Rs. 75,000 (under the new regime) or Rs. 50,000 (under the old regime). In your ITR, you would show your pension of Rs. 9,60,000 (Rs. 80,000 x 12) as salary income and deduct Rs. 75,000 as standard deduction, making your net taxable salary Rs. 8,85,000.

Q3. I work as an independent consultant and invoice my clients. Can I claim the standard deduction on my professional income? No. The standard deduction is exclusively available against income taxable under the head "Salaries." As a consultant, your income is classified under "Profits and Gains from Business or Profession" (PGBP). Under PGBP, you can claim actual business expenses incurred, or opt for presumptive taxation under Section 44ADA (where 50% of gross receipts is automatically treated as your income, implicitly covering expenses).

Q4. I have a part-time job where I earn Rs. 3 lakh per year, and I also do freelancing that earns me Rs. 2 lakh. Do I get the standard deduction on the combined Rs. 5 lakh? You get the standard deduction only on the Rs. 3 lakh from your part-time job (salary income). The Rs. 2 lakh from freelancing is PGBP income and does not qualify for the standard deduction. In your ITR, you will report Rs. 3 lakh under Salaries (with Rs. 75,000 standard deduction, making net salary Rs. 2.25 lakh) and Rs. 2 lakh separately under PGBP.

Q5. My employer has already deducted TDS based on the standard deduction of Rs. 75,000. Do I need to do anything extra at the time of filing my ITR? Nothing extra is required specifically for the standard deduction. Your employer has correctly factored it in while computing TDS, and it will appear in your Form 16. When you file ITR-1 or ITR-2, the pre-filled salary data will show the standard deduction already applied. Simply verify that the figure is Rs. 75,000 (or Rs. 50,000 if you are under the old regime), confirm the remaining details are correct, and proceed to file.
