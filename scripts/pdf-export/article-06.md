## Income Tax Slabs for FY 2025-26 — New Regime vs. Old Regime

### What Changed in Budget 2025 and What It Means for Your Wallet

Tax slab announcements in the Union Budget are one of the most widely watched events in India's financial calendar. For FY 2025-26, Finance Minister Nirmala Sitharaman introduced significant changes to the new tax regime, making it even more attractive for a majority of individual taxpayers -- while the old regime continues to exist for those who prefer to claim deductions.

If you have been filing under the old regime because of your home loan, PPF, and insurance deductions, this may be the year to run the numbers again.

## Tax Regimes in India — An Overview

India currently operates two parallel income tax regimes for individuals:

| Feature | Old Regime | New Regime (Default) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Tax slabs | Fewer slabs, higher rates at lower income levels | More slabs, lower rates across the board |
| Deductions (80C, 80D, HRA, etc.) | Available | Not available (except specific ones) |
| Standard deduction | Rs. 50,000 (salaried) | Rs. 75,000 (salaried, from FY 2024-25) |
| Default status | Opt-in required | Default for all taxpayers |
| Surcharge on income above Rs. 5 crore | 37% | 25% (capped) |
| Best for | High deduction claimers | Those with minimal deductions |

## New Tax Regime Slabs for FY 2025-26 (AY 2026-27)

The Budget 2025 revised the new regime slabs significantly:

| Income Slab | Tax Rate |
| --- | --- |
| Up to Rs. 4,00,000 | Nil |
| Rs. 4,00,001 to Rs. 8,00,000 | 5% |
| Rs. 8,00,001 to Rs. 12,00,000 | 10% |
| Rs. 12,00,001 to Rs. 16,00,000 | 15% |
| Rs. 16,00,001 to Rs. 20,00,000 | 20% |
| Rs. 20,00,001 to Rs. 24,00,000 | 25% |
| Above Rs. 24,00,000 | 30% |

Key Benefit: Under Section 87A, individuals with taxable income up to Rs. 12 lakh (after standard deduction) effectively pay zero tax under the new regime. Salaried individuals with income up to Rs. 12.75 lakh (Rs. 12 lakh + Rs. 75,000 standard deduction) also pay zero tax.

## Old Tax Regime Slabs for FY 2025-26

The old regime slabs remain unchanged:

| Income Slab | Tax Rate (Below 60 Years) |
| --- | --- |
| Up to Rs. 2,50,000 | Nil |
| Rs. 2,50,001 to Rs. 5,00,000 | 5% |
| Rs. 5,00,001 to Rs. 10,00,000 | 20% |
| Above Rs. 10,00,000 | 30% |

For senior citizens (60 to 79 years): basic exemption is Rs. 3 lakh For super senior citizens (80 years and above): basic exemption is Rs. 5 lakh

## Surcharge on High Income (Both Regimes)

| Income Range | Old Regime Surcharge | New Regime Surcharge |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Rs. 50 lakh to Rs. 1 crore | 10% | 10% |
| Rs. 1 crore to Rs. 2 crore | 15% | 15% |
| Rs. 2 crore to Rs. 5 crore | 25% | 25% |
| Above Rs. 5 crore | 37% | 25% (capped) |

Health and Education Cess: 4% on (tax + surcharge) -- applicable under both regimes.

## Which Regime Is Better? A Comparison

The answer depends on the deductions you can legitimately claim. Here is a quick break-even analysis:

| Annual Income | Old Regime Tax (with deductions of Rs. 3L) | New Regime Tax | Verdict |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Rs. 6 lakh | ~Rs. 0 (87A rebate) | Rs. 0 (87A rebate) | Tie |
| Rs. 10 lakh | ~Rs. 54,600 | ~Rs. 52,000 | New regime slightly better |
| Rs. 12 lakh | ~Rs. 78,000 | Rs. 0 (87A rebate, salaried) | New regime wins decisively |
| Rs. 15 lakh | ~Rs. 1,29,480 | ~Rs. 1,05,000 | New regime wins |
| Rs. 20 lakh | ~Rs. 3,37,400 (with Rs. 3L deductions) | ~Rs. 2,73,000 | New regime better |
| Rs. 30 lakh | ~Rs. 6,78,600 (with Rs. 4L deductions) | ~Rs. 4,75,000 | New regime better |

The old regime typically wins only when deductions (80C + 80D + HRA + home loan interest + NPS) are very high -- often Rs. 4.5 lakh to Rs. 5 lakh and above for income in the Rs. 15-20 lakh range.

## Practical Example: Comparing Tax at Rs. 15 Lakh Income

Neha, 35, works at a private firm. Gross salary Rs. 15 lakh.

Under New Regime:

- Gross salary: Rs. 15,00,000
- Less standard deduction: Rs. 75,000
- Taxable income: Rs. 14,25,000
- Tax: Nil on Rs. 4L + 5% on Rs. 4L (Rs. 20,000) + 10% on Rs. 4L (Rs. 40,000) + 15% on Rs. 2.25L (Rs. 33,750)
- Total tax: Rs. 93,750
- Cess @ 4%: Rs. 3,750
- Total: Rs. 97,500

Under Old Regime (with Rs. 3.5 lakh deductions: 80C Rs. 1.5L, 80D Rs. 25K, HRA Rs. 1.5L, standard deduction Rs. 50K):

- Taxable income: Rs. 15L minus Rs. 3.5L = Rs. 11.5L
- Tax: Rs. 1,12,500 on income above Rs. 10L + earlier slabs
- Total (approximate with cess): Rs. 1,29,000

New regime saves Neha Rs. 31,500 here. She should switch.

## Deductions Still Available Under the New Regime

People assume the new regime has no deductions at all. That is not entirely true:

| Deduction/Exemption | Available in New Regime? |
| --- | --- |
| Standard deduction (Rs. 75,000) | Yes |
| NPS employer contribution (Section 80CCD(2)) | Yes (up to 14% of salary for government employees, 10% for others) |
| Gratuity exemption (Section 10(10)) | Yes |
| Leave encashment exemption (Section 10(10AA)) | Yes |
| HRA exemption | No |
| Section 80C (PPF, ELSS, LIC, PF) | No |
| Section 80D (health insurance) | No |
| Home loan interest (Section 24b) | No (except let-out property) |
| Section 80G (donations) | No |

## Important Notes

Salaried employees can switch between old and new regime every year. Self-employed individuals and business owners can switch only once (from new to old), and if they switch back to new, they cannot switch again (with limited exceptions).

The new regime is the default from FY 2024-25. If you want the old regime, you must explicitly opt for it while filing your ITR or informing your employer via Form 10-IEA.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

Q1. I have a home loan with Rs. 2 lakh interest outgoing and invest Rs. 1.5 lakh in PPF every year. Should I still shift to the new regime? Run the numbers for your specific income. With Rs. 1.5 lakh (80C) + Rs. 2 lakh (home loan interest) + Rs. 75,000 (standard deduction under old regime) = Rs. 4.25 lakh in deductions, the old regime might still be competitive for incomes around Rs. 12-18 lakh. However, for incomes of Rs. 12 lakh or below, the Section 87A rebate under the new regime makes tax zero regardless, so the old regime rarely wins there.

Q2. My employer is deducting TDS under the new regime by default. Can I claim the old regime benefit at the time of ITR filing? Yes. You can opt for the old regime at the time of filing your ITR, even if your employer defaulted to the new regime for TDS. However, this means you may have had excess TDS deducted, which will be refunded when your return is processed. For efficiency, it is better to inform your employer at the start of the year and submit Form 10-IEA or a declaration so they adjust TDS accordingly.

Q3. I am a retired government employee receiving pension. Do both regimes apply to me? Yes. Pension from a former employer is taxed as salary. Retired individuals can choose either regime. Since retirees often do not have large 80C investments or HRA, the new regime frequently works out better for them. However, if you receive significant interest income and are above 60, the higher basic exemption (Rs. 3 lakh) under the old regime and the Rs. 50,000 deduction under 80TTB for interest should be factored in.

Q4. I earned Rs. 12 lakh in salary but also had Rs. 50,000 in FD interest. Does the Section 87A rebate still apply? Your total income (salary + FD interest) is Rs. 12.5 lakh. After the standard deduction of Rs. 75,000, taxable income is Rs. 11.75 lakh. Since this is below Rs. 12 lakh, the full rebate under Section 87A is available, and your tax is nil under the new regime. The FD interest is included in total income for this purpose.

Q5. Are NRI taxpayers eligible for the new tax regime? Yes, NRIs can opt for the new tax regime. However, certain exemptions specifically applicable to residents (like the basic exemption limit for senior citizens and super senior citizens, and Section 80TTA/80TTB) are not available to NRIs regardless of regime. The Section 87A rebate is also not available to NRIs, which is a significant difference from resident taxpayers.
