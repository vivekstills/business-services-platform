## Income from Other Sources — The Residual Head Explained

**The Tax Net That Catches Everything Else**

Four heads of income exist to cover specific economic activity: employment, property ownership, business activity, and investment sales. But what about interest from a bank deposit? What about money received as a gift? What about the cash prize you won in a quiz contest? Or the dividend your shares paid last quarter?

All of these find a home under the fifth and final head: Income from Other Sources (IFOS). It is the most versatile and often misunderstood of the five heads, because almost any income not covered elsewhere ends up here. Understanding it helps ensure you do not accidentally omit something from your return.

## Legal Basis

Income from Other Sources is governed by Sections 56 to 59 of the Income Tax Act, 1961. Section 56 begins with a clear principle: "Income of every kind which is not to be excluded from the total income under this Act shall be chargeable to income-tax under the head 'Income from Other Sources,' if it is not chargeable to income-tax under any of the heads specified in Section 14, items A to E."

In simpler terms: if it does not belong to Salaries, House Property, PGBP, or Capital Gains, it comes here.

## What Falls Under Income from Other Sources?

### Category A: Always Taxable Under IFOS

| Type of Income | Tax Rate |
| --- | --- |
| Interest on savings account | Slab rates (deduction u/s 80TTA up to Rs. 10,000) |
| Interest on fixed deposits (FDs) | Slab rates |
| Interest on recurring deposits (RDs) | Slab rates |
| Interest on NSC, bonds, debentures | Slab rates |
| Dividend from shares (domestic or foreign) | Slab rates (post FY 2020-21) |
| Family pension received after pensioner's death | Slab rates (1/3rd or Rs. 15,000 exempt, whichever is lower) |
| Income from subletting (not property owner) | Slab rates |
| Agricultural income from foreign country | Slab rates |
| Ground rent | Slab rates |

### Category B: Specifically Included Under Section 56(2)

These are incomes that the law specifically brings under IFOS to prevent tax avoidance:

| Income Type | Section | Key Condition | Tax Rate |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Gifts (cash or kind) from non-relatives | 56(2)(x) | Exceeds Rs. 50,000 in aggregate | Slab rates on full amount |
| Immovable property received without consideration | 56(2)(x) | Stamp value exceeds Rs. 50,000 | Slab rates on stamp value |
| Shares received at below fair market value (by firm/company) | 56(2)(viib) | FMV exceeds issue price | Excess taxed in issuer's hands |
| Lottery, gambling, card games, horse races | 56(2)(ib) | Any amount | 30% flat (no basic exemption) |
| Online gaming winnings | 115BBJ | Applicable from FY 2023-24 | 30% flat |
| Interest from income tax refund | 56 | Any amount | Slab rates |
| Insurance maturity proceeds (taxable portion) | 56 | When Section 10(10D) exemption is not available | Slab rates |

## The Gift Tax Rules Explained

Gift taxation under Section 56(2)(x) is one of the most frequently misunderstood areas. Here is a structured breakdown:

### What Is a "Gift" for Tax Purposes?

Any money, immovable property, or movable property received without consideration (or for inadequate consideration) is treated as a gift. The tax implications:

| Gift Amount (Aggregate in FY) | Tax Treatment |
| --- | --- |
| Up to Rs. 50,000 from all sources | Fully exempt |
| Above Rs. 50,000 (cash) | Full amount taxable (not just the excess) |
| Immovable property at below stamp duty value | Difference between stamp value and consideration is taxable |
| Movable property at below FMV | Difference between FMV and consideration is taxable |

### Who Is a "Relative" (No Gift Tax Among Relatives)?

The following persons are relatives for gift tax exemption purposes:

- Spouse
- Siblings (brothers and sisters of the individual)
- Siblings of spouse
- Siblings of parents
- Lineal ascendants/descendants and their spouses (parents, grandparents, children, grandchildren)

### Example

Vikram receives Rs. 70,000 in cash from his college friend (not a relative) as a wedding gift. The entire Rs. 70,000 is taxable under IFOS at slab rates (not just the Rs. 20,000 excess over the Rs. 50,000 threshold). However, if the same Rs. 70,000 came from his maternal uncle, it would be exempt because the maternal uncle is the sibling of a parent and hence a relative.

## Deductions Available Under Income from Other Sources

Unlike PGBP, which allows a wide range of deductions, IFOS has a limited set:

| Deduction | Applicable To | Limit |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Section 80TTA | Interest on savings bank account (individuals and HUFs) | Up to Rs. 10,000 |
| Section 80TTB | Interest on all deposits (senior citizens 60+) | Up to Rs. 50,000 |
| Commission or remuneration for collecting interest on securities | Individuals earning interest as business activity | Actual amount |
| Repairs, depreciation, insurance for plant/machinery/furniture let on hire | Owners who earn hire charges | Actual (reasonable) |

No deduction is available against lottery winnings, online gaming, or gambling income. These are taxed gross at 30% with no expense deduction allowed.

## Special Tax Rates Under Income from Other Sources

Certain IFOS income is taxed at a flat rate rather than slab rates:

| Income Type | Flat Rate | Surcharge/Cess Applicable? |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Lottery/puzzle winnings | 30% | Yes |
| Horse racing income | 30% | Yes |
| Online gaming winnings (Section 115BBJ) | 30% | Yes |
| Card game / other gambling | 30% | Yes |

No basic exemption benefit: If your only income is Rs. 3 lakh from a lottery win and you are below 60, you cannot claim the Rs. 2.5 lakh basic exemption against lottery income. The Rs. 3 lakh is taxed at 30% flat on the gross amount.

## Practical Example: Full Computation

Sumit, age 35, has the following income in FY 2025-26:

| Source | Amount |
| --- | --- |
| Salary (after standard deduction) | Rs. 9,00,000 |
| Interest on FD (HDFC Bank) | Rs. 40,000 |
| Interest on savings account (SBI) | Rs. 12,000 |
| Dividend from Infosys shares | Rs. 8,000 |
| Cash received from friend as gift | Rs. 60,000 |
| Total IFOS income | Rs. 1,20,000 |

- FD interest: Rs. 40,000 -- taxable at slab rates
- Savings account interest: Rs. 12,000 -- taxable, but Rs. 10,000 exempt under 80TTA; net taxable: Rs. 2,000
- Dividend: Rs. 8,000 -- taxable at slab rates
- Gift from friend: Rs. 60,000 -- full amount taxable (exceeds Rs. 50,000 threshold)
- Total IFOS taxable: Rs. 1,10,000 (after 80TTA deduction)

This Rs. 1,10,000 is added to his salary of Rs. 9 lakh, and the combined income is taxed at applicable slab rates.

## Important Notes

Since FY 2020-21, dividends are taxable in the hands of the shareholder (not the company). Companies deduct TDS at 10% before paying dividends if the annual amount exceeds Rs. 5,000. You must declare the gross dividend in your ITR and claim credit for TDS.

Family pension (pension received by the family of a deceased government employee) is specifically taxed under IFOS -- not under Salaries. A standard deduction of one-third of the pension or Rs. 15,000, whichever is lower, is available.

## Frequently Asked Questions (FAQs)

Q1. My parents gifted me Rs. 5 lakh for buying furniture. Is this taxable? Gifts received from parents are fully exempt regardless of the amount, because parents are specifically listed as relatives under Section 56(2)(x). You do not need to disclose this in your ITR as taxable income. However, it is good practice to keep a gift deed as documentation.

Q2. I won Rs. 1 lakh in an online fantasy sports platform. How is this taxed, and was TDS deducted? Online gaming winnings, including fantasy sports, are taxed at 30% flat under Section 115BBJ from FY 2023-24. The platform is required to deduct TDS at 30% on net winnings (winnings minus entry fees, where applicable) under Section 194BA. You must declare the gross winnings in ITR and claim TDS credit.

Q3. I received Rs. 8,000 monthly rent from my tenant as a subletting arrangement (I am a tenant myself, not the owner). Is this house property income? No. Income from subletting is specifically excluded from the House Property head because you are not the owner. It is taxable under Income from Other Sources at your applicable slab rate. You may deduct the rent you yourself pay for the subletting portion while computing this income.

Q4. My PPF account matured this year and I received Rs. 12 lakh. Is this income from other sources? No. Maturity proceeds from a Public Provident Fund (PPF) account are fully exempt under Section 10(11). This amount does not appear in your ITR as income at all, neither under IFOS nor any other head.

Q5. I received a gold chain worth Rs. 45,000 as a Diwali gift from my employer. Is this under IFOS or under Salary? Gifts from an employer are taxed as a perquisite under the Salary head (Section 17(2)), not under IFOS. However, there is an annual exemption of Rs. 5,000 for gifts received from an employer. So the first Rs. 5,000 of the gold chain's value is exempt, and Rs. 40,000 would be added to your taxable salary as a perquisite.
