ARTICLE 1 OF 4  ·  GST COMPLIANCE 
TDS Under GST for the Metal Scrap Industry 
📅 Updated: May 2026 📅 10 min read 📅 FY 2025–26 
 
ARTICLE SUMMARY 
From October 10, 2024, the government mandated TDS under GST specifically for the 
metal scrap industry to curb rampant tax evasion. Registered buyers of metal scrap must 
deduct 2% TDS (1% CGST + 1% SGST or 2% IGST) at the time of payment and file 
GSTR-7. This article explains the legal basis, who must deduct, registration requirements, 
GSTR-7 filing, and the full compliance framework with worked examples. 
 
Background: Why Metal Scrap Was Specifically Targeted  
The metal scrap industry in India is one of the most fragmented and unorganised sectors of the 
economy. The supply chain involves primary scrap generators (factories, dismantlers, 
construction sites), aggregators, dealers, and recycling manufacturers. This complex, multi-
layered chain has historically been fertile ground for tax evasion — through fake invoices, 
suppression of turnover, circular trading, and misclassification of goods. 
Large volumes of cash transactions, the absence of documentation for small lots, and 
widespread registration gaps allowed significant GST revenue to escape the tax net. The 
government estimated that annual tax leakage from the metal scrap sector alone ran into 
thousands of crores. To address this, the GST Council, in its 54th meeting held on September 
9, 2024, recommended extending the TDS mechanism under Section 51 of the CGST Act to 
cover all registered buyers of metal scrap — not just government entities. 
Pursuant to this recommendation, the Central Board of Indirect Taxes and Customs (CBIC) 
issued Notification No. 25/2024–Central Tax dated October 9, 2024, making TDS under GST 
applicable with effect from October 10, 2024. 
 
📅 Key Legal Reference 
CBIC Notification No. 25/2024–Central Tax, dated October 9, 2024. Applicable from October 10, 
2024. This notification amends the list of persons who are required to deduct TDS under Section 
51 of the CGST Act, 2017 to include all registered persons purchasing metal scrap from 
registered suppliers. 
 
Who Must Deduct TDS — Scope of Applicability 
Prior to this notification, TDS under GST under Section 51 was limited to government 
departments, local authorities, governmental agencies, and Public Sector Undertakings (PSUs). 
The amendment expanded this to include ALL registered persons in the context of metal scrap 
purchases — a significant widening of the compliance obligation.

The Rs. 2.5 lakh threshold applies per contract per supplier. It is not a monthly or annual 
aggregate. Each distinct contract (or purchase order) for metal scrap is assessed 
independently. If a single transaction exceeds Rs. 2.5 lakh, TDS applies on the entire taxable 
value of that transaction. If the transaction is Rs. 2 lakh, no TDS applies even if cumulative 
purchases from the same supplier are higher. 
 
TDS Rate, HSN Chapters, and Tax Mechanics 
The TDS rate under the metal scrap provision is 2% of the taxable value (excluding GST). In the 
case of intra-state supplies, this is split as 1% CGST and 1% SGST/UTGST. For inter-state 
supplies, it is 2% IGST. The TDS is not calculated on the gross invoice value (which includes 
GST) — it is computed only on the base taxable value.

Metal scrap covered includes goods under HSN Chapter 72 (Iron and Steel scrap), Chapter 74 
(Copper scrap), Chapter 75 (Nickel scrap), Chapter 76 (Aluminium scrap), Chapter 79 (Zinc 
scrap), Chapter 80 (Tin scrap), and other related metallic waste chapters. The key classification 
test is whether the goods constitute 'waste and residue' from manufacturing or processing of 
metals — not new/virgin metal. 
 
TDS Registration — A Separate Compliance Identity 
Every registered buyer who falls within the TDS obligation must obtain a separate TDS 
registration under the GST law, distinct from their regular GSTIN for business. This is done by 
---PAGE 4---
filing Form GST REG-07 on the GST portal (www.gst.gov.in). The TDS registration GSTIN will 
have '51' in positions 3–4 of the GSTIN, differentiating it from a regular business GSTIN. 
This separate TDS GSTIN is used exclusively for TDS-related activities — filing GSTR-7, 
depositing TDS amounts, and issuing TDS certificates (GSTR-7A). It cannot be used for 
claiming input tax credit or for issuing tax invoices for regular business supply. 
📅 Important 
Failure to obtain TDS registration while being liable to deduct TDS attracts penalties under 
Section 122 of the CGST Act, 2017. The penalty can be up to Rs. 10,000 or the amount of tax 
evaded, whichever is higher. Buyers who commenced scrap purchases after October 10, 2024 
must have applied for TDS registration before making the first purchase covered under this 
provision. 
 
GSTR-7: Filing Requirements and Due Dates 
GSTR-7 is the monthly return to be filed by every person registered under the TDS provisions. It 
contains details of all TDS deductions made during the month — supplier GSTIN, invoice 
details, taxable value, and TDS amounts. Filing GSTR-7 is mandatory even for months where 
no TDS deduction was made, if the registration is active.

You do not need state-wise TDS registrations. This simplifies compliance significantly for multi-
location manufacturers and traders.

Frequently Asked Questions 
Q1. Is TDS under GST applicable if the metal scrap supplier is unregistered under GST? 
No. The TDS mechanism under Section 51 of the CGST Act applies only when both the 
buyer (deductor) and the supplier (deductee) are registered persons under GST. If the 
supplier is unregistered, the TDS under GST obligation does not apply to that transaction. 
However, the buyer may still have a Reverse Charge Mechanism (RCM) obligation for 
purchases from unregistered suppliers under specific GST notifications, if applicable. 
Q2. Is TDS deducted on the full invoice amount including GST, or only on the base 
value? 
TDS under GST is deducted only on the taxable value (base price), not on the GST 
component. If an invoice is for Rs. 5,00,000 taxable value plus Rs. 90,000 GST (total Rs. 
5,90,000), TDS is 2% of Rs. 5,00,000 = Rs. 10,000 only. The GST amount is never included 
in the TDS calculation base. This is expressly provided in the statutory provision and 
departmental FAQs. 
Q3. Can the supplier (scrap seller) claim a refund if TDS credit exceeds their GST 
liability? 
Yes. Under Section 54 of the CGST Act, if the TDS credit accumulated in the Electronic 
Cash Ledger of the supplier exceeds the GST payable for the month or year, the excess 
amount is refundable. The supplier must apply for a refund by filing Form RFD-01 on the 
GST portal. The refund is processed after verification of the GSTR-7 filed by the buyer 
matching the supplier's claim. 
Q4. What if a manufacturer has multiple small purchases from the same scrap dealer, 
each below Rs. 2.5 lakh? 
The Rs. 2.5 lakh threshold applies per contract or per transaction, not on a cumulative basis. 
If each individual invoice or purchase order from the same dealer is below Rs. 2.5 lakh, TDS 
is technically not applicable on each such transaction even if annual purchases are much 
higher. However, if a single purchase order is structured across multiple invoices to avoid 
the threshold, this may be treated as tax avoidance and attract scrutiny. 
Q5. If both buyer and seller are outside India (say, a foreign buyer of Indian scrap), does 
TDS apply? 
---PAGE 7---
TDS under GST under Section 51 applies to supplies made within India (domestic supplies). 
If a foreign buyer (non-resident) is purchasing and exporting metal scrap from a registered 
Indian supplier, the transaction is treated as an export (zero-rated supply under GST). TDS 
under GST would not apply in this scenario as the foreign buyer is not a registered person 
under Indian GST law. However, normal export documentation and LUT/refund mechanisms 
apply to the Indian supplier.

ARTICLE 2 OF 4  ·  IT PORTAL GUIDE 
How To Make Tax Payment On The Income Tax 
Portal Without Logging In 
📅 Updated: May 2026 📅 8 min read 📅 AY 2026–27 
 
ARTICLE SUMMARY 
You do not need to log in to the Income Tax portal to pay your taxes. Using the e-Pay Tax 
facility, you can make advance tax, self-assessment tax, TDS deposits, and other 
payments using Challan 280 or 281 directly. This guide provides a complete step-by-step 
walkthrough of the process, explains all available payment modes, how to correctly fill in 
assessment details, how to download the acknowledgment, and what to do if something 
goes wrong. 
 
Overview: The e-Pay Tax Facility 
The Income Tax Department's portal (www.incometax.gov.in) offers a guest payment facility 
called 'e-Pay Tax' that allows taxpayers to make tax payments without a registered login ID or 
password. This facility is particularly useful in situations where a taxpayer needs to make an 
urgent payment but has not completed registration on the portal, wants to pay on behalf of 
another person, has a login that is locked, or simply wants to skip the login process for routine 
payments. 
The authentication for this guest facility is done through OTP verification on the mobile number 
registered against the taxpayer's PAN or TAN in the Income Tax records. This ensures that only 
the actual taxpayer (or someone with access to their registered mobile) can initiate the payment, 
maintaining a basic security layer without requiring full portal login.

Step-by-Step Guide: Paying Tax Without Login 
Step 1 — Navigate to e-Pay Tax 
Open your web browser and visit www.incometax.gov.in (the official Income Tax of India portal). 
On the home page, look for the 'Quick Links' section. You will find the option 'e-Pay Tax' listed 
there. Alternatively, on the home page header, hover over the 'Our Services' tab and select 'e-
Pay Tax' from the dropdown. This directly opens the guest payment gateway — no login 
required. 
Step 2 — Enter PAN and Verify Identity 
On the e-Pay Tax page, enter your PAN (Permanent Account Number) in the first field. Re-
confirm the PAN in the second field. Enter your mobile number registered with the Income Tax 
Department for this PAN. Click 'Continue'. The portal sends a 6-digit One-Time Password (OTP) 
to your registered mobile number. Enter the OTP within the stipulated time (usually 15 minutes) 
and click 'Continue' to proceed. 
Step 3 — Select Tax Payment Category 
After OTP verification, you are taken to a dashboard showing various tax payment categories as 
tiles or cards. For regular income tax payments (advance tax, self-assessment tax), click on 
'Income Tax'. For TDS/TCS deposits, select the appropriate category. Each tile clearly mentions 
the applicable challan number. 
Step 4 — Fill in Assessment Year and Payment Details 
This is the most critical step — incorrect entries here will cause misapplication of your tax 
payment. Select the correct Assessment Year from the dropdown. For taxes relating to income 
of FY 2025–26 (April 2025 to March 2026), the Assessment Year is 2026–27. Then select the 
Type of Payment from the available options (Advance Tax, Self-Assessment Tax, Regular 
Assessment Tax, etc.). 
On the next screen, fill in the individual tax amounts under the respective heads: Basic Tax, 
Surcharge, Health and Education Cess, Interest, Penalty, and Fees. You can calculate these 
amounts beforehand using the IT department's tax calculator or consult your CA. Enter the 
figures carefully and proceed. 
Step 5 — Choose Payment Mode 
The portal offers multiple payment modes. Select the one most convenient for you. The 
available options are described in detail in the Payment Modes section below. 
Step 6 — Review Challan Summary and Confirm 
A summary page displays all the details: PAN, Assessment Year, Type of Payment, Tax 
amounts, and Total amount payable. Carefully review every field before proceeding. Once you 
---PAGE 10---
click 'Confirm', the challan is generated and you are redirected to your bank's payment gateway 
or given NEFT/RTGS details. 
Step 7 — Complete Payment and Download Counterfoil 
Complete the payment on your bank's gateway. After successful payment, the portal generates 
a Challan Counterfoil containing the BSR Code (7-digit bank code), Challan Serial Number, 
Date of Deposit, and Amount Paid. Download and save this counterfoil immediately. This is your 
primary proof of tax payment and is required when filing your ITR. 
 
📅 Important 
Selecting the wrong Assessment Year is the single most common and costly error in tax 
payments. For taxes on FY 2025–26 income, the AY is 2026–27 — NOT 2025–26. Paying under 
the wrong AY causes the payment to be shown as credit in a different year, which may lead to 
demand notices for the actual year and refund claims for the incorrect year — a process that can 
take months to resolve.

Understanding the BSR Code and Challan Counterfoil  
The BSR Code (Basic Statistical Return Code) is a 7-digit number assigned to each bank 
branch authorised to collect taxes. Along with the Challan Serial Number (typically 5 digits) and 
Date of Deposit, the BSR Code uniquely identifies your specific tax payment in the 
government's records. This combination is what the Income Tax Department uses to match your 
payment to your ITR. 
When filing your ITR online, there is a schedule called 'Tax Paid' where you must enter these 
three details: BSR Code, Challan Serial Number, and Date of Deposit. The system validates 
these against the government's database. If any of these details are entered incorrectly in the 
ITR, the payment may not be matched, leading to outstanding demand notices. 
 
How to Verify Your Payment Was Credited 
After making a tax payment, verify it has been correctly credited to the government's account 
before filing your ITR. There are two primary verification methods: 
Method 1 — Form 26AS: Log in to the IT portal (www.incometax.gov.in) and navigate to 
Services > View Form 26AS. Scroll to Part C (Taxes Paid / Collected). Your advance tax or self-
assessment tax payment should appear here within 3 to 5 banking days of the payment date. 
Method 2 — NSDL Challan Status Enquiry: Visit the NSDL TIN website (tin.tin.nsdl.com), click 
on 'Challan Status Enquiry', and enter the BSR Code, Challan Tender Date, Challan Serial 
Number, and Amount. This directly queries the government database and provides real-time 
status. 
 
Correcting Errors in a Filed Challan 
If you discover an error in a challan after payment — such as wrong PAN, wrong Assessment 
Year, wrong head of payment (minor head), or wrong amount — a challan correction can be 
requested within specific time limits. For online payments, the IT portal allows corrections within 
30 days of the payment date. For payments made at bank branches, the correction must be 
requested at the branch within 7 days. 
Minor head correction (e.g., Advance Tax changed to Self-Assessment Tax) and Assessment 
Year correction are the most common. For PAN correction, you may need to visit the Assessing 
Officer. The Challan Correction Mechanism is available under the 'Challan Correction' option in 
the e-Pay Tax section.

Frequently Asked Questions 
Q1. My PAN is not linked to a mobile number. Can I still use e-Pay Tax without login? 
The e-Pay Tax facility requires OTP verification on the mobile number registered against 
your PAN in the IT department's records. If no mobile is registered, you cannot use the 
guest payment route. You would need to either log in to the portal (if registered) or link your 
mobile number to your PAN first. Alternatively, you can visit an authorised bank branch with 
your challan form (physical Form 280) and PAN to pay over the counter without any digital 
authentication. 
Q2. Can I pay advance tax for someone else (e.g., my elderly parent) without their login? 
Yes. You can use the e-Pay Tax facility using the other person's PAN and their PAN-
registered mobile number for OTP verification. The challan will be generated in their PAN's 
name. This is completely legal and there is no restriction on who physically initiates the 
payment — what matters is that the correct PAN, Assessment Year, and tax head are 
entered. The challan counterfoil should be kept by the person whose PAN was used. 
Q3. I paid tax successfully but the portal shows 'Payment Pending'. What should I do? 
If your bank has debited the amount but the IT portal shows 'Payment Pending' or 'Failed', 
first wait 24 hours as processing delays are common. Then check Form 26AS and your 
bank statement. If the bank account is debited but no counterfoil was generated and the 
amount does not appear in 26AS within 5 banking days, raise a complaint with your bank 
citing the transaction reference number. Banks are required to either generate the challan or 
reverse the debit within a reasonable period. 
Q4. Is there a maximum daily limit for tax payments through the e-Pay Tax facility? 
The IT portal itself does not impose an upper daily limit on the total amount payable. 
However, individual payment modes have their own limits — UPI is capped at Rs. 1 lakh per 
transaction by NPCI, debit cards have bank-set daily limits, and net banking also has bank-
imposed limits. For very large payments (crores), RTGS or NEFT are the appropriate modes 
as they have no upper limits and offer same-day credited settlement. 
Q5. If I pay advance tax in installments through multiple challans, do I need a separate 
counterfoil for each? 
---PAGE 13---
Yes. Each tax payment generates a separate challan with its own BSR Code, Challan Serial 
Number, and Date of Deposit. All these details must be entered in the Tax Paid schedule of 
your ITR — one row for each challan. There is no aggregation or linking of challans. The IT 
system validates each challan entry independently against its records, so maintaining all 
counterfoils is essential for accurate ITR filing.

ARTICLE 3 OF 4  ·  TAX COLLECTED AT SOURCE 
Tax Collected at Source (TCS) – Rates, Payment 
and Exemption 
📅 Updated: May 2026 📅 9 min read 📅 FY 2025–26 
 
ARTICLE SUMMARY 
Tax Collected at Source (TCS) is a mechanism under the Income Tax Act where the seller 
collects a percentage of the sale consideration as tax from the buyer. Governed by Section 
206C, TCS applies to specified goods — from alcoholic liquor and forest produce to motor 
vehicles and overseas remittances. This article provides a comprehensive analysis of all 
TCS provisions applicable for FY 2025–26, including the complete rate table, threshold 
limits, exemptions, Form 27EQ filing obligations, and how buyers claim credit in their ITR. 
 
What Is TCS? The Core Concept 
Tax Collected at Source (TCS) is a tax collection mechanism introduced to widen the tax base 
and bring high-value, often cash-intensive transactions into the formal economy. Under TCS, 
the seller of specified goods or services is required to collect a prescribed percentage of the 
consideration from the buyer at the time of receipt of payment and deposit it with the 
government. The buyer gets credit for the TCS in their income tax return, making it essentially a 
pre-payment of tax collected on behalf of the buyer. 
TCS was first introduced for goods like liquor, tendu leaves, and timber — sectors prone to 
unrecorded transactions. Over the years, its scope has expanded dramatically, covering motor 
vehicles (2016), overseas remittances (2020), tour packages, and sale of goods by large sellers 
(2020, under Section 206C(1H)).

📅 Budget 2025 Update on LRS TCS 
The threshold of Rs. 7 lakh per financial year for LRS remittances continues for FY 2025-26. The 
20% rate on non-educational, non-medical LRS remittances (applicable since October 1, 2023)

Section 206C(1H) — TCS on Sale of Goods (High-Value) 
---PAGE 17---
Section 206C(1H), introduced from October 1, 2020, is the most broadly applicable TCS 
provision for regular businesses. It mandates that a seller whose total sales, gross receipts, or 
turnover from business exceeds Rs. 10 crore in the immediately preceding financial year must 
collect TCS @ 0.1% from buyers when the aggregate sale consideration received from that 
buyer in the current financial year exceeds Rs. 50 lakh. 
The TCS is on the amount exceeding Rs. 50 lakh — the first Rs. 50 lakh in a year from any 
buyer is exempt. The rate doubles to 1% if the buyer does not furnish PAN or Aadhaar. This 
provision is applicable on a per-seller, per-buyer, per-year basis.

How Buyers Claim TCS Credit in Their ITR 
---PAGE 18---
For individual or business buyers, TCS collected by the seller automatically reflects in their 
Form 26AS (Part B) and Annual Information Statement (AIS). The IT portal pre-fills this data 
when the buyer files their ITR. The buyer simply needs to verify the pre-filled TCS amount in the 
Tax Paid schedule and confirm it before submitting the return. 
The TCS credit is set off against the total tax payable for the year. If after adjusting TDS, TCS, 
and advance tax, there is still an excess credit, the amount is refunded by the Income Tax 
Department. There is no need to separately apply for TCS credit — the pre-filling mechanism 
handles this automatically. 
 
📅 Pro Tip 
Salaried employees who remitted money abroad under LRS or bought a car above Rs. 10 lakh 
during the year must check their AIS and Form 26AS before filing their ITR. TCS amounts are 
often omitted during manual ITR preparation, causing excess tax payment or missed credit. 
Always reconcile your Form 26AS before finalising tax computation. 
 
✅  KEY TAKEAWAYS 
1.  TCS is collected by the seller from the buyer — the reverse of TDS which is deducted by the 
payer. 
2.  The LRS TCS rate of 20% on non-educational, non-medical remittances above Rs. 7 lakh per 
year continues in FY 2025-26. 
3.  Section 206C(1H) mandates TCS @ 0.1% on sale of goods exceeding Rs. 50 lakh per buyer 
when seller's turnover exceeds Rs. 10 crore. 
4.  Quarterly Form 27EQ must be filed by 15th of July, October, January, and May — late filing 
attracts Rs. 200/day penalty. 
5.  Buyers receive TCS credit in Form 26AS (Part B) and AIS; it is automatically pre-filled in ITR 
and set off against tax liability. 
6.  Government entities and manufacturing buyers (with Form 27C) are exempt from TCS under 
Section 206C(1H). 
7.  Non-collection of TCS attracts penalty equal to the uncollected amount; non-deposit attracts 
18% interest plus penalty. 
 
Frequently Asked Questions 
Q1. If TDS has already been deducted on a transaction, can TCS also be levied on the 
same transaction? 
Under Section 206C(1H) specifically, there is a statutory carve-out: TCS is not applicable if 
TDS has been deducted on the same transaction under any provision of the Income Tax 
Act. This prevents double taxation. The seller must verify from the buyer whether TDS has 
been deducted. If the buyer confirms TDS deduction in writing, the seller is relieved of TCS 
---PAGE 19---
collection. For other TCS provisions (scrap, minerals, liquor, etc.), TDS does not override 
the TCS obligation. 
Q2. A car dealer sold a vehicle for Rs. 12 lakh. Is TCS applicable, and on what amount? 
Yes. TCS under Section 206C(1F) applies when the value of a single motor vehicle exceeds 
Rs. 10 lakh. The TCS is charged on the entire consideration — Rs. 12 lakh — not just the 
excess above Rs. 10 lakh. TCS @ 1% on Rs. 12 lakh = Rs. 12,000. This amount is collected 
by the dealer from the buyer and deposited with the government. The buyer gets credit for 
Rs. 12,000 in their ITR. 
Q3. I remitted Rs. 15 lakh abroad under LRS for my child's education through a personal 
account (not an education loan). What is the TCS? 
For LRS remittances for education that are NOT funded through an education loan from a 
financial institution, the TCS rate is 5% on the amount exceeding Rs. 7 lakh. First Rs. 7 lakh: 
exempt. Remaining Rs. 8 lakh at 5% = Rs. 40,000 TCS. If the remittance were funded by an 
education loan from a bank/NBFC, the rate would be just 0.5% on the same Rs. 8 lakh 
excess = Rs. 4,000. Always declare the source of funds to your bank to get the correct TCS 
treatment. 
Q4. My company's turnover last year was Rs. 8 crore. Do I need to collect TCS this year 
under Section 206C(1H)? 
No. Section 206C(1H) applies only to sellers whose total sales, gross receipts, or turnover 
from business in the immediately preceding financial year exceeds Rs. 10 crore. Since your 
turnover was Rs. 8 crore, you do not qualify as a TCS collector under Section 206C(1H) for 
the current year, regardless of how much you sell this year. You would need to monitor this 
and start collecting TCS from the year following any year in which your turnover crosses Rs. 
10 crore. 
Q5. What happens if a seller fails to collect TCS from the buyer? 
If a seller fails to collect TCS, they are still liable to deposit the TCS amount with the 
government from their own funds. Under Section 206C(7), interest at 1% per month is 
charged from the date TCS was collectible to the date of actual deposit. Additionally, 
Section 271CA imposes a penalty equal to the amount of TCS that was not collected. There 
is also the risk of prosecution under Section 276BB for willful failure to pay TCS to the 
government.

ARTICLE 4 OF 4  ·  SPECIAL ENTITIES 
Taxation of Co-operative Society in India 
📅 Updated: May 2026 📅 8 min read 📅 AY 2026–27 
 
ARTICLE SUMMARY 
Co-operative societies occupy a unique and privileged position in India's tax framework. 
Treated as a separate class of assessees under the Income Tax Act, they have their own 
slab rates, powerful deductions under Section 80P, and are subject to Alternative Minimum 
Tax (AMT) rather than MAT. The Finance Act 2023 introduced Section 115BAD offering a 
flat 22% tax rate. This article explains every aspect of co-operative society taxation for AY 
2026–27 — from basic tax rates and the critical 80P deduction to AMT computation, filing 
requirements, and compliance deadlines. 
 
What is a Co-operative Society? Legal Definition 
A co-operative society is defined under Section 2(19) of the Income Tax Act, 1961 as a society 
registered under the Co-operative Societies Act, 1912, or under any other law for the time being 
in force in any State for the registration of co-operative societies. These are voluntary 
democratic organisations formed by members to meet their collective economic, social, and 
cultural needs. 
The co-operative sector in India is vast — spanning agricultural credit, dairy farming, fisheries, 
housing, consumer goods, and banking. Major categories include: Primary Agricultural Credit 
Societies (PACS), Urban Co-operative Banks, District Central Co-operative Banks (DCCBs), 
State Co-operative Banks, Milk Producers Co-operatives (like AMUL), Housing Co-operative 
Societies, Consumer Co-operative Stores, and Primary Co-operative Marketing Societies. 
 
Tax Rates — Option 1: Regular Slab-Based Rates 
Under the regular regime, co-operative societies are taxed on their net taxable income (after 
80P deductions) as per the following slab rates: 
Income Slab Tax Rate Example Calculation

Rs. 10,001 to Rs. 20,000 20% Max Rs. 2,000 
Above Rs. 20,000 30% On entire amount above Rs. 20,000 
 
To the tax computed above, the following additions are made: Surcharge @ 12% if total income 
exceeds Rs. 1 crore. Health and Education Cess @ 4% on the total of income tax plus 
surcharge. For income between Rs. 1 crore and Rs. 10 crore, the effective tax rate including 
cess is approximately 34.944%.

115BAA. This option was introduced to reduce the tax burden on societies willing to forego 
specified exemptions and deductions.

societies) may be significantly better off under the old regime despite the lower 22% rate.

Section 80P is the most powerful tax benefit available to co-operative societies. It provides a 
100% deduction (or limited deduction for specified activities) from the Gross Total Income of the

What Income Does NOT Qualify for 80P Deduction 
The following income categories of co-operative societies are explicitly excluded from Section 
80P benefits or have been held to be excluded by courts and tribunals: 
• Income from co-operative banks (other than PACS and PCARDB): Excluded by Finance 
Act 2006; urban co-operative banks do not get 80P on their banking income. 
• Rental income from properties let to non-members: Regular property rental without a co-
operative activity link. 
• Capital gains on sale of investments, property, or other assets: Capital gains are not 
from co-operative business activities. 
• Income from non-members: If the society transacts with non-members, proportionate 
income from such transactions may not qualify. 
• Interest from commercial banks on fixed deposits (beyond Rs. 50,000 limit): Excess 
interest income is taxable at normal rates. 
 
Alternative Minimum Tax (AMT) for Co-operative Societies 
Co-operative societies are NOT subject to MAT (Minimum Alternate Tax under Section 115JB, 
which applies to companies). Instead, they are liable to Alternate Minimum Tax (AMT) under 
Section 115JC. AMT applies when the regular income tax payable is less than 18.5% of the 
---PAGE 23---
'Adjusted Total Income' (ATI). ATI is broadly the total income before certain deductions 
including Chapter VI-A deductions like 80P. 
If AMT is triggered, the society pays 18.5% of ATI plus surcharge (12% if income exceeds Rs. 1 
crore) plus cess (4%). AMT Credit equal to (AMT paid minus regular tax) can be carried forward 
and set off in future years when regular tax exceeds AMT, for up to 15 assessment years. Form 
29C (AMT computation report) must be filed along with the ITR.

AMT can become the binding tax computation. Before the financial year ends, compute both 
regular tax and AMT to estimate advance tax accurately. Ignoring AMT when 80P deductions are 
large is a common cause of advance tax shortfall and consequent interest under Section 234B 
and 234C.

Frequently Asked Questions 
Q1. Can a housing co-operative society claim Section 80P on its maintenance charge 
collections from members? 
The taxability and 80P deductibility of maintenance charges collected by housing societies 
has been a subject of significant litigation. The prevailing view, supported by several ITAT 
decisions and High Court rulings, is that maintenance charges collected for upkeep of 
common areas and facilities are in the nature of mutual receipts and are not taxable income 
at all — under the principle of mutuality. If they are held taxable, 80P(2)(c) may apply to 
income from letting properties to members. Each society must evaluate its specific activity 
mix with a qualified CA. 
Q2. An MSME buys agricultural produce from a farmers' co-op. The co-op sells non-
members too. Does the 80P deduction apply to income from non-members? 
Section 80P(2) deductions generally apply to income from co-operative activities with 
members. Income from non-members does not qualify under most clauses of 80P. Courts 
have held that the deduction is limited to income arising from transactions with members. 
Income from non-members should be segregated in accounts and taxed at normal slab 
rates. Some courts have allowed proportionate deduction — however, this is fact-specific 
and should be determined on advice of a tax professional. 
Q3. Is a co-operative society required to audit its accounts even if turnover is below Rs. 1 
crore? 
Under income tax law, tax audit under Section 44AB is triggered when turnover exceeds Rs. 
1 crore (business) or Rs. 50 lakh (profession). However, co-operative societies may also be 
subject to audit under the Co-operative Societies Act of their respective state — which has 
independent criteria. State law audit requirements may mandate audit even below the 
income tax audit threshold. Societies must comply with the most stringent applicable audit 
requirement — whichever is triggered first. 
Q4. Can a new co-operative society opt for Section 115BAD in its very first year of 
existence? 
Yes. A newly registered co-operative society can opt for Section 115BAD right from its first 
assessment year. The option must be exercised by filing Form 10-IF on or before the due 
date for filing the ITR for that first year. Since the society has no prior years under the old 
regime, there is no comparison loss from switching. However, since 80P deductions would 
be lost permanently, new societies should project their income streams and whether their 
activities would have qualified for 80P before making this election. 
Q5. If a co-operative society has unabsorbed depreciation from earlier years, can it set it 
off against income in the current year? 
Yes, subject to conditions. Unabsorbed depreciation can be carried forward indefinitely and 
set off against business income in subsequent years under Section 32(2) of the Income Tax 
Act. For societies that have opted for Section 115BAD, set-off of brought-forward losses and 
depreciation is allowed only if those losses or depreciation were computed under the 
---PAGE 26---
115BAD regime. Losses computed under the old regime (with 80P deductions) cannot be 
set off in years when the society is operating under the new 115BAD regime.
