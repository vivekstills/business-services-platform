# Wholesale License for Foreign Liquor

## Detailed Overview

A Wholesale License for Foreign Liquor (IMFL Wholesale License) authorises an entity to procure IMFL — including whisky, rum, vodka, gin, brandy, beer, wine — from licensed manufacturers, importers, and bonded warehouses, and supply to authorised retail licensees, hotels, restaurants, clubs, and bar permit holders within a specified state or territory.

This is one of the most lucrative B2B alcohol licenses, often controlled by state corporations (KSBCL in Karnataka, MSWC in Maharashtra, TASMAC in Tamil Nadu) or held by major private players like United Spirits, Pernod Ricard, AB InBev distributors, etc.

## Advantages / Benefits

- Strategic position in liquor supply chain
- High revenue potential (₹100+ crores in major states)
- Long-term institutional client relationships
- Access to premium brand portfolios
- Significant economic moat once established
- Government-aligned business model

## Disadvantages / Limitations

- Extremely high entry barriers
- Massive license fees and bank guarantees
- Heavy regulatory scrutiny
- Strict source-and-destination tracking
- State monopolies in several states
- Significant working capital requirements (₹50+ crore)

## Frequently Asked Questions

1. **Can a foreign liquor company directly hold an Indian wholesale license?** Foreign companies typically operate through Indian subsidiaries or Indian wholesale partners. Direct foreign holding is restricted under FDI rules.
2. **What's the minimum working capital for IMFL wholesale operations?** Realistically ₹25–100 crore depending on state and territory. Stock holding alone can be ₹10+ crore.
3. **Can wholesalers also export liquor?** Export requires separate IEC and DGFT permissions. Wholesale license alone doesn't authorise exports.
4. **How is wholesale pricing regulated?** States typically fix maximum margins between distillery price and wholesale price. Wholesalers cannot exceed regulated margins.
5. **Can a wholesale license be transferred?** Transfer requires state government approval and is rarely granted. Usually entire entity must be acquired (not just license).
