# Import Export Code (IEC) Registration

## Overview

The Import Export Code (IEC) is a unique 10-digit identification number issued by the Directorate General of Foreign Trade (DGFT), Ministry of Commerce and Industry, Government of India. The IEC is the primary and foundational authorisation required by any Indian entity or individual to conduct import or export of goods and services. It is, in essence, the trade licence for international commerce in India.

The IEC was introduced under the Foreign Trade (Development and Regulation) Act, 1992 and the Foreign Trade Policy (FTP) issued thereunder. Since its integration with PAN in 2021, the IEC is now directly derived from the entity's PAN — the 10-digit IEC number is the same as the entity's 10-digit PAN, making the system more streamlined and reducing duplication.

Key characteristics of the IEC:

| Feature | Detail |
| --- | --- |
| Issuing authority | DGFT (Directorate General of Foreign Trade) |
| Legal basis | Foreign Trade (Development and Regulation) Act, 1992 |
| Format | 10-digit alphanumeric (same as PAN) |
| Validity | Lifetime — no expiry (annual update required) |
| Applicability | Mandatory for all imports/exports of goods |
| Fees | Nil (Free) |
| Processing time | 2–5 working days |
| Portal | dgft.gov.in |

The IEC is checked at every stage of the import/export transaction — by Customs at the port, by the bank for foreign exchange remittances, by DGFT for scheme applications, and by Export Promotion Councils for RCMC issuance. No import or export can legally proceed without a valid, active IEC.

Important exceptions: IEC is not required for:

- Imports/exports for personal use (non-commercial)
- Central and state government departments and ministries
- Goods exported under baggage rules
- Specific exemptions under the FTP (e.g., defence, security-related)

## Applicability

IEC registration is mandatory for:

| Entity / Activity | IEC Requirement |
| --- | --- |
| Indian companies importing goods | Mandatory |
| Indian companies exporting goods | Mandatory |
| Indian individuals / proprietors importing/exporting commercially | Mandatory |
| Partnership firms and LLPs in import/export | Mandatory |
| Trusts and societies importing/exporting | Mandatory |
| E-commerce sellers shipping products internationally | Mandatory |
| Service exporters above SOFTEX threshold | Mandatory (for SOFTEX filing) |
| Importers of services paying in foreign exchange | Mandatory |
| Indian companies receiving FDI and making ODI | Indirectly required (IEC linked to FEMA compliance) |
| Companies applying for RCMC with EPCs | Mandatory prerequisite |
| Advance Authorisation, EPCG, MEIS/RoDTEP applicants | Mandatory prerequisite |

Not required for:

- Persons importing/exporting for personal use (not connected with trade or commerce)
- Government departments (they use a special exemption code)
- Imports/exports of specific goods exempted under the FTP notifications

Applicable pan-India — one IEC covers all of India. A single IEC enables imports and exports from any port across all states — Mumbai (JNCH), Chennai, Kolkata, Delhi, Bengaluru, Hyderabad, Ahmedabad, Mundra, Kandla, Cochin, Vizag, and all other ports and ICDs.

## Benefits

- Gateway to global trade: IEC is the single authorisation that opens the door to all international commerce — without it, no import or export of goods can be legally completed in India
- Lifetime validity: Unlike many business licences that require annual renewal, IEC is a one-time registration with lifetime validity (subject to annual update confirmations) — no reapplication required as the business grows
- Free of cost: The DGFT charges no fee for IEC registration — it is one of the very few government business licences that is completely free
- Single IEC for all transactions: One IEC covers all import/export activities of the entity across all ports, all products, and all countries — no product-specific or port-specific licensing
- Customs clearance facilitation: IEC is referenced in every Bill of Entry (import) and Shipping Bill (export) — a valid IEC ensures smooth customs processing; an invalid or absent IEC causes immediate clearance holds
- Bank foreign exchange facilitation: Banks require IEC for processing foreign exchange payments for imports (advance payments, LCs) and for crediting export proceeds — IEC is the FEMA identification for trade transactions
- Export promotion scheme eligibility: All DGFT export incentive schemes — RoDTEP, Advance Authorisation, EPCG, Duty Drawback — require an active IEC. The IEC is the entity's identity in the export scheme ecosystem
- RCMC prerequisite: RCMC (Registration-cum-Membership Certificate) from Export Promotion Councils (EPCs) — required for sector-specific export benefits — can only be obtained after IEC
- GST LUT prerequisite: Filing LUT for zero-rated exports requires a valid IEC — particularly for goods exporters
- E-commerce export enablement: International marketplaces (Amazon Global Selling, eBay, Etsy, Alibaba) require an active IEC for Indian sellers listing on their international platforms
- Professional and service credibility: An IEC on a consultancy or service firm's profile signals global commercial readiness — useful for winning international contracts and project bids

## Limitations

- Annual update mandatory: Despite lifetime validity, IEC holders must complete the Annual IEC Update on the DGFT portal between April 1 and June 30 every year — missed updates lead to automatic deactivation
- PAN-based — PAN errors propagate: Since IEC is now PAN-derived, errors in the entity's PAN records (wrong name, wrong date of birth, wrong address) directly appear in the IEC — correcting IEC requires first correcting the underlying PAN, which can be a slow process
- Not a permit for restricted goods: IEC enables import/export in general — but certain products (arms and ammunition, narcotics, wildlife products, certain chemicals) require additional import/export licences beyond the IEC. IEC alone is not sufficient for restricted, prohibited, or canalised goods
- Sector-specific restrictions: For certain import/export activities (e.g., defence items, dual-use goods under SCOMET — Special Chemicals, Organisms, Materials, Equipment, and Technologies), additional DGFT authorisations beyond the IEC are mandatory
- No protection against misuse: The IEC number is semi-public — it appears on shipping documents and customs records. Fraudulent use of IEC by third parties (impersonation) is a known risk, though mitigated by Aadhaar and DSC authentication in the new system
- Deactivation impact: An IEC deactivated due to missed annual update immediately blocks all import/export transactions at customs and banks — the business impact is significant for active traders, making the annual update a critical compliance deadline

## Process

1. Step 1 — DGFT Portal Account Creation Visit dgft.gov.in. Click on "Login" → "Register." Create a user account using your business email ID and mobile number. Verify the account through OTP verification.
2. Step 2 — Aadhaar Verification Link the authorized signatory's Aadhaar to the DGFT portal account using OTP verification sent to the Aadhaar-registered mobile number.
3. Step 3 — IEC Application — Form ANF 2A After login, navigate to Services → IEC Profile Management → Apply for IEC. Form ANF 2A is the IEC application form. The portal auto-populates PAN-linked data (name, address, date of incorporation) from the Income Tax database.
4. Step 4 — Fill Application Details Complete all sections — entity details, principal business address, director/partner details, bank account details (cancelled cheque or bank certificate), and GST details.
5. Step 5 — Upload Documents Upload the required documents — address proof, bank certificate, entity registration documents (CoI, partnership deed), and other applicable documents. Documents must be in PDF or JPEG format within the size limits.
6. Step 6 — Fee Payment IEC registration is free — the DGFT portal confirms nil fee. Proceed to submit without any payment gateway step.
7. Step 7 — Aadhaar OTP Signing The application is submitted with Aadhaar OTP authentication of the authorized signatory (Aadhaar-linked mobile OTP). This replaces the earlier DSC requirement for most applicants.
8. Step 8 — Application Reference Number (ARN) Upon submission, the DGFT portal generates an ARN. The ARN can be used to track the application status on the portal.
9. Step 9 — DGFT Processing The DGFT system validates the application against PAN, Aadhaar, and GST databases. For clean applications (no data mismatches), IEC is generated automatically — often within minutes to a few hours. Where manual review is required (data mismatches, document clarity issues), processing takes 2–5 working days.
10. Step 10 — IEC Certificate Download Upon successful processing, the IEC Certificate (e-IEC) is generated and available for download from the DGFT portal under "Print IEC." The 10-digit IEC number is printed on the certificate along with the entity details and issue date.
11. Step 11 — Post-Registration Steps After IEC issuance:

- Register on ICEGATE (for customs e-filing)
- Obtain RCMC from relevant EPC (for export scheme eligibility)
- File LUT for exports without IGST payment
- Register with STPI (for software/service exporters)
- Update IEC details with AD bank for FEMA compliance

## Use Cases

1. First-Time Garment Exporter A Tiruppur textile manufacturer who has been selling domestically decides to explore export markets in Europe. Before approaching any overseas buyer, attending an international trade fair, or listing on export marketplaces, they need an IEC. With the IEC in place, they can apply for RCMC with AEPC (Apparel Export Promotion Council), register on ICEGATE for customs filings, file LUT with GST for zero-rated exports, and begin fulfilling international purchase orders with proper documentation.
2. E-Commerce Seller Going Global A Jaipur-based handicraft seller on domestic marketplaces wants to expand to Amazon's Global Selling programme (selling on Amazon US, UK, and EU). Amazon requires a valid IEC as part of its international seller verification. The seller registers for an IEC, uploads it to Amazon's global seller account, and can now list products for international buyers with proper export documentation support.
3. Import of Raw Materials A Pune-based specialty chemicals manufacturer wants to import specific chemical precursors from Germany and South Korea that are not available domestically. Without an IEC, their customs broker cannot file a Bill of Entry on their behalf. They obtain an IEC, register on ICEGATE, and work with their CHA to clear the chemical imports through customs — with proper REACH, SCOMET, and hazmat clearances where applicable.
4. IT Company Exporting Software Services A Hyderabad-based SaaS company with US and European clients is technically an exporter of services. While digital service exports don't pass through physical customs, an IEC is required for SOFTEX filing with STPI (for software exports above USD 25,000 per transaction), for FEMA compliance (export proceeds must be realised against an active IEC), and for GST LUT filing to export services without IGST payment. The IEC is their foundational EXIM compliance document even in the digital services context.
5. NGO Importing Donated Medical Equipment A Chennai-based charitable trust receives a donation of medical equipment from a US-based foundation. The equipment must be imported and customs-cleared — requiring an IEC for the trust, even for a non-commercial import. The trust registers for an IEC and applies for the relevant customs exemption (under notification for charitable imports) using their IEC as the importer identifier.

## Frequently asked questions

- **I am a freelancer/consultant providing services to clients in the US and Europe. Do I need an IEC even though I'm not exporting physical goods?** For purely digital service exports where you are an individual (not a registered business entity) and your earnings are below the SOFTEX threshold of USD 25,000 per transaction, the IEC requirement is less clear-cut — many individual service exporters operate without an IEC by receiving foreign payments directly into their savings accounts with AD bank oversight. However, if you want to formally register as a service exporter, claim DGFT export benefits, file SOFTEX with STPI, or your transactions exceed SOFTEX thresholds, an IEC is necessary. If you have a registered business entity (proprietorship, LLP, or Pvt Ltd) providing the services, an IEC for that entity is strongly advisable for complete FEMA compliance and future export incentive eligibility.
- **My IEC application was rejected due to a "name mismatch." What does this mean and how do I fix it?** The most common cause of IEC name mismatch rejection is a difference between the entity name in the PAN database (Income Tax department records) and the entity name as typed in the IEC application. Since IEC data is auto-validated against PAN records, even a minor difference — an extra space, an abbreviation (Pvt vs Private), or a spelling variation — triggers a mismatch. Fix by: (a) downloading your entity's PAN card and noting the exact name as it appears on PAN; (b) ensuring the IEC application uses the exact same name letter-for-letter. If the PAN itself has an incorrect name (different from your ROC incorporation certificate), first correct the PAN at the Income Tax department — IEC modification can only follow after PAN correction.
- **Can a company have multiple IECs for different divisions or business units?** No. Under the DGFT framework, one PAN = one IEC. A company can have only one IEC regardless of the number of divisions, subsidiaries, or business units it operates. All divisions of the same company must use the same IEC for their import/export transactions. If you want to track import/export activities separately for different divisions, this must be done through internal accounting systems — not by obtaining separate IECs. However, if the divisions are separate legal entities (separate companies with separate PANs), each entity gets its own separate IEC.
- **We exported goods 3 years ago and have never updated our IEC since then. Can we still use it?** If you have not completed the mandatory Annual IEC Update between April 1 and June 30 for each year since the update requirement was introduced (FY 2021-22 onwards), your IEC may have been automatically deactivated. Check the IEC status on the DGFT portal (Services → IEC Profile Management → Check IEC Status). If deactivated, complete the overdue Annual Update — the portal allows backdated completion of the update even after June 30. Once the update is submitted and verified, the IEC is reactivated. For any specific years where the update was missed, the IEC was technically inactive during that period — review whether any imports/exports during those periods need to be regularised.
- **Is an IEC required for importing goods for personal use — say, I want to import a vintage motorcycle from the UK as a collector's item?** Personal imports — goods for personal use that are not connected to any trade or business — are generally exempt from the IEC requirement. Customs clears personal imports under the Baggage Rules or as personal imports against the individual's PAN without an IEC. However, "personal use" has a specific meaning in customs law — the item must genuinely be for personal consumption or use and not for resale or commercial purpose. A vintage motorcycle imported once as a collector's personal item would typically qualify as personal import. If you plan to import multiple vehicles, deal in imported collectibles commercially, or if customs questions the personal use nature, an IEC may be required. Consult a CHA for the specific HS code and customs classification of your intended import before proceeding.
