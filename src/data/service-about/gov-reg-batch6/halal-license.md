# Halal License

## Overview

A Halal License (or Halal Certification) is a certification that attests that a product — typically food, beverage, cosmetics, pharmaceuticals, or food service — has been produced, processed, stored, and distributed in accordance with Islamic law (Shariah) principles. "Halal" means permissible in Arabic — the certification confirms that the product is permissible for consumption or use by Muslim consumers.
In India, Halal certification is not a government-mandated statutory license (unlike FSSAI or drug licenses). It is issued by private Halal certification bodies — both Indian and international — that are recognised by importing countries, Muslim-majority markets, and domestic Muslim consumer groups. The Halal India Pvt Ltd, Jamiat Ulama-e-Hind Halal Trust, and CDIL (Certification Division of Islamic Law) are among the well-known Halal certification bodies operating in India.
However, Halal certification is effectively mandatory for:

- Exports to Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC) countries (UAE, Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Kuwait, Oman, Bahrain) — where importing authorities require accredited Halal certification
- Exports to Malaysia, Indonesia, and other Muslim-majority countries — where Halal certification from recognised bodies is a legal import requirement
- Domestic market sale targeting Muslim consumers — especially for meat, poultry, processed foods, and packaged food products in states with significant Muslim populations
- International food retail chains in India — that have global sourcing policies requiring Halal certification

India is one of the world's largest exporters of Halal food — particularly beef, buffalo meat, poultry, seafood, and processed foods. Halal certification opens access to a global Halal food market estimated at over USD 2 trillion.

## Features

- Product-specific certification: Halal certification is issued for specific products manufactured at a specific facility — not a blanket company certification
- Facility audit: Certification involves inspection of the manufacturing facility, raw material sourcing, production process, storage, and distribution practices
- Ingredient verification: All ingredients used in the product (including additives, flavourings, processing aids) must be Halal-compliant — no pork derivatives, alcohol, or impermissible ingredients
- Slaughter compliance (for meat/poultry): Halal certification for meat products requires verification that animals are slaughtered by a Muslim slaughterman using prescribed Islamic method (Zabiha)
- Annual surveillance: Halal certification bodies conduct periodic surveillance audits to ensure ongoing compliance
- International recognition: Indian Halal certificates must be issued by bodies recognised by the importing country's Halal authority (e.g., ESMA in UAE, JAKIM in Malaysia, MUI in Indonesia)
- Digital certificate: Modern Halal certifications include digital certificates with QR codes for consumer and buyer verification

## Benefits

- Export market access: Access to the GCC, Southeast Asian, and other Muslim-majority markets — which collectively represent a multi-trillion-dollar food market — requires Halal certification
- Premium pricing: Halal-certified products command premium pricing in both export and domestic markets — Muslim consumers are willing to pay more for certified Halal products
- Domestic market expansion: India has approximately 200 million Muslim consumers — a significant domestic market for Halal-certified food, cosmetics, and pharmaceuticals
- Brand trust and loyalty: Halal certification builds deep brand trust within the Muslim community — both in India and globally
- Food tourism: India's Halal-certified hospitality and food service sector attracts Muslim tourists from abroad — particularly from Gulf countries, Malaysia, and Indonesia
- CSR and inclusivity: Halal certification demonstrates a brand's commitment to inclusive product design — products that meet Halal standards also often meet Kosher and vegetarian standards, broadening consumer appeal
- Regulatory compliance in export markets: Many GCC countries legally mandate Halal certification for all food imports — non-certified products are seized at customs

## Limitations

- Not a government license: Indian Halal certification is issued by private bodies — it does not carry the same legal force as FSSAI or drug licenses under Indian law
- Country-specific recognition: A Halal certificate from one Indian body may not be recognised in another country — exporters must ensure the certifying body is recognised by the specific importing country's Halal authority
- Ongoing surveillance cost: Annual surveillance audits add recurring costs — significant for SME food processors
- Process change requirements: Achieving Halal compliance may require changes to production processes, ingredient sourcing, and slaughterhouse practices — sometimes involving significant capital investment
- Religious sensitivity: Halal certification involves religious interpretation — different certifying bodies may have different standards and interpretations, causing certification complexities for multi-market exporters
- Not universally required domestically: Halal certification is not mandated by Indian law for domestic sale — it is voluntary in the domestic market (though commercially important for Muslim consumer targeting)
- Controversies and misuse: Halal labelling has been a subject of political controversy in India — businesses should be aware of the socio-political context in their operating states

## Applicability

- Food manufacturers: All categories — meat and poultry, seafood, dairy, processed foods, baked goods, confectionery, beverages, spices and condiments
- Food service (restaurants, catering): Restaurants targeting Muslim consumers or international Muslim tourists; airline catering for Muslim passengers
- Pharmaceutical manufacturers: Gelatin-containing capsules, vitamins with animal-derived ingredients
- Cosmetics manufacturers: Cosmetics using animal-derived ingredients or alcohol-based ingredients
- Exporters to GCC and Muslim-majority countries: Mandatory for most food and food-adjacent product exports
- Hotels and resorts: Halal kitchen certification for properties targeting Muslim guests

Applicable pan-India — particularly relevant for food exporters in states like Kerala, Tamil Nadu, Maharashtra, Uttar Pradesh, West Bengal, Telangana, Andhra Pradesh, and Karnataka, which have significant Muslim populations and active food export industries.

## Documents Required

- **Company registration documents** — Certificate of Incorporation, PAN, GST
- **FSSAI License / Registration** — Mandatory — Halal certification requires existing FSSAI compliance
- **List of products to be certified** — With ingredients list for each product
- **Full ingredient specification** — Including all additives, flavours, processing aids
- **Supplier declarations for ingredients** — Halal compliance declarations from ingredient suppliers
- **Manufacturing process flowchart** — Production process from raw material to finished product
- **Premises layout plan** — Factory floor plan
- **Quality control procedures** — HACCP plan, SOP documentation
- **Slaughter procedure (for meat)** — Details of slaughter process, staff qualifications
- **Previous Halal certificate (if any)** — For renewal applications

## Process

1. Step 1 — Select Recognised Certification Body Identify the Halal certification body recognised by your target export market(s). For UAE exports: ESMA-recognised bodies. For Malaysia: JAKIM-recognised. For Indonesia: MUI-recognised. For domestic/GCC general: Jamiat Ulama or Halal India.
1. Step 2 — Application Submission Submit the Halal certification application with product details, ingredient lists, and company documents to the chosen certifying body.
1. Step 3 — Document Review The certifying body reviews all ingredient specifications and supplier Halal declarations to confirm ingredient-level Halal compliance.
1. Step 4 — Factory Audit A qualified Halal auditor inspects the manufacturing facility — verifying production processes, ingredient storage, cross-contamination prevention, and slaughter procedures (for meat products).
1. Step 5 — Corrective Actions (if any) Address any non-conformances identified during the audit. Resubmit evidence of corrective actions.
1. Step 6 — Certification Grant Upon satisfactory audit and document review, the Halal certificate is issued — typically within 30–60 days of application.
1. Step 7 — Annual Surveillance Undergo annual surveillance audits and renew the certification before expiry.

## Validity

Halal certifications are typically valid for 1 year from the date of issue, subject to satisfactory ongoing compliance. Annual surveillance audits are required for renewal. Some certifying bodies issue 2-year certifications with mid-term surveillance. Certificates are product-specific and premises-specific — any change in product formulation, ingredient supplier, or manufacturing process requires re-certification.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. Is Halal certification legally mandatory in India for food products?** No. Halal certification is not mandated by Indian law for domestic food products. FSSAI regulates food safety standards in India and does not require Halal certification for domestic sale. However, for exports to GCC countries, Malaysia, Indonesia, and other Muslim-majority markets, Halal certification is effectively mandatory as per the importing countries' regulations. Domestically, it is a commercial decision — businesses targeting Muslim consumers obtain it voluntarily for market access and consumer trust.

**2. Can a non-Muslim-owned company obtain Halal certification?** Yes. Halal certification is product and process-based — not owner-religion-based. Any company (regardless of the religion of its owners or promoters) can obtain Halal certification for its products, provided the products and production processes comply with Halal standards. What matters is that the slaughter (for meat products) is performed by Muslim slaughtermen per Islamic rites, and that all ingredients and processes meet Halal requirements. Many multinational food companies (including non-Muslim-owned MNCs) hold Halal certifications globally.

**3. Which Halal certification body's certificate is recognised for exporting food to Saudi Arabia?** Saudi Arabia (SFDA — Saudi Food and Drug Authority) maintains a list of recognised foreign Halal certification bodies. Indian certification bodies must be specifically recognised by SFDA for their certificates to be accepted for Saudi imports. The list of SFDA-recognised Indian Halal bodies is subject to change — exporters should verify the current SFDA recognised bodies list before selecting a certification partner. Choosing a non-recognised body's certificate can result in shipment rejection at Saudi ports.

**4. We make vegetarian packaged foods. Do we still need Halal certification for Gulf export?** Yes, for many product categories. Even purely vegetarian products can have Halal compliance issues — if they contain alcohol-based flavourings, certain food additives derived from non-Halal sources, or if they are manufactured in a facility that also processes non-Halal products (cross-contamination risk). GCC importing authorities require Halal certification for most packaged food products — the vegetarian nature of the product does not automatically make it Halal-certified. Halal certification confirms both ingredient and process compliance.

**5. Our Halal certificate is from a domestic Indian body. Why is our shipment being rejected in Malaysia?** Malaysia's Halal authority (JAKIM — Jabatan Kemajuan Islam Malaysia) maintains a strict list of recognised foreign Halal certification bodies. Only certificates issued by JAKIM-recognised bodies are accepted for food imports into Malaysia. Not all Indian Halal certification bodies are recognised by JAKIM. You must obtain Halal certification from a JAKIM-recognised body — or through a JAKIM-accredited certification body's Indian affiliate — for Malaysian market access. Verify the importing country's specific recognised body list before selecting your certifying body for export markets.
