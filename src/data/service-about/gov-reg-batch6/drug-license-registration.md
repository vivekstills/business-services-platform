# Drug License Registration

## Overview

A Drug License is a mandatory regulatory authorisation issued by the Central Drugs Standard Control Organisation (CDSCO) at the central level and State Drug Licensing Authorities at the state level, under the Drugs and Cosmetics Act, 1940 and the Drugs and Cosmetics Rules, 1945. Any entity engaged in the manufacture, sale, distribution, import, or export of drugs, pharmaceuticals, cosmetics, or medical devices in India must obtain the appropriate drug license before commencing operations.
India's drug regulatory framework distinguishes between:

- Retail drug license: For pharmacies and retail chemist shops selling drugs to consumers
- Wholesale drug license: For wholesale dealers/distributors supplying drugs to retailers, hospitals, and institutions
- Manufacturing license: For pharmaceutical manufacturers (Form 25 for allopathic drugs, Form 28 for cosmetics)
- Import license: For importing drugs and medical devices into India (Form 8 / Form 10)
- Loan license: For manufacturers who use another licensee's manufacturing facility
- Restricted license: For small retailers selling only specified OTC (over-the-counter) drugs

Drug licenses are issued at the state level (for manufacture, retail, and wholesale) and the central level (for import, export, and central manufacturing sites). The licensing authority in each state is the State Drug Controller under the State Food and Drug Administration.

## Features

- Statutory requirement: Mandatory under the Drugs and Cosmetics Act, 1940 — operating without a drug license is a cognisable offence punishable with imprisonment
- Category-specific: Separate licenses for retail, wholesale, manufacturing, import, and cosmetics — each with distinct forms, fees, and compliance requirements
- Qualified person requirement: Drug licenses (especially retail and wholesale) require a licensed pharmacist (B.Pharm/D.Pharm degree holder with registration) to be in-charge
- Premises inspection: All drug license applications (except restricted license) involve mandatory inspection of premises by Drug Inspectors
- Storage compliance: Licensed premises must maintain storage conditions as prescribed — temperature control (refrigeration for certain drugs), security for Schedule H and X drugs
- Schedule H and X compliance: Drugs in Schedule H (antibiotics, antivirals, etc.) and Schedule X (narcotics, psychotropics) have stringent dispensing, record-keeping, and prescription requirements
- State-specific forms and procedures: While the Drugs and Cosmetics Act is central, state drug licensing authorities have state-specific forms, fees, and inspection protocols

## Benefits

- Legal authorisation to trade/manufacture: Without a drug license, any pharmaceutical business activity is illegal — the license is the gateway to the entire pharmaceutical supply chain
- Supplier eligibility: Pharmaceutical distributors and manufacturers only supply to licensed entities; an unlicensed retailer or distributor cannot purchase from legitimate sources
- Insurance reimbursement empanelment: Government health insurance schemes (Ayushman Bharat, ESIC, CGHS) empanel only licensed pharmacies and suppliers
- Hospital tenders: Government and private hospital pharmacy tenders require bidders to hold valid drug licenses
- Consumer trust: Patients and consumers trust licensed pharmacies for the authenticity and quality of medicines — the drug license number displayed at the pharmacy is a trust signal
- Export facilitation: Pharmaceutical exports require valid manufacturing and export licenses — DGFT and customs require drug license verification for all pharma export shipments
- Medical device market access: The medical device market (especially hospital and institutional buyers) requires manufacturers and distributors to hold valid device licenses under the Medical Devices Rules, 2017

## Limitations

- Qualified person mandatory: Retail and wholesale drug licenses require a registered pharmacist to be physically present at the premises — businesses without an in-house pharmacist cannot operate (a common challenge for non-pharmacy businesses entering healthcare)
- Premises inspection barrier: Drug Inspector inspections can be stringent — premises not meeting storage, security, and infrastructure standards face rejection or delay
- State-specific complexity: Each state's Drug Controller has different forms, fee structures, inspection timelines, and interpretation of rules — multi-state operations require managing multiple state licenses
- Schedule X strict controls: Schedule X drugs (narcotics, psychotropics) require separate registers, police reporting, and extremely strict compliance — even minor lapses can result in license cancellation
- Ongoing compliance burden: Drug-licensed businesses must maintain purchase/sale registers, issue prescriptions per rules, submit returns, and cooperate with Drug Inspector visits — ongoing compliance is significant
- Cancellation risk: Drug licenses can be suspended or cancelled for non-compliance — loss of license means immediate cessation of all drug-related business activities

## Process

1. Step 1 — Determine License Type Identify the correct license type (retail/wholesale/manufacturing/import) and the appropriate State Drug Controller or CDSCO for your application.
1. Step 2 — Premises Preparation Ensure the premises meets all requirements — minimum area (typically 10 sq meters for retail), proper storage conditions (refrigeration, dry storage, security), and required infrastructure.
1. Step 3 — Appoint Qualified Person Appoint a registered pharmacist (for retail/wholesale) or qualified Technical Director (for manufacturing) who will be named on the license.
1. Step 4 — Application Filing File the application (online or offline, depending on state) with the State Drug Controller with all required documents and prescribed fee.
1. Step 5 — Drug Inspector Inspection Drug Inspector inspects the premises. Address any observations made during inspection within the stipulated time.
1. Step 6 — Grant of License Upon satisfactory inspection, the Drug Controller grants the license. Download or collect the Drug License certificate.
1. Step 7 — Compliance Commencement Maintain all statutory registers (purchase register, sales register, etc.), display the license at the premises, and begin periodic compliance (half-yearly returns, renewal tracking).

## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. Can an online pharmacy (e-pharmacy) operate without a physical drug license?** No. E-pharmacies and online medicine delivery platforms must hold valid retail drug licenses at each of their dispensing locations (warehouses/fulfilment centres from which medicines are physically dispensed). The Supreme Court of India and CDSCO have repeatedly clarified that online medicine sale is subject to the same licensing requirements as physical pharmacies. Draft e-pharmacy rules are pending finalisation by CDSCO — in the interim, existing Drugs and Cosmetics Act licensing requirements apply fully.

**2. My registered pharmacist is leaving. What do I do about my drug license?** A drug license is tied to the qualified person (pharmacist) named on it. When a pharmacist leaves, you must appoint a new registered pharmacist and notify the Drug Controller within the prescribed time, submitting the new pharmacist's registration certificate. Some states require the license to be amended to reflect the new pharmacist; others allow a simple intimation. Operating without a qualified pharmacist on record — even temporarily — is a serious violation that can lead to license suspension.

**3. We manufacture Ayurvedic medicines. Do we need a drug license under the Drugs and Cosmetics Act?** Yes. Ayurvedic, Siddha, and Unani (ASU) medicines are regulated under the Drugs and Cosmetics Act, 1940 and require a manufacturing license for ASU drugs from the State License Authority. The forms and standards differ from allopathic drug manufacturing — ASU manufacturers must comply with the Drugs and Cosmetics Rules for ASU drugs, including GMP standards for ASU drugs. CDSCO's jurisdiction covers allopathic drugs; ASU drug licensing is managed by the Ayush Department / State Drug Controller under the ASU provisions of the Act.

**4. What is the minimum area required for a retail pharmacy to qualify for a drug license?** The minimum area requirement varies by state. Many states require a minimum of 10 square meters for a retail pharmacy. The premises must be an independent, enclosed space with a proper entrance — shared spaces or open kiosks may not qualify. The premises must also have proper shelving, storage (including refrigeration for cold-chain medicines), and a separate area for dispensing. The Drug Inspector assesses the premises during inspection and will flag non-compliance with space and infrastructure requirements.

**5. Can a drug license be transferred when a business is sold or ownership changes?** Drug licenses are generally not transferable in the traditional sense. When a business is sold or ownership changes, the new owner must apply for a fresh drug license in their own name. Some states allow a modification/transfer of license to a new proprietor in specific circumstances (e.g., death of proprietor, family succession) — but this is at the discretion of the State Drug Controller. In M&A transactions involving pharmaceutical businesses, drug license transfer/fresh licensing is a critical part of transaction planning and should be addressed well before deal completion.
