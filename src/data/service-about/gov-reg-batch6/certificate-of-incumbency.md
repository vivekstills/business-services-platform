# Certificate of Incumbency

## Overview

A Certificate of Incumbency (also called an Incumbency Certificate or Certificate of Good Standing in some jurisdictions) is an official document that certifies the current officers, directors, shareholders, and/or authorised signatories of a company at a specific point in time. In the context of Indian companies, it is typically a certificate issued by the Company Secretary or a recognised authority (Registrar of Companies, notary, or the company itself under seal) that confirms who the present office-holders of the company are.
In India, while the term "Certificate of Incumbency" is not formally defined under the Companies Act, 2013, such certificates are routinely required by:

- Foreign banks and financial institutions when an Indian company opens an account or enters into contracts abroad
- Foreign counterparties in cross-border commercial contracts requiring proof of authority of the Indian company's signatories
- FEMA compliance purposes where the foreign investor/lender requires certified proof of the Indian company's management structure
- Due diligence in international M&A, joint venture, and partnership transactions
- Apostille and consular legalisation processes for use of Indian company documents overseas

The certificate essentially answers the question: "Who is authorised to legally represent and sign on behalf of this company right now?" — a question that arises frequently in international business transactions.

## Features

- Certifies current directors and officers: Name, designation, date of appointment, and nationality of each current director and key officer
- Confirms shareholding: Names and shareholding percentages of current shareholders (particularly for private limited companies)
- Identifies authorised signatories: Specifies who is authorised to sign banking, legal, or commercial documents on behalf of the company
- Point-in-time certification: The certificate is dated and certifies the company's management structure as of the date of issue
- Issued under company seal: Typically signed by the Company Secretary and Managing Director and bears the company's common seal
- Notarised and apostilled: For international use, the certificate is notarised by a Notary Public and apostilled by the Ministry of External Affairs (MEA), Government of India
- Customisable scope: The certificate can be tailored to include specific information required by the foreign party — banking resolutions, specimen signatures, constitutional documents

## Benefits

- International banking access: Foreign banks (especially in the US, UK, EU, Singapore, UAE) require a Certificate of Incumbency before opening corporate accounts for Indian entities — it is a standard KYC document
- Cross-border contract execution: Foreign counterparties need proof that the person signing the contract on behalf of the Indian company is actually authorised to do so
- FEMA and RBI compliance: When Indian entities receive FDI or make ODI, the foreign investor/counterpart often requires incumbency certification as part of their KYC/AML compliance
- Legal proceedings: In international arbitration or litigation involving Indian companies, a Certificate of Incumbency provides certified evidence of corporate authority
- Anti-money laundering (AML) compliance: International financial institutions require incumbency certificates as part of their AML/CFT due diligence for Indian corporate clients
- Franchise and licensing agreements: International franchisors and technology licensors require incumbency certificates before executing master franchise or technology license agreements with Indian companies
- Investor relations: Foreign investors in Indian companies require incumbency certificates before disbursing investment tranches or executing shareholder agreements

## Limitations

- Point-in-time only: The certificate is valid only as of its date of issue — a change in directors or authorised signatories after issuance renders it potentially stale
- Not a statutory Indian document: Unlike a Certificate of Incorporation or Share Certificate, there is no standardised Indian statutory format for a Certificate of Incumbency — each certificate is custom-drafted
- Apostille process adds time: If the certificate requires Apostille (for use in Hague Convention countries), the MEA apostille process adds 5–15 working days
- Foreign party-specific requirements: Different foreign banks and counterparties have different requirements for what the certificate must contain — drafting errors or omissions cause rejections and re-issuances
- Notarisation costs: Notarisation fees vary across states and notaries; for urgent requirements, costs are higher
- Not accepted as substitute for power of attorney: A Certificate of Incumbency certifies authority within the company's constitutional structure; it does not replace a formal Power of Attorney for specific transactions

## Applicability

- Indian private limited and public limited companies engaging in cross-border transactions
- Indian LLPs with foreign partners or entering international agreements
- Indian companies opening bank accounts with foreign banks (in India or overseas)
- Indian companies receiving foreign investment or making overseas investments
- Indian entities entering into technology licensing, franchise, or distribution agreements with foreign counterparties
- Indian companies involved in international arbitration or litigation
- Indian subsidiaries of foreign companies requiring certification for their parent/group transactions

Applicable pan-India — companies registered in any state (Karnataka, Maharashtra, Delhi, Tamil Nadu, Gujarat, etc.) can obtain a Certificate of Incumbency as needed.

## Documents Required

- **Certificate of Incorporation** — From MCA — proof of company's legal existence
- **Memorandum and Articles of Association** — Constitutional documents of the company
- **Register of Directors** — Current list of directors with DIN, address, date of appointment
- **Register of Members / Shareholding pattern** — Current shareholding details
- **Board Resolution** — Authorising issuance of the Certificate and naming authorised signatories
- **Recent MCA filings (Form DIR-12, MGT-7A)** — To verify current director details with RoC records
- **PAN card of directors** — KYC of current directors
- **Passport copies of directors** — For international use — especially where directors are foreign nationals
- **Specimen signatures** — Signatures of directors/authorised signatories
- **Company common seal impression** — If the company has a common seal
- **Notary appointment (if required)** — Identification of the Notary Public for notarisation

## Process

1. Step 1 — Review Foreign Party Requirements Understand exactly what information the foreign bank/counterparty requires in the Certificate — directors, shareholders, authorised signatories, banking authority, constitutional documents, etc.
1. Step 2 — MCA Records Verification Verify the current director and shareholder details on the MCA portal to ensure the certificate reflects accurate, current information consistent with RoC records.
1. Step 3 — Draft the Certificate Draft the Certificate of Incumbency in the format required by the foreign party. The certificate must be precise, comprehensive, and consistent with MCA records.
1. Step 4 — Board Resolution Pass a board resolution authorising the issuance of the Certificate of Incumbency and specifying who is authorised to sign on behalf of the company.
1. Step 5 — Execution Under Company Seal Sign the certificate by the authorised directors/Company Secretary and affix the company's common seal (if applicable).
1. Step 6 — Notarisation Get the certificate notarised by a Notary Public. Notarisation is typically required for international use.
1. Step 7 — Apostille (if required) For use in Hague Convention countries (US, UK, most EU countries, Australia, Singapore), submit the notarised certificate to the MEA or State Home Department for Apostille stamp.
1. Step 8 — Consular Legalisation (if required) For use in non-Hague countries, get the apostilled document further legalised by the relevant country's embassy/consulate in India.
1. Step 9 — Delivery Deliver the original executed, notarised, and apostilled/legalised certificate to the foreign party — either physically or via secure courier.

## Validity

A Certificate of Incumbency does not have a fixed statutory validity period. However:
Most foreign banks and counterparties accept a Certificate of Incumbency that is not more than 3 to 6 months old from the date of use
For ongoing banking relationships, banks may require a fresh certificate annually
After any change in directors, officers, or authorised signatories, a fresh certificate must be issued

## Frequently Asked Questions

**1. Is a Certificate of Incumbency the same as a Certificate of Good Standing in India?** They are related but distinct. A Certificate of Good Standing (or Certificate of Status) confirms that a company is duly incorporated, is compliant with statutory filing requirements, and is in active status with the Registrar of Companies. A Certificate of Incumbency, on the other hand, certifies the current officers, directors, and authorised signatories of the company. In practice, for international use, many foreign parties require both — the Certificate of Good Standing to confirm the company is legitimately incorporated and in compliance, and the Incumbency Certificate to confirm who is authorised to represent it.

**2. Who can issue a Certificate of Incumbency for an Indian company?** Unlike some offshore jurisdictions (British Virgin Islands, Cayman Islands) where Registered Agents issue standardised incumbency certificates, India does not have a standardised statutory format. A Certificate of Incumbency for an Indian company is typically issued by the Company Secretary (if the company has one) or by the directors themselves under the company's common seal, supported by a board resolution. For international acceptance, notarisation by a Notary Public and MEA Apostille are typically required.

**3. Can a Certificate of Incumbency be issued for an LLP instead of a company?** Yes. An LLP can issue an equivalent certificate — often called a Certificate of Partnership Incumbency or Certificate of Designated Partners — certifying the current Designated Partners (DPs) of the LLP, their authority, and the LLP's registration details. The certificate is supported by the LLP's Certificate of Incorporation, LLP Agreement, and a resolution of the Designated Partners. The process for notarisation and apostille is the same as for companies.

**4. Our foreign bank is asking for a Certificate of Incumbency and also a Board Resolution specifically authorising the account opening and naming the account signatories. Are these the same document?** No, they serve different purposes. The Certificate of Incumbency confirms who the current officers/directors of the company are — it is a status certificate. The Board Resolution specifically authorises the opening of the bank account and designates the authorised signatories for that account with specimen signatures. Many foreign banks require both documents — the Incumbency Certificate for general KYC and the Board Resolution for the specific banking transaction authorisation. Both documents may need to be notarised and apostilled.

**5. How quickly can a Certificate of Incumbency be arranged for an urgent cross-border transaction?** The certificate itself can be drafted and executed within 1–2 working days if all company documents and director details are readily available. Notarisation can typically be arranged within 1–2 working days. MEA Apostille, however, takes 5–15 working days through normal channels; premium/tatkaal services can reduce this to 2–5 working days at a higher cost. Consular legalisation timelines vary by country (embassy appointment availability). End-to-end for an apostilled certificate, plan for 7–15 working days under normal circumstances; 3–7 working days on an express basis.
