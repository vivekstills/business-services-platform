# GST Advisory

## Overview

GST Advisory is a specialised consulting service that helps businesses navigate the complex, multi-layered Goods and Services Tax framework in India. Introduced on July 1, 2017, GST replaced a cascade of central and state indirect taxes — Central Excise Duty, Service Tax, VAT, CST, Entry Tax, and others — with a unified, destination-based consumption tax. While GST simplified the overall indirect tax structure conceptually, its operational complexity — with multiple tax rates, input tax credit rules, anti-profiteering provisions, e-invoicing mandates, HSN/SAC code classifications, and frequent CBIC circulars — makes professional advisory indispensable for businesses of all sizes.
GST Advisory covers the full spectrum of GST-related guidance: from registration structuring and HSN/SAC classification to ITC eligibility opinions, GST rate determinations, supply vs. non-supply analysis, place of supply rules, GST implications of cross-border transactions, GST audits, and litigation support before the GST Council, Appellate Authority for Advance Rulings (AAAR), High Courts, and the Supreme Court.
For businesses operating across multiple states, dealing with export-import transactions, providing inter-company services within group structures, or navigating sector-specific GST challenges (real estate, financial services, healthcare, IT/ITES), expert GST advisory is not a luxury — it is a risk management essential.

## Features

- HSN/SAC classification advisory: Identifying the correct 4/6/8-digit HSN (Harmonised System of Nomenclature) code for goods or SAC (Services Accounting Code) for services — critical for correct GST rate application and GSTR-1 filing
- GST rate determination: Advising on the applicable GST rate for specific products or services — including interpretation of notifications, exemptions, and conditional rates
- Place of supply analysis: Determining whether a supply is intra-state (CGST+SGST) or inter-state (IGST) — particularly complex for digital services, works contracts, and cross-border transactions
- ITC eligibility assessment: Advising on which input tax credits are eligible, blocked (Section 17(5)), or subject to reversal under Rule 42/43
- Advance Ruling applications: Drafting and filing applications before the Authority for Advance Rulings (AAR) for binding rulings on GST rate, classification, and ITC eligibility
- GST audit and assessment support: Assistance during departmental GST audits, scrutiny of returns, and assessment proceedings
- Litigation support: Representation before GST Appellate Authority, AAAR, High Courts, and Supreme Court
- Compliance health check: Review of GST filings, ITC claimed, and reverse charge compliance for the past 3–5 years to identify risks before departmental action

## Benefits

- Risk mitigation: Proactive GST advisory prevents incorrect tax payment, excess ITC claims, and classification errors that can attract GST notices, interest (18% p.a.), and penalties (up to 100% of tax due)
- Cash flow optimisation: Correct ITC maximisation (without excess claims) improves working capital — every rupee of legitimately available ITC that goes unclaimed is cash left on the table
- Business decision support: GST-efficient structuring of contracts, pricing, and supply chain can significantly reduce effective GST cost — particularly important in real estate, construction, and complex service arrangements
- Litigation avoidance: Most GST litigation arises from classification disputes, ITC reversal demands, and place of supply disagreements — pre-transaction advisory prevents these disputes
- Compliance confidence: Businesses with expert GST advisory support file accurate returns, respond appropriately to GST notices, and navigate audits without disruption
- Sector-specific guidance: Sectors with unique GST complexity (healthcare, education, financial services, real estate, IT) benefit from advisors with deep sector expertise — generic guidance creates risk
- Cross-border transaction clarity: Export-import GST treatment, OIDAR (Online Information and Database Access and Retrieval) services, and Liaison Office/branch office GST compliance require specialised advisory

## Process

1. Step 1 — Business Understanding Detailed discussion with the client to understand business model, supply chain, products/services, customer base (B2B vs B2C), and existing GST registration structure.
1. Step 2 — Issue Identification Identify specific GST issues, risks, or queries — classification disputes, ITC eligibility questions, place of supply ambiguities, notice responses, or structuring requirements.
1. Step 3 — Research and Analysis Deep research into applicable GST provisions, CBIC notifications, circulars, advance rulings, and judicial precedents relevant to the identified issues.
1. Step 4 — Advisory Opinion Prepare a written advisory opinion or legal memorandum with: the legal position, applicable provisions, relevant rulings/precedents, risk assessment, and recommended action.
1. Step 5 — Implementation Support Assist the client in implementing the recommended position — in contracts, invoicing, return filing, or ITC claims — with appropriate documentation to support the position adopted.
1. Step 6 — Advance Ruling (if required) Where the issue is material and the legal position is unclear, file an application for Advance Ruling before the AAR for a binding determination.
1. Step 7 — Litigation Support (if required) Where disputes arise despite correct advisory, represent the client before the GST Appellate Authority, AAAR, or courts.

## Compliance

- Stay current with CBIC notifications and circulars — GST rates, exemptions, and procedural requirements change frequently
- Ensure all advance rulings taken by the business are implemented consistently in filings
- Maintain documentation supporting classification positions and ITC claims for at least 6 years (statute of limitations for GST)
- Review ITC reversal obligations under Rule 42/43 at the end of each financial year
- Monitor GSTR-2B vs. GSTR-3B reconciliation monthly — excess ITC claims attract interest automatically

## Common Mistakes

- Incorrect HSN/SAC codes on invoices and GSTR-1: Using 2-digit or 4-digit codes when 6-digit or 8-digit is required for turnover above threshold — attracts penalty of ₹50,000
- ITC on blocked items: Claiming ITC on motor vehicles, personal expenses, food and beverages, health insurance, club memberships — these are blocked under Section 17(5)
- Ignoring reverse charge mechanism (RCM): Not paying GST on RCM supplies (legal services from individual advocates, GTA services, imported services) — both interest and penalty apply
- Place of supply errors: Treating an inter-state supply as intra-state (or vice versa) — leads to wrong tax payment and complex refund/recovery situations
- Not reconciling GSTR-2B vs GSTR-3B: Claiming ITC beyond GSTR-2B without following up with suppliers — results in automatic interest recovery under Rule 86B provisions

## Frequently Asked Questions

- **Our company provides both taxable and exempt services. How do we handle ITC on common expenses?** When a business has both taxable and exempt supplies, ITC on inputs and input services exclusively used for taxable supplies is fully available. ITC on inputs/input services exclusively used for exempt supplies is not available. ITC on common inputs/input services (used for both) must be attributed proportionately — using the formula under Rule 42 of the CGST Rules (taxable turnover / total turnover × common ITC). The exempt supply reversal is calculated annually and adjusted against the monthly provisional reversal. This requires careful tracking and is a common area of advisory focus.
- **We received a GST Advance Ruling from AAR that goes against us. What are our options?** An unfavourable AAR ruling can be appealed before the Appellate Authority for Advance Rulings (AAAR) within 30 days of the ruling. The AAAR consists of senior state and central GST officers and may modify or reverse the AAR ruling. If the AAAR ruling is also unfavourable, the matter can be challenged before the High Court through a writ petition under Article 226 of the Constitution — GST advance rulings are administrative decisions, not quasi-judicial orders. High Courts have frequently interfered with AAR/AAAR rulings on jurisdictional or interpretational grounds.
- **Can GST advisory help us reduce our GST liability legitimately?** Yes — within the bounds of law. Legitimate GST optimisation strategies include: choosing the correct supply structure (composite vs. mixed supply) to apply the most favourable rate; restructuring contracts to separate taxable and exempt components appropriately; maximising eligible ITC (including ITC on capital goods); utilising zero-rating for exports and supplies to SEZs; and ensuring that all available exemptions are correctly applied. GST advisory does not involve tax evasion — it focuses on ensuring the correct tax is paid on the correct basis, with all legitimate credits and exemptions claimed accurately.
- **We are a SaaS company. Do we need to pay GST on services provided to overseas customers?** SaaS services provided to overseas customers constitute export of services — and are zero-rated under GST (provided the place of supply is outside India, the payment is received in convertible foreign exchange, and the service recipient is outside India). No GST is payable on such exports, and the exporter can either claim a refund of GST paid on inputs (under the accumulated ITC route) or export under LUT (Letter of Undertaking) without paying IGST. This classification is critical — incorrect treatment as a taxable domestic supply results in unnecessary tax outflow and missed refund opportunities.
- **We're in talks to acquire a competitor. What GST due diligence should we conduct before the acquisition?** GST due diligence for M&A should cover: (a) verification of GST registration history — active registrations, cancellations, and state-wise coverage; (b) review of GSTR-1, GSTR-3B, and GSTR-9 for the past 3–5 years for filing accuracy and completeness; (c) ITC claims validation — any excess claims create future liability; (d) pending GST notices, demand orders, and litigation; (e) e-invoicing compliance history; (f) RCM compliance; (g) assessment of any GST liability arising from the transaction structure itself (slump sale vs. itemised sale has different GST treatment). GST liabilities discovered post-acquisition can be significant and may not be indemnifiable — pre-acquisition due diligence is essential.
