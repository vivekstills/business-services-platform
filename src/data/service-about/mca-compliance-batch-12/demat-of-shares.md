# Demat of Shares

## Detailed Overview

Dematerialization (Demat) of shares is the process of converting physical share certificates into electronic format held in a demat account with a Depository Participant (DP) — registered with NSDL or CDSL. The Companies (Prospectus and Allotment of Securities) Rules mandate that certain categories of companies issue and hold their shares only in demat form:

- All listed companies
- All public companies (mandatory)
- All unlisted public companies (mandatory)
- Specified private companies above certain thresholds

For private companies, demat is increasingly mandated for holding subsidiaries of listed companies and for certain large unlisted companies.

## Applicability

| Company Type | Demat Mandatory |
| --- | --- |
| Listed companies | Yes (since 1996) |
| Unlisted public companies | Yes (mandatory since 2018) |
| Private companies (large) | Yes (specified) |
| Other private companies | Optional (but increasingly common) |

## Benefits

- Eliminates physical certificate risks (loss, damage, theft)
- Faster share transfers (T+2 settlement)
- Reduced paperwork
- Online portfolio management
- Required for IPO/listing
- Mandatory compliance for certain companies

## Risks / Penalties

| Default | Penalty |
| --- | --- |

| Continued non-compliance | Restrictions on share transfers |

## Process / Procedure

- Engage Depository Participant (DP) — bank/broker
- Open Beneficial Owner Account (Demat account) at company level
- Appoint Registrar and Transfer Agent (RTA)
- Apply for ISIN through RTA
- Communicate to shareholders about demat
- Shareholders open individual demat accounts
- Submit physical certificates for dematerialization
- RTA cancels physical certificates and credits demat accounts

## Frequently Asked Questions

1. **Is demat mandatory for all private companies?** Currently mandatory for unlisted public companies and certain large private companies (particularly those that are subsidiaries of listed companies, or with ≥ ₹50 crore paid-up capital). Most other private companies have demat as optional.
2. **What is ISIN and why is it needed?** ISIN (International Securities Identification Number) is a unique 12-character code identifying each security globally. Required for trading and for demat purposes. Each class of shares has its own ISIN.
3. **Can shareholders refuse to convert their physical shares to demat?** For mandatory demat companies, transfer of physical shares is restricted — shareholders are essentially compelled to demat to enable any transfer. They retain ownership but in electronic form.
4. **What's the difference between NSDL and CDSL?** NSDL (National Securities Depository) and CDSL (Central Depository Services) are India's two depositories. Both serve similar functions. Companies typically have ISINs with both for shareholder convenience.
5. **Do small private companies benefit from voluntary demat?** Yes — easier share transfers (especially in fundraising), reduced paperwork in M&A, modernized governance, IPO readiness, and investor preference. Many startups voluntarily demat shares before Series A funding.
