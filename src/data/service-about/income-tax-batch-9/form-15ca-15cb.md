# Form 15CA & 15CB filing

## Overview

Form 15CA and Form 15CB are compliance documents required under Section 195 of the Income Tax Act, 1961 for remittances made to non-residents or foreign companies from India. They serve as the mechanism through which the Income Tax department monitors and tracks all foreign remittances — ensuring that applicable Tax Deducted at Source (TDS) has been correctly deducted and deposited before money leaves India.
Form 15CA is a declaration filed by the remitter (the person making the foreign payment) on the Income Tax e-filing portal. It contains details of the remittance — amount, nature, applicable TDS rate, DTAA (Double Taxation Avoidance Agreement) provisions relied upon, and confirmation that TDS has been or will be deposited.
Form 15CB is a certificate issued by a Chartered Accountant certifying the nature of the remittance, the applicable TDS rate under the Income Tax Act or DTAA, and confirming that the remitter has complied with the TDS provisions. Form 15CB must be obtained from a CA before filing certain categories of Form 15CA.
Together, these forms are submitted to the Authorised Dealer (AD) bank — which then processes the foreign remittance through SWIFT or other channels. Without the 15CA/15CB, AD banks are legally prohibited from processing foreign payments above the specified thresholds.
### The Relationship Between 15CA and 15CB:

| Scenario | 15CA Required | 15CB Required |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Remittance not taxable in India | Part A of 15CA (if < ₹5 lakh p.a.) | Not required |
| Remittance not taxable — certificate from AO | Part B of 15CA | Not required |
| Taxable remittance with 15CB | Part C of 15CA | Required |
| Remittance under specified exempt list | Part D of 15CA | Not required |


## Applicability

Form 15CA and 15CB are required for:

- All foreign remittances from India that are chargeable to tax under the Income Tax Act
- Payments to non-resident individuals, foreign companies, and overseas entities — royalties, technical fees, professional services, interest, dividends, rent, salaries to non-residents
- FDI-related outflows — loan repayments to foreign lenders, management fees to overseas group companies
- Payments under international software licenses, content licensing, and IP royalties
- Remittances under import of services from foreign vendors
**Not required for:**

- Remittances specifically listed in Rule 37BB(3) — a list of 33 specified categories including imports of goods, travel, education, medical treatment, and other current account transactions with documentary evidence
- Personal remittances below ₹5 lakh per financial year (Part D of 15CA — simplified declaration)
- Payments where the Assessing Officer has given a specific lower TDS or nil TDS certificate
- Applicable pan-India — all banks and entities making foreign remittances across all states must comply with 15CA/15CB requirements.


## Features

Four parts of Form 15CA: Part A (remittance up to ₹5 lakh, not taxable), Part B (AO certificate obtained for lower/nil TDS), Part C (taxable remittance above ₹5 lakh requiring CA certificate), Part D (exempt remittances from the Rule 37BB list)
Form 15CB — CA certification: Issued by a practising CA with a membership number — certifying DTAA applicability, TDS rate, nature of payment, and compliance
Online filing: Both 15CA and 15CB are filed online on the Income Tax e-filing portal (incometax.gov.in)
DTAA application: 15CB certification involves analysing the applicable DTAA between India and the recipient's country — for reduced TDS rates
Bank submission: Filed 15CA/15CB must be shared with the AD bank before the remittance is processed — banks maintain records as per RBI/FEMA guidelines
Multiple remittances — multiple forms: Each distinct remittance transaction requires a separate Form 15CA — the forms are not cumulative
TRC (Tax Residency Certificate) requirement: For DTAA benefits claimed in 15CB, the non-resident must provide a TRC from their country's tax authority — validating their tax residency
Revision allowed: Filed Form 15CA can be withdrawn within 7 days of filing if the remittance has not yet been made

## Benefits

DTAA benefit optimisation: Professional 15CB preparation ensures the most favourable DTAA provisions are applied — reducing TDS from the standard rate (often 30–40%) to the treaty rate (often 10–15%) — significant savings on cross-border payments
Bank compliance facilitation: Without 15CA/15CB, AD banks cannot process the remittance — proper filing ensures smooth and timely payment to overseas vendors, contractors, and group companies
Penalty avoidance: Failure to file 15CA/15CB before remittance attracts a penalty of ₹1 lakh per default under Section 271-I of the Income Tax Act — professional filing prevents this exposure
Tax treaty documentation: 15CB serves as a contemporaneous documentation of the DTAA position taken — protects against future reassessment challenges by the Income Tax department
FPI and FDI compliance: For foreign investors receiving dividend, interest, or royalty remittances from India, 15CA/15CB creates a paper trail that supports their home country tax compliance
Transfer pricing alignment: For intra-group service payments, 15CB analysis aligns with the transfer pricing documentation — ensuring consistent characterisation of the payment

## Process

1. Step 1 — Determine Form Category Identify the nature and amount of remittance. Determine whether Part A, B, C, or D of Form 15CA applies based on taxability and amount.
2. Step 2 — Obtain CA Certificate (Form 15CB) — for Part C Engage a Chartered Accountant to issue Form 15CB. The CA reviews the invoice, agreement, DTAA provisions, TRC of the non-resident, and computes the applicable TDS rate. The CA files Form 15CB online on the Income Tax portal and provides the acknowledgement number.
3. Step 3 — Deduct and Deposit TDS Deduct TDS at the applicable rate (as certified in 15CB) from the remittance amount. Deposit TDS using Challan No. 281 with the bank or on the income tax portal.
4. Step 4 — File Form 15CA on Income Tax Portal Log in to incometax.gov.in. Navigate to e-File → Income Tax Forms → Form 15CA. Select the applicable Part (A/B/C/D). For Part C, enter the 15CB acknowledgement number. Fill all details — remitter PAN, remittee details, country, amount, nature of payment, TDS deducted.
5. Step 5 — Submit and Download Submit Form 15CA. Download the filed Form 15CA with the Acknowledgement Number.
6. Step 6 — Submit to AD Bank Provide the bank with the 15CA (and 15CB, if Part C) along with the remittance instruction. The bank verifies the documents and processes the foreign payment.
7. Step 7 — Issue Form 16A / TDS Certificate Issue Form 16A (TDS Certificate) to the non-resident for the TDS deducted — enabling them to claim credit in their home country (under the DTAA).

## Errors

- Filing Part D for a taxable remittance: Incorrectly treating a taxable payment (e.g., royalty, technical fees) as an exempt Part D remittance — this is one of the most common and serious errors, attracting penalty under Section 271-I
- Not obtaining TRC before claiming DTAA benefit: Claiming reduced DTAA rates in 15CB without a valid Tax Residency Certificate from the non-resident — the DTAA benefit can be denied during reassessment
- Incorrect nature of payment characterisation: Mischaracterising a payment as "import of goods" (exempt) when it is actually "import of services" (taxable) — e.g., paying for software licenses and treating it as goods purchase
- Filing 15CA without 15CB for Part C remittances: Attempting to file Part C of Form 15CA without obtaining the 15CB acknowledgement number from the CA — the portal requires the 15CB ARN for Part C
- Not withdrawing cancelled remittances: Filing Form 15CA and then not making the remittance without withdrawing the form — creates a mismatch in the IT department's remittance tracking database


## Frequently asked questions

- **Our company pays a monthly retainer to a US-based software consultant. Do we need 15CA/15CB for every monthly payment?** Yes — each monthly remittance is a separate payment and requires a separate Form 15CA. For recurring payments of the same nature and amount, the Form 15CB (CA certificate) may be reused for a financial year if the payment terms are identical — check with your CA on whether a single 15CB covers the entire year's payments or needs to be refreshed annually. The 15CA must be filed separately for each remittance transaction. For efficiency, many companies batch these filings at the beginning of the year with a comprehensive 15CB covering the year's expected remittances.
- **The DTAA between India and Singapore reduces withholding tax on royalties to 10%. How do we claim this rate in Form 15CB?** To claim the DTAA-reduced rate of 10% in Form 15CB, your CA needs: (a) a Tax Residency Certificate (TRC) from the Singapore tax authority (IRAS) confirming the recipient is a tax resident of Singapore; (b) Form 10F self-declaration from the Singapore entity; (c) a copy of the relevant royalty/license agreement; and (d) the Singapore entity's PAN in India (if available). The CA reviews the DTAA article on royalties, confirms eligibility, and certifies the 10% rate in Form 15CB. Without TRC, the CA must apply the higher domestic rate (typically 20% plus surcharge and cess for royalties under Section 115A).
- **We forgot to file 15CA before processing a remittance through our bank. What should we do?** This is a significant violation — the bank should not have processed the remittance without 15CA. File Form 15CA immediately on the income tax portal (post-facto filing, even though the remittance has already occurred). Simultaneously, consult a CA about whether a voluntary disclosure or penalty compounding is advisable — the penalty under Section 271-I is ₹1 lakh per default. Inform your bank's forex/trade finance team about the oversight. Going forward, establish a pre-clearance checklist requiring 15CA/15CB confirmation before any remittance instruction is processed.
- **We are importing goods from Germany — not services. Do we still need 15CA/15CB?** Import of goods is listed in Rule 37BB(3) as an exempt category — Form 15CA Part D is sufficient (no 15CB required) for genuine import of goods transactions. However, ensure the transaction is genuinely a goods import — physical goods with a customs Bill of Entry, not a software/service bundled as goods. Post-GST and OIDAR regulations, the Income Tax department scrutinizes "goods vs. services" characterisation carefully. Software downloaded electronically (even if it appears to be a product) is often treated as a service payment requiring full 15CB treatment. Your CA should review the specific transaction before defaulting to the goods exemption.
- **The non-resident recipient is providing services from their home country and will never visit India. Is TDS still applicable and is 15CA/15CB required?** Yes — Section 195 applies to all payments to non-residents that are chargeable to tax in India, regardless of where the service is physically performed. The taxability depends on whether the income has a source in India (which payments for services rendered to an Indian entity generally do). However, if the non-resident entity is resident in a country with which India has a DTAA, and the payment constitutes "business profits" (rather than royalty or FTS — Fees for Technical Services), the non-resident may not be taxable in India unless they have a Permanent Establishment (PE) in India. This is a nuanced analysis — engage a CA to review the specific DTAA provisions, the nature of services, and PE risk before concluding on taxability and the 15CA/15CB requirements.
