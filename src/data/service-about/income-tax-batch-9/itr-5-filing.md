# ITR-5 form filing

## Overview

ITR-5 is the Income Tax Return form prescribed for entities other than individuals, HUFs, and companies — specifically Partnership Firms, Limited Liability Partnerships (LLPs), Association of Persons (AOPs), Body of Individuals (BOIs), Artificial Juridical Persons, Cooperative Societies, Local Authorities, and registered societies. It is a comprehensive return form that captures the full income profile of these entities — business income, capital gains, house property income, and other sources — along with complete financial statements.
For partnership firms and LLPs — which represent a large proportion of India's professional services, manufacturing, and trading businesses — ITR-5 is the annual tax filing form that consolidates all income, deductions, TDS credits, and tax payments into a verified, legally filed return.
ITR-5 requires financial statements (P&L account and Balance Sheet), depreciation schedules, partner/member details, and profit sharing ratio information. For firms and LLPs with annual turnover above ₹1 crore (or professional receipts above ₹50 lakh), a tax audit under Section 44AB is also mandatory — and the audit report must be filed before the ITR-5 is submitted.

## Features

Profit sharing schedule: Detailed capture of each partner's / member's name, PAN, profit share ratio, salary, and interest from the firm
Separate books compliance: ITR-5 requires full P&L and Balance Sheet — trading account data is required for manufacturing/trading firms
Tax audit integration: For audit cases, Form 3CA (for firms with statutory audit) or 3CB (for firms without statutory audit) and Form 3CD are filed by the auditing CA and referenced in ITR-5
Carry-forward losses: Full set-off and carry-forward schedules — business loss, capital loss, unabsorbed depreciation
TDS credit for firm: TDS deducted on firm's income (rent received, professional fees, etc.) is claimed as credit in the firm's ITR-5
Advance tax reporting: Advance tax paid in four installments is reported with challan details
Complete MAT applicability for LLPs: LLPs are not subject to MAT — important distinction from companies
AMT (Alternate Minimum Tax): LLPs with adjusted total income above ₹20 lakh are subject to AMT at 18.5% — Form 29C (AMT computation) must be filed if AMT liability arises

## Benefits

Entity-level tax compliance: ITR-5 ensures the firm/LLP meets its own tax obligations — separate from the partners' individual returns
Partner income legitimisation: A properly filed ITR-5 establishes the firm's income and the partners' shares — essential for partners to correctly report their salary/interest in individual returns
Business credibility: Firms with consistent ITR-5 filing history demonstrate financial accountability — important for bank credit, government contracts, and enterprise client onboarding
Loss carry-forward: Firm-level business losses can be carried forward for 8 years — ITR-5 captures and preserves these loss carry-forwards
TDS refund: Excess TDS on firm income can be claimed as a refund in the firm's ITR-5 — improving the firm's working capital
Statutory audit alignment: For firms requiring statutory audit (LLPs with turnover above ₹40 lakh or contribution above ₹25 lakh), the statutory audit and tax audit together provide a comprehensive financial compliance framework

## Process

1. Step 1 — Prepare Financial Statements Prepare firm's P&L account and Balance Sheet. Compute income under each head. Prepare depreciation schedule as per Income Tax Act rules.
2. Step 2 — Tax Audit (if applicable) Engage a CA for tax audit if turnover exceeds ₹1 crore. File Form 3CA/3CB and 3CD on the Income Tax portal before October 31.
3. Step 3 — Compute Firm's Total Income Business income (after audit adjustments), capital gains, house property income, and other sources income are totaled. Apply partner's salary and interest deduction under Section 40(b).
4. Step 4 — File ITR-5 Online Log in to incometax.gov.in using the firm's PAN credentials. Select ITR-5. Fill all schedules — financial statements, partner details, capital gains, deductions, tax computation.
5. Step 5 — Pay Tax Firms pay tax at 30% (plus surcharge and cess on applicable income). Pay any balance tax via Challan 280.
6. Step 6 — Verify Using DSC ITR-5 must be verified using the DSC of any one partner/designated partner — Aadhaar OTP is not available for firm-level returns.

## Errors

- Partners' salary exceeding Section 40(b) limits: Deducting partners' salary in excess of the limits prescribed under Section 40(b) of the Income Tax Act — leads to disallowance and additional tax
- Not filing ITR-5 even if the firm had no business activity: A dormant firm with a PAN is still required to file ITR-5 (NIL return) — missing this triggers notice and penalty
- Incorrect partner PAN entry: Wrong PAN of any partner leads to incorrect TDS credit and potential notices to both the firm and the partner
- Not accounting for AMT on LLP: LLPs with significant exemptions (Section 10AA, etc.) may attract AMT at 18.5% on adjusted total income — missing AMT computation is a common oversight


## Frequently asked questions

- **Our LLP had no income this year — it was dormant. Do we still need to file ITR-5?** Yes. Even a dormant LLP with nil income must file ITR-5 (as a NIL return). The LLP's PAN remains active, and the Income Tax department expects an annual return — non-filing is treated as failure to file, attracting late fee and potential notice. Filing a NIL ITR-5 takes minimal effort and keeps the LLP's compliance record clean. Similarly, the LLP must file its MCA Annual Return (Form 11 and Form 8) and maintain a bank account — all of which are obligations regardless of activity level.
- **What is the Section 40(b) limit for partner's salary deduction in a firm's ITR-5?** Section 40(b) of the Income Tax Act prescribes the maximum salary payable to working partners that is deductible as a business expense in the firm's computation. The limits are: (a) on the first ₹3 lakh of book profit (or where book profit is a loss): ₹1.5 lakh or 90% of book profit, whichever is higher; (b) on the balance of book profit: 60% of the remaining book profit. "Book profit" is the net profit of the firm as per P&L, adjusted for specific additions and deductions. Salary to partners beyond these limits is disallowed and added back to the firm's income. The partnership deed must specifically authorise the salary — salary not authorised by the deed is entirely disallowed.
- **Our LLP is required to file a statutory audit under the LLP Act. Does this satisfy the tax audit requirement under Section 44AB?** No. The statutory audit under the LLP Act (required by MCA for LLPs with turnover above ₹40 lakh or contribution above ₹25 lakh) and the tax audit under Section 44AB of the Income Tax Act are separate requirements. The statutory audit examines financial statements for true and fair view; the tax audit examines income computation and tax compliance under the Income Tax Act. Both are typically conducted by the same CA but produce different reports — the statutory auditor's report (under LLP Act) and Form 3CA/3CD (under Income Tax Act). If turnover exceeds ₹1 crore, both must be conducted and filed separately.
- **Can a firm claim House Rent Allowance (HRA) or personal deductions in ITR-5?** No. HRA, personal deductions under Chapter VIA (80C, 80D, etc.), and other personal allowances are individual-specific deductions under the Income Tax Act — they cannot be claimed by firms, LLPs, or other non-individual entities. A firm pays tax on its net income at the flat rate of 30% plus surcharge. The only deductions available to a firm are business deductions under Section 30–37 (rent, salaries, depreciation, etc.) and specific deductions like Section 80G (donations). Partners can claim personal deductions in their individual ITR-3 returns for their individual tax computations.
- **We are converting our partnership firm to an LLP. What are the ITR filing implications during the conversion year?** In the conversion year: (a) the partnership firm must file ITR-5 for the period it operated as a partnership — from April 1 to the date of conversion to LLP; (b) the LLP must file a separate ITR-5 for the period after conversion — from the date of LLP incorporation to March 31. The conversion itself, if done under the LLP Act provisions (by registering the firm as an LLP with the MCA), is generally tax-neutral — no capital gains arise on the transfer of assets from the firm to the LLP (subject to continuity conditions). However, ensure the tax audit (if applicable) covers both periods and the PAN of the firm and LLP are separately managed.
