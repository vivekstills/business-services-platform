# TAN registration

## Overview

TAN (Tax Deduction and Collection Account Number) is a unique 10-character alphanumeric code issued by the Income Tax Department of India to every person or entity that is required to deduct tax at source (TDS — Tax Deducted at Source) or collect tax at source (TCS — Tax Collected at Source) under the provisions of the Income Tax Act, 1961. TAN is issued under Section 203A of the Income Tax Act and is mandatory for all tax deductors.
Like PAN, TAN is issued through the authorised agencies NSDL and UTIITSL. The TAN format is: AAAA12345A — 4 letters (first indicates city code, next 3 are random), 5 numbers (sequential), and 1 check digit.
Every employer deducting TDS from employees' salaries, every business making payments to contractors (TDS under Section 194C), every company paying rent above ₹50,000/month (TDS under Section 194-I), and every entity collecting TCS on specified goods and services must obtain a TAN and use it for all TDS/TCS compliance — including TDS payments, TDS returns (24Q, 26Q, 27Q, 27EQ), and TDS certificates (Form 16, 16A, 27D).
Quoting an incorrect or false TAN — or making TDS payments without a TAN — attracts a penalty of ₹10,000 under Section 272BB of the Income Tax Act.


## Applicability

TAN is mandatory for any person/entity who:

| TDS/TCS Obligation | Who Needs TAN |
| --- | --- |
| Deducts TDS on salary | All employers |
| Deducts TDS on contractor payments | All businesses making payments to contractors |
| Deducts TDS on rent | Individuals/HUFs paying rent above ₹50,000/month |
| Deducts TDS on professional/technical fees | Businesses paying professional fees |
| Deducts TDS on interest, dividends | Banks, NBFCs, companies |
| Deducts TDS on property purchase | Individual buyers of property above ₹50 lakh |
| Collects TCS on sale of goods | Sellers of specified goods (Section 206C) |
| Deducts TDS on foreign remittances | Businesses making payments to non-residents |

**Not required for:**

- Individuals deducting TDS on property purchase — they use PAN (Form 26QB) without TAN
- Individuals deducting TDS on rent payment under Section 194-IB — Form 26QC, no TAN needed

## Features

Unique 10-digit TAN: Separate from PAN — used exclusively for TDS/TCS compliance
City-specific first character: The first letter of TAN indicates the city/region of the assessing officer's jurisdiction
Mandatory quoting: TAN must be quoted on all TDS payment challans (Challan 281), TDS returns, and TDS certificates — failure attracts ₹10,000 penalty
One TAN per PAN: Generally, one TAN is issued per entity. Large organizations with multiple branches can obtain a separate TAN for each branch, but all TDS returns for a PAN can be filed under a single TAN
Online application: TAN application (Form 49B) can be filed online through NSDL or UTIITSL portals
No Aadhaar linkage required: Unlike PAN, TAN does not require Aadhaar linking
TAN verification: TAN details can be verified on the Income Tax portal — valid TANs show the entity name and jurisdiction
Traces portal: TDSCPC.gov.in (Tax Deduction and Collection System) is the portal where TDS returns are filed, TDS certificates are generated, and TAN-based compliance is managed

## Benefits

Legal TDS compliance: TAN is the legal identifier for all TDS/TCS activities — without TAN, TDS payments cannot be made through the banking system (banks require TAN for Challan 281)
TDS certificate issuance: Form 16, Form 16A, Form 27D (TCS certificates) can only be generated by entities with a valid TAN — deductees need these certificates to claim TDS credit
TRACES portal access: TAN registration enables access to the TRACES portal for downloading TDS certificates, reconciling Form 26AS, and managing TDS compliance
Avoid higher TDS: Deductees receiving payments without TAN-backed TDS deduction face higher TDS rates — proper TAN compliance benefits both deductor and deductee
Payroll compliance: Employers must have TAN for monthly salary TDS (Section 192), Form 24Q filing, and Form 16 issuance — TAN is the backbone of payroll tax compliance
Bank mandate: Banks process TDS payment through Challan 281 only against a valid TAN — a TAN is operationally essential for any business making significant vendor or employee payments

## Process

1. Step 1 — Apply Online (Form 49B) Visit tin.nsdl.com or utiitsl.com. Click on "Apply for New TAN" — "Online Application for TAN (Form 49B)." Select the deductor type (company, firm, individual, etc.).
2. Step 2 — Fill Application Details Complete Form 49B — deductor's name, address, PAN, contact details, and nature of business. For companies, enter CIN and director details.
3. Step 3 — Pay TAN Application Fee Pay the application fee online (₹65 + GST = approximately ₹77 total) via net banking, debit card, or credit card.
4. Step 4 — Acknowledgement Upon submission, a 14-digit acknowledgement number is generated. The form can be submitted online (for most cases) or a physical copy sent to NSDL/UTIITSL for cases requiring physical verification.
5. Step 5 — Processing and TAN Issuance NSDL/UTIITSL processes the application and verifies details. TAN is issued within 7–15 working days. The TAN allotment letter is sent to the registered address.
6. Step 6 — Register on TRACES Portal After TAN receipt, register on the TRACES portal (tdscpc.gov.in) using the TAN, PAN, and a token number from a filed TDS statement (or for new deductors, use the Conso file of the first return). TRACES registration enables TDS return filing, TDS certificate generation, and TAN-linked compliance management.
7. Step 7 — Link with Banking for Challan 281 Update the TAN with your bank for TDS payment — the bank registers the TAN in their system to process Challan 281 payments.

## Errors

- Applying for TAN without PAN: TAN application requires the applicant's PAN — applying without PAN leads to rejection
- Using wrong TAN on challans: Quoting an incorrect or a previously surrendered TAN on Challan 281 — the TDS credit does not reflect in the deductee's Form 26AS, causing disputes
- Not registering on TRACES after TAN allotment: Many new deductors receive TAN but don't register on TRACES — inability to file TDS returns and generate certificates follows
- Multiple TANs for the same entity: Inadvertently applying for multiple TANs for the same entity — surrender duplicates immediately to avoid compliance complications


## Frequently asked questions

- **We are a startup that just hired our first employee at ₹8 lakh annual salary. Do we need a TAN?** Yes. As soon as your company has an employee whose salary income exceeds the basic exemption limit (₹2.5 lakh under the old regime or ₹3 lakh under the new regime), TDS on salary (Section 192) becomes applicable. Even if TDS is zero (because the employee's income after deductions doesn't result in taxable income), the employer must obtain a TAN and file a quarterly TDS return (Form 24Q) — even if it is a NIL return. Apply for TAN immediately upon hiring your first employee — before the first salary payment is due.
- **We are a freelancer (individual) who makes payments to other freelancers above ₹30,000 per contract. Do we need a TAN?** TDS under Section 194J (professional fees) at 10% is applicable when a person (other than an individual or HUF not subject to tax audit) makes professional payments above ₹30,000 per contract or ₹1 lakh per year to a single contractor. Individual freelancers who are NOT subject to tax audit are generally not required to deduct TDS on professional payments. However, if your professional receipts triggered a tax audit in the preceding year (above ₹50 lakh for professionals), you must deduct TDS and therefore need a TAN. Individual buyers of property above ₹50 lakh and individuals paying rent above ₹50,000/month have specific TDS obligations under Sections 194-IA and 194-IB respectively — but these use PAN-based forms (26QB, 26QC), not TAN.
- **Our company received a notice saying we deducted TDS but didn't quote TAN on the challan. What is the consequence and how do we fix it?** Failure to quote TAN (or quoting an incorrect TAN) on TDS payment challans attracts a penalty of ₹10,000 under Section 272BB. To fix the specific challan: file a "Challan Correction" request with the bank or through the OLTAS (Online Tax Accounting System) on the NSDL portal — to update the TAN on the challan. Once corrected, the TDS credit will reflect correctly in deductees' Form 26AS. For the forward-looking compliance, immediately update all payment mandates and accounting systems with the correct TAN. Respond to the notice with the correction evidence and request for penalty waiver based on bona fide mistake.
- **Can we use the same TAN for all our offices across different states?** Yes — one TAN can be used for TDS compliance across all offices and branches of the same legal entity, regardless of how many states they are in. However, large organizations sometimes prefer separate TANs for each branch/location for internal tracking purposes — this is permissible. If separate TANs are used for different branches, each branch files its own TDS returns under its respective TAN, and TDS certificates are issued from the respective branch TAN. Ensure all deductors' addresses in TDS returns correctly reflect the office address for the specific TAN to avoid jurisdictional confusion.
- **We obtained a TAN 3 years ago but never used it (no TDS obligations materialised). Do we need to do anything to keep it active?** Unlike PAN, TAN does not become "inoperative" from non-use. However, if you have a TAN and made no TDS deductions in any quarter, you should still file NIL TDS returns for those quarters — technically, the obligation to file TDS returns exists for every entity with a TAN, and non-filing (even NIL) can trigger notices. In practice, the TDSCPC system generates notices for TANs that filed returns in previous quarters but stopped filing — assume the deductor has active TDS obligations. If you genuinely have no TDS obligations and won't have any in the foreseeable future, you can apply for TAN deactivation/surrender at your jurisdictional TDS officer.
