# ITR-2 form filing

## Overview

ITR-2 is the Income Tax Return form prescribed for individuals and Hindu Undivided Families (HUFs) who have income from multiple sources — particularly salary, house property, and capital gains — but do not have income from business or profession. It is more comprehensive than ITR-1 (Sahaj) and is designed to capture the complexity of investors, high-income salaried employees, NRIs, and individuals with foreign income or assets.
ITR-2 is the most commonly misunderstood form — many taxpayers who file ITR-1 should actually be filing ITR-2 (because they have mutual fund capital gains, equity trading profits, rental income from multiple properties, or foreign assets). Using an incorrect form can result in income tax notices, defective return classification, and potential scrutiny.
The form captures all income heads except business/profession — making it ideal for salaried professionals who also invest actively in equities, mutual funds, and real estate.

## Features

Capital gains schedules: Detailed Schedule CG captures STCG (Short-Term Capital Gains) and LTCG (Long-Term Capital Gains) — equity shares, mutual funds, real estate, debt instruments, separately classified
Schedule AL (Assets and Liabilities): Mandatory for individuals with income exceeding ₹50 lakh — captures movable and immovable assets, investments, and liabilities
Schedule FSI (Foreign Source Income): For individuals with foreign income — salary abroad, foreign dividends, foreign interest, foreign rental income
Schedule TR (Tax Relief): For claiming DTAA relief on doubly-taxed foreign income
Schedule FA (Foreign Assets): Mandatory disclosure of foreign bank accounts, foreign immovable property, foreign financial interests
Schedule TPSA: Transfer pricing disclosure for specified domestic transactions
Multiple house properties: Unlike ITR-1, ITR-2 captures income from multiple house properties — including deemed let-out annual value computation for the second property
NRI-specific fields: Non-resident individuals use ITR-2 with specific schedules for FEMA residency status, repatriation status, and NRI-specific deductions

## Benefits

Captures full income complexity: One form covers salary, multiple properties, investments, capital gains, and foreign income — no need for multiple forms
LTCG set-off: Capital loss carry-forward and set-off opportunities are captured in ITR-2 — reducing future tax liability
NRI compliance: Provides the complete framework for NRI tax compliance — foreign income disclosure, DTAA relief, and repatriation documentation
Foreign asset compliance: Schedule FA in ITR-2 ensures compliance with the Black Money Act — mandatory disclosure of foreign assets; non-disclosure can attract penalty of ₹10 lakh per foreign asset
Loan eligibility: ITR-2 with capital gains and investment disclosures strengthens loan applications — particularly for high-value home loans and business loans
Regime optimisation: For high-income individuals, ITR-2 allows comparison of old vs. new tax regime — with capital gains and multiple deductions in play, the optimal regime choice significantly impacts net tax outflow

## Process

1. Step 1 — Determine Capital Gains Download capital gains statements from all brokers and mutual fund platforms. Classify as STCG/LTCG and by asset type (equity, debt, real estate).
2. Step 2 — Compile All Income Sources Gather salary from Form 16, house property income (rental income minus municipal taxes minus 30% standard deduction minus loan interest), and other sources income.
3. Step 3 — Compute Foreign Income and DTAA Relief If foreign income exists, compute tax payable in India and the DTAA relief available to avoid double taxation.
4. Step 4 — Prepare Schedule FA List all foreign assets — bank accounts, immovable property, investments — with acquisition cost, opening and closing values.
5. Step 5 — File ITR-2 Online Log in to incometax.gov.in. Select ITR-2. Review pre-filled data and enter all additional information — capital gains schedules, house property details, foreign income, and assets. Select tax regime.
6. Step 6 — Tax Computation and Payment Review the computed tax, surcharge, and cess. Pay any balance self-assessment tax via Challan 280.
7. Step 7 — Verify and Submit Submit and verify using Aadhaar OTP or EVC within 30 days.

## Errors

- Using ITR-1 instead of ITR-2 when capital gains exist: One of the most common errors — even ₹1 of capital gain from mutual fund redemption requires ITR-2
- Not disclosing all foreign assets in Schedule FA: Under-disclosure in Schedule FA can attract ₹10 lakh penalty per undisclosed asset under the Black Money Act — this is a critical compliance requirement
- Incorrect LTCG computation for equity: Not applying the grandfathering provision for equity LTCG (cost of acquisition = higher of actual cost or market value as on January 31, 2018) — leads to over-computation of LTCG
- Missing interest on tax-free bonds in Schedule OS: Interest on tax-free bonds is exempt but must still be disclosed — omission creates AIS mismatch notices
- Not filing Schedule AL when income exceeds ₹50 lakh: Mandatory asset and liability disclosure often omitted — leading to defective return notices


## Frequently asked questions

- **I am an NRI living in the US with Indian rental income. Which ITR should I file and do I need to declare my US income in India?** As an NRI, your India taxable income includes only income sourced in India — Indian rental income, Indian capital gains, Indian bank interest (subject to NRI-specific tax rates). Your US income is not taxable in India. File ITR-2 for NRI filers (select NRI residency status in the personal details section). Disclose Indian rental income, compute tax after applicable deductions (30% standard deduction, municipal taxes, loan interest), and claim TDS credit. You must disclose foreign assets (US bank accounts, US property, US investments) in Schedule FA even as an NRI — though this is for information purposes and these assets are not taxed in India.
- **I sold my house for ₹80 lakh after owning it for 6 years. Which ITR form do I use and how is the capital gain computed?** Use ITR-2 — long-term capital gain on immovable property is reported in Schedule CG of ITR-2. LTCG on property held for more than 2 years is taxed at 20% with indexation benefit (or 12.5% without indexation as per the Finance Act 2024 amendments — choose the more beneficial option). Compute indexed cost of acquisition using the Cost Inflation Index (CII) for the purchase year and sale year. If the LTCG is reinvested in a new residential property within 2 years (or construction within 3 years), the gain is exempt under Section 54. Alternatively, invest in Capital Gains Bonds under Section 54EC within 6 months for exemption up to ₹50 lakh.
- **I have both stock trading profits (F&O) and salary income. Can I use ITR-2?** No. F&O (Futures and Options) trading income is classified as non-speculative business income under the Income Tax Act — regardless of whether the trading is occasional or frequent. Business income disqualifies you from ITR-2. You must file ITR-3 (for individuals/HUFs with business/profession income). If your F&O turnover is within the presumptive taxation limits, you could potentially use ITR-4 — but F&O under presumptive taxation requires turnover to be calculated correctly (absolute sum of profits and losses on all F&O contracts). Misreporting F&O as capital gains in ITR-2 is a common error that attracts scrutiny.
- **How does the grandfathering provision work for Long-Term Capital Gains on equity shares and equity mutual funds?** LTCG on equity shares and equity-oriented mutual funds held for more than 12 months, in excess of ₹1 lakh per year, is taxed at 10% (12.5% from AY 2024-25 as per Finance Act 2024 amendments). The grandfathering provision applies to assets purchased before February 1, 2018 — the cost of acquisition for these assets is deemed to be the HIGHER of: (a) actual purchase price; or (b) Fair Market Value (FMV) as on January 31, 2018. This effectively exempts gains accrued before February 1, 2018. Your broker's capital gains statement typically accounts for this grandfathering automatically — verify the statement before entering data in Schedule CG of ITR-2.
- **My spouse and I jointly own 3 residential properties. We have no business income. Which form do we each file and how is rental income from the joint property split?** Both you and your spouse, being individuals without business income (assuming no other disqualifying factors), would file ITR-2 (if you have capital gains or multiple properties) or ITR-1 (only if you meet all ITR-1 criteria, which seems unlikely with 3 properties). Rental income from a jointly owned property is split between co-owners in proportion to their ownership share (typically 50:50 for spouses unless specified otherwise in the property documents). Each co-owner declares their share of rental income in their individual ITR. The 30% standard deduction, municipal taxes, and loan interest are also split in the same ownership proportion. If there is no rental agreement and both are co-owners, the income is generally split equally.
