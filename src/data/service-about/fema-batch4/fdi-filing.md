# FDI Filing

## Overview

Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) filing is a mandatory compliance requirement under the Foreign Exchange Management Act (FEMA), 1999, governed by the Reserve Bank of India (RBI). Whenever a company incorporated in India receives foreign investment — whether through equity, compulsorily convertible instruments, or other approved routes — it must report the same to the RBI within prescribed timelines. This reporting is done through the RBI's FIRMS (Foreign Investment Reporting and Management System) portal.

FDI compliance is not optional. Failure to report timely exposes Indian companies to compounding penalties, regulatory scrutiny, and restrictions on future foreign investment. Whether you are a startup receiving angel funding from an NRI, a private limited company attracting venture capital from a foreign entity, or a wholly owned subsidiary receiving share capital from its overseas parent, FDI filing obligations apply to you.

## Applicability

FDI filing requirements apply to:

- Indian companies (private limited, public limited, LLPs with FEMA approval) receiving foreign investment
- Companies issuing equity shares, preference shares, or compulsorily convertible debentures/preference shares (CCDs/CCPs) to foreign investors
- Indian residents transferring shares to non-residents or vice versa
- Foreign portfolio investors (FPI) crossing reporting thresholds
- Downstream investments by Indian entities that have received FDI
- Joint ventures with foreign collaboration agreements
- ESOP issuances to foreign employees of group companies

Applicability across India: FDI filing is a central compliance governed by RBI and FEMA, applicable uniformly across all states — Maharashtra, Delhi, Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, Telangana, Gujarat, West Bengal, Rajasthan, and others. State-level permissions may vary for certain sectors (e.g., real estate, retail), but the FDI reporting obligation to RBI remains pan-India.

### Governing Framework

- FEMA, 1999 — Primary legislation

- RBI Master Direction on Foreign Investment in India

### Pricing Guidelines

Shares cannot be issued to foreign investors below the fair market value (FMV) determined by a SEBI-registered merchant banker or a Chartered Accountant under DCF (Discounted Cash Flow) method. Shares cannot be transferred to a non-resident below FMV; transfers from non-resident to resident cannot be above FMV.

## Benefits

- Regulatory compliance: Avoids compounding penalties under FEMA which can be up to 3 times the foreign exchange involved or INR 2 lakh per day of default
- Clean cap table: Properly filed FDI ensures a clean and auditable cap table — essential for future fundraising rounds and M&A transactions
- Investor confidence: Overseas investors require proof of RBI reporting before remitting additional tranches
- Ease of repatriation: Future dividend repatriation, share buyback, and liquidation proceeds can only be repatriated if original investment is properly reported
- Legal certainty: Avoids risk of investment being treated as "illegal" under FEMA, attracting ED scrutiny
- Supports IPO readiness: SEBI and stock exchanges require clean FEMA compliance history during DRHP review

## Limitations

- Prohibited sectors: FDI cannot be accepted in lottery, gambling, chit funds (non-Nidhis), real estate business (not real estate construction), manufacturing of tobacco products, etc.
- Pricing constraints: Issuing shares at a price lower than FMV is not permitted, limiting flexibility in deal structuring
- Downstream compliance burden: If the funded entity makes further investments in Indian companies, downstream reporting requirements add layers of compliance
- Government route delays: Sectors requiring government approval (FIPB route, now replaced by respective ministries) can have approval timelines of 4–8 weeks or more
- FEMA violations are strict: Unlike company law where compounding is straightforward, FEMA violations attract ED attention and compounding procedures before RBI are more rigorous
- LLP restrictions: FDI in LLPs is permitted only in sectors where 100% FDI is allowed under automatic route; LLPs cannot accept FDI under government route

## Documents Required

### For FC-GPR Filing

- **Board resolution** — Approving allotment of shares to foreign investor
- **Special resolution (if applicable)** — For private placement under Companies Act
- **Valuation certificate** — From CA/merchant banker certifying FMV under DCF or NAV method
- **KYC of foreign investor** — Banker's certificate or passport/incorporation documents
- **FIRC / bank certificate** — Foreign Inward Remittance Certificate evidencing receipt of funds
- **Debit authorisation** — From the AD (Authorised Dealer) bank
- **Share allotment letter** — Copy of allotment letter issued to investor
- **Shareholding pattern** — Pre and post allotment

### Additional for FC-TRS

- Share purchase/transfer agreement
- Consent letter from buyer and seller
- Board resolution approving transfer
- Form SH-4 (instrument of transfer under Companies Act)

## Process

1. Step 1 — Pre-Filing Compliance Check: Review the sector of the company, identify applicable FDI cap, determine whether automatic or government route applies, and verify that the investee company is eligible to receive FDI (not in negative/restricted list).
1. Step 2 — Valuation: Obtain FMV valuation certificate from a SEBI-registered merchant banker or CA using the DCF method (for unlisted companies) or market price (for listed entities).
1. Step 3 — Receive Funds and Obtain FIRC: Ensure funds are received through proper banking channels. Obtain FIRC or bank certificate from the AD bank.
1. Step 4 — Allotment of Shares: Pass board resolution, allot shares within 60 days of receipt of consideration, and file PAS-3 with MCA within 30 days of allotment.
1. Step 5 — FIRMS Portal Registration and Filing: Register on RBI's FIRMS portal. File the relevant form (FC-GPR, FC-TRS, etc.) with all supporting documents within the prescribed timeline.
1. Step 6 — AD Bank Authentication: The AD bank authenticates the filing on the FIRMS portal after verifying the FIRC and KYC documents.
1. Step 7 — Unique Identification Number (UIN): Upon successful filing, RBI issues a UIN (formerly FRN — Foreign Registration Number) which must be preserved for all future reference.
1. Step 8 — Post-Filing Record Keeping: Maintain records of all filings for at least 10 years. Update RBI filing details in the annual FLA Return.

## Compliance Requirements

- Timeline adherence: FC-GPR within 30 days of allotment; FC-TRS within 60 days of transfer
- Annual FLA Return: Every entity that has received FDI or made ODI must file the FLA Return with RBI by July 15 each year
- Reporting of downstream investments: Entities funded by foreign investment must report their own investments in Indian entities
- Pricing compliance: Annual review recommended to ensure share transfers are within FMV bands
- Compounding for late filing: Delays must be compounded before RBI; delaying compounding further increases penalty exposure
- AD Bank intimation: The company's AD bank must be kept informed of all FDI receipts and filings
- No-objection from existing investors: Certain categories of share transfers require prior consent per shareholders' agreement terms

## FAQs

**Q1. What happens if I miss the FC-GPR deadline of 30 days?** Late filing constitutes a FEMA violation. You must file a compounding application before the RBI's compounding authority. The penalty is calculated based on the amount involved and the duration of delay. While compounding is available, repeated violations or large amounts can attract Enforcement Directorate (ED) scrutiny. It is strongly advisable to file proactively and seek compounding at the earliest.

**Q2. Can a startup receive FDI without a valuation certificate if the amount is small?** No. The valuation requirement under FEMA applies regardless of the quantum of investment. Every issue of shares to a foreign investor must be at or above FMV certified by a SEBI-registered merchant banker or a CA (for unlisted companies). There is no de minimis exemption under FEMA for valuation.

**Q3. Is FDI allowed in a One Person Company (OPC)?** No. OPCs are not permitted to accept FDI under FEMA. If a foreign investor wishes to invest, the OPC must first be converted into a private limited company. This is a common oversight among early-stage entrepreneurs receiving overseas angel investments.

**Q4. What is the difference between FDI and FPI for reporting purposes?** FDI involves investment of 10% or more in a company's equity and is reported through FC-GPR/FC-TRS. FPI (Foreign Portfolio Investment) is typically below 10% and handled through SEBI-registered FPI route, reported through custodians. If an FPI crosses 10% in a company, they must convert to FDI reporting within prescribed timelines.

**Q5. Is FC-TRS required for transfer of shares between two non-residents?** Yes, if the transfer involves a resident Indian company's shares (regardless of whether both buyer and seller are non-residents), FC-TRS is required within 60 days of transfer. The reporting obligation lies with the Indian company and the resident party, if any; otherwise the non-resident seller/buyer must report through their AD bank.
