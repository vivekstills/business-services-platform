# Partnership to Company Conversion

## Overview

A registered or unregistered partnership firm can be converted into a Private Limited or Public Limited Company under Section 366 of the Companies Act, 2013 read with the Companies (Authorised to Register) Rules, 2014. This conversion is popular among family businesses and trading firms that have grown in scale and seek limited liability, corporate governance, and fundraising capabilities.

## When Needed

Conversion is needed when the firm outgrows the informal structure and needs a corporate identity for raising bank loans or attracting investors; when the business needs to bid for large contracts or government tenders that require company registration; when partners wish to limit personal liability; when key employees need to be given equity through ESOPs; or when the firm wants to bring in external partners without restructuring the entire deed.

## Benefits

- Post-conversion, partners enjoy limited liability protection, and the company gains a separate legal identity.
- The company can issue shares, raise equity and debt, and participate in FDI.
- Succession planning becomes cleaner — ownership continues through shareholding.
- The company enjoys perpetual succession, making it easier to manage the business beyond the founders' active involvement.

## Limitations

- Unanimous consent of all partners is required.
- Secured creditors must provide NOCs.
- The conversion results in closure of the partnership firm (it is dissolved).
- All registrations must be updated — PAN, GSTIN, MSME, FSSAI, etc.
- Partners must take up shares in proportion to their capital, and for at least 5 years (for tax neutrality), they cannot transfer more than 50% of their voting power.

## Applicability

This conversion is available to partnership firms registered (or even unregistered) across all Indian states — Maharashtra, Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, Gujarat, Delhi, West Bengal, Haryana, Uttar Pradesh, Telangana, Kerala, Andhra Pradesh, Rajasthan, Punjab, Madhya Pradesh, Bihar, and all UTs. The application for company incorporation is made to the relevant RoC.

## Documents

- **Partnership Deed (registered/unregistered)** — Original
- **Consent of all partners** — Written and signed
- **NOC from secured creditors** — Original
- **Statement of assets & liabilities** — Certified by CA (< 6 months old)
- **Audited financial statements** — Last 3 years
- **Form URC-1** — Filed online
- **MOA & AOA of new company** — Drafted
- **DSC of all proposed directors** — Class 3
- **PAN, Aadhaar of all partners/directors** — KYC
- **Address proof of registered office** — Utility bill + NOC + rent agreement
- **Declaration by partners** — Affidavit
- **Proof of advertisement (newspaper)** — 2 newspapers

## Process

1. Pass a resolution by all partners consenting to conversion.
1. Obtain NOC from all secured creditors.
1. Advertise in 2 newspapers (one in English, one in regional language) inviting objections from creditors.
1. Prepare Statement of Assets and Liabilities certified by a CA.
1. File Form URC-1 with the RoC along with MOA, AOA, financial statements, advertisements, and declarations.
1. The RoC issues a Certificate of Registration of the company.
1. The partnership is thereby dissolved.
1. Update PAN, GSTIN, and all other registrations.

## Tax Impact

Under Section 47(xiii) of the Income Tax Act, the conversion of a partnership firm into a company is tax-neutral provided all assets and liabilities become the company's, all partners become shareholders in the same proportion as their capital, partners hold at least 50% voting power for 5 years, and no partner receives any consideration other than shares. If these conditions are violated within 5 years, the gains are taxed in the year of breach. Goodwill of the firm may be subject to capital gains if valued in the conversion. GST on conversion as a going concern is generally exempt. TDS obligations on transactions between firm and company must be noted post-conversion.

## FAQs

**Q1. Can an unregistered partnership firm convert into a company?** Yes, even unregistered partnership firms can convert into a company under Section 366. The absence of registration with the Registrar of Firms does not bar conversion.

**Q2. Is newspaper advertisement mandatory?** Yes, advertisement in two newspapers — one in English and one in the regional language of the state — is mandatory to invite objections from creditors or interested parties before conversion.

**Q3. What happens to the firm's PAN after conversion?** The firm's PAN becomes inactive. A fresh PAN is issued to the newly incorporated company. All income-earning activities after the date of conversion are reported under the company's new PAN.

**Q4. Can existing employees transfer to the company without fresh employment?** Yes, all employment contracts, their service continuity, and related PF/ESI contributions are deemed to continue. The company assumes the employer obligations of the erstwhile firm.

**Q5. Do partners need to contribute fresh capital into the company?** No, the existing capital of the partners as reflected in the firm's balance sheet is converted into share capital of the new company in the same proportion as their respective capital contributions.
