# Public to Private Company Conversion

## Overview

A Public Limited Company can be converted into a Private Limited Company under Section 14 of the Companies Act, 2013 by altering its Articles of Association. However, if the public company has issued shares to the public or has more than 200 shareholders, the conversion requires approval from the National Company Law Tribunal (NCLT) under Section 14(1) proviso. This is a significant structural step, often driven by delisting, promoter buyout, or strategic restructuring.

## When Needed

Conversion is considered when the promoters wish to take the company private (privatization / de-listing), when the company has fewer than 200 shareholders and wants simpler governance, when the company faces persistent regulatory or governance challenges under the public company regime, when private equity investors acquire majority control and wish to restructure, or when the company's scale no longer justifies the high compliance costs of a public entity.

## Benefits

- Converting to a private limited company significantly reduces the compliance burden — no SEBI LODR compliance (post-delisting), no mandatory independent directors (beyond prescribed thresholds), no quarterly disclosures, and simplified shareholder meetings.
- Share transfer restrictions can be re-introduced.
- Confidentiality of financials and strategy is better maintained under a private structure.

## Limitations

- The conversion requires NCLT approval if shares have been allotted publicly or if the shareholder count exceeds 200 — a lengthy and expensive process.
- Delisting (if listed) must be completed separately through SEBI's delisting regulations before or alongside the conversion process.
- Minority shareholders' interests must be addressed.
- If any public deposit was accepted, it must be repaid before conversion.

## Applicability

This conversion is available to public limited companies registered across India — Maharashtra, Karnataka, Delhi NCR, Tamil Nadu, Telangana, Gujarat, West Bengal, Haryana, Uttar Pradesh, Kerala, Andhra Pradesh, and other states. Listed companies must first comply with SEBI's delisting regulations before applying to NCLT/RoC for conversion.

## Documents

- **Certificate of Incorporation (Public Ltd)** — Existing
- **MOA & AOA (existing)** — Filed copy
- **Altered AOA (for private company restrictions)** — Drafted
- **Special resolution (EGM / postal ballot)** — Passed
- **MGT-14 (resolution filing)** — MCA
- **INC-27 (application for conversion)** — MCA
- **NCLT petition (if applicable)** — Filed at bench
- **Delisting approval from SEBI/Exchange** — For listed companies
- **List of shareholders (max 200 for Pvt)** — Updated
- **NOC from secured creditors** — Signed
- **Audited financial statements** — Latest year
- **DSC of directors** — Class 3

## Process

1. Pass a special resolution at the EGM authorizing the conversion and alteration of AOA.
1. File MGT-14 within 30 days.
1. If the company has more than 200 shareholders or has issued shares to the public, file a petition before the relevant NCLT bench.
1. Serve notice on all creditors and shareholders.
1. After NCLT approval, file the order and INC-27 with the RoC.
1. The RoC amends the Certificate of Incorporation to reflect "Private Limited" status.
1. For listed companies, the SEBI delisting process must be completed prior, which involves a reverse book-building process and paying the discovered price to public shareholders.

## Tax Impact

The conversion from a public to private company is tax-neutral — same PAN and CIN continue, no deemed transfer of assets, no capital gains triggered, and all tax losses, depreciation, and credits continue uninterrupted. Tax benefits applicable to public companies (such as specific exemptions or TDS rates applicable to listed securities) may change post-delisting. STT (Securities Transaction Tax) no longer applies to share transactions once the company is delisted. Buyback of shares from public shareholders during delisting is subject to buyback tax under Section 115QA in the hands of the company (20% on distributed income). Shareholders receiving buyback proceeds face no tax (exempt under Section 10(34A)), but capital gains may arise if shares are sold through the exit offer mechanism.

## FAQs

**Q1. Is NCLT approval always required for Public to Private conversion?** No. NCLT approval is required only when the company has issued shares to the public (made public offering) or has more than 200 shareholders. Unlisted public companies with fewer than 200 shareholders can convert via a simpler RoC route.

**Q2. Can a listed company convert to private without delisting?** No. A listed company must first complete the SEBI delisting process and delist from all stock exchanges before applying to convert to a private limited company. Both processes must be sequenced correctly.

**Q3. How long does the Public to Private conversion take?** For unlisted public companies (≤200 shareholders), the process takes 2–4 months. For listed companies, the SEBI delisting process alone takes 4–8 months, followed by 2–4 months for the conversion filing. Total: 6–12+ months.

**Q4. What happens to minority shareholders during this conversion?** Minority shareholders must be given a fair exit opportunity through SEBI's reverse book-building mechanism (for listed companies). For unlisted companies, minority rights must be addressed as part of the NCLT approval process.

**Q5. Can the company restore its public status after converting to private?** Yes, a private limited company can be reconverted into a public limited company at any time through the standard Private to Public conversion process — special resolution, INC-27, and RoC filing — subject to meeting the minimum membership and directorship requirements.
