# LLP to Company Conversion

## Overview

Converting an LLP into a Private Limited or Public Limited Company is governed by Section 366 of the Companies Act, 2013 read with the Companies (Authorised to Register) Rules, 2014. This process allows a registered LLP to transition into a company structure while retaining its existing business, assets, liabilities, and contracts. The conversion results in the LLP's dissolution and a new company inheriting its operations without a break in business continuity.

## When Needed

This conversion becomes relevant when the LLP wants to raise equity funding from venture capital or private equity investors who prefer company structures, plans to offer Employee Stock Options (ESOPs) to employees, seeks to expand internationally or apply for government tenders requiring company status, anticipates crossing thresholds that make a company structure more tax-efficient, or needs to list on a stock exchange. Startups registered as LLPs often convert to attract DPIIT benefits available to companies.

## Benefits

- Post-conversion, the entity can issue equity shares and raise investment from angels, VCs, and institutional investors.
- ESOPs become available as an employee retention tool.
- The company enjoys limited liability, perpetual succession, and a stronger credit profile.
- DPIIT startup recognition becomes accessible.
- Valuation of the entity becomes cleaner and easier for investors.
- Foreign Direct Investment (FDI) under the automatic route becomes straightforward.

## Limitations

- The conversion process is lengthy and involves multiple regulatory filings with MCA.
- All partners must consent to conversion — unanimity is required.
- A No Objection Certificate (NOC) from secured creditors is mandatory.
- Post-conversion, compliance requirements increase significantly compared to an LLP.
- The conversion results in a fresh Certificate of Incorporation with a new CIN, while the LLP registration is formally closed.

## Applicability

This conversion is available to LLPs registered under the LLP Act, 2008 across all Indian states — Maharashtra, Karnataka, Delhi, Tamil Nadu, Telangana, Gujarat, West Bengal, Haryana, Uttar Pradesh, Kerala, Andhra Pradesh, Rajasthan, Punjab, Madhya Pradesh, Odisha, and all Union Territories. The application is made to the Central Registration Centre (CRC) of MCA.

## Documents

- **LLP Agreement (latest amended)** — Filed copy from MCA
- **Certificate of Incorporation (LLP)** — Original
- **Consent of all partners** — Board resolution / written consent
- **NOC from secured creditors** — Original signed
- **Statement of assets & liabilities** — Certified by CA (not older than 6 months)
- **List of partners with addresses** — Self-attested
- **Declaration by designated partners** — Form URC-1
- **MOA & AOA of proposed company** — Drafted
- **PAN, Aadhaar, address proof of directors** — KYC documents
- **DSC of all proposed directors** — Class 3
- **Latest filed Form 8 & Form 11** — From MCA portal
- **Proof of registered office** — Utility bill + NOC + rent agreement

## Process

1. Begin by passing a resolution by all partners consenting to the conversion.
1. Obtain NOC from all secured creditors.
1. Prepare the Statement of Assets and Liabilities certified by a CA.
1. File Form URC-1 along with MOA, AOA, and supporting documents with the Registrar.
1. The Registrar issues a Certificate of Registration of the company.
1. The LLP is deemed dissolved from the date of this certificate.
1. File Form 14 (LLP) with the LLP Registrar to intimate dissolution.
1. Apply for fresh PAN, TAN, and update bank accounts, GST, and other registrations.

## Tax Impact

The conversion of LLP to a company is treated as a tax-neutral transfer under Section 47(xiiib) of the Income Tax Act, 1961, subject to conditions: all assets and liabilities of the LLP become assets and liabilities of the company, all partners become shareholders proportional to their capital, partners must hold at least 50% voting power in the company for 5 years, and no partner receives any consideration other than shares. If these conditions are breached within 5 years, the exemption is revoked and gains become taxable in the year of breach. No stamp duty on property transfer is applicable in most states if Section 47(xiiib) conditions are satisfied. GST implications: transfer as a going concern is exempt under Notification 12/2017 subject to conditions.

## FAQs

**Q1. Does the LLP need to be wound up formally before conversion?** No, winding up is not required. The LLP is automatically dissolved on the date of the new company's Certificate of Registration. The company inherits all assets, liabilities, and contracts.

**Q2. Can the company carry forward LLP's tax losses after conversion?** Yes, if the conditions under Section 47(xiiib) and Section 72A are satisfied, accumulated losses and unabsorbed depreciation of the LLP can be carried forward by the company.

**Q3. Is unanimous partner consent mandatory?** Yes, all partners must consent to the conversion. Even a single dissenting partner can block the conversion, unlike company resolutions that may be passed by majority.

**Q4. What happens to the LLP's GSTIN after conversion?** The GSTIN of the LLP is cancelled. A fresh GST registration must be obtained for the new company. However, unutilized ITC as of the conversion date can be transferred to the new entity using Form GST ITC-02.

**Q5. How long does the entire conversion process take?** The process typically takes 30–60 days including document preparation, filing, and MCA processing. State-level stamp duty and NOC from creditors may extend the timeline.
