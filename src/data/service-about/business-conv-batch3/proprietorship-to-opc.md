# Proprietorship to OPC Conversion

## Overview

Converting a Sole Proprietorship into a One Person Company (OPC) is not a formal "conversion" in the legal sense under the Companies Act — rather, it involves incorporating a new OPC and transferring the business, assets, and liabilities of the proprietorship to the new entity. This is one of the most common transitions for solo entrepreneurs who wish to graduate from an informal to a formal corporate structure.

## When Needed

This transition is appropriate when the proprietor wants to limit personal liability, when the business needs to establish higher credibility for bank loans, tenders, or B2B contracts, when the proprietor wants to separate personal and business finances legally, when the business is growing and needs to attract professional employees through structured compensation (including future ESOPs post-OPC to Pvt Ltd conversion), or when the entrepreneur aims to build a scalable, fundable business.

## Benefits

- The OPC provides limited liability, a separate legal identity, perpetual succession, and a more professional image.
- It enables the proprietor to open a corporate bank account, build business credit history, participate in government tenders requiring company registration, and claim business deductions more formally.
- Income is taxed at corporate rates (25%/22%), which may be lower than individual slab rates for higher incomes.

## Limitations

- The transition involves setting up a new OPC, obtaining fresh PAN, GSTIN, bank account, and updating all third-party contracts and licenses — unlike a formal conversion, there is no automatic legal continuity.
- The proprietor must bear the cost of new OPC incorporation.
- Compliance obligations increase.
- A nominee must be appointed.
- FDI is not permitted in an OPC.

## Applicability

This transition is available to proprietors operating businesses across all Indian states and UTs — Maharashtra, Karnataka, Delhi, Tamil Nadu, Gujarat, Telangana, West Bengal, Haryana, Uttar Pradesh, Kerala, Andhra Pradesh, Rajasthan, Punjab, Madhya Pradesh, Odisha, Bihar, and others. There are no state-specific restrictions on OPC formation.

## Documents

- **PAN of proprietor (for OPC)** — Mandatory
- **Aadhaar of proprietor** — ID proof
- **Passport-size photograph** — Proprietor
- **Address proof (utility bill/bank statement)** — Recent
- **Registered office proof** — Utility bill + NOC + rent agreement
- **DSC of director (proprietor)** — Class 3
- **MOA & AOA** — Drafted
- **INC-3 (nominee consent)** — Signed
- **INC-9 (declaration)** — Signed
- **Business transfer agreement** — Assets/liabilities list
- **Existing proprietorship registration proofs** — Trade license, GST, FSSAI, etc.

## Process

1. Incorporate a new OPC through SPICe+ (Steps as detailed in the OPC Registration page).
1. Once the Certificate of Incorporation is received, execute a Business Transfer Agreement (BTA) transferring all assets and liabilities of the proprietorship to the OPC.
1. The OPC pays consideration (in cash or by issuing shares) to the proprietor for the transfer.
1. Apply for fresh PAN, GSTIN, bank account, MSME, trade license, and other registrations in the OPC's name.
1. Inform suppliers, customers, and authorities of the change.
1. Cancel or surrender the proprietorship's registrations after the transfer.

## Tax Impact

Since the OPC is a new entity, the Business Transfer Agreement is a taxable transaction. Capital assets transferred attract capital gains tax in the hands of the proprietor (short-term or long-term based on holding period). Stock-in-trade transfer is taxable as business income. Goodwill transferred may attract capital gains. However, if the transfer is structured under Section 47(xiv) — where a sole proprietary concern transfers its business to a company in exchange for shares, and the proprietor holds 50% shares for 5 years — the transfer is tax-neutral. GST on the transfer of a going concern is exempt under Entry 2 of Exemption Notification 12/2017-CT(R), subject to conditions. GST registration of the proprietorship must be cancelled once transfer is complete and a fresh registration obtained for the OPC.

## FAQs

**Q1. Is there a formal "conversion" process for proprietorship to OPC?** No, unlike LLP or partnership conversions, there is no statutory conversion process. A new OPC is incorporated and the proprietorship's business is transferred via a Business Transfer Agreement.

**Q2. Can the OPC use the same trade name as the proprietorship?** Yes, subject to MCA name availability, the OPC can be incorporated with the same or similar trade name. However, the name must comply with the naming guidelines and end with "OPC Private Limited."

**Q3. What happens to the existing GST registration of the proprietorship?** The proprietary GST registration must be cancelled after the transfer. A fresh GSTIN is obtained for the OPC. Unused ITC can be transferred to the OPC using Form GST ITC-02.

**Q4. Does the transfer attract stamp duty?** Yes, the Business Transfer Agreement attracts stamp duty on the value of immovable property, if any, transferred. The rate varies by state. Movable assets and goodwill may or may not attract stamp duty depending on state laws.

**Q5. Is the OPC taxed at a lower rate than a proprietorship?** OPCs are taxed at 25% (turnover up to Rs. 400 crore) or 22% (under Section 115BAA), which can be significantly lower than the individual tax slabs of 30%+ for higher incomes. This is one of the primary tax reasons for conversion.
