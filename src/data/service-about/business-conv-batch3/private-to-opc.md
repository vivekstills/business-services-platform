# Private to OPC Company Conversion

## Overview

A Private Limited Company can be converted into a One Person Company (OPC) under Section 18 of the Companies Act, 2013 read with Rule 6(4) of the Companies (Incorporation) Rules, 2014. This is an uncommon but legally valid route, typically pursued when one shareholder acquires all shares and the business is best managed as a single-owner entity.

## When Needed

This conversion is pursued when one co-founder exits and the remaining shareholder wants to run the business solo; when the company has only one active shareholder and the structure needs to reflect operational reality; when the sole owner seeks a simpler compliance framework; or when restructuring within a group requires a single-owner entity.

## Benefits

- An OPC has slightly simplified compliance — no AGM requirement, abridged annual return (MGT-7A), and fewer governance formalities.
- It allows a single entrepreneur to retain limited liability protection and corporate structure.
- The conversion preserves the existing PAN, CIN, business history, bank accounts, and contracts.

## Limitations

- OPCs cannot have more than one shareholder or more than 15 directors.
- FDI is not permitted in OPCs.
- Voluntary conversion to OPC cannot happen within 2 years of incorporation of the original private company.
- The company cannot voluntarily convert back easily.
- The OPC must nominate a nominee.
- If the business grows substantially, mandatory or voluntary conversion back to a private limited company may be required.

## Applicability

This conversion is available across India for private limited companies registered in all states — Maharashtra, Karnataka, Delhi, Tamil Nadu, Telangana, Gujarat, West Bengal, Haryana, Uttar Pradesh, Kerala, and others. Filing is made with the relevant jurisdictional RoC through the MCA portal.

## Documents

- **Certificate of Incorporation (Pvt Ltd)** — Existing
- **MOA & AOA (Pvt Ltd)** — Existing + amended
- **Special resolution by shareholder** — Passed in EGM
- **MGT-14 (filing of special resolution)** — MCA filing
- **INC-6 (application for conversion)** — MCA filing
- **NOC from creditors** — If secured debt exists
- **Audited financial statements** — Latest year
- **Consent of nominee (INC-3)** — Nominee form
- **PAN, Aadhaar of sole shareholder** — KYC
- **DSC of director** — Class 3
- **Declaration (INC-9)** — By subscriber

## Process

1. Ensure that all shares are held by a single shareholder.
1. Hold an EGM (or resolution by sole member) and pass a special resolution to convert.
1. File MGT-14 within 30 days of the resolution.
1. File INC-6 with altered MOA and AOA, INC-3 (nominee consent), NOC from creditors, and financial statements.
1. The RoC reviews the application and issues an amended Certificate of Incorporation as an OPC.
1. Update GST, bank, and other registrations to reflect the OPC status.

## Tax Impact

The conversion from a Private Limited Company to an OPC is entirely tax-neutral — the same legal entity (same PAN and CIN) continues. There is no change of ownership (one shareholder consolidates 100%), and no deemed transfer occurs. All accumulated losses, depreciation, and tax history carry forward without interruption. Existing tax exemptions, startup benefits, and deductions continue. If the conversion involves a buyout of the exiting shareholder's shares, capital gains tax applies to the selling shareholder based on the period of holding and type of share (listed/unlisted). GST registration remains unchanged.

## FAQs

**Q1. Can a private limited company with two shareholders convert directly to an OPC?** Yes, but before filing for conversion, one shareholder must transfer all shares to the other, resulting in a single 100% shareholder. Only then can the conversion to OPC be initiated.

**Q2. Is there a minimum waiting period before conversion?** Yes, a private limited company must have been in existence for at least 2 years before voluntarily converting into an OPC, as per Rule 6(4) of the Companies (Incorporation) Rules, 2014.

**Q3. Does the OPC need a nominee?** Yes, every OPC must have a nominee who would take over in the event of the sole member's incapacitation or death. The nominee must give written consent in Form INC-3.

**Q4. Does this conversion trigger any stamp duty?** Stamp duty applies on the alteration of MOA and AOA, which varies by state. Since ownership does not change (the company continues as the same entity), no property transfer stamp duty is triggered.

**Q5. What compliances are reduced after becoming an OPC?** OPCs are exempt from holding AGMs, filing MGT-7 (they file the abridged MGT-7A instead), and certain procedural compliances related to meetings. However, statutory audit and income tax filing remain mandatory.
