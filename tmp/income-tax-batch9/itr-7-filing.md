# ITR-7 form filing

## Overview

ITR-7 is the Income Tax Return form prescribed for entities required to file returns under specific provisions of the Income Tax Act, primarily persons including companies who are required to furnish a return under Sections 139(4A), 139(4B), 139(4C), or 139(4D). In practice, ITR-7 is filed by charitable and religious trusts, political parties, scientific research institutions, universities and educational institutions, news agencies, mutual funds, hospital trusts, and other specified entities that claim income exemption under Sections 11, 12, or 10 of the Income Tax Act.

**The most common ITR-7 filers are:**

| Entity type | Applicable section |
| --- | --- |
| Charitable / religious trusts | Section 139(4A), income exempt under Section 11/12 |
| Political parties | Section 139(4B) |
| Scientific research associations, news agencies | Section 139(4C) |
| Universities, colleges, educational institutions | Section 139(4C) |
| Mutual funds | Section 139(4D) |
| Medical institutions and hospitals (trust-based) | Section 139(4A) |
| Infrastructure debt funds | Section 139(4D) |

ITR-7 is distinct from ITR-5 (for firms/LLPs) and ITR-6 (for companies), it specifically applies to entities claiming exemption under the charitable/religious or specified institutional categories. The form captures detailed information about the trust's or institution's activities, income, application of income, accumulation, and compliance with conditions for exemption.

## Applicability

**ITR-7 is applicable for:**

- Trusts registered under Section 12AA or Section 12AB of the Income Tax Act (charitable/religious trusts)
- Institutions approved under Section 10(23C), universities, educational institutions, hospitals
- Political parties claiming exemption under Section 13A
- Scientific research associations approved under Section 35(1)(ii)/(iii)
- News agencies approved under Section 10(22B)
- Trade unions, employers' associations under Section 10(24)
- Notified institutions under Section 10(23A) to 10(23F)
- Mutual funds under Section 10(23D), through SEBI-registered AMCs
- Infrastructure debt funds under Section 10(47)
- Any other entity specifically required to file under Sections 139(4A) to 139(4D)

**Not applicable for:** Individuals, HUFs, firms, LLPs, or regular companies not claiming charitable/institutional exemption.

## Key features

- Schedule ER (Application of Income): Detailed schedule of how the trust/institution applied its income, towards charitable objects, accumulation under Section 11(2), religious activities
- Schedule AI (Accumulation of Income): For trusts that accumulate income under Section 11(2), requires investment in specified modes within the prescribed period
- Audit report under Section 12A/12AB: Trust registration and audit under Section 12A/12AB is a prerequisite, auditor's report in Form 10B or 10BB must be filed
- Foreign contribution reporting: Trusts receiving foreign contributions under FCRA must disclose such amounts separately
- Section 13 violations: If any part of trust income has been used for the benefit of specified persons (trustees, donors), Schedule 13 captures these violations which can lead to denial of exemption
- Anonymous donations: Anonymous donations above the prescribed threshold are taxable at 30%, captured in ITR-7
- Corpus donations: Corpus donations (to the permanent fund) are not income and are separately reported
- Section 80G certification: Trusts with 80G approval must disclose donations received, for donors' deduction compliance

## Benefits

- **Exemption preservation:** Correct ITR-7 filing with all required disclosures preserves the trust's income exemption under Section 11/12, incorrect or missing filings can lead to denial of exemption for the year
- **Donor deduction facilitation:** Properly filed ITR-7 with Section 80G certification details enables donors to claim deductions, critical for NGO fundraising
- **Regulatory transparency:** Detailed disclosures in ITR-7 (income, expenditure, beneficiaries, governance) demonstrate accountability to donors, government, and the public
- **Carry-forward of excess application:** If a charitable trust applies more than 85% of income in a year, the excess application can be carried forward as a set-off against future years' mandatory 85% application, ITR-7 preserves this benefit
- **Audit compliance:** ITR-7 requires Form 10B/10BB audit, ensuring annual professional review of the trust's activities and finances, strengthening governance

## Process

1. **Complete trust audit** Engage a CA to conduct the audit of the trust and file Form 10B (for trusts with income above ₹5 crore or with foreign contributions or accumulation under Section 11(2)) or Form 10BB (for others) on the Income Tax portal. The UDIN of the filed audit form is referenced in ITR-7.
2. **Compute trust's income** Compute income from all sources, voluntary contributions (excluding corpus), interest, rental income, business income (from incidental business). Compute 85% mandatory application requirement and verify actual application.
3. **Prepare application and accumulation schedules** Prepare detailed Schedule ER (income application) and Schedule AI (accumulation under Section 11(2), if any). Prepare Form 10 for accumulation if income is being set aside for future years.
4. **File ITR-7 online** Log in to incometax.gov.in using trust PAN credentials. Select ITR-7. Complete all schedules, trust details, registration details, income computation, application of income, investment details, and trustee information.
5. **Verify and submit** ITR-7 can be verified through DSC of the managing trustee or by Aadhaar OTP of the authorised signatory. Submit the return.

## Common errors

- Not applying 85% of income: Failing to apply at least 85% of income towards charitable/religious objects in the year, the unapplied income becomes taxable at 30%
- Anonymous donation limit: Receiving anonymous donations above ₹1 lakh per donor or 5% of total donations (whichever is higher), the excess is taxable at 30%
- Section 13 violations: Using trust income for the benefit of trustees, founders, or their relatives, leads to complete denial of Section 11 exemption for the year
- Not filing Form 10B/10BB before ITR-7: ITR-7 requires the audit form UDIN, filing ITR-7 without completing the audit first makes the return defective

## Frequently asked questions

**Our NGO received ₹25 lakh in grants from a foreign foundation. Do we need to disclose this in ITR-7?** Yes, foreign contributions received by an NGO must be disclosed in ITR-7 under the foreign contribution schedule. Additionally, receiving foreign contributions without a valid FCRA registration is a violation of the Foreign Contribution (Regulation) Act, 2010, ensure FCRA registration is active before accepting foreign grants. The income tax treatment of FCRA grants depends on whether they are corpus donations (not income) or programme grants (income requiring 85% application). Proper documentation and characterisation of the grant is essential for correct ITR-7 reporting and FCRA annual returns.

**A charitable trust received ₹1.5 crore in donations but spent only ₹1 crore on charitable activities (67%). What are the tax implications?** The trust must apply at least 85% of its income (₹1.5 crore × 85% = ₹1.275 crore) towards its charitable objects. Since only ₹1 crore was applied (67%), the shortfall of ₹27.5 lakh (₹1.275 crore – ₹1 crore) is subject to tax at 30%. However, before paying tax, the trust has options: (a) set aside the shortfall as "accumulation" under Section 11(2), file Form 10 with the Assessing Officer, specifying the purpose and timeline for utilising the accumulated amount (maximum 5 years); (b) deposit the unapplied amount in a specified investment mode. If neither option is exercised, the ₹27.5 lakh shortfall is taxable. ITR-7 must correctly reflect this position.

**We are a Section 8 Company (non-profit company) under the Companies Act. Should we file ITR-6 or ITR-7?** The answer depends on whether the Section 8 Company has obtained Section 12A/12AB registration under the Income Tax Act. If yes, the Section 8 Company files ITR-7 (like any other registered charitable institution). If the Section 8 Company has NOT obtained Section 12A/12AB registration, it files ITR-6 (like any regular company) and pays tax on its net income at the normal corporate tax rate. Obtaining Section 12A/12AB registration is strongly advisable for all genuine non-profit Section 8 Companies, it provides income exemption and significantly reduces the tax burden.

**Our trust's Section 12AB registration was recently approved. Which year's ITR-7 do we file first?** Once Section 12AB registration is granted, the exemption is generally effective from the assessment year specified in the registration order. File ITR-7 for all years from the effective date of registration. For years before registration where ITR-5 or ITR-7 (without valid registration) may have been filed, the registration order may specify retroactive applicability (for new registrations, the exemption typically applies prospectively). Consult a CA on whether any prior-year returns need to be revised and whether the exemption can be availed for prior years based on the specific registration order language.

**Is a management committee member of a trust personally liable if the trust fails to file ITR-7?** The primary obligation for ITR-7 filing rests with the trust itself, but under Section 161 and related provisions of the Income Tax Act, the trustee(s) are representative assessees and are personally liable (in their capacity as trustees, not in their personal capacity) for the trust's tax dues and compliance obligations. The Income Tax department can proceed against the managing trustee for the trust's defaults, including non-filing penalties. In cases of willful non-filing or tax evasion by a trust, prosecution under Section 276CC (for willful failure to furnish return) can also be initiated against the responsible trustee(s). Maintaining a strict compliance culture within the trust, including annual ITR-7 filing, is therefore a governance imperative for trustees.
