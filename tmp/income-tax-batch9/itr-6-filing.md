# ITR-6 form filing

## Overview

ITR-6 is the Income Tax Return form prescribed for all companies registered in India under the Companies Act, 2013 (or the earlier Companies Act, 1956), except those companies claiming exemption under Section 11 (income of religious or charitable trusts), which file ITR-7. Every company incorporated in India, whether private limited, public limited, one person company, Section 8 company (unless Section 11 exemption is claimed), producer company, or Nidhi company, is mandatorily required to file ITR-6 every assessment year.

ITR-6 is the most comprehensive and data-intensive return form, capturing complete financial statements, detailed tax computation, Minimum Alternate Tax (MAT) computation, transfer pricing data, international transactions, Schedule AL-2 (assets and liabilities), and multi-year depreciation schedules. For large companies with complex operations, ITR-6 preparation involves multiple teams, accounts, tax, legal, and statutory audit, working in coordination.

The filing of ITR-6 is invariably preceded by the statutory audit under the Companies Act, the tax audit under Section 44AB of the Income Tax Act (mandatory for all companies regardless of turnover), and for larger companies, the transfer pricing audit under Section 92E.

ITR-6 must be filed using the authorised signatory's DSC, electronic verification is mandatory; physical ITR-V is not accepted for company returns.

## Key features

- MAT computation: All companies (except eligible start-ups and some others) are subject to Minimum Alternate Tax at 15% of book profits under Section 115JB, ITR-6 captures the MAT computation and MAT credit carry-forward
- Transfer pricing schedule: Schedule for International Related Party Transactions and Specified Domestic Transactions, mandatory for companies with cross-border transactions above ₹1 crore per transaction
- Detailed depreciation schedule: Block-wise depreciation as per Income Tax Act rates, separate from the Companies Act depreciation used in financial statements
- Tax audit mandatory: Unlike other entities, ALL companies are required to undergo tax audit under Section 44AB regardless of turnover, Form 3CA and 3CD must be filed before ITR-6
- ESOP deduction: Companies providing ESOPs to employees can claim deduction at the time of exercise, captured in ITR-6
- Expenditure on scientific research: R&D expenditure deduction under Section 35, weighted deduction for approved R&D companies
- DSC mandatory: ITR-6 must be digitally signed, no physical or OTP-based verification accepted
- Comprehensive disclosure: Directors' details, PAN of directors, major transactions (loans, investments, guarantees) are disclosed
- Form 29B for MAT: A CA certificate in Form 29B certifying the book profit computation must be filed where MAT applies

## Benefits

- **Statutory compliance:** Companies are legally obligated to file ITR-6, non-filing attracts fees, penalties, and prosecution for directors under Section 276CC
- **MAT credit carry-forward:** Excess MAT paid over regular tax can be carried forward as MAT credit for 15 years, ITR-6 captures and preserves this credit
- **TDS refund processing:** Companies with excess TDS on income (rent received, interest income, professional fees) can claim refunds in ITR-6
- **Loss carry-forward for companies:** Business losses, capital losses, and unabsorbed depreciation are carried forward, for companies, unabsorbed depreciation can be carried forward indefinitely
- **80-IAC startup exemption:** DPIIT-recognised startups with IMB approval claim the Section 80-IAC income tax holiday in ITR-6, three consecutive years of 100% profit deduction
- **Transparent financials:** A well-filed ITR-6 aligned with audited accounts demonstrates financial transparency, essential for fundraising, DRHP filing, and M&A transactions

## Process

1. **Complete statutory audit** Ensure the statutory audit under the Companies Act is completed and the auditor's report (including CARO) is signed off.
2. **Complete tax audit** Engage a CA for tax audit under Section 44AB. File Form 3CA and 3CD on the IT portal before October 31.
3. **Transfer pricing audit (if applicable)** For companies with international related party transactions above ₹1 crore, complete the transfer pricing audit. File Form 3CEB before November 30 (in transfer pricing cases, ITR-6 due date is November 30).
4. **Prepare tax computation** Compute company's total income, starting from net profit per books, adding back disallowed expenses, applying depreciation as per IT Act, claiming deductions (80C, 80G, 80-IAC for startups, etc.), computing MAT.
5. **File ITR-6 online** Log in to incometax.gov.in using company credentials. Select ITR-6. Fill all schedules, P&L, Balance Sheet, capital gains, deductions, MAT, taxes paid, and director details.
6. **Pay remaining tax** Pay any balance self-assessment tax via Challan 280. Enter challan details in the return.
7. **Sign with DSC and submit** Submit the return. Digitally sign using the authorised director's DSC. The return is instantly processed upon DSC-based submission.

## Common errors

- Not computing MAT correctly: Using book profit from the wrong version of financial statements (draft vs. audited), MAT must be computed on audited book profit
- Incorrect depreciation rate application: Using Companies Act depreciation rates in the tax computation instead of Income Tax Act rates, leads to incorrect taxable income
- Forgetting Section 43B disallowances: PF/ESI contributions, statutory dues, and other items payable but not paid by March 31 are disallowable under Section 43B, commonly missed
- Not referencing Form 3CA/3CD before filing: ITR-6 must reference the correct UDIN of the filed tax audit report, a common mismatch that creates processing errors
- Missing transfer pricing compliance: Not filing Form 3CEB when international related party transactions exceed the ₹1 crore threshold, attracts penalty of 2% of the transaction value

## Frequently asked questions

**Our startup has not made any profit in the last 3 years. Are we still required to file ITR-6?** Yes. Every company incorporated in India must file ITR-6 for every assessment year, regardless of whether it made a profit, a loss, or had no activity at all. A loss-making startup still has a filing obligation. In fact, filing ITR-6 for loss years is particularly important for carrying forward business losses and unabsorbed depreciation, which can be set off against future profits. Unabsorbed depreciation can be carried forward indefinitely for companies; business losses for 8 years. Non-filing deprives the company of these carry-forward benefits.

**Our company paid advance tax in all four quarters but our final tax liability is lower than what we paid. Can we claim a refund in ITR-6?** Yes. Excess advance tax paid over the final tax liability (after TDS credit) is refundable and can be claimed in ITR-6. The refund is credited to the company's pre-validated bank account linked to the PAN. Additionally, if TDS was deducted on the company's income (e.g., TDS on rental income, interest income, professional fees received) and the cumulative TDS exceeds the final tax liability, the entire excess is refundable. Income Tax refunds for companies are typically processed within 30–90 days of return processing.

**We are a private limited company with only one director/shareholder (OPC, One Person Company). Do we have the same filing requirements as a regular Pvt Ltd company?** Yes, an OPC (One Person Company) is a company under the Companies Act, 2013 and has identical income tax filing obligations as any other private limited company. ITR-6 with DSC-based verification is mandatory, tax audit under Section 44AB is mandatory, and all company-level tax obligations (MAT, advance tax, etc.) apply. The simplified nature of an OPC for corporate law purposes does not translate to simplified income tax compliance. The sole director's DSC is used for ITR-6 filing.

**Our company is a subsidiary of a US company and pays a management fee to the US parent. What are the transfer pricing and ITR-6 implications?** Payment of management fees to the US parent constitutes an international related party transaction, subject to India's Transfer Pricing regulations under Sections 92 to 92F of the Income Tax Act. The management fee must be at arm's length, documented in a Transfer Pricing study prepared by an accountant. A Transfer Pricing auditor must file Form 3CEB before November 30 certifying the arm's length nature of the transaction. ITR-6 must be filed by November 30 (the extended deadline for TP cases). The management fee is also subject to TDS under Section 195 (with 15CA/15CB compliance), making it a multi-regulatory transaction requiring coordinated compliance.

**Our company received a demand notice after ITR-6 was processed, saying there is a mismatch between TDS credit claimed and Form 26AS. How do we resolve this?** TDS mismatch notices are among the most common post-processing issues. Steps to resolve: (a) download Form 26AS and compare TDS credited against TDS claimed in ITR-6, identify the specific TDS entry causing the mismatch; (b) if TDS appears in ITR-6 but not in Form 26AS, it means the deductor (your client/tenant/bank) hasn't filed their TDS return or has used a wrong PAN, follow up with them to file a correction; (c) once the deductor files a correction and the Form 26AS updates, apply for rectification under Section 154 on the income tax portal to get the demand revised; (d) if the demand is otherwise incorrect, file a response under "Response to Outstanding Demand" on the portal, citing the correction application. Paying the demand without resolving the root cause leaves the excess TDS credit permanently lost.
