# ITR-1 form filing (Sahaj)

## Overview

ITR-1, popularly known as Sahaj (meaning "simple" in Hindi), is the simplest income tax return form designed for ordinary resident individuals with straightforward income profiles. It is prescribed by the Central Board of Direct Taxes (CBDT) under the Income Tax Act, 1961, and is applicable for individual taxpayers whose income arises primarily from salary, one house property, and other sources like interest, without the complexity of business income, capital gains, or foreign assets.

ITR-1 is a single-page form that has been continuously simplified over the years, with extensive pre-filling from employer data (Form 16), bank TDS data (Form 26AS), Annual Information Statement (AIS), and Taxpayer Information Summary (TIS). For most salaried individuals with standard incomes, filing ITR-1 involves primarily verifying the pre-filled data and making necessary corrections, before submitting the return online.

Despite its simplicity, ITR-1 filing requires careful attention, errors in claiming deductions (Section 80C, 80D, HRA exemption, LTA), incorrect selection of the tax regime (old vs. new), or failure to declare all income sources (bank interest, savings account interest, rental income) are common mistakes that lead to income tax notices.

## Applicability

ITR-1 can be filed by individuals who meet **ALL** of the following:

| Criterion | Requirement |
| --- | --- |
| Residency | Ordinarily Resident in India |
| Total Income | Up to ₹50 lakh |
| Income from Salary | One employer salary (including pension) |
| Income from House Property | Maximum one house property (self-occupied or let-out) |
| Income from Other Sources | Interest income, family pension |
| Agricultural Income | Up to ₹5,000 |

**ITR-1 CANNOT be used if:**

- Total income exceeds ₹50 lakh
- There is income from business or profession
- Capital gains exist (short-term or long-term)
- More than one house property
- Foreign income or foreign assets are held
- Income from lottery, horse races, or speculative activity
- The individual is a Director in a company
- Unlisted equity shares are held
- TDS deducted under Section 194N (cash withdrawal above threshold)
- The individual is a Non-Resident (NR) or Resident but Not Ordinarily Resident (RNOR)

## Key features

- Pre-filled return: Income Tax portal pre-fills salary (from Form 16), TDS (from Form 26AS), interest income (from AIS/TIS), and deductions, reducing data entry effort
- Old vs. new tax regime choice: Salaried individuals must choose between the Old Tax Regime (with deductions) and the New Tax Regime (lower rates, no most deductions) when filing ITR-1
- Single online form: Available in online mode directly on incometax.gov.in, no software download needed
- JSON upload option: Also available as offline JSON file for those preferring offline preparation
- Instant processing: Simple ITR-1 filings are often processed within a few hours to a few days, refunds credited directly to pre-validated bank accounts
- EVC verification: Can be verified through Aadhaar OTP, net banking EVC, or bank ATM EVC, physical ITR-V posting optional
- Section 80C to 80U deductions: Under the old regime, all standard deductions (80C, 80D, 80CCD, HRA, LTA, standard deduction of ₹50,000) can be claimed in ITR-1

## Benefits

- **Simplicity:** The form's straightforward structure makes it accessible to most salaried taxpayers without professional assistance, though professional review improves accuracy
- **Fast refunds:** Simple ITR-1 returns are processed quickly, refunds are often credited within 7–30 days of filing and verification
- **Pre-filling reduces errors:** Auto-populated data from employer and bank records reduces manual entry errors
- **Free filing option:** The Income Tax portal provides free online filing of ITR-1, no cost to file independently
- **Compliance record:** Annual ITR-1 filing creates an income proof document widely used for home loans, vehicle loans, visa applications, and insurance policies
- **Regime optimisation:** Comparing old vs. new regime during ITR-1 preparation helps identify the tax-efficient choice for each financial year

## Process

1. **Gather documents** Collect Form 16 from employer, download Form 26AS and AIS from the IT portal, and compile bank statements and deduction proof documents.
2. **Log in to Income Tax portal** Log in to incometax.gov.in using PAN and password. Navigate to e-File → Income Tax Returns → File Income Tax Return.
3. **Select assessment year and ITR-1** Select the Assessment Year (e.g., AY 2025-26 for FY 2024-25). Select "ITR-1, Sahaj" as the applicable form.
4. **Review pre-filled data** Review all pre-filled sections, Personal Information, Gross Total Income (salary, house property, other sources), and TDS. Verify against Form 16 and AIS, correct any discrepancies.
5. **Choose tax regime** Select Old Tax Regime or New Tax Regime. Under old regime, enter all eligible deductions (80C, 80D, HRA, etc.). Under new regime, most deductions are not available, only standard deduction of ₹50,000 for salaried.
6. **Enter additional income** Add any income not pre-filled, savings account interest, FD interest, family pension, rental income from one house property.
7. **Verify tax computation** Review the auto-computed tax liability. Compare with TDS already deducted. If self-assessment tax is payable, pay via Challan 280 before filing.
8. **Submit ITR** Submit the return after review. The portal generates an acknowledgement (ITR-V).
9. **Verify return** Verify within 30 days, using Aadhaar OTP, net banking EVC, or by posting signed ITR-V to CPC Bengaluru. An unverified return is not treated as a filed return.

## Common errors

- Not reporting savings account interest: Interest on savings accounts (including the ₹10,000 exemption under Section 80TTA for non-seniors) must still be declared as income, many taxpayers omit this
- Wrong ITR form selection: Using ITR-1 when capital gains exist (even small amounts from mutual fund redemptions), requires ITR-2
- Incorrect HRA exemption computation: Manual HRA computation errors, using metro city limits for non-metro locations, using annual rent instead of monthly
- Not reconciling Form 26AS with Form 16: TDS claimed must match Form 26AS, discrepancies cause demand notices after processing
- Filing under old regime without opting correctly: Not explicitly selecting the old regime in the portal, in the absence of a proper selection, the new regime is applied by default in some cases

## Frequently asked questions

**I redeemed some mutual funds this year. Can I still use ITR-1?** No. Any capital gains, including from mutual fund redemptions, whether short-term or long-term, whether gain or loss, disqualify you from using ITR-1. You must use ITR-2 (for individuals and HUFs without business income) when capital gains are involved. Even a ₹500 long-term capital gain from a small SIP redemption technically requires ITR-2. Most mutual fund platforms provide capital gains statements in formats compatible with ITR-2 pre-fill, making the switch to ITR-2 less complex than it appears.

**My employer has two branches and I received two Form 16s for the year. Can I still use ITR-1?** Yes, receiving two Form 16s (from the same or different employers during different periods of the year) does not disqualify you from ITR-1, provided all other ITR-1 eligibility criteria are met (total income under ₹50 lakh, no capital gains, no business income, etc.). You must combine the salary income from both Form 16s and report the total in ITR-1. Be careful about TDS, the second employer may have applied the full basic exemption again, resulting in under-deduction. Compute the net tax liability on the combined income and pay any self-assessment tax before filing.

**I am a salaried employee but also earn ₹3,000 per month from YouTube content creation. Which ITR form should I use?** YouTube income (from Google's AdSense or brand deals) is typically treated as income from business/profession under the Income Tax Act, since it arises from a commercial activity, even if small. Income from business or profession disqualifies ITR-1. You should file ITR-3 (for individuals with business/profession income alongside salary) or ITR-4 if you opt for presumptive taxation under Section 44ADA (for professionals) or Section 44AD (for business income up to ₹2 crore turnover). The appropriate form depends on the quantum of YouTube income and whether you want to maintain regular books of accounts.

**I have a home loan on my self-occupied property. How do I claim this deduction in ITR-1?** In ITR-1, under the "Income from House Property" section, select "Self-Occupied." The annual value of a self-occupied property is NIL, so no rental income is added. However, you can claim a deduction for interest paid on the home loan under Section 24(b), up to ₹2 lakh per year for a self-occupied property (under the old tax regime). Enter the actual interest paid (from the bank's interest certificate for the financial year) subject to the ₹2 lakh limit. Note: home loan interest deduction is NOT available under the new tax regime (except under Section 24(b) for let-out property).

**I missed the July 31 deadline for ITR-1 filing. What should I do now?** File a Belated Return under Section 139(4), available until December 31 of the Assessment Year. A belated ITR-1 attracts: (a) a late filing fee of ₹1,000 (income ≤ ₹5 lakh) or ₹5,000 (income > ₹5 lakh) under Section 234F; (b) interest under Section 234A at 1% per month on the unpaid tax from July 31 to the date of filing; and (c) inability to carry forward certain losses (like house property loss). After December 31, you can only file ITR-U (updated return), which attracts additional tax of 25%–70%. File immediately to minimise interest accumulation.
