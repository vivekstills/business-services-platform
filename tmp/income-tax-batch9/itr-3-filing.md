# ITR-3 form filing

## Overview

ITR-3 is the Income Tax Return form for individuals and Hindu Undivided Families (HUFs) who have income from business or profession, in addition to any other income they may have (salary, house property, capital gains, other sources). It is the most comprehensive ITR form for individuals, capturing the full complexity of a business-running individual's tax position.

ITR-3 is applicable for freelancers, consultants, doctors, lawyers, architects, chartered accountants, stock traders with F&O positions, YouTubers, small business owners, and any individual who earns income from a business or profession alongside (or instead of) a salary. Unlike ITR-4 (which uses a simplified presumptive taxation system), ITR-3 requires the taxpayer to maintain regular books of accounts under Section 44AA and report detailed profit and loss, balance sheet, and trading account data.

The form has expanded significantly over recent years, capturing trading account data, partner's income from firms, speculative and non-speculative business income, and complete financial statements, making it effectively a mini-audit-quality return for individual business income earners.

## Applicability

| Income source | ITR-3 |
| --- | --- |
| Income from business (regular, speculative) | ✓ |
| Income from profession (doctor, lawyer, CA, architect, engineer) | ✓ |
| F&O (Futures and Options) trading income | ✓ |
| Intraday equity trading (speculative business income) | ✓ |
| Partner's income from a firm (share of profit) | ✓ |
| Any business/profession income PLUS salary | ✓ |
| Any business income PLUS capital gains | ✓ |
| Proprietary business with turnover above presumptive limit | ✓ |
| Professionals with gross receipts above ₹50 lakh (above 44ADA limit) | ✓ |

**ITR-3 vs. ITR-4:** ITR-4 (Sugam) uses presumptive taxation under Sections 44AD/44ADA/44AE. If total turnover from business exceeds ₹2 crore (44AD) or gross professional receipts exceed ₹50 lakh (44ADA), or if the taxpayer opts out of presumptive taxation (even below the threshold), ITR-3 must be used with regular books of accounts.

## Key features

- Part A, General Information and P&L: Trading Account, Manufacturing Account, and Profit & Loss Account, for businesses maintaining books
- Balance sheet disclosure: Complete balance sheet of the proprietor's business as on March 31, assets, liabilities, capital, loans
- Quantitative details: For manufacturing businesses, quantity of goods produced, purchased, and sold
- Schedule BP (Business/Profession): Detailed schedule capturing taxable business income, disallowances, and adjustments
- Schedule CYLA, BFLA, CFL: Current year loss adjustment, brought forward loss adjustment, and loss carry-forward schedules
- Speculative and non-speculative income separation: F&O income (non-speculative) and intraday trading (speculative) are reported separately with different loss set-off rules
- Partner's income schedule: For individuals who are partners in a firm, share of profit, remuneration, interest from firm
- Tax audit report reference: If tax audit under Section 44AB is applicable, the audit report number and CA details must be reported in ITR-3
- Complete set-off of losses: Business losses from one source can be set off against income from other business sources (with restrictions for speculative losses)

## Benefits

- **Complete loss capture and carry-forward:** Businesses with losses can carry forward non-speculative business losses for 8 years, reducing future tax liability, ITR-3 is the mechanism to preserve and utilise these losses
- **Tax audit alignment:** For businesses under tax audit, ITR-3 directly references the audit report, ensuring consistency between the return and the audit findings
- **F&O tax compliance:** F&O trading losses (non-speculative) can be set off against salary, house property, and other income in the same year (with certain restrictions), ITR-3 is the correct form to capture this set-off
- **Professional income flexibility:** Doctors, lawyers, CAs, and other professionals not opting for presumptive taxation can claim all actual expenses (depreciation, rent, salaries, subscriptions) against their professional income in ITR-3
- **Partner's exempt income:** Share of profit from a partnership firm is exempt from tax in the hands of the partner, ITR-3 correctly captures this as exempt income, with remuneration and interest from the firm as taxable income

## Process

1. **Prepare financial statements** Compile the P&L account and Balance Sheet for the financial year. Ensure GST turnover reconciles with income tax turnover. Compute depreciation as per Income Tax Act (not Companies Act) rates.
2. **Tax audit (if applicable)** If turnover from business exceeds ₹1 crore (or ₹10 crore for digital transactions) or professional receipts exceed ₹50 lakh, get tax audit done under Section 44AB. The auditor files Form 3CA/3CB and 3CD on the Income Tax portal.
3. **Compute business income** Compute net business income, net profit from P&L account adjusted for disallowances (Section 40A, 43B), additions (deemed income), depreciation adjustments.
4. **Compile all income heads** Add salary (from Form 16), house property, capital gains (from broker statements), and other income to business income for total income computation.
5. **Loss set-off** Apply loss set-off rules, business loss against other business income, speculative loss only against speculative income, capital loss only against capital gains.
6. **File ITR-3 online** Log in to incometax.gov.in. Select ITR-3. Fill all schedules, BP (business income), P&L, Balance Sheet, Capital Gains, other income, deductions, and tax computation.
7. **Pay self-assessment tax** Pay any balance tax via Challan 280. Enter challan details in the return.
8. **Submit and verify** Submit and verify using DSC (mandatory for companies and LLPs; optional but recommended for individuals under audit) or Aadhaar OTP / EVC.

## Common errors

- Using ITR-4 (presumptive) when books are maintained: Once regular books of accounts are maintained, the taxpayer cannot use presumptive taxation in ITR-4, must use ITR-3
- Not separating speculative and non-speculative income: Combining intraday trading (speculative) with F&O (non-speculative) in the same schedule, incorrect loss set-off rules result
- Missing tax audit requirement: Not getting tax audit done when turnover exceeds the Section 44AB threshold, attracts penalty of 0.5% of turnover (max ₹1.5 lakh) under Section 271B
- Not reconciling GST turnover with IT turnover: Differences between GSTR-3B turnover and ITR-3 business income trigger income tax scrutiny, document and explain all differences
- Depreciation rate errors: Using Companies Act depreciation rates instead of Income Tax Act rates (Schedule II vs. Appendix I of Income Tax Rules), common for small business owners

## Frequently asked questions

**I am a doctor with a private clinic and also a salaried employee at a hospital. Which ITR form should I file?** You should file ITR-3, combining your hospital salary (reportable under "Income from Salary") and your private clinic income (reportable under "Income from Business or Profession"). Since you have professional income from the clinic, ITR-1 and ITR-2 are both inapplicable. If your gross professional receipts from the clinic are below ₹50 lakh and you want to opt for simplified filing, you can consider ITR-4 with presumptive taxation under Section 44ADA (presuming 50% of gross receipts as profit, with no need to maintain detailed books). However, if your actual expenses exceed 50% of receipts (making actual profit less than 50%), ITR-3 with regular books is more tax-efficient.

**My F&O trading resulted in a loss of ₹2 lakh this year. How do I report this and can I set it off against my salary?** F&O loss is classified as non-speculative business loss, it cannot be set off against salary income in the same year (salary is a separate head). However, it can be set off against other business/profession income in the same year if you have any. If the loss cannot be fully set off in the current year, it can be carried forward for 8 subsequent assessment years and set off against business income in those years. The F&O loss must be reported in Schedule CFL (Carry Forward Losses) in ITR-3 to preserve the carry-forward benefit. Missing this schedule means the loss is permanently forfeited.

**I run a small grocery store with annual sales of ₹40 lakh. Do I need to maintain books of accounts and file ITR-3?** For a business with turnover of ₹40 lakh, you have the option of presumptive taxation under Section 44AD in ITR-4, declaring 8% of turnover (6% for digital transactions) as profit without maintaining detailed books. This is simpler and suitable for small traders. Alternatively, if your actual profit is less than 8%, you can opt for ITR-3 with regular books, maintaining records and claiming lower actual profit. However, opting for ITR-3 below the presumptive threshold (₹2 crore for 44AD) triggers mandatory book-keeping and, if net income is below the presumptive rate, requires tax audit if income is below the exemption limit. Choose based on your actual profitability versus the presumptive rate.

**I am a partner in two partnerships, a law firm and a consultancy. How do I report this in ITR-3?** As a partner, your share of profit from each firm is exempt from income tax (since the firm pays tax on its profits). However, any salary, commission, or interest received from the firms as a partner is taxable as business/profession income in your hands. In ITR-3, report the exempt share of profit in Schedule EI (Exempt Income) for each firm separately. Report the taxable remuneration and interest from the firms in Schedule BP (Business/Profession). You will also need to fill Schedule Part B1, "Part B, Statements of partners of firms" for each partnership. Ensure the remuneration claimed matches the partnership deed provisions and the firm's computation of income.

**My turnover crossed ₹1 crore this year (mainly from e-commerce sales). Is a tax audit mandatory?** Under Section 44AB, a tax audit is mandatory if total sales/turnover/gross receipts from business exceed ₹1 crore in a financial year (with an exception, if more than 95% of transactions are digital, the threshold is ₹10 crore). For most e-commerce sellers receiving payments through digital channels (UPI, credit cards, netbanking), the higher ₹10 crore threshold may apply, verify the exact proportion of digital vs. cash transactions. If the ₹1 crore threshold applies and is breached, engage a CA for tax audit under Section 44AB, file Form 3CD (the audit report) before October 31, and file ITR-3 (also before October 31 for audit cases). Non-compliance with tax audit attracts a penalty of 0.5% of turnover (up to ₹1.5 lakh) under Section 271B.
