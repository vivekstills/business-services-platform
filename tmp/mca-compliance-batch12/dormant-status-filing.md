## Dormant status filing

### Detailed overview

Section 455 of the Companies Act, 2013 introduced the concept of Dormant Company, a company that has not been carrying on any business or operations, has not made any significant accounting transaction during the last two financial years, or has not filed financial statements and annual returns. Such companies can apply for Dormant Company status with the MCA, which grants relaxed compliance obligations during the dormancy period.

Dormant status is useful for companies holding assets, IPRs, or being preserved for future use without active business, startups in pivot mode, holding companies, IP-holding companies, or companies awaiting strategic decisions.

### Key features

- MSC-1 application, special resolution, and simplified annual track (MSC-3) when active
- Limits on significant accounting transactions and maximum dormancy period
- MSC-4 path to resume active operations

## Applicability

Eligible for dormant status: companies formed for future projects, companies holding assets or IPRs, companies with no significant accounting transactions in the last 2 FYs. Not eligible: companies with pending criminal proceedings, companies in default of tax filings, companies that have not filed annual returns.

## Compliance requirements

| Requirement | Detail |
| Form MSC-1 | Application for dormant status |
| Special resolution | Shareholder approval (75%) |
| No significant accounting transactions | In last 2 years |
| No outstanding statutory dues | All filings up to date |
| Annual returns | Even in dormant status, simplified returns required |

## Benefits

- Reduced compliance burden during dormancy
- Lower MCA filing fees
- Avoid director disqualification due to non-filing
- Preserve company name and corporate identity
- Easy revival when business resumes

## Risks and penalties

| Risk | Detail |
| Status revocation | If company commences significant transactions, dormant status revoked |
| Maximum dormancy period | Cannot remain dormant for more than 5 consecutive years |
| Failure to apply for revival | After 5 years, RoC may strike off |

## Documents required

| Document | Purpose |
| Form MSC-1 | Application for dormant status |
| Special resolution | Shareholder approval |
| Statement of affairs | Showing minimal assets and liabilities |
| Auditor's certificate | Confirming dormancy criteria |
| Affidavit by directors | Confirming non-operation |
| Last 2 years' financials | Showing minimal transactions |

## Process

1. Verify eligibility (2 years of no significant transactions)
2. Hold EGM and pass special resolution
3. Prepare Form MSC-1 with all attachments
4. File MSC-1 with MCA
5. RoC reviews and may grant dormant status (Form MSC-2)
6. Annual filings in simplified form (MSC-3)

## Government fees

| Form | Fee |
| MSC-1 | ₹2,000 |
| MSC-3 (annual filing) | ₹400 |

## Timeline

30 to 60 days.

## Frequently asked questions

**1. What constitutes a significant accounting transaction for dormant status?** Includes payment of fees to RoC, payment for office maintenance, payment to fulfill statutory requirements. Other transactions (sales, purchases, salaries) typically disqualify.

**2. Can a dormant company hold a bank account?** Yes, but the account should have minimal transactions (only for statutory payments). Significant balance movements may disqualify dormant status.

**3. Can a dormant company own properties or IPRs?** Yes, many companies become dormant precisely to hold assets or IPRs. Holding assets does not constitute significant accounting transactions.

**4. What is the maximum dormancy period?** 5 consecutive years. After 5 years, the company must either revive operations and file regular returns, or undergo strike-off.

**5. How does a dormant company resume operations?** File Form MSC-4 (Application for Active Status) along with Form INC-22A (KYC) and most recent financial statements. Pay applicable fees. RoC grants active status upon verification.
