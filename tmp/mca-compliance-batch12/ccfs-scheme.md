## CCFS scheme

### Detailed overview

The Compromises, Arrangements and Amalgamations under the Companies Act, 2013, Sections 230 to 240, and the Companies (Compromises, Arrangements and Amalgamations) Rules, 2016 (CCFS Rules), provide the legal framework for corporate restructuring activities including mergers, demergers, amalgamations, debt restructuring, and other compromises and arrangements involving companies and their stakeholders (creditors, members).

The CCFS framework has streamlined corporate restructuring through fast-track mergers (between holding-subsidiary or between two small companies), formal NCLT-approved schemes for complex transactions, and structured shareholder and creditor approval processes.

### Key features

- NCLT or Regional Director routes depending on scheme type
- Class meetings, sanctioned schemes, and RoC filings to give effect
- Cross-border and tax or stamp aspects need parallel planning

## Applicability

Applicable to all companies undergoing merger, demerger, or amalgamation, companies undertaking compromise with creditors (debt restructuring), companies undertaking arrangement with members, and cross-border mergers (with prescribed jurisdictions). Pan-India applicability.

## Compliance requirements

| Scheme type | Authority |
| Fast-track merger (holding-subsidiary, two small companies) | Regional Director (RD) |
| Standard scheme (mergers, demergers) | National Company Law Tribunal (NCLT) |
| Cross-border merger | NCLT and RBI approval |
| Class meetings | Of creditors and shareholders separately |

## Benefits

- Legal framework for complex restructuring
- Tax-neutral mergers under Income Tax Act (subject to conditions)
- Stamp duty relief in certain cases
- Creditor protection mechanism
- Court-approved binding effect on all stakeholders

## Risks and penalties

| Risk | Mitigation |
| Scheme rejection by NCLT | Strong commercial rationale, stakeholder consultations |
| Creditor objection | Engage major creditors early |
| Tax challenges | Pre-merger tax structuring |
| Long timelines | Plan restructuring 6 to 18 months in advance |

## Documents required

Comprehensive: scheme document, valuation reports, board resolutions, creditor lists, fairness opinion, no-objection certificates, audited financials, NCLT applications.

## Process

1. Strategic decision and structuring of scheme.
2. Valuation reports from registered valuers.
3. Board approvals.
4. Application to NCLT (or RD for fast-track).
5. NCLT directs convening of meetings of shareholders and creditors.
6. Class meetings, approval by 75% majority by value.
7. Final NCLT hearing for sanction.
8. NCLT order, filed with RoC, scheme effective.

## Government fees

NCLT fees, RoC fees, stamp duty (state-specific), significant amounts depending on transaction size.

## Timeline

6 to 18 months typically for standard schemes, 3 to 6 months for fast-track mergers.

## Frequently asked questions

**1. What is a fast-track merger?** Fast-track merger (Section 233) allows mergers between holding-subsidiary or between two small companies through a simplified process, approved by Regional Director rather than NCLT, significantly faster (3 to 6 months).

**2. Is creditor approval mandatory in all schemes?** Creditor approval (75% by value) is required when the scheme affects creditor rights, debt restructuring, mergers with significant debt restructuring component. Pure share-swap mergers may not need separate creditor approval.

**3. Can a foreign company merge with an Indian company?** Yes, cross-border mergers permitted from October 2017. Inbound merger (foreign into Indian): permitted from any country. Outbound merger (Indian into foreign): permitted only with companies from prescribed jurisdictions notified by Government.

**4. What stamp duty applies to mergers?** Stamp duty varies significantly by state, Maharashtra, Karnataka, Tamil Nadu have different rates. NCLT order itself attracts stamp duty in some states. Pre-merger structuring should consider stamp duty implications.

**5. Are mergers tax-neutral?** Mergers complying with Section 2(1B) of the Income Tax Act are tax-neutral, no capital gains tax on transfer of assets or shares. Conditions include continuing business, shareholder continuity, and asset-share exchange ratio compliance.
