# Company incorporation in China

## Overview

China is the world's second-largest economy and the largest manufacturing and export hub globally. For Indian businesses in manufacturing, trading, technology, or consumer goods, establishing a formal legal presence in China enables direct participation in the market, local hiring, and access to Chinese government contracts. The most common structure for foreign investors is the Wholly Foreign-Owned Enterprise (WFOE), which allows 100% foreign ownership in eligible sectors.

China's company law and foreign investment framework underwent significant reform under the Foreign Investment Law (2020), which replaced the older WFOE, Equity Joint Venture (EJV), and Cooperative Joint Venture (CJV) laws with a unified foreign investment regime.

**Note:** India-China business relations carry geopolitical sensitivities and regulatory scrutiny, particularly post-2020. Indian businesses must assess current RBI and FEMA guidelines before investing.

## Key benefits

- Access to the world's largest manufacturing ecosystem
- Proximity to raw material suppliers and supply chain networks
- 100% foreign ownership possible in most sectors (WFOE)
- Large domestic consumer market of 1.4 billion+
- Special Economic Zones (SEZs) with tax concessions
- Well-developed logistics, port, and infrastructure network
- China ASEAN FTA access (RCEP framework)

## Limitations

- Geopolitical sensitivity, India-China relations add regulatory risk
- RBI/FEMA scrutiny on Overseas Direct Investment from India to China
- Mandatory local language (Mandarin) for all government filings
- Restricted sectors, certain industries require Chinese JV partners
- Strict internet and data regulations (China's Great Firewall)
- Minimum registered capital requirements vary by industry
- Tax and accounting must follow Chinese GAAP (not IFRS)
- Repatriation of profits can be slow and subject to SAFE (State Administration of Foreign Exchange) approval

## Documents required

For Indian founders registering a WFOE in China:

- Notarised and apostilled copies of passport (all shareholders and directors)
- Board resolution from Indian parent company (if applicable), apostilled
- Articles of Association / Business Scope Description
- Chinese registered address proof (lease agreement with local landlord)
- Bank reference letter from Indian bank
- Business plan / feasibility report (for certain sectors)
- Legal representative appointment letter
- Company name pre-approval from SAMR
- Chop / seal application (required for all Chinese legal entities)

## Step-by-step process

1. **Step 1 - Define Business Scope:** China requires precise definition of business activities. The scope directly determines licensing, taxes, and permissible operations.
2. **Step 2 - Name Pre-Approval:** Submit proposed Chinese name to SAMR. The name must follow Chinese naming conventions (city/province + trade name + industry + entity type).
3. **Step 3 - Registered Address:** Obtain a registered lease in mainland China. Virtual offices are accepted in some cities.
4. **Step 4 - Capital and Shareholding Structure:** Determine registered capital. While there is no statutory minimum, SAFE expects capital to be sufficient for the business scope.
5. **Step 5 - Submit SAMR Application:** File Articles of Association, shareholder documents, and appointment of legal representative.
6. **Step 6 - Obtain Business License:** Issued by SAMR, the core company document.
7. **Step 7 - Register with Tax Bureau:** Obtain national and local tax registration numbers (similar to India's PAN + GST).
8. **Step 8 - Open RMB and Foreign Currency Bank Accounts:** Required for capital injection and operations. Separate accounts needed for each.
9. **Step 9 - Register with SAFE:** For cross-border capital flows and dividend repatriation.
10. **Step 10 - Capital Injection:** Transfer registered capital into the Chinese bank account within the prescribed timeframe.

## Tax implications for Indian founders

Standard Chinese Corporate Income Tax (CIT) is 25%. High-tech enterprises qualify for 15%.

Value Added Tax (VAT) in China applies at 13% (standard), 9% (services), or 6% (financial services).

Withholding tax on dividends remitted to India is 10% under the India-China DTAA.

Indian ODI to China requires RBI approval under FEMA's Overseas Direct Investment (ODI) regulations. Post-2020 geopolitical tensions have added compliance scrutiny.

Transfer pricing documentation is mandatory for related-party transactions.

CBDT requires Indian residents to disclose Chinese company ownership in Schedule FA of ITR.

## Frequently asked questions

**Q1. Can Indian companies invest in China freely under FEMA?** Indian companies can invest in China through the Overseas Direct Investment (ODI) route under FEMA. However, post-2020 India-China tensions mean additional government scrutiny. Press Note 3 (2020) mandates government approval for investments from countries sharing a land border with India, which includes China. Both automatic and government approval routes exist depending on the structure.

**Q2. What is a WFOE and is it suitable for Indian founders?** A Wholly Foreign-Owned Enterprise (WFOE) is the most common structure for foreign investors in China, allowing 100% ownership without a Chinese JV partner. It is suitable for businesses in manufacturing, trading, consulting, and technology. However, certain restricted sectors (listed in China's Negative List) still require a Chinese joint venture partner.

**Q3. Is it possible to operate a Chinese company entirely from India?** Technically no, Chinese law requires a legal representative (faren daibiao) who is physically accountable in China. This person does not need to be Chinese, but nominee legal representative services are commonly used. All official government correspondence is directed to this individual.

**Q4. How are profits repatriated from China to India?** Profits are repatriated as dividends after paying Chinese CIT (25%) and withholding tax (10% under DTAA). Repatriation requires SAFE clearance and can take 4–8 weeks. Operational smoothness depends on clean bookkeeping and timely annual audit completion.

**Q5. Which Indian businesses typically incorporate in China?** Pharmaceutical companies (Gujarat, Maharashtra), textile and garment traders (Gujarat, Tamil Nadu), IT services firms (Bengaluru, Hyderabad), and commodity trading companies (Delhi NCR, Mumbai) are the most common Indian businesses with China-registered entities.
