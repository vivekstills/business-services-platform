# One Day Permission for Country Liquor

## Short overview

A One Day Permission for Country Liquor is a temporary liquor license issued by state excise departments authorising the sale or service of country liquor (locally produced traditional liquor, often referred to as "desi daru" or "deshi sharab") at a specific event or function on a single day. This permission is typically sought for cultural festivals, community gatherings, fairs, agricultural events, and traditional ceremonies in regions where country liquor consumption is prevalent.

Country liquor is significantly cheaper than IMFL and is regulated under different sections of state excise acts, with separate manufacturing, distribution, and retail frameworks. The One Day Permission allows event organisers to legally serve country liquor at a controlled, time-bound event without obtaining a permanent country liquor license.

## Key features

- Single-day validity
- Specific event or location designated
- Quantity capped (typically based on guest count and excise norms)
- Source restriction to government-authorised distilleries

## Benefits

- Time-bound, low-commitment liquor service authorisation
- Lower fees compared to permanent licenses
- Enables legal service at cultural and traditional events
- Quick approval (1 to 2 weeks typically)
- No need for permanent premises licensing

## Limitations

- Limited to one specific day
- Strict quantity limits (based on attendee count)
- Heavy penalties for unauthorised quantities or extended hours
- Source restriction, must purchase from authorised state corporation or distillery
- Many urban states heavily restrict or prohibit country liquor

## Applicability

Country liquor licensing is more common in rural areas of: Maharashtra (rural districts), Karnataka (rural areas, certain districts), Madhya Pradesh (rural), Rajasthan (specific districts), Andhra Pradesh and Telangana (rural), Punjab and Haryana (limited), Uttar Pradesh (rural belts). States like Tamil Nadu, Kerala, and metro Mumbai have heavily restricted country liquor.

## Documents required

| Document | Details |
| --- | --- |
| Application form | State excise prescribed form |
| Event details | Date, venue, expected attendance |
| Premises permission | NOC from venue owner |
| Local body permission | Municipal or panchayat NOC |
| Police NOC | Event security clearance |
| Applicant ID | PAN, Aadhaar |
| Liquor procurement plan | From authorised source |

## Process

Submit application 15 to 30 days before event with documents and fees. Excise officer verifies and grants permission. Procure liquor only from designated source on event day.

## Government fees

| Event size | Approximate fee |
| --- | --- |
| Small event (under 100 guests) | ₹2,000 to ₹5,000 |
| Medium event (100 to 500 guests) | ₹5,000 to ₹15,000 |
| Large event (500+ guests) | ₹15,000 to ₹50,000+ |

## Validity and renewal

Single day only, no renewal. Fresh application required for each event.

## Frequently asked questions

**1. Can country liquor be served in a city like Mumbai or Pune under this permission?** Country liquor service in major urban centers is heavily restricted. Most metro and Tier 1 city licenses cover only IMFL events. Check state-specific rules.

**2. Is country liquor cheaper than IMFL for event purposes?** Yes, country liquor costs significantly less per litre than IMFL, making it viable for budget events. However, the procurement quota and permitted brands may be restricted.

**3. What happens if attendees consume more than the permitted quantity?** The licensee is responsible for compliance. Excessive quantities can result in license cancellation, fines, and potential prosecution.

**4. Can foreign nationals or tourists consume country liquor at such events?** Generally yes, but the licensee must ensure all consumers meet legal drinking age and follow state guidelines. Some states restrict foreign nationals to specific liquor categories.

**5. Do I need a separate FSSAI license for serving country liquor at an event?** If food is also served, FSSAI license or temporary food permission is required separately. The liquor permission doesn't cover food service.
