# Proprietorship to partnership transition

## Overview

This is not a formal statutory "conversion" under the Companies or LLP Act. The proprietor and new partner(s) execute a **Partnership Deed** and move the business, assets, and agreed liabilities to the firm through a **Business Transfer Agreement (BTA)**. The proprietorship is wound up in practice; registrations and bank are taken in the **partnership** name.

> [!TIP] **When it helps**  
> You need a co-owner, family member, or co-investor; a deed-based profit share; and you are not yet ready to incorporate a company.

## Key features

- **Partnership Deed** on stamp paper, notarized; ROF registration is **optional** in law but **advisable** in most growth plans.
- A **new** firm bank; you cannot use the old proprietorship account as the firm account.
- **BTA** for a going-concern handover; re-KYC and re-licence in the firm name or cancel old IDs as rules require.

| Item | Note |
|------|------|
| Liability | Default partnership: **unlimited** liability for firm debts |
| Tax | Firm is taxed on income; **salary** and **interest** on capital to partners is deductible in the firm return as per law |
| ROF | Unregistered firms have suit restrictions; ROF **registration** is **advisable** |

## Benefits

- Pool capital and share risk; a clear deed sets profit share, capital, and roles.
- Simpler and lower cost than a CIN-based company for small teams.
- Deed-based partner remuneration can be tax-efficient; run the numbers with a CA.

> [!NOTE] **Limitations**  
> Partners have **unlimited** liability in the classical model. A BTA can trigger tax in the proprietor unless a **reorg** exemption (e.g. **Section 47(iii)**, subject to all conditions) applies. A thin deed can cause future disputes.

## Process

1. Draft the **Partnership Deed** with the ex-proprietor and new partners: capital, profit share, roles, and admission.
2. Execute on **non-judicial** stamp as per state; notarize as local practice needs.
3. Apply for **firm PAN**; open a **new** bank; execute the **BTA** from the proprietorship into the firm.
4. **Surrender** or re-file **GST**; use **ITC-02** on a qualifying going-concern move when the law allows.
5. Update **MSME**, **trade**, **FSSAI**, and other licences; novate or re-sign contracts in the **firm** name.

## Tax impact (summary)

The BTA is **taxable** unless you meet a **reorg** exemption (e.g. **Section 47(iii)**: sole proprietor becomes partner with at least 50% profit share for 5 years and no other consideration, with all other IT Act conditions). **GST** on going-concern transfer is often exempt (Notification 12/2017) if conditions are met. Otherwise map CGT, business income, and GST line by line with your CA.

## Applicability

Sole proprietors in **all** states and UTs. ROF fees and step-by-step forms are set by the **state** where you file.

## Frequently asked questions

**Must the firm register with the Registrar of Firms?** Not **mandatory** by law, but **highly** advisable. An unregistered firm cannot sue in some situations.

**Can the new partner skip fresh capital?** The deed can set capital in cash, kind, or agreed terms. Document everything clearly in the **Partnership** **Deed** and in books.

**Can we keep the sole prop bank?** **No**; the firm needs its **own** bank in the **firm** name.

**What about the old GST?** **Cancel** the proprietorship’s GST and **obtain** a new registration for the firm. **ITC-02** may move credits on a qualifying going-concern handover when rules allow.

**Is partnership always cheaper than a company?** Not always. Compare **30%** firm rate, partner slabs, and admin cost with a company model in a one-page **model** with your **CA** before you lock the deed.
