# Trust Registration

## Overview

A Trust is a legal arrangement in which the author (settlor) transfers property to trustees to be held and managed for the benefit of beneficiaries. Trusts are governed by the Indian Trusts Act, 1882 (for private trusts) and various state-level Public Trust Acts (for public charitable trusts). Trusts are ideal for charitable, religious, educational, and family estate planning purposes.

## Features

A trust requires a minimum of 2 trustees. Private trusts have identified beneficiaries, while public trusts serve the public at large. The trust is constituted through a Trust Deed executed on non-judicial stamp paper. The trustees manage the trust property and income for the trust's objects. Once created, a public charitable trust is generally irrevocable. It has a separate legal identity for tax purposes.

## Advantages

Trusts offer a simple and inexpensive setup for charitable or private purposes. They are eligible for 12A and 80G tax exemptions, CSR funds, and FCRA registration (for foreign donations). Trusts offer great flexibility in governance through the Trust Deed. They are not subject to the intense compliance regime applicable to companies. Trusts are effective for perpetual management of assets for specific purposes.

## Limitations

Public trusts are difficult to modify or revoke once created. Governance depends heavily on the terms of the Trust Deed. Trust deeds must be meticulously drafted to avoid future disputes. State-level variations create procedural differences ,  Maharashtra requires dual registration under the Bombay Public Trusts Act. Trusts have lower institutional credibility than Section 8 companies for large donors.

## Applicability

Trust registration applies across India. Public trusts in Maharashtra and Gujarat are registered under the Bombay Public Trusts Act, 1950 with the Charity Commissioner. Other states (Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, Delhi, Telangana, West Bengal, Kerala, Uttar Pradesh, Rajasthan, Punjab, Haryana, Madhya Pradesh, etc.) register trusts with the local Sub-Registrar.

## Process

Choose a unique name for the trust. Draft the Trust Deed including name, objects, settlor's contribution (settlement amount ,  minimum Rs. 1,000 typically), trustees' details, and operational rules. Execute the Trust Deed on non-judicial stamp paper (value varies by state and settlement amount). Get it signed by the settlor, trustees, and two witnesses. Register the deed at the Sub-Registrar's office (or Charity Commissioner in Maharashtra/Gujarat). Apply for PAN and 12A/80G registration.

## Timeline

Trust registration is completed in 10 - 20 days. Drafting the Trust Deed: 2-3 days. Stamp duty payment: 1-2 days. Sub-Registrar registration: 5-10 days. PAN issuance: 7-15 days. 12A/80G application post-registration: 1-3 months. FCRA registration (if required): 3-6 months.

## FAQs

**Q1. What is the difference between a private and public trust?** A private trust benefits specific, identifiable individuals (e.g., family members), governed by the Indian Trusts Act, 1882. A public trust benefits the public at large for charitable/religious purposes, governed by state-specific Public Trust Acts.

**Q2. Is registration of a trust mandatory?** Registration is not strictly mandatory for validity, but a registered trust is essential to claim 12A/80G benefits, open bank accounts smoothly, receive donations, purchase immovable property, and gain legal enforceability.

**Q3. Can a trust be modified or dissolved?** A private trust may be modified or dissolved as per the Trust Deed. A public charitable trust is generally irrevocable ,  any modification requires court approval under the Cy-près doctrine or Charity Commissioner approval (in Maharashtra/Gujarat).

**Q4. How is trust income taxed?** A trust with valid 12A registration is exempt on income applied for charitable purposes. If 85% of income is not applied in the year, it must be accumulated per Sections 11 - 13. Without 12A, the trust is taxed at the maximum marginal rate.

**Q5. Can trustees receive remuneration?** Trustees can receive reasonable remuneration for services rendered if expressly permitted in the Trust Deed. However, excessive remuneration or benefits to trustees (or their relatives) can lead to denial of tax exemption under Section 13.
