# Certificate of incumbency

## Overview

A **Certificate of Incumbency** (also called an incumbency certificate or, in some places, a certificate of good standing) is an official or company-issued document that certifies the **current** officers, directors, shareholders, and often **authorised signatories** of a company at a point in time. In India, the **Companies Act, 2013** does not use one rigid statutory name for a single “incumbency” form, but banks and parties abroad still ask for a document that answers: *who may legally act for this company right now*.

Such certificates are common for **foreign banks** opening accounts, **cross-border** contracts, **FEMA** and **RBI**-related KYC, **M&A** diligence, and **apostille** and legalisation. They are usually **drafted** to match the counterparty’s checklist, then signed under a **seal** (if used) and **notarised**; for **Hague** countries, **MEA apostille** often follows.

## Key features

- **Current officers:** Directors, KMPs, and sometimes officers with titles the foreign side expects.
- **Shareholding (where required):** Names and percentages, especially in private companies.
- **Signatories:** Who can bind the company for banking or deals.
- **Dated, point-in-time** snapshot, not a permanent status for all time.
- **Execution:** Company Secretary, directors, sometimes common seal, board resolution in support.
- **Notarisation and apostille** for international use when requested.
- **Custom scope:** Banking pack, **specimen signatures**, and resolutions can be bundled as needed.

## Benefits

- **Overseas banking** KYC and account opening.
- **Contract** counterparties can verify who signs for India.
- **FEMA, RBI, FDI**-related packs often need authority proof.
- **Disputes and arbitration** may use a certified authority structure.
- **AML** and bank policy in many countries expect this or an equivalent.
- **Franchise, licence, and** international partner onboarding.

## Limitations

- **Stale data:** A change of directors or signatories can make the old certificate outdated; refresh after material changes.
- **No one Indian statutory** format for all uses; you must match the other side’s spec.
- **Apostille time** (often a week or more) adds lead time; rush fees may apply.
- **Each bank** has its own template expectations; errors cause rejections and redrafts.
- **Not a full substitute** for a **power of attorney** for a one-off large transaction, though often used together.
- **Not the same** as a **Certificate of Incorporation** or **MGT-7A**; each serves a different role.

## Process

1. **Collect the foreign** party’s list (directors, shareholders, signatories, resolution wording).
2. **Verify** against **MCA** and latest filed forms (e.g. **DIR-12**).
3. **Draft** the certificate to match that list and the **MoA** / **AoA** limits.
4. **Board resolution** authorising the certificate and, if needed, the specific transaction.
5. **Sign and seal** as per the company’s practice; attach schedules if the bank asked for a cap table.
6. **Notary**; then **apostille** (MEA) for Hague use, or **legalisation** for non-Hague.
7. **Send** the original or certified copies as the counterparty requires.

## Documents required (typical)

| Document | Notes |
|----------|--------|
| Certificate of incorporation | Legal existence |
| MoA and AoA | Authority and object clauses |
| Register of directors / latest MCA | Alignment with **RoC** |
| Register of members or cap table | If shareholding is certified |
| Board resolution | Authorising the certificate and signing |
| Recent filings | **DIR-12**, **MGT-7A** as relevant |
| Director PAN, passport | KYC, especially for foreign use |
| Specimen signatures | If the pack includes banking |
| Common seal | If the company still uses a seal for this purpose |

**LLP:** A parallel certificate for **designated partners** and the **LLP agreement** is used with a similar notary and apostille path.

## Applicability

**Private and public** companies, **LLPs** with **foreign** partners or banks, and any Indian entity in **cross-border** deals, **inward and outward** investment, and **overseas** account opening. **Pan-India**; place of registration does not change the concept, but local notary and apostille channels may differ in logistics.

## Validity

No fixed **statutory** “expiry” for a certificate, but most banks accept **3–6** months from issue for use. After **any** change in **directors** or **signatories**, issue a new certificate. Some banks want a **fresh** document **annually** for active relationships.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is it the same as a certificate of good standing?** **Related but different.** *Good standing* or *status* often confirms the company is active and compliant on **RoC** filings. *Incumbency* focuses on *who* runs and signs. Many foreign banks ask for **both**.

**Who can issue it for an Indian company?** Often a **Practising Company Secretary** or the **directors** under **board** approval and **seal** (if used), following the counterparty and **FEMA** practice. It is not like a BVI *registered agent* one-line standard form.

**We need a board resolution and incumbency. Are they the same?** **No.** The **incumbency** is a **status** statement; the **resolution** is a **specific** approval (e.g. to open a named account and list signatories). Many banks need **both**, sometimes **apostilled** **together** or in one pack.

**LLP?** **Yes:** equivalent certificate for **designated partners** and **authority**, with **LLP** deed and **Form** updates checked against **RoC**.

**How fast?** **Drafting and execution** in **1–2** business days is often possible. **Notary** a few days. **Apostille** commonly **5–15** business days, faster with paid channels when available. **End-to-end** for apostille use often **1–2**+ weeks; express can shorten the middle leg.
