# Drug license registration

## Overview

A drug license is a mandatory authorisation to manufacture, sell, distribute, import, or export drugs and to carry on related activities, under the Drugs and Cosmetics Act, 1940 and the Drugs and Cosmetics Rules, 1945. The CDSCO and state drug control organisations issue licences. Common forms are retail (Form 20/21 and related), wholesale (Form 20B/21B and related), manufacturing (Form 25, 28, and variants by sector), and import (Form 8, 10, and the current e-import track). A registered pharmacist in charge is usually required for retail and wholesale, and a qualified technical person for manufacturing, as the rules state. Operating without a licence for a subject activity is a serious offence, with search, seizure, and prosecution in serious cases. Ayurvedic, Siddha, and Unani products also need the correct manufacturing and sale authorisations under the same Act, with different parts of the rules.

## Key features

- Licences are activity-specific: retail, wholesale, manufacturing, import, export, and loan or contract manufacturing, each with its own form and conditions.
- Statutory: no licence, no legal trade in the licensed class.
- Premises must pass inspection, with area, storage, and hygiene requirements that vary by state and licence type.
- Schedules (for example H, H1, X) drive extra record-keeping, display, and prescription rules.
- E-pharmacy and online models still need a physical licenced point of sale or fulfilment, as the Supreme Court and regulator have underlined, pending any final e-pharmacy rules.

## Benefits

- Legal right to place drugs in the Indian supply chain and to import or export in scope.
- Distributors and hospitals deal only with licenced stockists; without a licence you cannot source properly.
- Tenders, insurance, and empanelment expect a current licence.
- Public trust: the display licence number is a standard piece of store credibility.

## Limitations

- A pharmacist in charge (or a replacement named in time) is a live dependency for retail and wholesale; absence is a major compliance gap.
- State-by-state process and field inspection culture can add delay and cost.
- Schedule X and similar lines need tight registers, safe custody, and police reporting where the rules say so.
- Licence suspension or cancellation stops all activity in that class until restored.

## Process

1. Fix the exact licence class (retail, wholesale, manufacture, import) and the right state or CDSCO as per law.
2. Finalise premises: area, cold chain, racking, separate dispensing if needed, and all India layout norms as your inspector expects.
3. Appoint the pharmacist (retail, wholesale) or technical person (manufacture) and gather degrees, state council registration, and appointment letters.
4. File the online or paper application, pay the state fee, and schedule inspection in states that do so.
5. Close Drug Inspector comments with proof of rectification in the time given.
6. On grant, open registers, print labels in line with the rules, display the certificate, and start half-yearly or annual returns as applicable.
7. Track renewal, usually a five-year cycle for many retail and wholesale licences, before expiry; exact validity is on the certificate.

## Applicability

Pharmacy shops, hospital pharmacies, C and F, stockists, importers, and manufacturers in all states and UTs. Each state uses its own portal or office for state licences; import and some central items use CDSCO channels.

## Frequently asked questions

**Can e-pharmacy work without a physical licence?** No. Medicines are dispensed from a licenced point; the draft e-pharmacy rules and Supreme Court line both treat e-pharmacy as subject to the same licencing as physical pharmacies, with dispatch from licenced sites.

**Our pharmacist is leaving. What should we do?** Appoint a new registered person, inform the drug controller, and get the file amended or updated as the state process requires, before the shop runs without a named qualified person in charge.

**Do Ayurvedic products need a drug licence?** Yes, ASU products need the correct ASU manufacturing or sale authorisation; forms and GMP for ASU differ from allopathy.

**Minimum area?** Varies by state. Ten square metres is a figure many states use for retail, but the Drug Inspector is the one who approves; layout and independence of the area matter as much as a single number.

**Can we “transfer” a licence in a business sale?** You usually need a new licence in the new owner’s name. Some states help with succession; do not assume the old file moves automatically on share sale or asset sale.
