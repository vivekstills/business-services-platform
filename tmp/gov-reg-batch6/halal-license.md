# Halal license (halal certification)

## Overview

Halal certification is a private or semi-private assurance that a product, usually food, beverage, cosmetic, or pharmaceutical, and sometimes a kitchen or service, is produced, stored, and handled in line with Shariah rules for what is lawful for Muslim consumers. In India, halal is not a single statutory FSSAI-style licence. Certifying bodies (Indian and international) audit plants and issue certificates that buyers in the GCC, Malaysia, Indonesia, and other markets often require. India is a large exporter of halal-eligible agri, meat, and consumer goods, so the certificate is a commercial and export access tool as much as a faith label in the domestic market.

## Key features

- Product, site, and process-based: a certificate is tied to a plant and a defined product list.
- Facility and ingredient review: all inputs, including additives, must be free of haram or doubtful materials where the scheme requires.
- For meat, slaughter, equipment, and traceability are audited.
- Annual surveillance; export markets need a certifier recognised or accepted by the destination authority (JAKIM, MUI, ESMA, country lists change).
- Digital certificates with QR and serial numbers are now common.
- In India, some political and social discussion exists around the label; brands should be aware of local public messaging.

## Benefits

- Market access in GCC, Malaysia, Indonesia, and other halal import regimes, where law or buyers demand it.
- Premium positioning and community trust in India’s large Muslim market for compliant goods.
- Retail and airline catering can require halal in certain customer segments and routes.
- Often aligns with strong hygiene and supplier discipline (also helps in non-halal channels).

## Limitations

- Not a government food safety approval by itself: FSSAI and other product laws still apply.
- Recognition is not automatic in every country: the importer’s authority must accept the certifier.
- Ongoing audit cost and time for small firms.
- Formula or supplier changes can need re-cert before label change.
- Vegetarian and vegan lines still need process halal in many GCC import rules, because alcohol flavourings, cross-contact, and factory lines matter.

## Process

1. Fix your export or domestic market, then short-list certifiers that the buyer country accepts (example: ESMA, JAKIM, MUI, SFDA, each publishes lists; verify before you sign).
2. File an application, full ingredient and supplier data, and your FSSAI and factory licence.
3. Document review of each raw material, including certificates from ingredient vendors.
4. Onsite audit: flow, storage, line clearance, and for meat, slaughter and storage.
5. Fix nonconformities and re-submit evidence.
6. On pass, get the year-one certificate and label rules.
7. Surveillance visits each year, then renewal.

## Applicability

Food, drinks, animal feed in some cases, meat and poultry, seafood, confectionery, cosmetics with animal or alcohol input, and pharma (gelatin, excipients) for Muslim markets and for export. Strongest pull in states with an export and meat-processing base, but all-India in principle.

## Validity

Usually one year, with a mid-year or annual audit. Some bodies give two years with an interim check. Re-cert for major recipe or line change. Export buyers check “current, not expired” on every load.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is halal mandatory in India for all food?** No for the domestic open market. FSSAI is the public safety and standards regulator. For export to listed Muslim markets, the importer and port rules often need halal in practice.

**Can a non-Muslim company certify?** Yes. The factory and process, not the owner’s religion, are audited. Slaughter must be by trained Muslim slaughter for meat, under the certifier’s way.

**Saudi: which body?** SFDA and related circulars name acceptable foreign certifiers. The list is not static; re-check per shipment and product class.

**Vegetable snack, still need halal?** Many GCC and Asian ports still want a halal or equivalent letter for the plant and non-alcohol, non-pork, clean-line proof. “Vegetarian” is not a full substitute for a formal halal for those buyers.

**Malaysia reject our Indian cert?** JAKIM only takes listed foreign bodies. Re-cert with a JAKIM-approved partner for that export lane.
