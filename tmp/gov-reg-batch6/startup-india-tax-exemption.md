# Startup India Tax Exemption

## Overview

Startup India Tax Exemption refers primarily to the income tax exemption under Section 80-IAC of the Income Tax Act, 1961, available exclusively to eligible startups recognised by DPIIT under the Startup India initiative. This exemption allows eligible startups to claim a 100% deduction on profits and gains from business for any 3 consecutive assessment years out of the first 10 years from the year of incorporation. In addition to Section 80-IAC, the Startup India framework includes several other tax-related benefits: Tax Benefit Section / Provision Beneficiary 3-year income tax holiday Section 80-IAC DPIIT-recognised startups (with IMB approval) Angel tax exemption Section 56(2)(viib) exemption DPIIT-recognised startups on share issuance Capital gains exemption on residential property Section 54GB Individual investors investing in eligible startups Capital gains exemption on sale of LTCG assets Section 54EE Individuals investing in Startup India Fund of Funds Carry forward of losses Section 79 relaxation DPIIT-recognised startups ,  losses can be carried forward even on shareholding change

The Section 80-IAC exemption is the most significant of these benefits ,  for a startup with ₹5 crore annual profit, three tax-free years save approximately ₹3.9 crore in income tax (at 26% effective rate) ,  a massive saving that directly improves runway and reinvestment capacity.

## Features

100% profit deduction: Complete income tax exemption on profits for 3 chosen years ,  zero tax liability on business income for those years Choice of 3 years: The startup can choose any 3 consecutive assessment years out of the first 10 years to avail the exemption ,  allowing strategic timing to maximise benefit IMB approval required: The Income Tax Department does not automatically grant 80-IAC exemption to all DPIIT-recognised startups ,  a separate application to the Inter-Ministerial Board (IMB) is required Minimum Alternate Tax (MAT) applicability: Section 80-IAC exemption does not exempt startups from MAT ,  MAT at 15% (plus surcharge and cess) may still apply; for startups incorporated after March 31, 2024, the amended provisions may affect MAT applicability Angel tax exemption (Section 56(2)(viib)): DPIIT-recognised startups are exempt from the "angel tax" on shares issued at a premium ,  provided conditions are met (including aggregate paid-up share capital and premium not exceeding ₹25 crore as per recent amendments) Section 79 relaxation: Normally, a company cannot carry forward and set-off losses if 51% of shareholders change. DPIIT-recognised startups are exempt from this restriction ,  allowing loss carry-forward even through multiple funding rounds that change shareholder composition

## Benefits

Direct tax saving: 3 years of 100% income tax exemption translates to direct cash conservation equal to 25–26% of profits in each exempt year ,  money that can be reinvested into growth Improved unit economics for investors: Tax-exempt profits during the growth phase make startup investments more attractive ,  supporting better valuation and easier fundraising Angel investment facilitation: Exemption from angel tax (Section 56(2)(viib)) removes a significant friction point that has historically deterred angel investors from investing in early-stage Indian startups Flexible year selection: Choosing the optimal 3 years for exemption (typically the years with the highest profitability) allows tax-efficient planning ,  startups can use the loss years without exemption and the profit years with exemption Loss carry-forward protection: Section 79 relaxation ensures that early-stage losses (common in the first few years) are not forfeited due to shareholding changes in funding rounds ,  protecting the tax asset for future set-off Capital gains benefits for investors: Section 54GB and 54EE exemptions make startup investment more attractive for individual investors by providing capital gains tax relief ,  indirectly improving the startup's access to capital

## Limitations

IMB approval is rigorous: Not all DPIIT-recognised startups receive 80-IAC approval ,  the IMB evaluates the innovation, technology, and scalability of the business model carefully; generically described businesses face rejection MAT is not exempt: Even with 80-IAC exemption, startups may be subject to Minimum Alternate Tax (MAT) at 15% of book profits ,  reducing (but not eliminating) the tax benefit for some startups Revenue-stage dependency: The 80-IAC exemption is only valuable for startups that are actually generating profits ,  pre-revenue or deep-loss-making startups gain no immediate benefit Angel tax exemption conditions: The exemption from Section 56(2)(viib) is subject to conditions including investment value limits ,  DPIIT-recognised startups receiving investment from non-registered investors above prescribed limits may still face angel tax complications 10-year and ₹100 crore window: Both the DPIIT Recognition and the 80-IAC benefit are time-limited ,  startups that don't reach profitability within the 10-year window lose the exemption opportunity Retroactive risk: If a startup is later found to have obtained DPIIT Recognition on the basis of incorrect information, the income tax benefits may be reversed with interest and penalties

## Applicability

The Section 80-IAC exemption applies to eligible startups meeting ALL of the following: Criterion Requirement DPIIT Recognition Must have valid DPIIT Recognition Certificate Entity type Private Limited Company or LLP (partnership firms are NOT eligible for 80-IAC) Incorporation date On or after April 1, 2016 (for IMB applications under current provisions) Annual turnover Below ₹100 crore in any year Nature Engaged in innovation, development, deployment of new products/processes/services IMB approval Must obtain specific IMB (Inter-Ministerial Board) approval for 80-IAC

Applicable pan-India ,  all states and UTs. Eligible startups anywhere in India can apply for IMB approval.

## Process

Step 1 ,  Ensure DPIIT Recognition Obtain DPIIT Recognition Certificate from the Startup India portal (startupindia.gov.in) ,  mandatory prerequisite. Step 2 ,  IMB Application on Startup India Portal Log in to the Startup India portal. Navigate to "Tax Exemption ,  80IAC" under the Benefits section. Fill and submit the detailed IMB application form. Step 3 ,  Detailed Business Documentation Prepare a comprehensive document package ,  audited financials, business plan, innovation description, product documentation, and team profiles. This is the most document-intensive step. Step 4 ,  IMB Review The Inter-Ministerial Board ,  constituted by DPIIT with members from DPIIT, CBDT, and other relevant ministries ,  reviews the application. The IMB may seek clarifications or additional information. Step 5 ,  IMB Approval If satisfied, the IMB approves the application and issues an acknowledgement. DPIIT notifies the applicant of the IMB's decision. Approval timelines can range from 2–6 months. Step 6 ,  Filing Income Tax Return with 80-IAC Deduction After IMB approval, the startup claims the 80-IAC deduction in its Income Tax Return (ITR) for the chosen assessment years. The IMB approval reference number is cited in the ITR. Step 7 ,  Angel Tax Exemption (if applicable) For angel tax exemption under Section 56(2)(viib), DPIIT Recognition alone is sufficient (subject to conditions) ,  no separate IMB approval needed for angel tax exemption.

## Validity

The 80-IAC exemption applies for any 3 consecutive assessment years out of the first 10 years from the year of incorporation. The DPIIT Recognition must be valid at the time of claiming the exemption. The IMB approval, once granted, remains effective for the 3 chosen years ,  it does not need to be renewed annually. The exemption years must be chosen strategically ,  once a year is chosen as a "non-exempt" year, it cannot be changed retroactively.

## Frequently asked questions

1. Our startup is DPIIT-recognised but we haven't applied for 80-IAC exemption and we're in our 5th year. Is it too late? No, it is not too late ,  but act quickly. The 80-IAC exemption is available for any 3 consecutive years within the first 10 years. You still have 5 years of the eligibility window remaining. Apply for IMB approval immediately. The exemption can be claimed for future profitable years ,  even if you've already filed ITRs for past years without the exemption, you cannot revise closed assessments retroactively to add 80-IAC (subject to specific circumstances). Planning the remaining exempt years carefully with a CA is strongly advisable. 2. Our startup has never made a profit. Can we "bank" the 80-IAC exemption for future profitable years? Yes ,  and this is precisely the intent of the flexible year selection mechanism. If your startup is loss-making in the early years, you don't need to use any of your exempt years during those years. Once the startup reaches profitability (within the 10-year window), you select your 3 consecutive exempt years starting from that point. You choose WHEN to start the 3-year clock ,  the key is that (a) your DPIIT Recognition must be active, and (b) the IMB approval must be obtained before the years you wish to claim. 3. What is "angel tax" and how does DPIIT Recognition help avoid it? Section 56(2)(viib) of the Income Tax Act (popularly called "angel tax") taxes the amount received by a company from an investor above the Fair Market Value (FMV) of shares as "income from other sources" ,  effectively taxing the startup on the premium it receives over FMV. DPIIT-recognised startups are exempt from Section 56(2)(viib) subject to conditions ,  primarily that the aggregate paid-up share capital and securities premium after the proposed issue does not exceed ₹25 crore (excluding certain investor categories). This exemption is critical because most early-stage startups issue shares at significant premiums over FMV (based on future growth expectations), and angel tax would otherwise impose a heavy and disproportionate tax burden on capital raising. 4. Can a startup claim 80-IAC exemption for a year in which it also claimed carry-forward of losses? The 80-IAC exemption covers profits ,  in a loss year, there are no profits to exempt. Section 80-IAC and loss carry-forward work at different stages: loss years generate tax losses that are carried forward to be set off against future profits; when those future profits arise, the startup can choose to claim 80-IAC exemption (effectively exempting those profits) rather than set off the brought-forward losses. Setting off losses first and then applying 80-IAC optimisation requires careful multi-year tax planning with a CA ,  the sequencing significantly affects total tax paid over the startup's life. 5. Our startup was incorporated in 2018. Can we still apply for IMB approval in 2026? Yes. As of 2026, a startup incorporated in 2018 is in its 8th year ,  within the 10-year eligibility window. You can apply for IMB approval now. If approved, you can claim 80-IAC exemption for any 3 consecutive assessment years within the remaining window (i.e., from AY 2026-27 onwards, you have approximately 2–3 assessment years of eligibility remaining depending on the exact incorporation date). Delaying further would narrow the window for claiming the benefit ,  apply for IMB approval immediately to preserve your ability to use the exemption.
