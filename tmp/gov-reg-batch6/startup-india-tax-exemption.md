# Startup India tax exemption (80-IAC and related)

## Overview

The main Startup India–linked income tax measure is the deduction under **Section 80-IAC** of the Income Tax Act, 1961. For eligible private companies and LLPs that are DPIIT-recognised and then approved by the **Inter-Ministerial Board (IMB)**, 100% of the profits and gains of the business can be excluded for any three consecutive assessment years out of the first ten from incorporation, subject to the law in force. You choose which three-year block, often when profits are meaningful. DPIIT recognition alone is not 80-IAC: you need a full IMB application, supporting documents, and, if the board agrees, a decision you cite in the ITR for those years. Other Startup-linked items include **Section 56(2)(viib)** “angel” premium relief (with conditions, including a cap on post-issue paid up and premium in the relevant rules) for DPIIT-recognised startups, **Section 54GB** and **54EE**-style benefits for some investors, and **Section 79** loss carry in certain cap-table changes, each with their own test. **MAT** and amended company tax rules (including for companies incorporated after 31 March 2024) can limit the *cash* effect of 80-IAC in part of the life; take professional advice. This page is a map, not a substitute for the current Act, forms, and your auditor.

## Key features (80-IAC)

- 100% of eligible profit can be out of the tax base in the three chosen years, if the IMB has approved the startup and the ITR claims match.
- You elect the three years within the 10-year window, subject to the rules, often to overlap with early profit years.
- IMB is a separate, heavier review than DPIIT recognition, not all recognised startups are approved.
- **Partnership firms are not in the 80-IAC company/LLP line**; check the current Act.
- 56(2)(viib) is often used at funding rounds, without IMB, but the premium and investor classes must be in the current safe harbour.
- 54GB and 54EE: residential property and certain asset re-invest paths into eligible startups, with limits, for resident investors, under the then law.

## Benefits

- Large cash saving in profit years, improving runway and follow-on.
- 56(2)(viib) in range helps priced rounds with foreign and domestic angels, subject to the cap and investor rules.
- Loss carry in fast-changing cap tables, where Section 79 is relaxed for qualifying recognised startups, keeps early-year losses usable, subject to the text of the time.
- Investor pitch and term-sheet stories often mention “IMB 80-IAC path” and “angel safe harbour” when true.

## Limitations

- IMB is selective; a generic services firm may fail the innovation and genuine startup test.
- If you are pre-profit, 80-IAC does not return cash; you plan for future profit years in the 10-year window.
- 56(2)(viib) has a rupees 25-crore style guardrail on post-issue base and premium, excluding some investor classes; foreign VC and a few other categories are treated differently; read the live Finance Act and CBDT circulars.
- MAT at 15% of book (plus surcharges) may still run in a company where 80-IAC is claimed, in part of the period, unless the set you sit in is exempt; post-2024 incorporate rules and concessional 22% or MAT interplay need a live table from your CA.
- If DPIIT data was false, the benefit can be challenged with interest and penalty risk.

## Process (80-IAC, outline)

1. Obtain and keep a valid **DPIIT** recognition in the class that matches the 80-IAC pre-condition.
2. On the Startup India portal, open the 80-IAC / IMB application after the “Benefits” path as live on the site.
3. Assemble: audited or examined accounts, cap table, full innovation paper, product evidence, user or revenue data, and team biographies, as the form asks in your year.
4. File and pay attention to e-mail from IMB: often two or three rounds of detail.
5. If approved, keep the order number, map it to the three AYs you will claim, and file ITRs with 80-IAC in those years, with a CA sign-off in your stat audit line if applicable.
6. For 56(2)(viib), work with a CA and company secretary on the Form 1 and filing path for the issue of shares, not only DPIIT, as the return year requires.

## Applicability (80-IAC)

DPIIT-recognised private limited company or **LLP,** under the incorporation date, turnover, and innovation tests of the 80-IAC section and the IMB, not formed only to split a tax asset from an old business, and in active innovation as the board reads it. **Not** a partnership or proprietorship. Pan-India.

## Validity

You can only claim 80-IAC in three assessment years in one continuous block, within the first 10 from incorporation, and only after and while IMB approves, and you still meet the definition for those years. The choice of years, once the law allows, is strategic with your CA. 56(2)(viib) follows its own per-issue and per-year compliance.

## Frequently asked questions

**DPIIT done, in year 5, still time for 80-IAC?** You must still be inside the 10-year window and meet all tests. IMB is open even late in the 10, but a tight remaining window to put three profit years in can be a planning issue.

**Never profitable: waste of IMB?** You can time IMB to align with the first years you expect real profit, subject to the application window; loss years are not 80-IAC years, but 56(2)(viib) and others may still help.

**What is angel tax?** Broadly, tax on the amount above fair market value of shares, under **Section 56(2)(viib);** for qualifying DPIIT startups, much of the risk is off if the issue is in the current safe rule set.

**80-IAC and old losses?** 80-IAC and loss set-off are different rules; the order of set-off, MAT, and which years you burn as 80 years needs a year-by-year model with your CA.

**Incorporated 2018, IMB in 2026, ok?** If you are still within 10 years from incorporation, under turnover, and IMB is willing, the window to pick three AYs in the 10 is still a math question with your CAs, do not wait until year 9 without a file.
